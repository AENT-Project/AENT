Romans Chapter 1

Paul Introduces Himself and His Message

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul opens his letter by identifying himself as a servant of Jesus the Messiah chosen by God to be an apostle and set apart to tell the good news. He traces this good news back through the prophets and the Holy Scriptures — it is about God's Son who as a human being was born into the family of King David but through the Holy Spirit was shown to be the powerful Son of God by being raised from the dead. He is Jesus the Messiah and Lord. Through Jesus God gave Paul the privilege of being an apostle with a mission to call people from every nation to trust and obey him for the honor of his name. The Roman believers are among those who have been called to belong to Jesus the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

A servant of Jesus the Messiah — the Greek word is doulos which means slave or bondservant. Paul does not open with his credentials or his education or his status. He opens by identifying himself as owned by Jesus. In the ancient world a slave's identity was defined by who owned them. Paul is saying his entire identity is defined by belonging to Jesus. That is not false modesty. It is the most foundational statement he could make about himself. Everything else follows from it.

Set apart to tell his good news — the Greek word for set apart is aphorismenos from the same root as Pharisee which means separated one. Paul was a Pharisee — a separated one — before his conversion. Now he is still set apart but for an entirely different purpose. The separateness that had once meant keeping himself from Gentile contamination now meant being designated for a mission to the Gentiles. God used the very shape of Paul's former identity to describe his new one.

Born into the family of King David — Paul establishes that Jesus was genuinely human and genuinely descended from David. This matters because the Hebrew prophets had promised that the Messiah would come through David's line. Jesus was not a spiritual being who appeared to be human. He was a real human being with a real genealogy that connected him to Israel's greatest king. The resurrection did not make him divine — it revealed and confirmed what he already was.

Shown to be the powerful Son of God by being raised from the dead — the Greek word for shown is horizō from which we get horizon. The resurrection marked Jesus out, declared him, designated him as the Son of God with power. Paul is not saying Jesus became the Son of God at the resurrection. He is saying the resurrection was the definitive public declaration of what he already was. The horizon is what makes visible what was always there.

To call people from every nation to trust and obey him — the phrase trust and obey translates the Greek hypakoē pisteōs which can be rendered obedience of faith or the obedience that comes from faith. Paul's mission was not to produce intellectual agreement with propositions about Jesus. It was to call people to a life of trust that expresses itself in changed direction. Faith and obedience are not two separate things in Paul's understanding. Genuine trust produces the kind of life that reflects what you actually believe.

Why It Still Matters

Paul opens his most theologically dense letter by grounding everything in a person and a story rather than in abstract principles. The good news is about God's Son. It has a history — promised through the prophets. It has a human face — born into David's family. It has a decisive event — raised from the dead. Everything Paul will argue theologically in the following fifteen chapters rests on this foundation. Before the theology comes the story. Before the doctrine comes the person. Romans is not a theological system that happens to mention Jesus. It is an explanation of what God has done through a specific person in a specific moment in history.

Greeting to the Church in Rome

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul writes to all the believers in Rome. God loves them and has called them to be his holy people. He wishes them grace and peace from God the Father and from the Lord Jesus the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

God loves you and has called you to be his holy people — the identity Paul gives the Roman believers has two parts. Loved by God and called to be holy. The loved comes first and the called follows from it. They are holy — set apart — because they are loved, not loved because they are holy. The order matters enormously. A community that understands itself as holy first tends toward pride and exclusion. A community that understands itself as loved first tends toward gratitude and welcome. Paul establishes the order at the very opening of the letter.

Grace and peace — this greeting combines the standard Greek greeting charis (grace, favor) with the standard Jewish greeting shalom (peace, wholeness). Paul used this combined greeting in every letter he wrote. It was not a formula he had inherited. He created it. The combination announced something — a community where the Greek world and the Jewish world met in a common greeting, where the favor of God and the wholeness of God were offered together to everyone. The greeting itself was a small embodiment of the Gospel Paul was about to explain.

Paul had never been to Rome — he is writing to a church he did not plant and has not visited. He greets them as fully his own — all of you in Rome, God loves you, his holy people — without any reservation or qualification. The Roman church was not a second-tier community because Paul had not founded it. It was fully the people of God receiving the same grace and peace as any church Paul had personally established. His letter to them is an act of apostolic inclusion.

Why It Still Matters

May you have grace and peace from God our Father and from the Lord Jesus the Messiah. The greeting is a wish that has become a prayer across two thousand years. Every time the Church has gathered it has in some form wished this for the people gathered — the unearned favor of God and the wholeness that comes from right relationship with him. Grace and peace are not just nice words. They describe two of the most fundamental human needs — to be accepted without deserving it and to be made whole from the inside out. Paul wished both for strangers he had never met. The wish was also a declaration that both were available through the one he was about to spend fifteen chapters explaining.

Paul's Desire to Visit Rome

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul thanks God for all the Roman believers because people all over the world are talking about their faith. He calls God as his witness that he serves him with all his heart by telling the good news about his Son and that he always remembers them in his prayers. He prays that if it is God's will he will finally be able to come and see them. He wants to share something that will help them stand strong in their faith and he expects they will be able to encourage each other. He has planned to visit many times but something has always stopped him. He wants to see good results among them as he has seen among other non-Jewish peoples. He has a duty to all people — educated and uneducated, wise and not wise. That is why he is so eager to tell the good news to them in Rome.

What You Might Not Know

People all over the world are talking about your faith — the Roman church's reputation had spread widely before Paul ever wrote to them. Rome was the capital of the empire — the city where roads ended and from which information radiated outward. A church in Rome that was genuinely transformed by the Gospel would be visible and discussed in a way that a church in a provincial city would not be. The Roman believers may not have realized how widely their community was being observed and discussed. Paul tells them.

I want to share something with you that will help you stand strong — the Greek word is stērizō meaning to strengthen, establish, or make firm. Paul's purpose in visiting was not to showcase his own ministry or correct errors he had heard about. It was to strengthen what was already there. And then he adds something remarkable — when I come we will be able to encourage each other. The apostle expected to be encouraged by the community he was coming to strengthen. That mutuality — the teacher expecting to receive as well as give — is the mark of genuine rather than hierarchical relationship.

Something has always stopped me — Paul does not say what stopped him. His other letters suggest the demands of his active church planting work in regions that had not yet heard the Gospel kept him from going where the Gospel had already arrived. He did eventually reach Rome — as a prisoner as Acts 28 records. The circumstances of his arrival were not what he had hoped when he wrote this letter. But the arrival happened. The promise to come was kept even if the manner of keeping it was unexpected.

I have a duty to all people — the Greek word opheiletēs means debtor or one who owes something. Paul understood his apostleship as a debt — not a privilege he had earned but an obligation he had been given. He owed the Gospel to educated and uneducated, wise and simple. The Gospel was not for the sophisticated or the spiritually advanced. It was for everyone and Paul felt himself obligated to everyone. That sense of owing the Gospel to people who had not heard it drove everything he did.

Why It Still Matters

I have a duty to all people — those who are educated and those who are not, those who are wise and those who are not. Paul's sense of obligation crossed every social and intellectual boundary. The Gospel was not more natural for the sophisticated than for the simple or more accessible to the educated than to the unlearned. The debt Paul felt was to everyone equally. The AENT project stands in that same tradition — plain language scripture for people who have no church background, no theological training, no familiarity with religious vocabulary. The duty Paul felt to the uneducated and the simple is the same duty that drives making the text accessible to everyone who will pick it up.

The Power of the Good News

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul declares he is not ashamed of the good news. It is the power of God to save everyone who trusts him — first for the Jews and now for everyone else too. This good news shows how God makes people right with himself from trusting him at the start all the way to the end. As the Scripture says the person who is right with God will live by trusting him.

What You Might Not Know

I'm not ashamed of the good news — this declaration was not rhetorical. In Rome the Gospel was genuinely embarrassing by the standards of the culture. It proclaimed a crucified Jewish criminal from the provinces as the Lord of the universe. Crucifixion was the most shameful form of death available — reserved for the lowest of criminals and enemies of the state. Educated Romans would have found the Gospel laughable. The community that believed it was largely composed of slaves freed persons and working people rather than the elite. Paul's declaration that he was not ashamed was a direct confrontation with the social pressure the Roman believers faced every day.

The power of God to save — the Greek word is dynamis from which we get dynamite. The Gospel was not a religious philosophy or a moral improvement program. It was power — divine power — directed toward a specific outcome which was salvation. The good news did not just explain how to be saved. It was itself the instrument through which God's saving power was released into the world. When the Gospel was proclaimed the power of God was active.

First for the Jews and now for everyone else too — the order matters to Paul throughout Romans. The Gospel came to Israel first — through the prophets the Scriptures the Messiah himself. Then it extended to everyone else. This is not a statement that Jews are more important than Gentiles. It is a statement about the shape of the story. God had been working with Israel for centuries preparing the world for what was coming. The arrival of the Messiah was the fulfillment of that long preparation. The Gentile inclusion was not the replacement of Israel but the completion of what God had always been working toward through Israel.

From trusting him at the start all the way to the end — the Greek phrase ek pisteōs eis pistin literally means from faith to faith. The righteousness God gives is not given once at conversion and then maintained by human effort. It is received by faith and sustained by faith from beginning to end. There is no point in the Christian life where trust in God is replaced by self-sufficiency. The same faith that opens the door is the same faith that walks through it every day for the rest of your life.

The person who is right with God will live by trusting him — Paul quotes Habakkuk 2. The prophet had written in a time of crisis when Babylon was about to overwhelm Judah. How do you live faithfully when everything around you is falling apart? Habakkuk's answer was trust. The righteous person lives by trusting God even when the evidence of God's faithfulness is not visible. Paul takes this word from a crisis moment in Israel's history and makes it the thesis of his letter — this is how righteousness works, this is how life works, this is the foundation of everything he is about to explain.

Why It Still Matters

I'm not ashamed of the good news. The declaration is worth sitting with because the pressure that produced it has not gone away. The Gospel still sounds foolish to sophisticated ears. A crucified savior, a bodily resurrection, forgiveness of sin through another person's death — none of this fits naturally into the frameworks most people in any culture bring to it. The temptation to soften the message, to make it more culturally acceptable, to lead with something more palatable before getting to the difficult parts — that temptation was real in Rome and it is real now. Paul's declaration was not bravado. It was a decision to let the Gospel be what it actually is rather than what would have been easier to explain.

The Consequences of Rejecting God

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

God shows his anger from heaven against all the sin and evil of people who hide the truth by the wrong things they do. What can be known about God is plain to them because God has made it plain — ever since creation people have been able to see his invisible qualities his eternal power and divine nature in everything he has made. So people have no excuse. They knew about God but did not honor or thank him. Their thinking became worthless and their confused hearts grew dark. Claiming to be wise they became fools and traded the glory of the immortal God for statues. So God let them go their own way to follow sinful desires and do shameful things with each other's bodies. They traded the truth of God for a lie and worshiped created things instead of the Creator. Because of this God let them follow shameful passions including same-sex relations. Since they did not think it important to know God he let them have useless minds that led them to every kind of sin — evil greed hate jealousy murder fighting lying gossip and worse. They know God's law says people who live like this deserve to die yet they not only keep doing these things but cheer for others who do them too.

What You Might Not Know

God shows his anger from heaven — this is the first mention of God's anger in Romans and it comes before the Gospel is fully explained. Paul is not trying to frighten people into belief. He is establishing the reality that makes the Gospel necessary. If there is no problem there is no need for a solution. The anger of God is not emotional volatility or cosmic irritability. It is God's settled opposition to everything that destroys what he made and loves. The word wrath in the New Testament describes a consistent orientation rather than a sudden outburst.

People have no excuse — this is one of the most important theological claims in Romans. Paul argues that the created world itself reveals enough about God that people are responsible for responding to what they can see. The power and divine nature of God are visible in creation to anyone who looks honestly. This does not mean everyone can figure out the Gospel from looking at trees and stars. It means no one can honestly claim they had no evidence that God existed. The suppression of that evidence — hiding the truth by the wrong things they do — is itself the first act of rebellion Paul describes.

God let them go their own way — this phrase appears three times in this passage. God gave them over. Gave them over. Gave them over. The judgment Paul describes is not God striking people with thunderbolts. It is God releasing people to the consequences of their own choices. The deepest form of judgment in this passage is not punishment from outside but the internal logic of turning away from God playing itself out. When you reject the source of truth your thinking becomes worthless. When you reject the source of life your desires become disordered. The consequences are built into the rebellion itself.

They traded the truth of God for a lie — the exchange language runs through this passage. They traded the glory of the immortal God for statues. They traded the truth for a lie. They traded natural relationships for unnatural ones. The word traded suggests a deliberate choice — something given up in exchange for something received. Paul is not describing people who were simply ignorant. He is describing people who knew enough and chose against it. The tragedy is the deliberateness of the exchange.

The list of sins at the end of the chapter — Paul's catalog of sins that flow from rejecting God is comprehensive and uncomfortable. It includes the dramatic sins people expect — murder, sexual immorality — and the sins people tend to overlook — gossip, pride, failing to show kindness. The list does not allow the reader to feel superior by pointing at the obvious offenders. Everyone is somewhere on the list. That is part of Paul's rhetorical strategy. In Chapter 2 he will turn directly on the person who has been reading Chapter 1 with a sense of righteous agreement and say — you have no excuse either.

Why It Still Matters

They not only keep doing these things — they cheer for others who do them too. Paul's final observation in Chapter 1 is that the deepest form of moral corruption is not just doing wrong but celebrating it in others. The person who sins privately has not fully given themselves to the rebellion. The person who applauds others for the same sin has completed the journey from personal failure to communal endorsement of failure. Paul is not primarily describing a particular sexual sin here — he is describing the complete inversion of moral judgment that happens when people have fully traded the truth of God for a lie. The cheering is the sign that the exchange is complete. Romans 1 ends here not as a conclusion but as a setup — the reader who has been nodding along in agreement is about to be told they are not outside the story they have been reading.

Romans Chapter 2

You Have No Excuse

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Paul turns on the person who has been reading Chapter 1 with a sense of moral superiority. You may think you have an excuse but you do not. If you judge others you are condemning yourself because you do the very same things. God is right when he judges people who do these things. Do you really think you will escape? Do you think God's kindness and patience mean he does not care? His patience is meant to give you time to change. But if you are stubborn and refuse to change you are piling up anger against yourself for the day when God shows his anger and gives to each person exactly what they deserve. Eternal life for those who keep doing good and seeking his glory. Anger and fury for those who are selfish and choose evil. Trouble and suffering for every person who does evil — Jew first then non-Jew. But glory honor and peace for everyone who does good — Jew first then non-Jew. God does not play favorites.

What You Might Not Know

You may think you have an excuse but you don't — Chapter 1 ended with a catalog of Gentile sin that a Jewish reader or a morally serious person would have nodded along with. Chapter 2 opens by targeting exactly that reader. The rhetorical move is deliberate. Paul has drawn the reader into agreement with his diagnosis of human sinfulness and then turned the diagnosis on the one who was agreeing. The person who judges others has just been told they are doing the very things they condemn. The trap has closed.

God is being patient with you so that you'll change your heart and turn to him — the Greek word for change your heart is metanoia — repentance, a turning of the whole person in a new direction. Paul is interpreting the apparent silence of divine judgment not as approval but as opportunity. God's patience is not indifference to sin. It is the grace of a longer window in which to respond. The person who mistakes patience for permission has misread the situation entirely.

You are piling up anger against yourself — the image is of treasure being accumulated. Instead of storing up good things the stubborn person is storing up the consequences of their own refusal. The judgment does not arrive immediately but it is being prepared and the preparation is happening through the person's own choices. Paul does not say God is piling up anger against them. He says they are piling it up against themselves. The agency belongs to the human who refuses to turn.

He will give to each person exactly what they deserve for what they have done — Paul quotes Psalm 62 here. This raises an immediate question for readers of Paul — does this mean salvation is earned by good works? The rest of Romans will answer this at length. What Paul is establishing here is the principle of moral realism — God takes human choices seriously and the outcome reflects them. He is not yet explaining how anyone is rescued from the outcome their choices deserve. That comes in Chapters 3 through 8. First he establishes the problem in full.

God doesn't play favorites — the Greek is ou gar estin prosōpolēmpsia para tō theō — there is no partiality with God. The word prosōpolēmpsia literally means receiving of faces — judging by outward appearance or social status. God does not judge by ethnicity religious heritage social position or any external marker. The Jew who has the law and breaks it is in the same position as the Gentile who never had the law and breaks what they know. The reverse is also true — the Gentile who does good stands equally before God as the Jew who does good. The impartiality cuts both ways.

Why It Still Matters

Do you think his great kindness and patience mean nothing? The misreading of divine patience as divine approval is one of the most persistent human errors in every generation. When judgment does not come immediately people conclude it will not come at all. When consequences are delayed people interpret the delay as a verdict of innocence. Paul's diagnosis is that this misreading is not innocent ignorance — it is a form of contempt for the very kindness that is giving more time to respond. The patience of God is one of his most extraordinary attributes. Treating it as evidence that he does not care is the most profound possible ingratitude.

God Judges Everyone Fairly

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

People who sin without ever hearing God's written law will still be lost. People who sin while having the law will be judged by that law. Just hearing the law does not make a person right with God — only those who obey it will be made right. Sometimes people who are not Jews do the things the law requires even though they have never had the written law. They show that they know in their hearts what is right and wrong. Their own thoughts and conscience tell them when they are doing wrong or right. This will all come out on the day God judges the secret thoughts of everyone through Jesus the Messiah just as the good news says.

What You Might Not Know

People who sin without ever hearing God's written law will still be lost — Paul is addressing a fairness question that every thoughtful person eventually asks. What about people who never had the Bible? Paul's answer is that the standard they are held to is the standard they actually had access to. The person who never heard the written law is not judged by the written law. They are judged by the moral knowledge available to every human being through conscience and creation. This does not mean they are off the hook — it means the hook is proportional to what they were given.

The law written on their hearts — Paul is describing what natural law theologians and moral philosophers have called the moral law accessible to human reason and conscience. Even without revelation people generally know that murder is wrong, that fairness matters, that promises should be kept, that gratitude is owed. The variations across cultures are real but so is the remarkable consistency in basic moral categories. Paul sees this as evidence that the Creator has written something of his moral character into human nature itself.

Their own thoughts accuse them or defend them — the conscience functions as an internal judicial process. It brings charges and it offers defenses. The fact that human beings experience guilt and justification internally — that something in us responds to moral categories even when no external judge is present — is evidence for Paul that the moral law is not merely cultural convention. It is built into what it means to be human.

God judges the secret thoughts of everyone through Jesus the Messiah — the final judgment Paul describes is not only of visible actions but of the interior life. Secret thoughts. Hidden motivations. The things people do when no one is watching and the reasons behind the things they do when people are watching. Nothing is outside the scope of the judgment Paul describes. The judge he names is Jesus the Messiah — which means the one Paul has been identifying as Lord and Savior is also the one before whom every human being will eventually stand.

Why It Still Matters

Sometimes people who are not Jews do the things the law requires even though they have never had the written law. Paul's observation here has significant implications for how the Church relates to people outside its boundaries. Genuine goodness, real integrity, actual kindness — these appear in people who have never heard the Gospel. Paul does not explain this away or dismiss it. He acknowledges it and locates it in the moral capacity God has built into human beings. The person who has never read a Bible but lives with genuine honesty and care for others is not doing nothing. They are responding to something real that God has placed in them. That response does not save them — Paul will explain why in Chapter 3 — but it is real and God sees it.

The Jews and the Law

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Paul addresses the Jewish person directly. You call yourself a Jew. You trust in the law and brag about your relationship with God. You know what he wants. You've been taught the law and know the difference between right and wrong. You believe you are a guide for the blind and a light for people in the dark — you can teach foolish people and instruct children because you have all knowledge and truth in the law. But you teach others — why don't you teach yourself? You tell others not to steal — do you steal? You say people should not commit adultery — do you? You hate false gods — do you steal from their temples? You brag about having the law but you bring shame to God by breaking it. As the Scriptures say the other nations speak evil of God's name because of you.

What You Might Not Know

You call yourself a Jew — Paul is not attacking Jewish identity. He is challenging the assumption that Jewish identity provides automatic standing before God. The Jewish person he addresses has real privileges — the law, knowledge of God's will, the prophets, the covenant. Paul will list these privileges explicitly in Chapter 3. He is not denying them. He is questioning whether possession of the privileges is the same thing as fulfilling what the privileges were given for.

You believe you are a guide for the blind and a light for people in the dark — this language comes from Isaiah where God describes Israel's calling to be a light to the nations. The calling was real. Israel was meant to be the community through which the knowledge of God spread to the rest of the world. Paul's question is whether the Jewish person he addresses is actually fulfilling that calling or merely claiming its status. The calling to be a light is genuine. The light has to actually shine.

Do you steal from their temples — this was a specific and recognizable charge. Jewish law strictly forbade the desecration of pagan temples even while condemning the idols within them. There are references in ancient literature to instances where the line between condemning idolatry and profiting from the wealth associated with it got blurred. Paul is not making a general accusation against all Jewish people. He is using a specific example of the gap between stated principle and actual practice to illustrate his larger point.

The other nations speak evil of God's name because of you — Paul quotes Isaiah 52 which in its original context was directed at Israel in exile. The nations were blaspheming God's name because his people were in captivity — it looked as if their God had been defeated. Paul applies it to a different kind of failure — when the people who claim to represent God live in contradiction to what they represent the watching world draws conclusions about the God they claim. The reputation of God among those who do not believe him is affected by the lives of those who say they do.

Why It Still Matters

You teach others — why don't you teach yourself? The gap between what we teach and what we live is not unique to first-century Judaism. It is one of the most consistent features of human religious life across every tradition and every era. The person who knows the right thing to do and does not do it is not better off than the person who does not know — in Paul's analysis they are worse off because they cannot claim ignorance. Knowledge without corresponding life does not produce righteousness. It produces accountability. The nations speak evil of God's name because of you is the consequence Paul names for that gap. The credibility of the message depends in part on the lives of the messengers.

What Really Makes Someone a Jew

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Being a Jew has value if you obey the law but if you break the law it is as if you were never a Jew at all. If a man who is not a Jew obeys the law won't God accept him as if he were a Jew? In fact the person who obeys the law without the physical mark of a Jew will stand in judgment over the one who has the written law and the physical mark but still breaks the law. A person is not a Jew just because of how they look on the outside. True membership is not something done only to the body. A real Jew is one on the inside. True change happens in the heart — a work of the Spirit not just following a written rule. A person like that does not look for praise from people but from God.

What You Might Not Know

Being a Jew has value if you obey the law — Paul is not saying that Jewish identity is worthless. He will argue in Chapter 3 that being Jewish has great value in many ways. What he is challenging is the assumption that the external marker of Jewish identity provides standing before God independent of the life it was meant to shape. The circumcision — the physical mark — was the sign of the covenant. A sign points to the reality it represents. When the sign is present but the reality is absent the sign becomes empty. Paul is not attacking the sign. He is insisting that the sign must correspond to something real.

The person who obeys the law without the physical mark will stand in judgment over you — this is a striking reversal. The Gentile who lives with genuine integrity stands in a better position before God than the Jew who has every external marker of covenant membership but lives in contradiction to what those markers represent. Paul is not saying Gentile obedience saves. He is establishing the principle that the interior reality matters more than the exterior sign — and he is preparing the reader for what he is about to say about what that interior reality actually is.

A real Jew is one on the inside — Paul is drawing on a long prophetic tradition. Jeremiah had predicted a new covenant where the law would be written on hearts rather than stone tablets. Ezekiel had spoken of God putting his Spirit within his people so they would follow his decrees. Deuteronomy had called Israel to circumcise their hearts. Paul is not inventing a new idea. He is saying the prophets had always pointed beyond the external sign to an interior reality that the external sign was meant to express.

A work of the Spirit not just following a written rule — the Greek contrast is between pneuma and gramma — Spirit and letter. The written rule — however good and true — cannot produce the inner transformation it describes. It can inform the conscience and identify the standard. It cannot change the heart. That is the work of the Spirit. Paul will develop this contrast extensively in Chapter 7 and 8. Here he introduces it briefly at the end of Chapter 2 as the first hint of where the argument is going.

A person like that doesn't look for praise from people but from God — the Greek word for Jew comes from the Hebrew Yehudah which means praise. Paul ends his discussion of what makes a real Jew with a wordplay on the meaning of the name. A genuine member of the people of praise is not someone seeking human approval but someone whose life is directed toward God. The true Jew — and by extension the true member of God's people — is defined not by what others see from the outside but by what God sees on the inside.

Why It Still Matters

True change is something that happens in the heart — a work of the Spirit not just following a written rule. Paul's distinction between letter and Spirit is one of the most important in the entire New Testament. Religion has a persistent tendency to locate faithfulness in external conformity — the right practices the right words the right markers of belonging. Paul insists that these externals are only as good as the interior reality they express. A life shaped by the Spirit rather than merely conforming to written requirements looks different from the outside too — but the difference starts inside. The Church in every generation has had to fight the same tendency Paul is fighting here — the substitution of religious performance for genuine transformation.

Romans Chapter 3

The Advantage of Being a Jew

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

Paul raises the obvious question that follows from Chapter 2 — if being Jewish does not automatically guarantee standing before God what is the point of being Jewish at all. His answer is that there is great value — most importantly God trusted the Jews to keep his holy words. But what if some were unfaithful? Does their failure make God unfaithful? Not at all. God will always be true even if every human being is a liar. Someone might then argue that if human wrongdoing makes God's righteousness shine brighter it is unfair for God to punish the wrongdoer. Paul dismisses this — if God were not fair he could not judge the world. Others might claim that since their lies bring glory to God by contrast why should they be judged as sinners. What Is Happening Here

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 4
What Is Happening Here

Paul asks the precise question that determines whether the blessing of Abraham is available to everyone or only to Jews — when did God accept Abraham's faith as making him right? Was it before or after he received the mark of circumcision? It was before. He was not yet circumcised when God accepted him. He received the mark later as a sign — a seal to show he was already right with God because of the faith he had before the mark came. What You Might Not Know

The chronological argument Paul makes here is precise and checkable from the text of Genesis. God credited Abraham's faith as righteousness in Genesis 15. Abraham was circumcised in Genesis 17 — at least fourteen years later and possibly as many as twenty-nine years later depending on how the chronology is read. The faith came first. The circumcision came after. This sequence is not accidental in Paul's reading — it was God's way of establishing that the covenant relationship was grounded in faith before any physical marker existed. The marker pointed back to what was already there.

A sign — a seal — the circumcision was both a sign pointing to the covenant and a seal confirming it. It did not create the covenant relationship. It confirmed one that already existed through faith. Paul's point is that signs and seals are always secondary to the reality they represent. When the sign is confused with the reality it represents — when the mark is treated as the substance rather than the symbol — the whole system gets inverted. The mark was meant to declare what faith had already accomplished. It was never meant to accomplish what faith was supposed to do.

Abraham is the father of all who trust God — this statement has enormous implications. Abraham's fatherhood is not biological or ethnic. It is spiritual and defined by the same faith Abraham exercised. A Gentile who trusts God the way Abraham trusted God is Abraham's child in the sense that matters most. A Jewish person who has the physical mark but does not walk in Abraham's faith is missing the essential thing the mark was meant to represent. Paul is not attacking Jewish identity. He is recovering the meaning of Jewish identity by connecting it back to its actual source.

Why It Still Matters

The sequence matters — faith first then the sign. Paul's argument from chronology is not a technicality. It reveals something about how God has always worked. The relationship precedes the ritual. The inner reality precedes the outer marker. When rituals and markers are allowed to become the substance rather than the symbol of the relationship they represent the faith community loses its center. Every generation of the Church has had to fight this same tendency — the drift toward treating the forms of faith as the substance of faith. Paul's argument from the order of events in Abraham's life is the permanent corrective to that drift.

What Is Happening Here

If the promise only went to those who keep the law then faith would mean nothing and the promise would be worthless. The law brings anger because people cannot keep it. What You Might Not Know

The promise that Abraham would inherit the world — Paul expands the original promise slightly here. Genesis speaks of Abraham inheriting the land of Canaan and his descendants becoming a great nation. Paul speaks of inheriting the world. He is reading the Abraham promise in light of its ultimate fulfillment — not just one piece of real estate but the whole creation restored and given to God's people. This expansive reading of the covenant promise is consistent with how the prophets developed it. Isaiah spoke of a new heavens and a new earth. Paul understands the Abraham promise as pointing toward that ultimate inheritance.

If the promise only went to those who keep the law then faith would mean nothing — this is a logical argument about the internal coherence of the covenant. If keeping the law were the condition of the promise then the promise would depend entirely on human performance. Given what Paul has established about human inability to keep the law consistently the promise would become effectively null — no one could ever be confident they had qualified. A promise that depends on imperfect human performance is not really a promise. It is a performance standard with a reward attached. God's covenant with Abraham was structured as a promise precisely so it would be reliable.

The law brings anger because we can't keep it — the Greek word for anger is orgē — the same word used for God's wrath. The law does not eliminate the problem of sin. It clarifies it and in doing so increases accountability. When the standard is explicit the failure to meet it is explicit. The law makes the human condition worse in one sense — it transforms vague wrongdoing into named violation. That is its diagnostic function. It was never designed to be the solution to the problem it diagnoses.

Because it's a gift the promise is certain — this is the theological heart of Paul's argument for faith over law. A gift does not depend on the recipient's performance. It depends on the character of the giver. God's promise to Abraham was certain because God is faithful — not because Abraham was perfect. The certainty of the promise is grounded in God's character rather than in human achievement. That grounding is what makes the promise available to everyone who trusts rather than only to those who perform well enough.

Why It Still Matters

He is the father of us all. Paul's conclusion from the promise to Abraham is the most inclusive possible — all who share Abraham's faith are Abraham's children regardless of ethnicity religious background or prior moral history. The family of God defined by faith rather than by biological or religious lineage is genuinely universal. That universality was always the intention — God told Abraham that through him all the families of the earth would be blessed. Paul is not expanding the promise beyond its original intention. What Is Happening Here

There was no human reason to hope but Abraham kept hoping and trusting anyway and because he did he became the father of many nations just as God had promised. Abraham was about a hundred years old. What You Might Not Know

The God who gives life to the dead — the God who speaks of things that do not yet exist as if they are already here. Paul's description of God in this passage is one of the most theologically rich in all his letters. Two divine characteristics are named. God raises the dead — he is not limited by what mortality has foreclosed. God calls into existence things that do not yet exist — he is not limited by what currently is. Both characteristics are relevant to Abraham's situation. The child God promised did not exist. Abraham and Sarah's capacity to produce one was as good as dead. The God who made the promise operated in exactly the realm where human possibility had run out.

Against all hope Abraham kept hoping — the Greek phrase is par' elpida ep' elpidi — against hope in hope. The hope Paul describes is not optimism based on favorable circumstances. He did not doubt God's promise — the Greek word for doubt is diakrithē from the same root as discriminate or distinguish. To doubt in this sense is to make a distinction between what God has said and what seems possible given the evidence. Abraham refused to let the evidence of his own body and Sarah's age override what God had spoken. This was not denial of reality. It was a decision about which reality was more fundamental — the reality of their physical situation or the reality of God's word. Abraham chose the latter without pretending the former did not exist.

His faith made him strong and he gave glory to God — the connection between strong faith and glorifying God is important. Abraham's trust in God's promise was itself an act of worship. It declared that God was trustworthy and capable. Weak faith — hesitating between what God has said and what seems possible — implicitly questions God's reliability. Strong faith — holding to the promise when circumstances argue against it — honors God by treating his word as more real than present appearances.

Why It Still Matters

He was completely sure that God had the power to do what he had promised. Abraham's certainty was not certainty about the mechanism or the timing or the details. It was certainty about the character of the one who had made the promise. The faith Romans commends throughout is not a feeling of confidence that things will work out. It is a settled trust in the God who has revealed himself as faithful — who raises the dead and calls into existence things that do not yet exist. That trust can hold when circumstances argue against it because it is not grounded in circumstances. It is grounded in who God is.

Peace with God

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Paul announces the results of justification by faith. Since we have been made right with God through faith we have peace with him through our Lord Jesus the Messiah. We have peace with God — this is the first result Paul names from justification. The peace he describes is not a feeling of calm. It is an objective state — the end of hostility between God and the person who has been justified. Before justification the relationship was one of enmity — Chapter 5 will describe us as enemies of God. After justification the enmity is over. The war has ended. Peace with God is the legal and relational reality that results from being declared right with him. The feeling of peace may or may not accompany it at any given moment. The reality of the peace does not depend on the feeling.

Paul is describing something that would have been unmistakably vivid to a Roman reader — being brought before the emperor was not something you could do on your own. It is not a one-time entry but a permanent standing.

We celebrate in our hardships — the Greek word for hardships is thlipsis meaning pressure afflictions or tribulations. Paul is not suggesting that suffering is pleasant or that we should pretend otherwise. He is describing a specific kind of response to suffering that is possible for people who understand where the suffering is going. The chain he describes — hardship produces grit, grit produces character, character produces hope — is not automatic. It is the result of suffering received within the framework of faith rather than suffered in isolation or despair.

This hope never lets us down — the Greek is literally the hope does not put us to shame. In the ancient world shame was one of the most serious social consequences a person could face. Paul is saying that the hope the Gospel produces will not ultimately embarrass or humiliate the one who holds it. It will be vindicated. The person who has hoped in God will not stand at the end of things and discover they were wrong to hope. The hope is grounded in something real enough to be trusted all the way to the end.

God's love is not rationed carefully into human hearts. It is poured in extravagantly by the Holy Spirit. The indwelling Spirit is the means by which the love of God becomes a present subjective experience rather than only a theological proposition. The same Spirit who raised Jesus from the dead is the one who brings God's love into direct contact with human experience.

Hardship builds grit and grit builds character and character builds hope. Paul's chain of development through suffering is one of the most practically useful passages in Romans for people going through hard times. He is not minimizing the hardship or offering cheap comfort. He is naming what suffering can produce in a person who receives it within the framework of faith — not bitterness and despair but a tested and proven hope. The key word is proven. The hope that emerges from suffering is a hope that has been tested by the very thing that threatened to destroy it and has survived. That kind of hope is more solid than the hope that has never been tested at all.

A love that was given freely while we were sinners cannot be lost by the very condition that was already present when it was given. The security Paul is building toward in this passage rests on the character of the one who loved us — not on the consistency of those who were loved.

What Jesus did affects everyone who receives it in the same structurally representative way. Paul is arguing that this kind of representative headship — one person's action having consequences for many — is the shape of how God has always worked in human history.

Death spread to every single person because every single person has sinned — Paul holds two things together that seem in tension. Death spread through Adam's sin. And every person has sinned. Both are true simultaneously. The condition Adam introduced is the condition every person has lived in and confirmed through their own choices. The universality of death is both inherited and personally confirmed. Paul is not saying people die only because of Adam or only because of their own sin — he is saying both realities are operating together.

Sin was not being counted the same way — the Greek word for counted is ellogeitai the accounting term Paul used in Chapter 4. Before the written law existed sin was still present and still deadly — death reigned from Adam to Moses even without a written code. But the explicit accounting of specific violations against a specific written standard did not yet exist. The law made sin more formally identifiable even though the underlying condition predated the law by centuries.

Grace overflowed and covered far more than one failure ever could — Paul insists that the comparison between Adam and Christ is asymmetrical in favor of Christ. It is not simply that what Christ did undoes what Adam did — as if it were a zero-sum reversal. What Christ did is greater in scope and power than what Adam did. One failure produced death. The gift covers countless failures. Death reigned through one man's act. Grace reigns to produce life that is qualitatively richer than what was lost. The surplus of grace over sin is one of Paul's central convictions in Romans.

Why It Still Matters

Those who receive God's grace and are made right with him will actually reign in life. Paul's vision of what the Gospel produces is not merely rescue from judgment. It is a transformed status — from being subject to death's reign to actually reigning in life. The direction reverses. Instead of death exercising dominion over people, people exercise something like dominion through the life God gives. This is not pride or self-sufficiency. It is the restoration of what was lost when sin entered — the dignity and purpose God originally gave humanity when he placed Adam and Eve as stewards of creation. The Gospel is not just a ticket out of judgment. It is the beginning of restored humanity.

Grace Is Greater Than Sin

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Just as one failure brought a guilty verdict for everyone one act of righteousness brings a not guilty verdict that gives life to everyone. Just as many were made sinners through one man's disobedience many will be made right through one man's obedience. The law came into the picture so that sin would be clearly seen for what it is. But where sin increased grace overflowed even more. Just as sin once reigned through death grace now reigns through righteousness — bringing eternal life through Jesus the Messiah our Lord.

What You Might Not Know

One act of righteousness brings a not guilty verdict that gives life to everyone — Paul is describing the universal scope of what Christ accomplished. The one act of righteousness — Christ's perfect obedience culminating in his atoning death — has made available a not guilty verdict for everyone. This does not mean everyone will receive it regardless of their response. Paul has been clear throughout Romans that the gift is received through faith. What he is establishing is that the accomplishment of Christ is sufficient in scope for everyone — there is no one for whom it was not done, no category of person for whom it is insufficient.

Many will be made right through one man's obedience — the word obedience is significant here. Christ's saving work is described not primarily as a sacrifice in isolation but as an act of obedience. The whole life of Jesus — his faithfulness to the Father, his resistance to temptation, his submission to the cross — was a comprehensive act of obedience that reversed the comprehensive act of disobedience through which sin entered. Adam refused to obey. Christ chose to obey. The reversal is as deep as the original act that needed reversing.

The law came into the picture so that sin would be clearly seen — Paul uses an unusual Greek word here for came into the picture — pareisēlthen — suggesting something that entered alongside the main action rather than being the main action itself. The law was not the central plot of human history. It entered alongside to serve a specific function — making sin visible and measurable. Once the law had done its diagnostic work it had fulfilled its purpose. It was never meant to be the permanent mechanism of the relationship between God and humanity. That mechanism was always faith.

Where sin increased grace overflowed even more — the Greek words are striking in their contrast. Sin increased — pleonazo — multiplied or became more abundant. Grace overflowed — hyperperisseuo — super-abounded or went far beyond what was needed. The prefix hyper intensifies the verb beyond what any single English word captures. Grace did not merely match the increase of sin. It went so far beyond it that the comparison becomes almost ridiculous. This is the logic behind the concern Paul addresses in Chapter 6 — does this mean we should sin more so grace can abound more? The answer is no but the question at least shows that Paul's Gospel was radical enough to prompt it.

Why It Still Matters

Grace now reigns through righteousness bringing eternal life through Jesus the Messiah our Lord. Paul ends Chapter 5 with a declaration about what is actually governing reality. Sin once reigned — it was the ruling power that determined the outcome of human life. Now grace reigns. The transfer of power has happened. The reign of grace is not a future hope — it is the present reality for everyone who has received it. Eternal life is not only a future destination. It is the quality of life that has already begun for the person who trusts in Jesus. The life that sin was designed to destroy has been restored and placed under a new reign that cannot be overthrown.

Romans Chapter 6

Dead to Sin, Alive in Christ

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

Paul addresses the obvious misreading of his Gospel — if grace increases where sin increases should we keep sinning to get more grace? Absolutely not. We died to sin — how can we keep living in it? When we were baptized into Christ Jesus we were baptized into his death. We were buried with him through baptism into that death so that just as Christ was raised by the Father's glory we too can walk in a brand new kind of life. If we have been joined with him in a death like his we will certainly be joined with him in a resurrection like his. Our old self was crucified with him so that sin's power over us would be broken — the goal is that we would no longer be slaves to sin. A dead person is free from sin's claim. Now if we died with Christ we believe we will also live with him. Since Christ was raised from the dead he can never die again — death no longer has power over him. The death he died he died to sin once and for all. The life he lives he lives for God. In the same way think of yourselves as dead to sin but alive to God in Christ Jesus.

What You Might Not Know

Should we keep on sinning so that grace can keep on increasing — Paul anticipated this objection in Chapter 3 and dismissed it quickly. Now he addresses it thoroughly. The objection is not absurd on its surface — if grace covers sin and more sin produces more grace then sinning seems to serve a useful purpose. Paul's answer is not primarily that sinning is wrong — though it is. His answer is that the question misunderstands what has happened to the believer. The person who has been united with Christ in his death is not the same person who used to live under sin's dominion. The question assumes a continuity that the Gospel has broken.

Baptized into his death — Paul is not primarily making a statement about the ritual of baptism here. He is describing a theological reality of which baptism is the public declaration. To be baptized into Christ is to be identified with everything that happened to Christ — including his death. The believer's old self — the person defined by sin and subject to its power — died with Christ. This is not a metaphor for moral improvement. It is a statement about a change of identity and status that is as real as death itself.

Walk in a brand new kind of life — the Greek word for brand new is kainotes meaning newness in quality not just in time. The life available to the believer is not the old life with improvements. It is a qualitatively different kind of life — life on the other side of death, life powered by resurrection, life that death itself has been unable to hold. Paul is describing something that does not have a category in ordinary human experience. The closest analogy is death and resurrection — which is exactly the category he is using.

Our old self was crucified with him — the old self is not the physical body or the human personality. It is the person defined by their relationship to sin — the self that was under sin's dominion, shaped by sin's power, producing sin's fruit. That self was crucified with Christ. It is not reformed or improved. It is executed. The new self that emerges is not the old self cleaned up. It is a new creation living in union with the risen Christ.

Think of yourselves as dead to sin but alive to God — the Greek word for think is logizesthe — the accounting word Paul has used throughout Romans. Count it. Reckon it to be so. Put it on your personal ledger as a fact. Paul is not saying feel like you are dead to sin or try to become dead to sin. He is saying recognize what is already true about you. The death to sin happened at the cross. The believer's job is to reckon that death as applying to themselves and to live accordingly.

Why It Still Matters

Think of yourselves as dead to sin but alive to God in Christ Jesus. Paul's instruction here is one of the most practically important in Romans. The problem many believers face is not that the old self has not died — it has. The problem is that they keep acting as if it hasn't. They continue responding to sin's demands as if they were still obligated to obey them. Paul's instruction is to reckon the truth — to count as real what is already real. Sin is speaking to someone who is dead. The dead do not respond to commands. The believer who understands their actual status stops responding to what no longer has authority over them.

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 6

No Longer Slaves to Sin

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

Do not let sin run your life making you give in to its cravings. Do not hand over any part of yourself to sin as a tool for doing wrong. Instead offer yourselves to God as people brought from death to life. Give every part of yourself to God as a tool for doing what is right. Sin is no longer your master because you are not living under the law anymore — you are living under grace. So should we sin because we are under grace and not the law? Absolutely not. When you give yourself to someone as a slave you belong to whoever you obey. You are either a slave to sin which leads to death or a slave to obedience which leads to being right with God.

What You Might Not Know

Don't let sin run your life — the Greek word for run is basileuetō meaning reign or rule as a king. Paul personifies sin as a would-be monarch that has been deposed but still issues commands as if it were in power. The deposed king still shouts orders. The question is whether the people who are no longer subject to that king will obey anyway out of habit or fear. Paul is saying — you are no longer under that king's authority. Do not obey the orders of someone who has no more claim on you.

Don't hand over any part of yourself to sin as a tool for doing wrong — the Greek word for tool is hopla meaning weapons or instruments. Every part of the human person — thoughts desires words actions — can be offered as an instrument either for sin or for God. Paul is describing a moment-by-moment choice about which direction each capacity will be oriented. The language of offering — presenting yourself — suggests a deliberate volitional act rather than passive drifting. Sin gets its grip through passivity. Righteousness grows through active presentation of oneself to God.

You're not living under the law anymore — you're living under grace. This statement requires careful reading. Paul is not saying the moral content of the law is irrelevant. He is saying the law as a system of earning standing before God is no longer the governing framework for believers. Under law the operative principle is performance — do this and live. Under grace the operative principle is gift — receive this and live. The motivational structure is completely different. Obedience under law is driven by the need to earn. Obedience under grace is driven by gratitude for what has already been given.

You belong to whoever you obey — Paul's slavery analogy cuts through any ambiguity about what freedom actually means. There is no condition of absolute autonomy in which a person belongs to no one and obeys nothing. Everyone serves something. The person who has been freed from sin does not thereby become self-governing. They become servants of God and obedience. The question is not whether you will serve but what you will serve. Paul is insisting that serving God and righteousness is genuine freedom compared to serving sin — even though both involve submission.

Why It Still Matters

Sin is no longer your master. Paul's declaration is both a theological statement and a pastoral challenge. Theologically it is true — sin's dominion over the believer has been broken by union with Christ. Pastorally the challenge is that sin continues to act like a master even after it has been deposed. The habits formed under sin's reign do not automatically disappear when the reign ends. They have to be actively resisted and replaced. Paul's instruction to offer every part of yourself to God is the practical counterpart to the theological reality. The truth sets you free. The discipline of offering yourself to God is how the freedom becomes visible in daily life.

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 6

Slaves to God

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

Thank God — the Roman believers used to be slaves to sin but they chose from the heart to follow the teaching they were given. They were set free from sin and became devoted to doing what is right. Paul uses the word slave because it is the clearest way to say it. Just as they once gave every part of themselves over to impurity and lawlessness which only led to more lawlessness — now they should give every part of themselves over to doing right which leads to a holy life. When they were slaves to sin doing right had no hold on them at all. And what did that get them? Nothing but shame — and those things end in death. But now they have been set free from sin and belong to God. The life they are growing now leads to holiness and where that road ends is eternal life. Sin pays its workers with death. But God's gift is eternal life through Christ Jesus our Lord.

What You Might Not Know

You chose from the heart to follow the teaching you were given — this phrase describes something important about how obedience works under grace. The Roman believers did not simply comply with a new set of rules. They chose from the heart — the obedience came from inside rather than being imposed from outside. The teaching they received shaped their desires so that following it became an expression of who they were becoming rather than an external constraint on who they still were. This is the pattern Jeremiah and Ezekiel had predicted — the law written on the heart rather than only on stone.

Impurity and lawlessness which only led to more lawlessness — Paul describes sin as self-compounding. The person who gives themselves to sin does not reach a stable plateau of manageable wrongdoing. Sin escalates. Each act of lawlessness loosens the inner constraints that might have limited the next one. The person who begins by crossing a small line finds that the next line is easier to cross. This is the internal logic of sin that Paul is naming — it is not a stable condition but a progressive one moving toward greater and greater disorder.

Now give every part of yourself over to doing right which leads to a holy life — the same compounding dynamic works in the other direction. Righteousness also builds on itself. Each act of doing right strengthens the habit of doing right. Each offering of a part of yourself to God makes the next offering easier. The Greek word for holy life is hagiasmos meaning sanctification or progressive holiness. It is a process — the life they are growing — not an instant condition. Holiness is the direction of travel not a fixed destination already reached.

Sin pays its workers with death — the Greek word for pays is opsōnia meaning wages — what a soldier or worker is owed for their service. Paul personifies sin as an employer who owes its workers something. What it owes is death. The workers of sin are not victims of an unfair system. They are receiving exactly what the employment relationship entitles them to. The only escape is to stop working for that employer — which is exactly what Paul has been describing throughout Chapter 6.

God's gift is eternal life — the contrast with wages is deliberate. Sin pays wages — something owed and earned. God gives a gift — something freely given that cannot be earned. The parallel structure makes the contrast vivid. Both are receiving what the relationship produces. But the relationship with sin is an employment relationship that produces what is owed. The relationship with God is a gift relationship that produces what is given. The difference is the difference between earning death and receiving life.

Why It Still Matters

Sin pays its workers with death. But God's gift is eternal life through Christ Jesus our Lord. Paul ends Chapter 6 with one of the sharpest contrasts in all of Scripture. Two employers. Two kinds of compensation. One pays what is owed. The other gives what cannot be earned. The choice of which employer to serve is the most consequential choice any person makes. Paul has spent the chapter explaining that the believer has already been freed from the first employer. The question now is whether they will live out that freedom or keep reporting for work at a job they no longer hold. The answer shapes every day of the life that follows.

Romans Chapter 7

The Analogy of Marriage

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Paul addresses those who know the law with an analogy from marriage. The law only has authority over a person while they are alive. A married woman is bound to her husband while he lives. If her husband dies she is free — she can marry another man without doing anything wrong. In the same way the believers died to the law through the body of Christ. Now they belong to another — the one raised from the dead — so they can live and grow for God. Before when they were living according to their sinful nature the law stirred up sinful passions inside them and everything they produced led to death. But now they have died to what once held them prisoner. They have been set free from the law to serve God in the new way of the Spirit not in the old way of following a written code.

What You Might Not Know

The law only has authority over a person while they are alive — Paul's marriage analogy is making a precise legal point. The law's jurisdiction is limited to the living. Death terminates the law's claim. This is not a comment about whether the law is good or bad. It is a comment about the scope of the law's authority. The believer who has died with Christ has moved outside the law's jurisdiction — not because the law has been abolished but because death has terminated the relationship. The believer is now in a new relationship governed by different terms.

The analogy requires careful reading — Paul is not saying the law is like a first husband who dies. He is saying the believer is like the woman who dies — or rather the believer's old self died — releasing them from the law's authority. The analogy is not a perfect one-to-one correspondence and Paul does not press it into every corner. The point is the termination of one relationship through death and the freedom to enter a new relationship. The believer died to the law and now belongs to Christ.

The law stirred up sinful passions inside us — this is one of the most psychologically acute observations in Romans. The command not to do something activates desire for that very thing in a way that the absence of the command does not. The forbidden fruit is more attractive because it is forbidden. Paul is not blaming the law for this dynamic — the law is not the cause of sinful desire. But the law's prohibition gives sin a specific target to aim at and an explicit boundary to cross. The law makes sin worse in practice even though the law itself is good.

Serve God in the new way of the Spirit not in the old way of following a written code — Paul is describing two fundamentally different motivational structures. The written code produces external compliance. The Spirit produces internal transformation. The written code says do this. The Spirit produces a person who wants to do this. The difference is the difference between following a map because you are required to and following a map because you genuinely want to reach the destination. Both may produce the same behavior in the short term. Only one produces sustainable transformation.

Why It Still Matters

We have been set free from the law so that we can serve God in the new way of the Spirit. The freedom Paul describes is not freedom from serving God. It is freedom from a particular mechanism of service that could not produce what it required. The believer freed from the law is not freed into lawlessness — Chapter 6 has already addressed that misreading. They are freed into a relationship with the Spirit who produces from the inside what the law required from the outside. The goal is the same. The power source is completely different.

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 7

The Law and Sin

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Is the law sinful? Of course not. But the law showed Paul what sin actually was. He would never have known what it meant to covet if the law had not said do not covet. But sin grabbed hold of that command and used it to produce every kind of coveting inside him. Apart from the law sin was just lying there dead. Paul was alive before the law came. But when the command arrived sin came to life and he died. The very command meant to bring life actually brought death. Sin used the command to trick him and kill him. So the law itself is holy. The command is holy right and good. Did something good become death to him? No. Sin used something good to bring about his death — so that sin could be seen for exactly what it is.

What You Might Not Know

I would never have known what it meant to covet if the law hadn't said do not covet — Paul chooses coveting as his example deliberately. Unlike murder or theft covetousness is entirely internal. You cannot observe it from the outside. It has no visible victim in the moment it occurs. It is the sin of the interior life — wanting what belongs to another without any external action necessarily following. The law prohibiting covetousness reaches inside the person in a way that prohibitions against external acts do not. It is the command that most directly exposes the inner life rather than regulating outer behavior.

Apart from the law sin was just lying there dead — the Greek word for dead is nekra meaning lifeless or inactive. Paul is not saying sin did not exist before the law. He is saying sin was not activated in the same way — it did not have a specific command to violate. The law gave sin a target. Before the law sin was a general condition. After the law sin became specific transgression with identifiable content. The law did not create sin. It gave sin definition and direction.

I was alive before the law came — Paul is probably describing the experience of a child before moral awareness arrives. There is a period in childhood before the force of moral command is fully felt — before the do not is experienced as a restriction requiring active resistance. When moral consciousness arrives — when the law comes in Paul's terms — the conflict begins. Paul is describing the universal human experience of moral awakening producing not moral achievement but moral failure.

Sin used the command to trick me and kill me — the word for trick is exapatao meaning to deceive completely or lead thoroughly astray. Sin's deception is to take the good command and use it as the occasion for desire and transgression. The command that was meant to protect became the occasion for the very thing it prohibited. This is not the law's failure. It is sin's perversion of the law's goodness. The law remains good. Sin's use of the law is the problem.

Why It Still Matters

The law is holy and the command is holy right and good. Paul's defense of the law in the middle of his argument about the law's limitations is important. He is not attacking the law. He is establishing precisely what it can and cannot do. The law can identify sin. It can name it clearly. It can even intensify awareness of it. What it cannot do is eliminate the desire for it or provide the power to resist it. That inability is not the law's fault — it is the condition of human nature that the law was given to people who could not consistently keep it. The law is good. The problem is not the law.

The Internal Conflict

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Paul knows God's law is good and right. But he is not. It is like being a slave to sin he cannot break free from. He does not even understand his own behavior sometimes. He wants to do what is right but does not do it. He does not want to do what is wrong but does it anyway. When he does what he does not want to do he is agreeing that the law is right — because he knows better. What is going on? It is not the best part of him doing these things — it is the sin that lives inside him. He knows that nothing good lives in his sinful nature. He wants to do good genuinely — but he cannot seem to follow through. The good he wants to do he does not do. The wrong he does not want to do he keeps doing anyway. When that happens it is not the true him acting — it is the sin living inside him. Even when he wants to do good evil is right there waiting. Deep inside he loves God's law. But there is another force at work fighting against what his mind knows is right. It takes him prisoner and hands him over to the sin that lives in him.

What You Might Not Know

Who is Paul describing in this passage — this is one of the most debated questions in Romans scholarship. Is Paul describing his pre-conversion experience, his post-conversion experience, or the experience of a person under the law more generally? The present tense verbs throughout the passage suggest a current experience rather than a past one. Many interpreters — including Augustine and Luther — understood this as describing the ongoing struggle of the regenerate person. Others argue Paul is describing the pre-Christian condition viewed retrospectively. The debate matters because it shapes how Christians understand the ongoing tension between desire and performance in the Christian life. What is clear is that the tension Paul describes is real and recognizable regardless of where exactly he is locating it.

I don't even understand my own behavior sometimes — the Greek word is ginosko meaning to know or recognize. Paul is expressing the bewilderment of a person whose behavior does not match their own self-understanding. He intends to do good. He does evil. The gap between intention and action is experienced as a kind of self-alienation — the person who acts does not feel like the true self. This is one of the most psychologically acute descriptions of moral failure in ancient literature. It captures something every person who has tried seriously to be good has experienced.

It's the sin that lives inside me — Paul is not denying personal responsibility for his actions. He is identifying the source of the problem more precisely than surface behavior reveals. The sin that lives inside him is not something imposed from outside. It is a condition of his own nature. But it is a condition he experiences as alien — as not-truly-me. This alienation between the self that wants good and the condition that produces evil is the experience of moral seriousness. The person who does not experience this tension has probably not yet encountered the full demand of the moral law.

Deep inside I love God's law — this is the crucial detail that distinguishes the person Paul is describing from someone who simply does not care about righteousness. The person who loves God's law and cannot consistently follow it is in a different situation from the person who has no interest in it. The love for the law is real. The failure to keep it is also real. Both are present simultaneously. This is not hypocrisy — it is the genuine conflict of a person who wants what they cannot consistently produce.

Why It Still Matters

The good I want to do I don't do. The wrong I don't want to do I keep doing anyway. These words have been recognized by serious people in every generation as an accurate description of their own moral experience. They are not an excuse for moral failure. They are an honest diagnosis of the human condition before the solution Paul is about to announce in Chapter 8. The person who reads Chapter 7 and recognizes themselves is being prepared to receive Chapter 8 with the gratitude it deserves. The more honestly you face the problem the more the solution means. Paul is not wallowing in despair here. He is clearing the ground for what comes next.

The Resolution

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

What a miserable condition this is. Who will rescue from this life that is slowly dying? Thank God — he does it through Jesus the Messiah our Lord. So then in his mind Paul wants to serve God's law — but his sinful nature makes him a slave to the law of sin.

What You Might Not Know

What a miserable condition I'm in — the Greek word is talaiporos meaning wretched or miserable — a word that communicates exhaustion from a long struggle. This is not a casual complaint. It is the cry of someone who has been trying and failing for a long time and has reached the end of their own resources. The cry is not despair about the future — Paul immediately answers his own question with gratitude. It is an honest reckoning with the present condition that makes the answer necessary. You cannot receive rescue unless you know you need it.

Who will rescue me from this life that is slowly dying — the Greek word for rescue is rysetai meaning to deliver or rescue from danger. Paul is describing his condition as a kind of captivity — the body of death. In the ancient world condemned prisoners were sometimes chained to a corpse as punishment — forced to carry the decaying body until they themselves died from disease. Whether Paul has this image in mind or not the phrase captures something precise — a living person bound to a dying thing being dragged toward death by the very thing they are carrying.

Thank God — he does it through Jesus the Messiah our Lord — the answer to the cry of Chapter 7 is the content of Chapter 8. Paul announces the answer before explaining it. The exclamation of gratitude interrupts the description of the problem with the certainty of the solution. The one who will rescue is already known. The rescue has already happened through Jesus. Chapter 8 will explain what that rescue actually consists of — life in the Spirit that does what the law could not do.

In my mind I want to serve God's law but my sinful nature makes me a slave to the law of sin — Paul ends Chapter 7 with the tension unresolved at the level of experience. The rescue has been announced but the conflict has not disappeared. The two realities coexist — the mind that loves God's law and the sinful nature that resists it. Chapter 8 does not eliminate the tension by denying the sinful nature. It provides the power of the Spirit to navigate the tension toward righteousness. The struggle continues. The outcome is no longer in doubt.

Why It Still Matters

Who will rescue me from this life that is slowly dying? Thank God — he does it through Jesus the Messiah our Lord. The movement from desperate question to grateful answer in two sentences is the movement of the Gospel itself. The question is real. The condition it describes is real. The answer is also real and it is specific — not a technique or a spiritual discipline or a moral resolve. A person. Jesus the Messiah. The rescue Paul is describing is not self-improvement. It is deliverance from outside the self by someone with the power to do what the self cannot do for itself. That is still the only rescue that works.

Romans Chapter 8

No More Condemnation

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Paul opens Chapter 8 with one of the most significant declarations in the New Testament — there is now no condemnation for those who belong to Christ Jesus. The reason is that the power of the life-giving Spirit in Christ Jesus has set them free from the power of sin and death. The law could not do this because human nature made it too weak. But God did it — he sent his own Son in a human body like ours capable of sin and dealt with sin right there in human flesh. He did this so that everything the law required could be fully met in people who no longer live according to their old nature but according to the Spirit.

What You Might Not Know

No condemnation — the Greek word is katakrima meaning a verdict of guilty with the sentence that follows it. Paul is not saying there is no conviction of sin or no awareness of wrongdoing. He is saying there is no binding legal verdict against those who belong to Christ. The court has ruled. The verdict is not guilty. That verdict is final and it cannot be revisited. The person in Christ is not awaiting a future judgment that might go against them. The judgment has already happened — in Christ — and the outcome is already known.

The life-giving Spirit — Paul introduces the Holy Spirit here in a way that sets up the rest of Chapter 8. The Spirit is described as life-giving — zōopoiountos — the one who produces life. This is the same Spirit who raised Jesus from the dead. The same power that overcame physical death is now at work inside believers overcoming the power of sin. The Spirit is not a supplement to the Christian life. The Spirit is the Christian life — the presence and power of the risen Christ operating within the believer.

He sent his own Son in a human body like ours — a body capable of sin — the Greek phrase is en homoiōmati sarkos hamartias — in the likeness of sinful flesh. Paul is careful with his language here. Jesus came in a body like our sinful flesh — genuinely human, genuinely subject to the same conditions we face. But Paul says likeness not identical — he does not say Jesus had sinful flesh, only that he came in the same kind of body. The distinction preserves both the genuine humanity of Jesus and his sinlessness. He was in every way tempted as we are yet without sin.

Dealt with sin right there in human flesh — the Greek is katekrinen tēn hamartian en tē sarki — condemned sin in the flesh. God did not deal with the sin problem from a distance. He entered the very territory where sin had established its dominion — human flesh — and condemned it there. The victory was won on sin's own ground. The incarnation was not just a delivery mechanism for getting Jesus to the cross. It was itself part of how the victory was accomplished.

Why It Still Matters

There is now no condemnation for those who belong to Christ Jesus. This sentence has been called the greatest sentence in the Bible by more than one theologian and with good reason. It is the answer to everything Chapter 7 described — the miserable condition, the slavery to sin, the inability to do what the law requires. The condemnation that the law's verdict against sin would have produced has been absorbed and cancelled. The person who belongs to Christ stands before God with no outstanding verdict against them. Not because they have been perfect but because the verdict has already been rendered and it came out not guilty. That is where Chapter 8 begins and everything that follows flows from it.

The Old Nature vs. The Spirit

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

People who live by their old nature have their minds fixed on what that nature wants. But those who live by the Spirit have their minds fixed on what the Spirit wants. When the old nature controls the mind it leads to death. When the Spirit controls the mind it leads to life and peace. The old nature is hostile toward God — it refuses to follow God's law and is not even capable of it. People ruled by their old nature cannot please God. But that is not the believers — they are controlled by the Spirit because God's Spirit lives in them. Anyone who does not have the Spirit of Christ does not belong to him at all. If Christ is in them their body is still heading toward death because of sin but their spirit is alive because they have been made right with God. And if the Spirit of the one who raised Jesus from the dead lives in them then God will also give life to their dying bodies through his Spirit living inside them.

What You Might Not Know

The mind controlled by the old nature versus the mind controlled by the Spirit — the Greek word for mind here is phronēma meaning the whole orientation of thought, desire, and will. Paul is not describing two kinds of thinking in the narrow intellectual sense. He is describing two fundamental orientations of the whole inner person. The old nature orients everything toward itself — its desires, its comfort, its survival. The Spirit orients everything toward God — his character, his purposes, his life. These are not parallel options available to the same person simultaneously. They are competing governing principles and only one can actually be in charge.

The old nature is hostile toward God — the Greek word is echthra meaning enmity or active hostility. This is not indifference to God. It is opposition. The old nature does not simply fail to seek God — it actively resists him. This means that moral effort within the old nature does not move a person toward God. It may produce external compliance with certain standards but the underlying orientation remains adversarial. The problem Paul is diagnosing is not behavioral. It is foundational — the governing center of the natural person is set against the God it was made to know.

If anyone doesn't have the Spirit of Christ they don't belong to him at all — this is one of the clearest statements in the New Testament about what constitutes Christian identity. The defining mark is not moral achievement or religious practice or cultural affiliation. It is the presence of the Spirit. A person either has the Spirit or does not. If they have the Spirit they belong to Christ. If they do not have the Spirit they do not belong to him regardless of what else is true about them. This binary is not harsh. It is clarifying — the question of belonging to Christ reduces to whether the Spirit is present.

Your body is still heading toward death but your spirit is alive — Paul holds the tension of the believer's condition honestly. Physical death has not been cancelled for believers. The bodies of people in Christ are still dying. The resurrection of the body is still future. What has changed is the spirit — the inner person is alive because justification has happened. The already and the not yet coexist in the believer. Already alive in spirit. Not yet redeemed in body. Chapter 8 holds both honestly and points toward the resolution that is coming.

Why It Still Matters

When the Spirit controls your mind it leads to life and peace. The peace Paul mentions here connects back to Chapter 5 — peace with God is the result of justification. The peace that comes from a Spirit-controlled mind is the experience of that objective peace in daily life. The person whose mind is oriented toward the Spirit finds a quality of inner life that the old nature cannot produce — not because they have achieved moral perfection but because the governing center of their inner life has been reoriented toward the one who is himself life and peace. The direction of the mind determines the quality of the life that follows from it.

God's Children and Heirs

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

The believers do not owe the old nature anything. They do not have to live the way it wants. If they live by that old nature they are going to die. But if by the Spirit they put to death the wrong things their bodies keep pushing toward they will live. Anyone led by God's Spirit is a child of God. They did not receive a spirit of slavery that makes them afraid. They received the Spirit that makes them God's own child. By that Spirit they cry out Abba — Father. The Spirit himself joins with their spirit to confirm that they are God's children. And if they are children they are heirs — heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ. They share in his sufferings now so that they may share in his glory later.

What You Might Not Know

Put to death the wrong things your body keeps pushing toward — the Greek word is thanatoute meaning to put to death or mortify. This is the active side of what Chapter 6 described as reckoning. The believer is not passive in the struggle against sin. The Spirit provides the power but the believer applies it — specifically and deliberately — to the particular wrong desires that their body keeps generating. This is called mortification in Christian theology — the ongoing and deliberate putting to death of sinful impulses. It is not a one-time event. It is a continuous practice that requires active engagement.

Abba — Father — Abba is an Aramaic word meaning father — the language Jesus spoke in daily life. It appears three times in the New Testament — here, in Galatians 4, and in Jesus's own prayer in Gethsemane. The word carries the warmth and intimacy of a child's address to a parent — not the formal word for father but the familiar one. When the Spirit prompts the believer to cry out Abba the Spirit is producing in the believer the same relationship Jesus himself has with the Father. The intimacy is not merely permitted. It is the natural expression of the relationship that adoption creates.

The Spirit himself joins with our spirit to confirm that we are God's children — the Greek word for joins with is summarturei meaning to testify together or co-witness. The Spirit is not the only witness to the believer's identity. The believer's own spirit is also a witness. The two testimonies agree — the inner sense of being God's child and the Spirit's own confirmation of that identity. Paul is describing something that is not merely theological proposition but experienced reality. The believer knows they are a child of God not only because the Bible says so but because the Spirit produces that knowledge as a living experience.

Heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ — the inheritance language is significant. An heir receives what belongs to the one whose heir they are. The believer is heir to God — which means the inheritance is as large as God himself and everything that is his. And they are fellow heirs with Christ — which means they inherit alongside the Son, sharing in what the Son inherits. The scope of the inheritance is staggering. Everything that will be Christ's in the age to come is shared with those who are united with him. Paul will say in the next section that creation itself is part of what will be inherited.

Why It Still Matters

You didn't receive a spirit of slavery that makes you afraid. You received the Spirit that makes you God's own child. The contrast Paul draws is between two completely different relationships with God — the slave relationship driven by fear and the child relationship driven by love and belonging. Fear-based religion and grace-based faith can produce similar external behaviors in the short term. They produce completely different inner lives. The slave obeys because they are afraid of what happens if they do not. The child obeys because they love the parent and want to please them. The Spirit produces the second kind of relationship — not the elimination of reverence but the transformation of fear into love.

Future Glory

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

What believers are going through right now is nothing compared to the glory God is going to reveal in them. All of creation is holding its breath waiting for that day. Creation did not choose to be broken — God allowed it to happen but with a promise attached. One day creation itself will be set free from death and decay and will share in the freedom and glory of God's children. All of creation has been groaning like a woman in labor right up to this moment. And it is not just creation — believers groan too even though they already have the Holy Spirit living in them. The Spirit is just the first taste of what is coming. They are still waiting for the rest — the day when God fully adopts them and rescues their bodies from death completely. That is what they were saved for — a hope. Hope only makes sense for something you do not have yet. But when hoping for something you cannot see yet you wait for it with patience.

What You Might Not Know

All of creation is holding its breath — the Greek word is apokaradokia meaning eager expectation with the head stretched forward — the posture of someone straining to see what is coming. Paul is describing the whole created order as animated with expectation. The trees, the animals, the mountains, the seas — all of it groaning and straining toward a future that has not yet arrived. This is not sentimentalism or poetry in a vague sense. Paul is making a theological claim about the relationship between the redemption of humanity and the redemption of the created world. The two are connected. When God's children are fully revealed creation itself will share in the liberation.

Creation didn't choose to be broken — God allowed it to happen — the Greek phrase is hē ktisis hypetage tē mataiotēti — creation was subjected to futility. The word futility — mataiotēs — is the same word used throughout the book of Ecclesiastes for the vanity of life under the sun. Creation's subjection to futility was not its own choice. It was subjected — by the one who subjected it — with a promise of future liberation. Paul is reading Genesis 3 here — the curse on creation that followed Adam's sin. Creation bears the consequences of human rebellion without having chosen it. It waits for the human story to reach its conclusion so its own story can be resolved.

The Spirit is just the first taste of what's coming — the Greek word is aparchē meaning firstfruits — the first portion of a harvest that represents and guarantees the rest. The Spirit's presence in the believer is not the full inheritance. It is the deposit that guarantees the full inheritance is coming. The life, the love, the peace, the transformation that the Spirit produces now are real — but they are the beginning of something that will be immeasurably larger. The best of the Christian life now is a fraction of what is promised. That promise is not fantasy. It is guaranteed by the very presence of the Spirit who is himself the down payment.

Hope only makes sense for something you don't have yet — Paul's reflection on hope is one of the most practically grounding statements in Chapter 8. Hope by definition is oriented toward the future. When people say they hope for something they already have they are misusing the word. Biblical hope is not wishful thinking about what might happen. It is confident expectation of what will happen based on the character and promises of God. The not-yet dimension of the Christian life is not a deficiency. It is the proper shape of life lived between promise and fulfillment.

Why It Still Matters

All of creation has been groaning like a woman in labor right up to this very moment. Paul's vision of the redemption of creation is one of the most expansive in the New Testament. The Gospel is not a rescue plan for human souls to escape the physical world. It is the beginning of the restoration of everything God made. The physical world matters to God. Its groaning matters. The suffering of creation is not something God is indifferent to — it is something he has promised to address. The Christian hope is not disembodied existence in a spiritual realm. It is resurrection bodies in a renewed creation — the whole story redeemed, not just the human part of it.

The Spirit Speaks for Us

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

The Spirit helps believers in their weakness. They do not always know what they should pray for but the Spirit himself speaks up for them with groans that go beyond words. And God who searches hearts knows exactly what the Spirit is thinking because the Spirit pleads for God's people in perfect harmony with God's own will. For those who love God everything works together for good — for those called according to his purpose. Those God knew in advance he chose in advance to be shaped into the image of his Son so that the Son would be the firstborn among many brothers and sisters. Those he chose he called. Those he called he made right with himself. And those he made right he brought into his glory.

What You Might Not Know

The Spirit helps us in our weakness — the Greek word for helps is synantilambanetai — a compound word meaning to take hold of something together with someone on the opposite side. The image is of two people carrying something heavy — one on each end. The Spirit does not carry the prayer burden for the believer while the believer does nothing. The Spirit takes hold of what the believer is carrying and helps bear it. The weakness Paul names is the weakness of not knowing what to pray for — one of the most honest admissions in all his letters. Even the most spiritually mature believer does not always know what to ask God for in a specific situation.

Groans that go beyond words — the Greek is stenagmois alalētois meaning unutterable or inexpressible groanings. The Spirit's intercession is not verbal — it transcends language. Paul is not describing a particular spiritual practice. He is describing something the Spirit does within the believer at a level deeper than conscious thought or verbal expression. The Spirit takes the inchoate longings and yearnings of the believer — the things they feel but cannot articulate — and brings them before God in perfect form. The prayer the believer cannot form is formed for them by the Spirit who knows both the believer's need and God's will.

Everything works together for good — this is one of the most quoted and most misunderstood verses in Romans. Paul is not promising that everything that happens to believers will be pleasant or that suffering will always resolve into obvious benefit. He is making a theological claim about divine sovereignty — that God is at work in all circumstances directing them toward an ultimate good for those who love him. The good in view is defined in the next verse — being shaped into the image of Christ. That good may be produced through circumstances that are themselves painful or difficult. The promise is not that everything feels good. It is that God is working everything toward a good end.

Those God knew in advance he chose in advance — foreknew and predestined. These words have generated enormous theological debate across the centuries. What Paul is primarily establishing here is the security of the believer — the process from divine foreknowledge to glory is an unbroken chain. The believer can trust that what God began he will complete. Whether one understands foreknowledge as God's advance knowledge of who would believe or as God's advance personal relationship with specific individuals the point is the same — the believer's ultimate destiny is secure in God's purpose not dependent on their own performance.

Why It Still Matters

We don't even always know what we should pray for but the Spirit himself speaks up for us. This admission from Paul is one of the most comforting in Romans precisely because it is so honest. The person who sits before God not knowing what to ask is not failing at prayer. They are in the exact situation Paul describes as normal — a situation the Spirit is equipped to address. The Spirit takes the believer's confusion and inadequacy and brings it before God in a form that perfectly expresses the believer's need. Prayer is not primarily a human performance evaluated by its eloquence or specificity. It is a conversation in which the Spirit helps make up what the believer cannot supply on their own.

More Than Conquerors

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

If God is for believers who can possibly stand against them? He did not even spare his own Son — he gave him up for all of them. Will he not also give them everything else along with him? Who will bring any charge against the people God has chosen? God is the one who makes them right — so who is left to condemn them? No one. Christ Jesus is the one who died and more than that he was raised to life and right now he is sitting at God's right hand speaking up for them. Who can separate believers from the love of Christ? Can trouble? Hardship? Persecution? Hunger? Poverty? Danger? War? As the Scripture says for God's sake they are being killed all day long — treated like sheep headed for slaughter. No. In all these things they are more than conquerors through the one who loved them. Paul is absolutely convinced that neither death nor life neither angels nor demons neither the present nor the future neither any power in heaven nor any power on earth nor anything else in all creation will ever be able to separate them from the love of God that is found in Christ Jesus our Lord.

What You Might Not Know

If God is for us who can possibly stand against us — the question assumes a specific answer. No one. But Paul does not simply assert no one and move on. He demonstrates it through a series of rhetorical questions that build on each other. If God gave his Son — the most costly thing imaginable — will he withhold lesser things? The logic is from the greater to the lesser. The hardest gift has already been given. Everything else follows from it. The person who has received the Son has already received the greatest proof of God's commitment to them that exists.

Who will bring any charge against the people God has chosen — Paul is describing a courtroom scene. The question assumes an accuser who might bring charges. The answer is that the accusation cannot stand because God himself has already issued the verdict. The Judge has declared not guilty. If the Judge has already ruled no subsequent charge can overturn that ruling. The accuser — whether understood as Satan, conscience, other people, or the law itself — has no standing to bring a charge that God has already dismissed.

Christ Jesus is sitting at God's right hand speaking up for us — this is one of the clearest statements of the ongoing intercessory work of Jesus in the New Testament. Jesus's work did not end at the cross or the resurrection. He is currently active — sitting at the right hand of the Father and interceding for his people. The person who worries that their sins will eventually exhaust God's willingness to forgive does not understand the present ministry of the risen Christ. He is not silent before the Father. He is speaking up for his people continuously.

More than conquerors — the Greek is hypernikōmen — hyper plus conquerors. The prefix hyper means over and above, exceeding. Believers are not barely surviving. They are not simply getting through. They are exceeding conquest — winning by a margin that goes beyond what the word conqueror normally implies. The excess is important. In ordinary conquest the victor wins at cost. The hyper-conqueror wins through the one who loved them — receiving the victory through relationship rather than achieving it through effort. The margin of excess is the love of Christ that undergirds the whole enterprise.

Neither death nor life — Paul's list of potential separators covers every category of existence and experience. Death cannot separate because Christ has already conquered death. Life cannot separate because the risen Christ is Lord of the living. Angels and demons cannot separate because no spiritual power exceeds the power of the Spirit who dwells in the believer. Present and future cannot separate because God inhabits both. Powers in heaven and on earth cannot separate because Christ is Lord over all powers. And finally — anything else in all creation. Paul has tried to cover every conceivable category and then adds a catch-all. Nothing that exists can do this thing.

Why It Still Matters

Nothing in all creation will ever be able to separate us from the love of God that is found in Christ Jesus our Lord. Romans 8 ends where it began — with a declaration of security grounded in what God has done rather than in what humans can maintain. Chapter 8 opened with no condemnation. It closes with nothing can separate. Between those two declarations Paul has described the Spirit's work, the groaning of creation, the intercession of the Spirit, the golden chain from foreknowledge to glory, and the rhetorical dismantling of every possible threat to the believer's standing. The security Romans 8 establishes is not the security of a person who is confident in themselves. It is the security of a person who is confident in the love of the God who has demonstrated that love at the cross and confirmed it by raising the crucified one from the dead. That security is the solid ground on which everything else in the Christian life is built.

Romans Chapter 9

Paul's Deep Sorrow for His People

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Paul opens with an oath — he is telling the truth as someone who belongs to Christ and his conscience guided by the Holy Spirit confirms it. His heart is filled with deep never-ending sadness. He would honestly be willing to be cut off from Christ himself if it would help his own Jewish brothers and sisters. They are the people of Israel — God adopted them as his children, showed them his glory, made covenants with them, gave them the law the temple worship and his promises. The great patriarchs belong to them and Christ himself came from their family line — he who is God over all praised forever. Amen.

What You Might Not Know

Paul opens with an oath — I am telling the truth as someone who belongs to Christ I am not lying my conscience guided by the Holy Spirit confirms it. The triple reinforcement — truth claim, denial of lying, Spirit-confirmed conscience — signals that Paul knows what he is about to say will be hard to believe. A man who had been beaten, imprisoned, and nearly killed multiple times by Jewish communities across the Roman world is about to say that his deepest grief is for those very people. The oath is necessary because the claim is extraordinary.

I would be willing to be cut off from Christ myself — the Greek word is anathema meaning cursed or cut off — the same word Paul uses in Galatians for anyone who preaches a false gospel. Paul is expressing a willingness to bear the most extreme possible spiritual consequence if it would bring his people to salvation. He is not claiming this is actually possible theologically. He is expressing the depth of his love in the strongest terms available to him. The closest parallel in Scripture is Moses asking God to blot his name out of the book of life if God will not forgive Israel after the golden calf. Both expressions reveal a love that has moved beyond self-interest entirely.

God adopted them as his children — Paul lists eight privileges that belong to Israel. Adoption as God's children. The divine glory. The covenants. The law. The temple worship. The promises. The patriarchs. And the Messiah himself. This is the most concentrated summary of Israel's covenant privileges in Paul's letters. He is not minimizing Jewish heritage or treating it as irrelevant. He is establishing how enormous the tragedy is — a people with every possible advantage available to them had missed the very thing all those advantages were pointing toward.

Christ himself came from their family line — he who is God over all praised forever — this is one of the clearest statements of the full deity of Christ in Paul's letters. Jesus is identified as God over all — theos epi pantōn — the one who is supreme over everything. The Messiah who came through Israel's family line is simultaneously the God who reigns over all creation. Paul places this declaration at the end of his list of Israel's privileges as the culmination — the greatest gift God gave through Israel was God himself in human flesh.

Why It Still Matters

Paul's anguish for his people is one of the most important emotional notes in Romans. Chapters 9 through 11 deal with one of the hardest theological questions in the New Testament — what has happened to Israel in light of the Gospel — and Paul approaches it not as an abstract theological puzzle but as a personal grief. The man doing the theology is a man in pain. His people — the people he was born into, trained among, and nearly killed by — are missing the salvation that came through them. That grief shapes the tone of everything that follows. Theological precision in the service of pastoral love rather than academic exercise is the model Romans 9 through 11 provides.

God's Promise Has Not Failed

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

God's word has not failed. Not everyone who descended from Israel is truly part of Israel. Not all of Abraham's descendants are his true children — as the Scripture says your descendants will be traced through Isaac. It is not the naturally born children who are God's children but the children of the promise. God's promise was that at the right time he would return and Sarah would have a son. And before Rebekah's twin sons were even born before they had done anything good or bad God made a choice — to show that his purpose depends on his own choice not on what people do but on his call. He told Rebekah the older son will serve the younger. As it is written — I loved Jacob but I rejected Esau.

What You Might Not Know

God's word has not failed — this is the theological problem Paul is addressing in Chapters 9 through 11. If God promised to save Israel and Israel has largely rejected the Gospel then it appears God's promise has failed. Paul's answer unfolds over three chapters but begins here — the promise was never about physical descent from Abraham. It was always about the children of the promise. The definition of Israel that God was always working with was narrower and deeper than ethnic lineage.

Not everyone who descended from Israel is truly part of Israel — Paul is drawing a distinction between Israel according to the flesh and Israel according to promise. This is not an attack on Jewish identity. It is a recovery of what the covenant relationship with God was always about. Even within the physical descendants of Abraham Isaac and Jacob there was always a distinction between those who responded to God in faith and those who did not. The remnant theme runs throughout the Hebrew prophets — a smaller group within the larger group who were truly responsive to God.

Before the twins were born before they had done anything good or bad — the selection of Jacob over Esau before birth is one of the strongest statements of divine prerogative in the Old Testament. Paul quotes it here to establish a principle — God's choice does not operate on the basis of human merit foreseen or otherwise. The choice preceded any possibility of merit. This does not mean the choice was arbitrary — Paul insists God is not unfair — but it does mean the basis of the choice is in God's purpose rather than in human performance.

I loved Jacob but I rejected Esau — Paul is quoting Malachi 1 where God is speaking about nations not just individuals. The statement is about the corporate election of Israel over Edom rather than primarily about the eternal destiny of two individuals. Paul is using it to make a point about the principle of God's selective purpose operating within the covenant people. The choice of Jacob over Esau established the pattern — God's redemptive purposes operate through selection rather than through universal inclusion of everyone in a particular group.

Why It Still Matters

It is not the naturally born children who are God's children — it is the children of the promise. Paul's distinction between physical descent and covenant membership is one of the most important in Romans for understanding what the Church is. The community of God's people has never been defined by biology or ethnicity. It has always been defined by the promise and the response of faith. Abraham's true children are those who trust God the way Abraham trusted God — regardless of their ethnic background. This principle is not Paul's invention. It is what the story of Isaac and Ishmael, Jacob and Esau, was always demonstrating.

Is God Being Unfair?

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Is God being unfair? Absolutely not. He said to Moses — I will show mercy to anyone I choose and I will have compassion on anyone I choose. So it does not depend on what a person wants or how hard they work — it depends entirely on God's mercy. The Scripture says to Pharaoh — I raised you up for this very reason to demonstrate my power through you so that my name would be known all over the earth. So God shows mercy to those he wants to and he hardens the hearts of those he wants to.

What You Might Not Know

Is God being unfair — the Greek word is adikia meaning unrighteousness or injustice. Paul anticipates the most natural objection to what he has just said about Jacob and Esau. If God chooses before birth without regard to merit is he not being unjust? Paul's answer is direct — absolutely not — and he supports it from two Old Testament examples that work in opposite directions. Moses received mercy. Pharaoh was hardened. Both serve God's purposes. Both demonstrate that God's freedom to act in sovereign mercy is not constrained by human expectation of fairness based on merit.

I will show mercy to anyone I choose — Paul quotes Exodus 33 where God responds to Moses's request to see his glory. God's answer is that he will show mercy and compassion according to his own sovereign will. The context is significant — this declaration of divine freedom came immediately after the golden calf incident when Israel had grievously sinned. God's mercy was not being given in response to Israel's merit. It was being given in spite of their failure. The freedom God claims is the freedom to be gracious beyond what justice requires.

Pharaoh — the hardening of Pharaoh's heart is one of the most theologically difficult passages in the Old Testament. Paul's use of it here is not to argue that God arbitrarily damns some people. He is using Pharaoh as the limiting case of what divine sovereignty looks like when it operates through judgment rather than mercy. Pharaoh's hardening served a specific purpose — the demonstration of God's power that would be proclaimed throughout the earth. Even the negative expression of divine sovereignty serves the larger purposes of making God's name known.

It doesn't depend on what a person wants or how hard they work — it depends entirely on God's mercy. This statement is Paul's summary of the principle he has established through Moses and Pharaoh. Human desire and human effort are not the determining factors in God's redemptive purposes. This does not eliminate human responsibility — Paul will address that in the next section. It establishes that the initiative belongs to God. Salvation is not the reward for successful human seeking. It is the gift of divine mercy extended to those God chooses to extend it to.

Why It Still Matters

It depends entirely on God's mercy. Paul's insistence on divine sovereignty in salvation is not primarily a philosophical position about determinism and free will. It is a pastoral position about the security of the believer and the freedom of God. If salvation depended on human effort then the person who worked harder would have more grounds for assurance and more reason for pride. If it depends on God's mercy then the grounds of assurance are as stable as God's character and the grounds for pride are eliminated entirely. Paul is not trying to satisfy philosophical curiosity about how sovereignty and freedom interact. He is establishing the foundation on which genuine humility and genuine security can coexist.

The Potter and the Clay

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Someone might ask — then why does God still blame us? Who can resist what he wants to happen? But who is a mere human to argue with God? Does a pot say to the potter why did you make me like this? Does not the potter have the right to take the same lump of clay and make one jar for special occasions and another for everyday use? What if God wanting to show his anger and demonstrate his power put up with people headed for destruction with great patience? And what if he did this to make the incredible riches of his glory known to those he prepared in advance for glory? That includes people he called not only from among the Jews but also from among the Gentiles.

What You Might Not Know

Who are you — a mere human — to argue with God — Paul's response to the objection is not a philosophical argument but a reorientation of perspective. The question who are you shifts the frame from the creature's rights to the Creator's prerogative. Paul is drawing on Isaiah 29 and 45 where the same imagery appears — the absurdity of the clay questioning the potter. This is not an attempt to silence legitimate questions. It is a reminder that the frame of reference matters. Judging God's justice by the standards of human fairness assumes that human standards are the appropriate measure for divine action — which is precisely what Paul is questioning.

One jar for special occasions and another for everyday use — Paul's pottery analogy is drawing on Jeremiah 18 where the potter reshapes the clay as he sees fit. The two kinds of vessels are not a statement about eternal destiny in isolation. They are a statement about the diversity of purposes God works through in human history. Some vessels serve to display his glory directly. Others serve to display it indirectly — through the contrast of judgment with mercy. Both serve the potter's purposes. Neither has the standing to demand a different shape.

People headed for destruction — the Greek word is apōleia meaning destruction or ruin. Paul is not saying God created these people for the purpose of destroying them. He is saying God endured with great patience people who were on a trajectory toward destruction — presumably through their own choices — in order to make his glory known through and despite them. The patience of God toward those who resist him is itself an expression of his character. He is not eager to destroy. He endures long before judgment falls.

People he called not only from among the Jews but also from among the Gentiles — Paul's conclusion from the potter and clay argument is the Gentile inclusion. The God who has the sovereign right to form vessels for different purposes has chosen to include Gentiles among the vessels prepared for glory. This is not favoritism against Israel. It is the expansion of the covenant community in fulfillment of what God had always intended — a people drawn from every nation who bear his name.

Why It Still Matters

Does a pot say to the potter why did you make me like this? The pottery image is one of the most recurring in Scripture precisely because it captures something true about the Creator-creature relationship that human beings persistently resist. The creature did not bring itself into existence. It did not choose its capacities or its context. The instinct to demand an explanation from the one who made us is understandable — but it assumes a position of equality with the Creator that does not actually exist. The proper posture before the Potter is not argument but trust — trust that the one who made us knows what he is doing even when we cannot see the shape he is working toward.

The Gentiles Are Included

What Is Happening Here

God says in Hosea — I will call those who were Not My People My People and I will call her who was Not Loved My Loved One. And in the very place where they were told you are not my people they will be called children of the living God. Isaiah says about Israel — even if the people of Israel are as many as the grains of sand on the seashore only a remnant will be saved. The Lord will carry out his sentence on the earth quickly and completely. And as Isaiah predicted — if the Lord of Heaven's Armies had not left some descendants they would have ended up like Sodom and Gomorrah.

What You Might Not Know

Hosea's Not My People becoming My People — Paul quotes Hosea 2 where God is speaking about restoring Israel after their unfaithfulness. Hosea's own life was a living parable of God's relationship with Israel — he married a woman who was unfaithful to him and was called to take her back as a picture of God's persistent love for an unfaithful Israel. The children in Hosea's story were named Lo-Ammi meaning Not My People and Lo-Ruhamah meaning Not Loved. Paul applies these words to the Gentiles — people who were definitively outside the covenant are now being called into it. God's covenant-making capacity extends beyond Israel's borders.

Only a remnant will be saved — Paul quotes Isaiah 10 where the prophet predicts that only a remnant of Israel will survive the Assyrian invasion. Paul applies the remnant concept to the situation of Israel in his own time — not all physical Israel will be saved but a remnant will be. This is not a new principle introduced by the Gospel. It was present in Israel's own prophets. The remnant theme runs throughout Isaiah — a smaller faithful core within the larger unfaithful mass. Paul is saying that Israel's current partial response to the Gospel is consistent with the pattern the prophets had always described.

They would have ended up like Sodom and Gomorrah — Paul quotes Isaiah 1 where the prophet opens his book with a devastating indictment of Judah — they have become like Sodom in their corruption. The only reason they were not completely destroyed was the preservation of a remnant. Paul uses this to establish that the current situation — a remnant of Israel responding to the Gospel while the majority does not — is not a novelty or a failure of God's plan. It is the continuation of a pattern Isaiah had described eight centuries earlier. God has always worked through a remnant.

Why It Still Matters

I will call those who were Not My People My People. The inclusion of the Gentiles in the covenant people of God is not an afterthought or a plan B after Israel's rejection. Paul is showing from Hosea that God had always intended to extend his covenant beyond the boundaries of ethnic Israel. The calling of Gentiles is the fulfillment of the prophets not the abandonment of them. The Church that includes people from every nation is not a replacement for Israel's story. It is the continuation and expansion of it — the moment when the promise to Abraham that all nations would be blessed through him begins to be visibly fulfilled on a global scale.

Israel Missed It

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

The conclusion — the Gentiles who were not even looking for a right relationship with God found one based on faith. But Israel who tried so hard to be right with God by following the law never reached that goal. Why? Because they tried to get there through their own efforts instead of through faith. They tripped over the stumbling stone — as it is written look I am placing a stone in Jerusalem that makes people stumble a rock that makes them fall. But whoever trusts in him will never be put to shame.

What You Might Not Know

The Gentiles who weren't even looking found it — the irony Paul is describing is one of the most striking in Romans. The people who had every advantage — the law the prophets the covenants the promises — missed the goal. The people who had none of those advantages and were not even seeking found it. This is not because the Gentiles were better or more deserving. It is because they received the Gospel as a gift by faith rather than as an achievement to be earned by effort. The very eagerness with which Israel pursued righteousness through law-keeping became the obstacle to receiving righteousness as a gift.

They tripped over the stumbling stone — Paul combines two passages from Isaiah 8 and 28. The stone placed in Zion that causes stumbling is identified elsewhere in the New Testament as Jesus himself. The Messiah who should have been Israel's greatest treasure became the rock they tripped over. The stumbling was not inevitable — whoever trusts in him will never be put to shame. The stone is a stumbling block only to those who approach it by the wrong method. Those who approach it by faith find it to be a foundation rather than an obstacle.

Through their own efforts instead of through faith — the Greek is ex ergōn rather than ek pisteōs — from works rather than from faith. The distinction Paul has been building throughout Romans reaches its clearest expression here. Two ways of approaching righteousness. One treats it as an achievement — something to be reached by sufficient effort and performance. The other treats it as a gift — something to be received by trusting the one who offers it. Israel's error was not in taking righteousness seriously. It was in misunderstanding how righteousness is obtained.

Why It Still Matters

They tried to get there through their own efforts instead of through faith. Israel's mistake in Romans 9 is not unique to first-century Judaism. It is the default human approach to God in every culture and every era. The instinct to earn standing before God through religious effort is perhaps the most universal religious impulse there is. It feels right — it seems like the relationship with God should be proportional to the effort invested. Paul's insistence that righteousness comes through faith rather than effort is not just a correction of first-century Judaism. It is a correction of the deepest default setting of human religiosity in every generation including this one.

Romans Chapter 10

Paul's Prayer for Israel

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

Paul's heart's deepest desire and his prayer to God is that the people of Israel may be saved. He can say this about them — they are deeply passionate about God. But their passion is not based on a true understanding of him. They did not understand how God makes people right with himself so they tried to set up their own way of doing it. In doing so they refused to accept God's way. For Christ is the end of the law — he provides a right relationship with God for everyone who believes.

What You Might Not Know

My heart's deepest desire and my prayer to God is that the people of Israel may be saved — Paul opened Chapter 9 with grief and he opens Chapter 10 with prayer. The grief has not turned into contempt or indifference. He is still praying for the people who have beaten him imprisoned him and tried to kill him. The pastoral heart that wept for the Ephesian elders is the same heart that prays for the people who have opposed his ministry most fiercely. Paul's love for Israel was not contingent on Israel's treatment of him.

They are deeply passionate about God — the Greek word is zēlon meaning zeal — the same word used for the zealots who were willing to die for Torah observance. Paul is not dismissing Jewish religious seriousness. He is acknowledging it as genuine and intense. The problem he names is not lack of passion but misdirection of passion. Zeal without accurate knowledge is not a virtue — it is a dangerous combination. Paul himself had been the living proof of this before Damascus. He had been the most zealous persecutor of the Church precisely because his zeal was not guided by truth.

They tried to set up their own way — the Greek is idian dikaiosynēn histantes — establishing their own righteousness. The word own is the key. Israel was not pursuing righteousness in the abstract. They were pursuing a righteousness that belonged to them — produced by their own effort and therefore something they could claim credit for. The problem was not the pursuit of righteousness. It was the source they were looking to for it. God's righteousness is received as a gift. Their own righteousness was constructed as an achievement.

Christ is the end of the law — the Greek word telos means both end and goal — fulfillment and termination simultaneously. Christ is the termination of the law as a system for achieving righteousness before God. He is also the goal the law was always pointing toward. The law was a road that had a destination. Christ is the destination. The person who reaches the destination no longer needs to keep walking the road. The road was always meant to lead somewhere and it has led there.

Why It Still Matters

Their passion isn't based on a true understanding of him. The combination of intense religious passion and theological misdirection is one of the most dangerous conditions a person or a community can be in. Passion without accurate knowledge does not produce less harm than indifference — it often produces more because it is pursued with conviction and energy. Paul's diagnosis of Israel applies to every generation of religious zeal that pursues its own construction of righteousness rather than receiving the righteousness God offers. The correction is not less passion. It is passion redirected toward accurate understanding of who God is and how he works.

Faith vs. Following Rules

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

Moses wrote about the way of being right with God through keeping the law — the person who does these things will find life through them. But the way of faith says something different — do not say in your heart who will go up to heaven as if to bring Christ down or who will go down into the deep as if to bring Christ up from the dead. So what does it say? The message is very close to you — it is in your mouth and in your heart. And that is exactly the message of faith Paul preaches — if you openly declare with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead you will be saved. It is by believing in your heart that you are made right with God and it is by openly declaring your faith that you are saved.

What You Might Not Know

Paul quotes Deuteronomy 30 and applies it to the Gospel — Deuteronomy 30 is Moses speaking to Israel before they enter the promised land. He is describing the word of God as near — not hidden in heaven or across the sea but close, accessible, in their mouths and hearts. Paul takes this passage about the nearness and accessibility of God's word and applies it to the Gospel. The point of contact is the theme of nearness and accessibility. The way of faith is not a distant impossible achievement — it is close. The message is already near. The response required is not a heroic ascent or descent but simply trust in what has already been done.

Who will go up to heaven as if to bring Christ down — Paul is making the point that the incarnation has already happened. There is no need to ascend to heaven to retrieve Christ — he has already come down. Who will go down into the deep as if to bring Christ up from the dead — the resurrection has already happened. There is no need to descend to retrieve Christ from death — he has already risen. The two great acts that the Gospel proclaims — incarnation and resurrection — have already occurred. What remains is not an impossible quest but a response to accomplished fact.

If you openly declare with your mouth that Jesus is Lord — the confession Jesus is Lord was the most politically charged statement in the Roman Empire. Caesar was Lord — Kyrios Kaisar was the Roman imperial confession. To declare Kyrios Iesous — Jesus is Lord — was to place Jesus above Caesar in the hierarchy of allegiance. This was not a private spiritual opinion. It was a public declaration with social and potentially legal consequences. Paul is describing genuine saving faith as something that has a public dimension — it is not merely an internal conviction but an expressed allegiance.

Believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead — the resurrection is the content of the belief Paul names as essential. Not merely that Jesus existed or that he was a good teacher or that he died for sins in some general sense. Specifically that God raised him from the dead. The resurrection is the hinge point of everything — it is what validates the death as effective, what demonstrates Jesus's lordship, and what guarantees the future resurrection of believers. Faith in the resurrection is not one option among several. It is the irreducible core.

Why It Still Matters

The message is very close to you — it is in your mouth and in your heart. Paul's insistence on the nearness and accessibility of the Gospel is one of the most pastoral notes in Romans. The Gospel is not an achievement for the spiritually advanced or the intellectually sophisticated. It is close — as close as the words in your mouth and the convictions in your heart. The response it requires — trust and declaration — is available to everyone regardless of their education or religious background or moral history. The accessibility of the Gospel is not a lowering of standards. It is the shape of grace — bringing salvation within reach of everyone rather than reserving it for those capable of the heroic spiritual effort that law-keeping required.

No Difference Between Anyone

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

As the Scripture says anyone who trusts in him will never be put to shame. There is no difference between Jews and Gentiles in this — the same Lord is Lord over everyone and he gives generously to everyone who calls on him. As the Scripture says everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.

What You Might Not Know

No difference between Jews and Gentiles — Paul has used this phrase before in Romans 3. It appeared there in the context of universal sinfulness — there is no difference because all have sinned. Here it appears in the context of universal access to salvation — there is no difference because the same Lord is Lord over everyone. The same principle that levels humanity before the problem also levels humanity before the solution. The equality of condemnation and the equality of access to salvation are two sides of the same coin. The Gospel does not create a new hierarchy to replace the old one. It eliminates the basis for hierarchy entirely.

The same Lord is Lord over everyone — the word Lord here is Kyrios — the same word used for both Jesus and God throughout the New Testament and the same word used in the Greek translation of the Old Testament for the divine name YHWH. When Paul says Jesus is Lord over everyone he is making a claim that goes beyond religious preference or cultural affiliation. He is claiming that the risen Jesus has universal sovereignty — over Jews and Gentiles, over believers and unbelievers, over the living and the dead. The universality of his lordship is the basis for the universality of the offer.

He gives generously to everyone who calls on him — the Greek word for generously is ploutōn meaning being rich toward or lavishing upon. God does not give sparingly to those who call on him. He gives from abundance — the riches of his grace poured out on everyone who calls. The word everyone appears again — no category of person is excluded from the invitation or from the generosity with which the invitation is honored. The only condition is calling — which is the verbal expression of the faith Paul has been describing throughout the chapter.

Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved — Paul quotes Joel 2 where the prophet describes the outpouring of the Spirit on all flesh in the last days. Peter had quoted the same passage at Pentecost. The everyone of Joel's prophecy is being fulfilled — the calling on the name of the Lord is now open to everyone regardless of ethnic or religious background. The day of the Lord that Joel anticipated has arrived in the person and work of Jesus and the outpouring of his Spirit.

Why It Still Matters

Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved. The word everyone in this verse is as absolute as any word in Scripture. Paul has spent nine and a half chapters establishing the universality of the human problem — everyone has sinned, everyone falls short, everyone is under condemnation. He now announces the universality of the solution with the same word — everyone who calls. The scope of the invitation matches the scope of the need. No one is excluded from the diagnosis and no one is excluded from the remedy. The only question is whether you will call.

Someone Has to Tell Them

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

How can people call on him if they have not believed in him? How can they believe in him if they have never heard about him? How can they hear unless someone tells them? How can anyone tell them unless they are sent? As the Scripture says — how beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news. But not everyone accepted the good news. Isaiah said — Lord who has believed our message? So faith comes from hearing and hearing comes through the word about Christ.

What You Might Not Know

Paul's chain of questions works backward from the goal — calling on the Lord — to the starting point — being sent. Call requires belief. Belief requires hearing. Hearing requires someone telling. Telling requires being sent. The chain is unbroken and each link is necessary. Paul is not making an abstract argument about epistemology. He is making a practical argument about mission. If salvation comes through calling on the Lord and calling requires hearing and hearing requires someone telling then the mission of proclamation is not optional for the Church. It is the necessary mechanism through which salvation reaches people who would otherwise never call.

How beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news — Paul quotes Isaiah 52 where the prophet describes a messenger running across the mountains bringing news that God has rescued Israel from exile. The beauty is not in the messenger's eloquence or appearance. It is in the message they carry and the relief it brings to people who have been waiting. The feet are beautiful because of where they have gone and what they have brought back. Paul applies this to everyone who carries the Gospel — their feet are beautiful because they bring the news that changes everything for everyone who receives it.

Not everyone accepted the good news — the Greek word for accepted is hypēkousan meaning obeyed or responded to. Paul immediately qualifies the enthusiasm of the beautiful feet passage with realism. The sending and the proclaiming do not guarantee the receiving. Isaiah had already warned about this — Lord who has believed our message? The prophet who spoke most clearly about the suffering servant and the redemption of Israel was also the prophet who most honestly confronted the reality of unbelief. Proclamation and rejection have always gone together.

Faith comes from hearing and hearing comes through the word about Christ — this is one of the most important statements about how faith is produced in Romans. Faith is not self-generated. It is not the result of sufficient moral effort or religious sincerity. It comes from hearing — akoes — and what is heard is the word about Christ. The implication is that the Gospel must be proclaimed in order for faith to arise. People do not naturally arrive at saving faith through general religious reflection. They hear something specific about a specific person and respond to what they hear.

Why It Still Matters

How can they believe in him if they have never heard about him? And how can they hear unless someone tells them? Paul's chain of questions is the theological foundation of every missionary and evangelistic effort in Christian history. The logic is simple and unavoidable — if hearing is necessary for faith and telling is necessary for hearing then someone must tell. The urgency of the mission is proportional to the stakes involved. If everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved and if calling requires hearing then the telling is not a hobby for the especially enthusiastic. It is the necessary link in the chain between God's offer and humanity's need.

Did Israel Hear? Did Israel Understand?

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

Did Israel not hear? Of course they did — the message has gone out to the whole earth and their words have reached the ends of the world. Then did Israel not understand? First Moses said — I will make you jealous of people who are not even a nation I will make you angry at a nation that has no understanding. And Isaiah goes even further — I was found by people who were not looking for me I showed myself to those who were not asking for me. But about Israel God says — all day long I have held out my hands to a disobedient and stubborn people.

What You Might Not Know

Did Israel not hear — Paul's question anticipates a possible excuse for Israel's unbelief. Perhaps they had not heard. Perhaps they had not understood. He dismisses both excuses with Scripture. The message has gone out to the whole earth — Paul quotes Psalm 19 which describes the heavens declaring the glory of God to all the earth without words. Applied to the Gospel proclamation the point is that the message has gone everywhere. Israel in particular — the people with the prophets and the Scriptures and the Messiah in their midst — has had every possible opportunity to hear.

I will make you jealous of people who are not even a nation — Paul quotes Deuteronomy 32 where Moses predicts that Israel will provoke God to jealousy through idolatry and God will respond by showing favor to a non-people — a nation without the covenant standing of Israel. Paul reads this as a prediction of the Gentile inclusion in the Gospel. God's extension of salvation to Gentiles was designed in part to provoke Israel to jealousy — to show them through the Gentiles' reception of what Israel had rejected how valuable was the thing they had turned away from. This theme will be developed more fully in Chapter 11.

I was found by people who were not looking for me — Paul quotes Isaiah 65 where God describes his availability to people who had not sought him. Applied to the Gentiles this describes precisely what had happened — people with no prior relationship with Israel's God, no covenant history, no Scriptures in their own language had encountered the Gospel and believed. The seeking and the finding ran in the opposite direction from what would have been expected. God found the Gentiles rather than the Gentiles finding God.

All day long I have held out my hands to a disobedient and stubborn people — Paul quotes Isaiah 65 again but this time the words are about Israel rather than Gentiles. The image is of God standing with arms extended in invitation — all day long — and Israel refusing to come. The refusal is not passive indifference. It is active disobedience and stubbornness. God's hands have been open. The problem is not that the invitation was not given. The problem is that the invited people have chosen not to respond.

Why It Still Matters

All day long I have held out my hands to a disobedient and stubborn people. The image of God standing with outstretched hands is one of the most moving in all of Scripture. It is not the image of a God who is indifferent to human response or who has predetermined everything so that human choice is irrelevant. It is the image of a God who invites, who waits, who holds out his hands — and who is genuinely grieved when the invitation is refused. The stubbornness Paul names is real and the responsibility for it belongs to the people who have refused. The hands are still extended. That is where Chapter 10 ends — with God's arms open and Israel's back turned. Chapter 11 will ask whether it will stay that way.

Romans Chapter 11

God Has Not Rejected Israel

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Did God reject his own people? Absolutely not. Paul is an Israelite himself — a descendant of Abraham from the tribe of Benjamin. God has not rejected the people he chose from the very beginning. Paul points to the story of Elijah who complained to God that he was the only faithful one left and they were trying to kill him too. But God's answer was that he had kept seven thousand men who had never bowed to Baal. It is the same way today — there is a group of people God has chosen by his grace. And since it is by grace it is not based on anything they have done. If it were based on works grace would not really be grace.

What You Might Not Know

I am an Israelite myself — a descendant of Abraham from the tribe of Benjamin — Paul's personal identity is his first argument against the idea that God has rejected Israel. If God had completely cast off Israel Paul himself would be outside the covenant. But Paul is clearly not outside the covenant — he is an apostle of Jesus the Messiah who has received the Spirit and been entrusted with the Gospel. His own existence as a Jewish follower of Jesus is evidence that God has not rejected his people. The remnant that God has preserved includes Paul himself.

The story of Elijah — Elijah had reached the end of his strength after the confrontation with the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel. He fled into the wilderness and told God he was the only faithful person left in Israel. God's response was to tell him that seven thousand had not bowed to Baal. The point Paul draws from this is that the visible remnant is never the whole remnant. Elijah could not see the seven thousand. He thought he was alone. The hidden work of God in preserving faithful people was invisible to the prophet who was in the middle of the crisis. The same principle applies in Paul's time — the visible response to the Gospel does not represent the full extent of what God is doing.

Chosen by grace — the Greek is kat' eklogēn charitos meaning according to the election of grace. The remnant Paul describes is not the remnant of the particularly righteous or the especially diligent. It is the remnant preserved by God's grace — chosen not on the basis of what they have done but on the basis of God's own sovereign mercy. This connects directly to the argument of Chapter 9 — God's purposes operate through selection rather than through universal ethnic inclusion, and that selection is driven by grace rather than merit.

If it were based on works grace wouldn't really be grace — Paul states the logical incompatibility of grace and works with characteristic bluntness. Grace by definition is unearned. A grace that is earned by sufficient works is not grace — it is wages. The two systems are mutually exclusive. You cannot have a grace-based system and a works-based system operating simultaneously for the same outcome. The remnant either exists because of grace or because of works. Paul insists it is grace — which means the remnant cannot take credit for being the remnant.

Why It Still Matters

I have kept for myself seven thousand who have never bowed to Baal. The invisibility of the faithful remnant is one of the most practically important truths in Romans 11. Every generation of believers has had moments of Elijah-like despair — the sense that faithfulness has dwindled to almost nothing and that you might be the only one left. God's consistent answer across Scripture is the same as his answer to Elijah — you cannot see them all but they are there. The work of grace is larger than what is visible to any one observer at any one moment. That truth has sustained faithful people through every period of apparent decline in the history of the Church.

What Happened to the Rest of Israel?

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Most of Israel did not find what they were looking for. Only the chosen ones found it while the rest had their hearts hardened. As the Scripture says God gave them a spirit of deep sleep — eyes that do not see and ears that do not hear right down to this very day. And David says — let their blessings become a trap and a snare something that trips them up and brings them down. Let their eyes be darkened so they cannot see and keep their backs bent under their burdens forever.

What You Might Not Know

A spirit of deep sleep — Paul quotes Isaiah 29 where the prophet describes Israel's spiritual stupor in his own day. The people who should have been most responsive to God's word had become the least responsive. Their very familiarity with the things of God had produced a kind of spiritual numbness — they had the forms of religion without the responsive hearts that the forms were meant to express. Paul sees the same pattern in his own time — those who should have recognized their Messiah most readily were the ones who most consistently failed to recognize him.

Eyes that don't see and ears that don't hear — this phrase echoes throughout the prophets and the Gospels. Isaiah experienced it in his commissioning vision in Chapter 6 — go to a people who will hear but not understand see but not perceive. Jesus quoted the same passage to explain why he spoke in parables. Paul quotes it here about Israel's response to the Gospel. The recurring pattern suggests that spiritual blindness and deafness are not unusual or surprising outcomes when God's word comes to people whose hearts are not oriented toward receiving it.

Let their blessings become a trap — Paul quotes Psalm 69 which is one of the most widely quoted psalms in the New Testament — applied to Jesus's passion in the Gospels and here to Israel's judgment. The irony Paul highlights is that the very blessings Israel received — the law the temple the promises the Messiah — became the occasion for their stumbling. The table set before them became the trap. The light they were given became the means of their darkening. Blessing received without responsive faith can become an occasion for greater hardness rather than greater gratitude.

Why It Still Matters

God gave them a spirit of deep sleep — eyes that don't see and ears that don't hear. The hardening Paul describes is not God arbitrarily blinding people who were trying to see. It is God confirming and deepening a condition that the people themselves had chosen. The hardening in Romans 11 is judicial — it is God's response to persistent refusal rather than the cause of the refusal. The same sun that melts wax hardens clay depending on the nature of the material it shines on. The Gospel proclaimed to people whose hearts are set against it does not leave them unchanged — it tends to confirm and deepen the resistance that was already there.

Salvation Has Come to the Gentiles

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Did Israel trip so badly that they fell forever? No. Because of their stumble salvation has come to the Gentiles and this was meant to make Israel jealous. Now if their stumble brought such riches to the world and their failure brought such riches to the Gentiles imagine how much greater the blessing will be when all of Israel is restored. Paul is speaking especially to Gentiles. Since he is an apostle to the Gentiles he takes his work seriously. He hopes that somehow he might make his own people jealous and save some of them. For if God's setting them aside meant that the rest of the world was brought back to him what will it be like when he accepts them back? It will be nothing less than life from the dead.

What You Might Not Know

Did Israel trip so badly that they fell forever — Paul's question is the hinge of Chapters 9 through 11. He has established that most of Israel has stumbled over the stumbling stone of the Messiah. The natural question is whether the stumble is permanent. Paul's answer is emphatically no — the stumble has served a purpose and the story is not over. The word for fell forever is pesōsin meaning to fall completely and finally. Paul insists the fall is neither complete nor final.

Because of their stumble salvation has come to the Gentiles — Paul is describing a providential purpose in Israel's rejection of the Gospel. He does not say Israel's stumble was good in itself. He says God has worked through it to bring salvation to the Gentiles. The rejection of the Gospel by the Jewish community in each city Paul visited was the repeated occasion for turning to the Gentiles — in Pisidian Antioch Corinth and Rome. What looked like failure from one angle was the mechanism of expansion from another.

This was meant to make Israel jealous — the jealousy theme comes from Deuteronomy 32 which Paul quoted in Chapter 10. God's strategy for drawing Israel back is not argument or coercion. It is the sight of what they have turned away from being received and treasured by people who had no prior claim on it. The Gentile community joyfully receiving what Israel had rejected was designed to provoke Israel to reconsider what they had turned away from. Paul's own mission to the Gentiles was itself part of this strategy.

Life from the dead — Paul's description of Israel's future restoration uses the language of resurrection. The acceptance of Israel back into covenant relationship will be so dramatic and so transforming that it can only be described as life from the dead. Whether Paul means this literally — that Israel's restoration will coincide with or trigger the general resurrection — or metaphorically — that it will be as dramatic as resurrection — is debated. What is clear is that Paul expects Israel's restoration to be one of the most significant events in redemptive history.

Why It Still Matters

If their stumble brought such riches to the world imagine how much greater the blessing will be when all of Israel is restored. Paul's argument from the lesser to the greater is one of the most hope-filled passages in Romans. If Israel's failure produced the Gentile mission which has spread the Gospel across the world then Israel's restoration will produce something even greater — something Paul can only describe as life from the dead. The story of Israel and the Gospel is not finished. The stumble is not the end of the chapter. The God who works all things together for good is still working and the best is still ahead.

The Olive Tree

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

If the first piece of dough is holy the whole batch is holy. If the root of a tree is holy the branches are holy too. Imagine some branches of an olive tree were broken off and a wild olive branch was grafted in among the others now sharing in the rich life of the cultivated olive root. Do not brag about being better than the broken branches. The root supports you — you do not support the root. The branches were broken off because they did not believe and you are there only because you do believe. So do not be arrogant — be grateful and humble. If God did not spare the natural branches he will not automatically spare you either. God is both kind and severe — severe toward those who fell but kind to you as long as you keep living in his kindness. Otherwise you will be cut off too. And if the people of Israel turn from their unbelief God will graft them back in — he has the power to do it. If you were cut from a wild olive tree and grafted onto a good one which goes against nature it will be even easier for God to graft the original branches back onto their own tree.

What You Might Not Know

The olive tree — in the Hebrew prophets the olive tree was a common image for Israel. Paul takes this image and develops it into one of the most sustained metaphors in his letters. The cultivated olive tree represents the covenant community rooted in the patriarchs and the promises. The natural branches are Jewish people who belong to the covenant by birth. The broken off branches are Jewish people who have not believed the Gospel. The wild olive branches are Gentile believers grafted into a tree that is not naturally their own.

Grafting in agriculture — Paul's agricultural metaphor is technically unusual. In actual olive cultivation you would normally graft a cultivated branch onto a wild rootstock — not a wild branch onto a cultivated tree as Paul describes. Paul acknowledges this goes against nature. His point is that the grafting of Gentiles into Israel's covenant tree is itself an act of divine grace that goes beyond the natural order. It should not produce pride in the grafted branch. It should produce astonishment and gratitude.

You don't support the root — the root supports you — this is Paul's most direct warning to Gentile believers in Romans. The temptation to look down on Jewish people or to treat the Church as a replacement for Israel that has now superseded and surpassed its predecessor is directly addressed and rejected. The Gentile church does not support the Jewish root — the Jewish root supports the Gentile church. The covenants the promises the Scriptures the Messiah himself — all came through Israel. Gentile arrogance toward Israel is ingratitude toward the very source of the blessing the Gentiles have received.

God will graft them back in — Paul's insistence that the broken off natural branches can be grafted back in is the theological basis for ongoing hope for Jewish salvation. The breaking off was not permanent and irreversible. It was conditional on unbelief and reversible through faith. The same God who broke them off because of unbelief will graft them back in when they turn from unbelief. The power to restore is explicitly affirmed — he has the power to do it. Paul is establishing that Jewish salvation is not a theological impossibility that requires rewriting God's purposes. It is the continuation of God's original plan for his people.

Why It Still Matters

Don't brag about being better than the branches that were broken off. Remember you don't support the root — the root supports you. Paul's warning to Gentile believers in Chapter 11 is one of the most important and most consistently ignored passages in the New Testament. The history of Christian anti-Semitism — the contempt for Jewish people that has characterized much of the Church's relationship with Jewish communities across two millennia — is precisely the attitude Paul forbids here. Gentile believers who look down on Jewish people have forgotten what Paul insists they remember — everything they have came through Israel. The appropriate posture is not superiority but gratitude and humility before the root that supports them.

A Mystery Revealed

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Paul wants the brothers and sisters to understand this so they do not become proud. Part of Israel has become stubborn but this will last only until the full number of Gentiles has come to God. And then all Israel will be saved — as the Scripture says the Savior will come from Zion and remove all ungodliness from the family of Jacob. And this is my covenant with them — I will take away their sins. As far as the good news goes they are currently treated as enemies for the Gentiles' sake. But as far as God's choice goes they are still loved because of the promises God made to their ancestors. God never changes his mind about the people he calls or the gifts he gives. At one time the Gentiles were disobedient to God but now they have received mercy because of Israel's disobedience. In the same way Israel has now become disobedient so that they too might receive the same mercy the Gentiles received. God has allowed everyone to fall into disobedience so that he can show mercy to everyone.

What You Might Not Know

A mystery — the Greek word is mystērion which in Paul's letters means not something mysterious or puzzling but a divine secret that has now been revealed. The mystery Paul is about to disclose was hidden in previous ages but is now being made known through the Gospel. The mystery is the plan for Israel and the Gentiles — the hardening of part of Israel until the full number of Gentiles comes in and then the salvation of all Israel. This was not something that human reasoning could have deduced from the available evidence. It required revelation — which is what Paul claims to be providing.

The full number of Gentiles — the Greek is plērōma tōn ethnōn meaning the fullness or completion of the Gentiles. Paul is describing a divinely determined point — when the complete number of Gentiles who will come in has come in — at which point the dynamic will shift and Israel's hardening will be lifted. What determines the full number is not stated. Whether this refers to a specific numerical count or to the completion of the Gentile mission in some qualitative sense is debated. What Paul is clear about is that the Gentile mission has a completion point built into God's plan.

All Israel will be saved — this is one of the most debated phrases in Romans. Does all Israel mean every individual Jewish person? A representative fullness of Jewish people? The complete covenant community including both Jewish and Gentile believers? Most interpreters understand it as referring to the Jewish people as a corporate entity — a large scale turning of Israel to the Messiah at some future point. Paul's quotation from Isaiah 59 — the Savior will come from Zion and remove ungodliness from Jacob — supports the idea of a future decisive act of God toward the Jewish people.

God never changes his mind about the people he calls or the gifts he gives — the Greek word is ametamelētos meaning without repentance or irrevocable. The gifts and calling God extended to Israel have not been revoked. This directly refutes any theology that treats the Church as having simply replaced Israel so that God's promises to Israel are now transferred entirely to the Church. The promises were made to Israel. They remain in force for Israel. The current partial hardening is temporary and purposeful — not a permanent cancellation of the covenant.

Why It Still Matters

God has allowed everyone to fall into disobedience so that he can show mercy to everyone. Paul's summary of the entire argument of Chapters 9 through 11 is one of the most theologically compressed and emotionally powerful statements in Romans. The universal scope of human disobedience — everyone has fallen — is matched by the universal scope of divine mercy — mercy for everyone. The story of Israel and the Gentiles is the story of God working through failure and disobedience to produce mercy that reaches everyone. The plan was not derailed by human sin. Human sin was the occasion through which the plan worked itself out toward a mercy larger than anyone had imagined.

Praise to God

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

How great are God's riches and his wisdom and knowledge. No one can explain his decisions or understand his ways. Who has ever known the mind of the Lord? Who has ever been his advisor? Who has ever given anything to God that God must pay back? Everything comes from him exists through him and is for him. To him be glory forever. Amen.

What You Might Not Know

The doxology that ends Chapter 11 is the conclusion of the theological argument of Chapters 1 through 11. Paul has spent eleven chapters explaining the Gospel — the human problem, the divine solution, justification by faith, union with Christ, life in the Spirit, the hope of glory, and the plan for Israel and the Gentiles. Having reached the end of the argument he does not draw a practical conclusion or issue an application. He breaks into worship. Theology done properly produces doxology. The right response to understanding what God has done is not primarily intellectual satisfaction but adoration.

His wisdom and knowledge — the Greek words are sophia and gnōsis. God's wisdom is his ability to achieve his purposes through means that human wisdom could not have anticipated or devised. God's knowledge is his comprehensive understanding of all things — past present and future. Both are described as unfathomable — depths that cannot be plumbed by human inquiry. The plan Paul has just described — working through Israel's failure to bring Gentile salvation and then using the Gentile mission to restore Israel — is precisely the kind of wisdom that no human counselor would have devised.

Who has ever given anything to God that God must pay back — Paul quotes Job 41 where God challenges Job from the whirlwind. The point is that God is not in anyone's debt. No one has done something for God that puts God under obligation to repay. This eliminates the foundation of a merit-based relationship with God. If no one has ever given God something he had to pay back then no one has any claim on God based on their own contribution. Everything God gives is grace — not repayment of a debt.

Everything comes from him exists through him and is for him — the three prepositions ex autou di autou eis auton describe God as the source the sustainer and the goal of all things. From him — all things originate in God. Through him — all things are sustained and governed by God. For him — all things move toward God as their ultimate purpose and destination. Creation is not self-originating self-sustaining or self-directing. It is entirely dependent on and oriented toward the God from whom it came. The doxology that ends the theological section of Romans is a declaration of this comprehensive divine sovereignty.

Why It Still Matters

To him be glory forever. Amen. Paul ends eleven chapters of some of the most demanding theological writing in human history with a simple declaration of praise. The theology was not an end in itself. It was always in the service of this — a deeper and more grounded adoration of the God who has done what Paul has spent eleven chapters describing. When theology is done well it does not produce pride in the theologian or mere intellectual satisfaction in the reader. It produces exactly what Paul expresses here — an overwhelming sense of the depth of God's wisdom and an inability to respond with anything except worship. The Amen is the only appropriate conclusion to what has come before it.

Romans Chapter 12

Give Yourself Completely to God

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Because God has shown such great mercy Paul urges the believers to offer their lives as a living sacrifice — keeping themselves holy and doing what pleases him. This is the truest form of worship there is. Do not copy the behaviors and customs of this world. Instead let God transform you into a new person by changing the way you think. Then you will learn to know God's will for you — which is good pleasing and perfect.

What You Might Not Know

Because God has shown us such great mercy — the therefore that opens Chapter 12 in most translations is one of the most important words in Romans. It connects everything Paul is about to say about the Christian life to everything he has said in Chapters 1 through 11 about what God has done. The practical instructions that fill Chapters 12 through 15 are not a separate moral code. They are the response to the mercy Paul has spent eleven chapters describing. The Christian life is not lived in order to earn the mercy. It is lived because the mercy has already been received. The order is everything — grace first then the response to grace.

Offer your lives as a living sacrifice — the Greek word for offer is parastēsai — the same word used in Chapter 6 for offering the parts of yourself to God. Paul is drawing on the imagery of the Temple sacrificial system. In the old system animals were killed and placed on the altar. Paul describes the new sacrifice as living — the person is not destroyed but offered while still alive. The sacrifice is the ongoing dedication of the whole life to God rather than a single act at a single moment. The altar is the daily decisions about how to live.

Keep yourselves holy and do what pleases him — the Greek word for holy is hagios meaning set apart or consecrated. The living sacrifice is described in two ways — holy in its orientation toward God and pleasing in its effect on God. These are the qualities that made an animal sacrifice acceptable in the old system — it had to be without blemish and acceptable to God. Paul transfers these qualities to the life of the believer. The life offered to God should be set apart from what is corrupt and oriented toward what is pleasing to him.

This is the truest form of worship there is — the Greek is logikēn latreian meaning reasonable or rational worship or spiritual worship. The word logikēn connects worship to the logos — the word or reason. True worship is not primarily a ritual performed at set times in a sacred place. It is the offering of the mind and the life in response to who God is and what he has done. Paul is not dismissing gathered worship. He is expanding the category of worship to include the whole of life lived in conscious response to God's mercy.

Let God transform you by changing the way you think — the Greek word for transform is metamorphousthe from which we get metamorphosis. The transformation Paul describes is not self-improvement through moral effort. It is a divine work — let God transform you. The mechanism is the renewal of the mind — the reorientation of the whole inner life toward truth. As the mind is renewed by truth the behavior follows. Paul is reversing the order that most religious systems assume — behavior does not produce character. Renewed thinking produces transformed living.

Why It Still Matters

Don't copy the behaviors and customs of this world. Instead let God transform you by changing the way you think. Paul's instruction is as countercultural now as it was in first-century Rome. Every culture exerts pressure on the people living in it to conform to its assumptions about what matters what is worth pursuing and how life should be organized. The pressure is not always obvious — it operates through what is taken for granted rather than through explicit demands. The transformation Paul calls for is a fundamental reorientation of the mind that produces a different set of assumptions about what is real and what matters. That reorientation is ongoing — it is not accomplished once and finished. It is the work of a lifetime of renewed thinking.

Use the Gifts God Gave You

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Because of the grace God has given him Paul has something to say to each person — do not think of yourself as more important than you really are. Be honest in how you see yourself measured by the faith God has given you. Just as our bodies have many parts and each part has its own job it is the same with the body of Christ. Though we are many we are one body and we all belong to each other. In his grace God has given each person different gifts. If your gift is prophecy speak out with as much faith as God has given you. If your gift is serving others serve them well. If you are a teacher teach well. If your gift is encouraging others be encouraging. If it is giving give generously. If God has given you leadership ability take that responsibility seriously. And if you have a gift for showing kindness do it gladly.

What You Might Not Know

Don't think of yourself as more important than you really are — the Greek is mē hyperphronein par ho dei phronein — do not think beyond what you ought to think. Paul uses the word think four times in this one verse in the original Greek — phronein, hyperphronein, sōphronein, phronein. The wordplay is deliberate. There is a thinking that goes beyond what is appropriate — pride. There is a thinking that is sound and measured — humility. The corrective to inflated self-assessment is not deflated self-assessment. It is accurate self-assessment — seeing yourself as you actually are in relation to the grace you have received and the gifts you have been given.

Measured by the faith God has given you — this phrase locates the standard of self-assessment outside the self. You measure yourself not by comparison with other people but by the measure of faith God has given you. Each person has received a measure of faith — a particular capacity for trust and service. The honest assessment of yourself involves recognizing both what you have been given and what you have not been given. Neither inflation nor deflation serves the body. Honest assessment does.

We are one body and we all belong to each other — the body metaphor Paul develops here appears more fully in 1 Corinthians 12. The essential point is that diversity of function within unity of organism is the design rather than the problem. The hand does not need to become the eye to be valuable. The eye does not need to become the hand to be useful. Each part contributes what it is designed to contribute and the body functions because of the diversity not despite it. A body made entirely of eyes or entirely of hands would be non-functional.

The gifts Paul lists — prophecy serving teaching encouraging giving leadership and showing kindness — are a representative rather than exhaustive list. Paul gives similar but different lists in 1 Corinthians 12 and Ephesians 4. No single list in any letter is meant to be the complete catalog. The consistent principle across all the lists is that every believer has received something — a capacity for service — and that thing is given for the benefit of the whole body rather than for the personal advancement of the one who has it.

Why It Still Matters

We are one body and we all belong to each other. Paul's vision of the Christian community as a body in which every part belongs to every other part is one of the most radical social visions in the ancient world. In a culture rigidly organized by social class ethnic background and economic status Paul describes a community in which a slave and a senator belong to each other in the same body. The belonging is mutual and complete — not the senator belonging to the church in a patronage sense but both belonging to each other as fellow members of the same organism. That vision of mutual belonging across every social boundary is still the most subversive community vision on offer.

How to Treat Each Other

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Do not just pretend to love others — really love them. Hate what is wrong and hold tightly to what is good. Love each other with genuine affection and take delight in honoring each other. Never be lazy but work hard and serve the Lord with an enthusiastic heart. Rejoice in the hope you have. Be patient in trouble and keep on praying. When God's people are in need help them. Always be eager to welcome others into your home. Bless those who persecute you — do not curse them but pray that God will bless them. Be happy with those who are happy and weep with those who weep. Live in harmony with each other. Do not be too proud to enjoy the company of ordinary people. And do not think you know it all.

What You Might Not Know

Don't just pretend to love others — really love them — the Greek is hē agapē anypokritos meaning love without hypocrisy or love that does not wear a mask. The word anypokritos gives us the English word hypocrite — a stage actor who wore a mask to play a role. Paul is calling for love that is not a performance — not the behavior of love without the reality of love underneath it. Genuine love is not always warm feeling — it includes hating what is wrong and holding to what is good. The love Paul describes has moral content and moral backbone. It is not merely affectionate. It is committed to the wellbeing of the other in ways that require honesty and sometimes difficulty.

Take delight in honoring each other — the Greek is tē timē allēlous proēgoumenoi meaning outdoing one another in showing honor. The competitive instinct that most social environments direct toward personal advancement Paul redirects toward honoring others. The race is to see who can honor others most — not who can accumulate the most honor for themselves. This inversion of the normal social competition is one of the most practically countercultural instructions in Romans. In a Roman world obsessed with social status and honor Paul describes a community competing to give honor rather than to receive it.

Serve the Lord with an enthusiastic heart — the Greek is tō pneumati zeontes meaning boiling or burning in spirit. The word zeontes gives us the English word zealous through its Latin equivalent. The enthusiasm Paul calls for is not manufactured emotional intensity. It is the natural overflow of a spirit that has been set on fire by the mercy of God described in the first eleven chapters. When you understand what God has done for you working hard in his service is not a burden — it is the expression of a life that has been genuinely transformed.

Weep with those who weep — this short instruction is one of the most practically important in Romans. It does not say fix those who weep or explain to those who weep why things are not as bad as they seem or tell those who weep about the hope of glory. It says weep with them. The ministry of presence and shared grief is not inferior to the ministry of explanation or comfort. Sometimes the most faithful thing you can do for someone in pain is to sit with them in it. Paul names this as the shape of genuine community alongside the rejoicing with those who rejoice.

Why It Still Matters

Bless those who persecute you. Don't curse them — pray that God will bless them. The instruction to bless persecutors is not a pious aspiration disconnected from reality. Paul is writing to a community in Rome that faces real social pressure and periodic danger. The instruction is not easy — it runs directly against the instinct of self-protection and the desire for justice. Paul is not saying persecution is acceptable or that wrong should not be named. He is saying the response to persecution should be prayer for the persecutor rather than cursing. That response requires a transformation of the heart that only the mercy of God described in Chapters 1 through 11 can produce.

Overcome Evil with Good

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Never pay back evil with more evil. Live in such a way that everyone can see you are honorable. Do everything you can to live in peace with everyone. Dear friends never take revenge — leave that to God. For the Scripture says — I will take revenge and pay them back says the Lord. Instead if your enemies are hungry feed them. If they are thirsty give them something to drink. In doing this you will heap burning coals of shame on their heads. Do not let evil conquer you. Conquer evil by doing good.

What You Might Not Know

Never take revenge — leave that to God — the Greek is mē heautous ekdikountes — do not avenge yourselves. The instruction does not say wrongdoing has no consequences or that justice does not matter. It says the believer is not to take justice into their own hands. The reason is not that the wrong does not deserve a response. It is that God has reserved the response to himself — I will take revenge and pay them back says the Lord. Leaving room for God's wrath means trusting that God will deal with wrongdoing justly. The believer does not need to ensure justice is done — God will ensure it. The freedom that creates is the freedom to respond to enemies with kindness rather than retaliation.

Heap burning coals of shame on their heads — Paul quotes Proverbs 25. The image of burning coals heaped on someone's head has been interpreted in different ways. The most common understanding is that acts of kindness toward enemies produce shame and remorse in them — the burning sensation of recognizing how they have treated someone who is treating them well. Another interpretation draws on an Egyptian ritual in which a person demonstrated repentance by carrying a pan of burning coals on their head. Either way the point is that kindness toward enemies is not passive — it is a powerful act that can produce transformation in the one receiving it.

Do not let evil conquer you — conquer evil by doing good — the word conquer is nikaō — the same root as the more than conquerors of Chapter 8. Evil wins when the person who encounters it responds in kind — adding more evil to the existing supply. Good wins when the person who encounters evil refuses to be changed by it and instead changes it through a different kind of response. The victory Paul describes is not passive endurance. It is active overcoming — engaging evil with a force that evil is not equipped to counter.

Why It Still Matters

Don't let evil conquer you. Conquer evil by doing good. Paul ends Chapter 12 with one of the most practically actionable statements in Romans. The Christian response to evil is not primarily defensive — not primarily avoiding being contaminated by evil. It is offensive — actively bringing good into the space where evil has been operating. The good Paul calls for is not vague benevolence. It is the specific acts he has named throughout the chapter — genuine love honor hospitality generosity patience prayer care for enemies. These specific goods deployed in specific situations where evil is present are the means by which evil is conquered. Not all at once and not without cost — but genuinely and permanently.

Romans Chapter 13

Respect for Authorities

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Everyone should respect and obey the governing authorities. There is no authority that has not been allowed by God — the authorities that exist have been put in place under his oversight. Anyone who rebels against authority is resisting what God has arranged and those who do so will bring judgment on themselves. Public officials are not a threat to people who do what is right — only to those who do wrong. Do you want to live without fear of the authorities? Then do what is good and they will treat you well. The person in authority is God's servant working for your benefit. But if you do what is wrong you should be afraid — because they do not carry the power of punishment for no reason. They are God's servants sent to bring justice to those who practice evil. So obey — not just to avoid punishment but because your conscience tells you it is the right thing to do. This is also why you pay taxes. Give everyone what you owe them — taxes fees respect and honor.

What You Might Not Know

The governing authorities in Rome — Paul is writing to believers in the capital of the Roman Empire. The emperor at the time was almost certainly Nero in the early years of his reign before the persecution of Christians that followed the fire of Rome in 64 AD. The Roman government that Paul is telling believers to respect is the same government that would eventually execute both Paul and Peter. Paul is not telling believers to obey a perfect or just government. He is establishing a general principle about the role of civil authority in God's ordering of human society.

There is no authority that hasn't been allowed by God — the Greek is ou gar estin exousia ei mē hypo theou — there is no authority except from God. Paul is not saying every government is good or that every law is just. He is saying that the institution of governing authority — the existence of ordered human society with laws and rulers — is part of God's design for a world in which human sin would otherwise produce chaos. The alternative to imperfect human government is not a utopia of freedom. It is the war of all against all.

They are God's servants — the Greek word is diakonos meaning servant or minister — the same word used for deacons in the Church. Paul applies it to Roman government officials. This does not mean government officials are consciously serving God or that they are believers. It means they function as God's instruments for maintaining order and restraining evil regardless of their own intentions or beliefs. God uses the governing authority to serve his purposes in the world even when those authorities have no interest in serving him.

They don't carry the power of punishment for no reason — the Greek word for power of punishment is machairan meaning sword. The sword was the symbol of the magistrate's power to execute judgment including capital punishment. Paul is acknowledging that civil government has legitimate coercive power — including the power of deadly force — as part of its God-given function of restraining evil. This is one of the most important passages in the New Testament for the theology of civil government and has been debated extensively in discussions of capital punishment just war and civil disobedience.

Not just to avoid punishment but because your conscience tells you it's the right thing — Paul draws a distinction between two kinds of obedience to civil authority. External compliance motivated by fear of consequences is one kind. Internal obedience motivated by conscience recognizing the legitimacy of ordered authority is another. The second kind is what Paul calls for. The Christian obeys governing authority not primarily because of what will happen if they do not but because conscience recognizes that ordered human society serves genuine goods that God values.

Why It Still Matters

Paul's teaching on governing authorities has been both the most used and the most abused passage in Romans across history. It has been used to justify Christian submission to unjust regimes and to forbid legitimate resistance to tyranny. It has also been used correctly to establish that believers are not inherently anti-government or anarchic — that they have positive reasons from within their own theology to be law-abiding members of society. The passage has limits that Paul does not spell out here but that the rest of Scripture implies — when governing authority commands what God forbids or forbids what God commands the apostles' own example provides the answer. We must obey God rather than men. But that exception does not eliminate the general principle that Paul is establishing.

Love Fulfills the Law

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Do not owe anyone anything except the ongoing debt of loving one another. The person who loves others has fulfilled everything the law requires. The commandments — do not commit adultery do not murder do not steal do not covet — and every other command are all summed up in this one rule — love your neighbor as yourself. Love does no harm to a neighbor. So love is the complete fulfillment of the law.

What You Might Not Know

Don't owe anyone anything except the ongoing debt of loving one another — Paul connects the teaching on civic obligations — pay your taxes pay your fees — to the broader principle of debt. The Christian should be in the business of paying what they owe — financial social and civic obligations. The one debt that can never be fully paid off is the debt of love. You cannot love your neighbor so completely that you have discharged the obligation and no longer owe them love. The debt of love is permanent and ongoing — it is the one debt that grows rather than shrinks as you pay it.

Love your neighbor as yourself — Paul quotes Leviticus 19 — the same commandment Jesus identified as the second great commandment alongside love for God. Jesus himself had made this combination — love for God and love for neighbor — the summary of the whole law and the prophets. Paul is not innovating. He is recovering what Jesus had already made clear — the whole moral demand of the law reduces to love understood not as a feeling but as a committed orientation toward the wellbeing of the other.

Love does no harm to a neighbor — this is the negative formulation of the love command. Paul says love does no harm — it does not commit adultery does not murder does not steal does not covet. The positive commands of the law are expressions of what love looks like in specific situations. The negative prohibitions of the law are expressions of what love refuses to do. When love is genuinely present the specific prohibitions are fulfilled automatically — not because you are following a list but because you are genuinely oriented toward the other person's wellbeing.

The complete fulfillment of the law — the Greek word is plērōma meaning fullness or completion. Love does not merely satisfy the minimum requirements of the law. It fulfills the law completely — brings it to its full expression and intent. Paul has argued throughout Romans that the law cannot produce righteousness because it cannot change the heart. Love — the genuine orientation toward the other that the Spirit produces — does what the law pointed toward but could not accomplish. Love is therefore not a replacement for the law but the completion of what the law was always aimed at.

Why It Still Matters

The person who loves others has fulfilled everything the law requires. Paul's summary of the Christian ethical life as love is not a reduction of the moral demand. It is an intensification of it. Following a list of rules can be done externally without any genuine concern for the person the rules are designed to protect. Love cannot. Love requires genuine orientation toward the other — their actual wellbeing not just the performance of required behaviors toward them. The person who genuinely loves their neighbor will not commit adultery against them murder them steal from them or covet what belongs to them — not because they are tracking a list but because none of those things is consistent with genuinely caring about the person. Love is harder than law because it reaches into the motivation rather than stopping at the behavior.

Wake Up — the Day Is Coming

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Do all of this because you know what time it is. It is time to wake up from sleep. Salvation is closer now than when we first believed. The night is almost over and the day is about to dawn. So get rid of the things done in the dark and put on the armor of light. Live decent lives — lives that can stand up to the daylight. No wild parties or drunkenness. No sexual immorality or shameful behavior. No fighting or jealousy. Instead clothe yourselves with the Lord Jesus the Messiah and stop making plans to feed the selfish desires of your old nature.

What You Might Not Know

You know what time it is — Paul is using eschatological language — language about the end times. He is not talking about the time of day. He is talking about the position of believers in the timeline of redemptive history. The resurrection of Jesus has inaugurated the last days — the period between the first coming and the second coming of Christ. Believers are living in the overlap between the old age that is passing away and the new age that is coming. The awareness of what time it is — the urgency of the moment in redemptive history — should shape how believers live every ordinary day.

Our salvation is closer now than when we first believed — Paul is describing the progressive nearness of the final salvation — the redemption of the body that he described in Chapter 8. Every day that passes is a day closer to the full realization of what began at justification. The salvation Paul describes is both already received — we have been justified — and not yet complete — we await the resurrection of the body and the renewal of creation. The not yet dimension is getting closer. That nearness has practical implications for how you live today.

Put on the armor of light — the military metaphor of armor appears in Ephesians 6 in its fullest development. Here Paul introduces it briefly — the believer is to clothe themselves as for battle in the daylight rather than for the activities of darkness. The armor of light is not a defensive posture — it is the full equipment of someone ready to engage the day and its demands rather than retreating into the behaviors that belong to darkness.

Clothe yourselves with the Lord Jesus the Messiah — this is one of the most striking images in Romans. To clothe yourself with Jesus is to put on his character, his priorities, his way of engaging the world. The believer's identity is to be so thoroughly defined by Jesus that the person who looks at them sees Jesus first. Paul elsewhere describes believers as having already put on Christ at baptism — the clothing metaphor describes an identity that has been assumed and needs to be lived out consistently.

Stop making plans to feed the selfish desires of your old nature — the Greek is mē pronoian poieisthe eis epithumias — do not make provision for the desires of the flesh. The word pronoian means forethought or advance planning. Paul is not only talking about resisting temptation in the moment. He is talking about not setting up the conditions in advance that make temptation more likely. The person who plans their evenings around situations likely to produce the behaviors Paul has listed is not resisting sin — they are provisioning it. The practical wisdom of not making advance arrangements for the old nature is as relevant now as it was in Rome.

Why It Still Matters

The night is almost over and the day is about to dawn. Paul's urgency about the time is not a prediction about when Jesus will return — he does not set a date. It is a description of the posture that awareness of Christ's return should produce in daily life. The person who genuinely believes that the day is coming lives differently than the person who assumes the night will continue indefinitely. The practical instructions Paul gives — no wild parties no sexual immorality no fighting no jealousy clothe yourself with Christ — are the natural expression of a life oriented toward the coming day rather than settling into the behaviors of the current night. The dawn is coming. Live like it.

Romans Chapter 14

Welcome Those Whose Faith Is Weak

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Welcome anyone whose faith is weak but do not start arguments with them over their personal opinions. One person's faith allows them to eat anything while another person whose faith is weak eats only vegetables. The one who eats everything should not look down on the one who does not and the one who does not eat everything should not judge the one who does — because God has accepted them both. Who are you to judge someone else's servant? They answer to their own Master not to you. And they will stand because the Lord is able to make them stand. One person thinks certain days are more sacred than others while someone else thinks every day is the same. Each person should be fully convinced in their own mind. Whoever observes a special day does it to honor the Lord. Whoever eats meat does it to honor the Lord and gives thanks to God. And whoever does not eat meat also does it to honor the Lord and gives thanks to God.

What You Might Not Know

The strong and the weak — Chapter 14 addresses a specific conflict in the Roman church between two groups Paul calls the strong and the weak in faith. The strong are those whose faith allows them to eat any food without scruple. The weak are those who feel they must restrict their diet — eating only vegetables — and who observe certain days as more sacred than others. The most likely background is Jewish believers in Rome who maintained Torah food laws and Sabbath observance alongside Gentile believers who felt no such obligation. Paul navigates this conflict without declaring either side wrong on the substance — he focuses instead on how the two groups should relate to each other.

Weak faith — Paul's use of weak to describe the more restrictive group is not a condemnation. He means weak in the sense of not yet fully developed — a faith that has not yet grasped the full freedom that the Gospel brings in relation to food and calendar. The weakness is not moral failure. It is an incomplete understanding of Christian liberty. The appropriate response to someone with weaker faith is not argument or contempt. It is welcome and patience — allowing time and teaching to do what argument cannot.

Who are you to judge someone else's servant — the Greek is su tis ei ho krinōn allotrion oiketon — who are you the one judging another's household servant. In the Roman world a slave was accountable to their master not to other people's masters. The question cuts through the presumption of the one who judges — you are not this person's master. They answer to the Lord not to you. The judgment seat belongs to God not to the believer who has decided their own convictions are the standard everyone else should meet.

They will stand because the Lord is able to make them stand — Paul's confidence about the weak believer's future is grounded not in the weak believer's own strength but in the Lord's ability to sustain them. The person the strong believer is tempted to dismiss as unlikely to make it spiritually is being held up by a power greater than both the strong and the weak. The Lord who accepted them is capable of keeping them. That reality is a check on the contempt of the strong.

Each person should be fully convinced in their own mind — this principle of personal conviction is one of the most important in Chapter 14. Paul is not saying truth is relative or that every opinion is equally valid. He is saying that in matters of Christian liberty — things that the Gospel does not clearly require or forbid — each person must act from their own genuine conviction before God rather than from external pressure or social conformity. Acting against your own conviction even if the action is objectively permissible is its own kind of unfaithfulness.

Why It Still Matters

God has accepted them both. Paul's statement about the strong and the weak believer is the foundation of everything that follows in Chapter 14. The person the strong believer is tempted to look down on and the person the weak believer is tempted to judge have both been accepted by God. That acceptance is not conditional on resolving the dispute about food or days. It is grounded in the same grace that accepted both of them regardless of their level of understanding. When God has accepted someone it is not the place of another believer to treat them as unacceptable. The welcome Paul calls for in the opening verse is rooted in the welcome God has already extended.

We All Belong to the Lord

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

None of us lives only for ourselves and none of us dies only for ourselves. If we live we live for the Lord. If we die we die for the Lord. So whether we live or die we belong to the Lord. This is why the Messiah died and came back to life — so that he might be Lord of both the dead and the living. So why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you look down on them? We will all stand before God's judgment seat one day. As it is written — as surely as I live says the Lord every knee will bow before me and every tongue will acknowledge God. So then each of us will give an account of ourselves to God.

What You Might Not Know

None of us lives only for ourselves — the Greek is oudeis gar hēmōn heautō zē — no one of us lives to himself. Paul is establishing the foundational principle that individual Christian existence is not self-referential. The believer does not set their own agenda live for their own purposes or answer only to themselves. Every dimension of life — eating drinking observing days or not observing them living or dying — is oriented toward the Lord. This is the theological basis for both the freedom Paul will assert and the responsibility to the community he will call for.

The Messiah died and came back to life so that he might be Lord of both the dead and the living — this is one of the most compressed Christological statements in Romans. The death and resurrection of Jesus were not only for the forgiveness of sins. They established his universal lordship. He is Lord of the dead — those who have already died in faith. He is Lord of the living — those still alive. The believer in Rome arguing about food and days is arguing in a space where Jesus is Lord. That lordship puts every dispute about secondary matters in its proper perspective.

We will all stand before God's judgment seat — the Greek word for judgment seat is bēma — the raised platform from which a Roman magistrate or emperor issued verdicts. Paul is describing a future accounting before God that every person will face. The strong believer who looks down on the weak and the weak believer who judges the strong will both stand before the same judge and give account of themselves — not of each other. The proper response to the awareness of this future accounting is to stop evaluating your brother and start examining yourself.

Every knee will bow and every tongue will acknowledge God — Paul quotes Isaiah 45 where God declares his universal sovereignty over all nations. In the original context it is a declaration of God's supremacy over the gods of Babylon. Paul applies it to the future universal acknowledgment of God at the judgment. Every person regardless of their response to the Gospel in this life will ultimately bow before God. The person who bows now in worship will bow then in vindication. The person who has refused to bow now will bow then in judgment. The bowing is universal. The circumstances differ.

Why It Still Matters

Each of us will give an account of ourselves to God. Paul's reminder about personal accountability before God is one of the most clarifying statements in Chapter 14. The energy spent judging other believers — evaluating their food choices their calendar observances their level of spiritual maturity — is energy that would be better spent on the account you yourself will give. The judgment seat is not a peer review where believers evaluate each other's performance. It is a personal accounting where each individual stands before God for their own life. That awareness does not produce indifference to others. It produces the humility that stops looking sideways long enough to look upward.

Don't Be a Stumbling Block

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Stop judging each other. Instead make up your mind not to put anything in the way of a brother or sister that would cause them to stumble. Paul is fully convinced by the Lord Jesus that no food is unclean in itself. But if someone believes something is unclean then for that person it is unclean. If your brother or sister is hurt because of what you eat you are no longer acting out of love. Do not let your food choices destroy someone for whom the Messiah died. Do not let what you consider good become something others speak evil about. The kingdom of God is not about what you eat or drink — it is about doing right living in peace and finding joy in the Holy Spirit. Anyone who serves the Messiah this way is pleasing to God and respected by others.

What You Might Not Know

No food is unclean in itself — Paul's personal conviction expressed here aligns with Jesus's teaching in Mark 7 that what goes into a person does not defile them. The food laws of the Torah were part of the old covenant administration and are not binding on believers in the new covenant. Paul himself is among the strong — those whose faith allows them to eat anything. But he immediately qualifies his own freedom with the principle that governs its exercise — the wellbeing of the brother or sister for whom Christ died matters more than the freedom to eat what you want.

Don't let your food choices destroy someone for whom the Messiah died — the Greek word for destroy is apollye meaning to ruin or bring to destruction. Paul is not saying that a strong believer eating meat will cause a weak believer to lose their salvation. He is saying that the strong believer's contemptuous exercise of their freedom can damage the weak believer's faith in ways that have serious consequences. The person who flaunts their freedom in front of someone whose conscience is troubled by that freedom is not using their freedom well. They are using it as a weapon against a fellow member of Christ's body.

The kingdom of God isn't about what you eat or drink — this is one of the clearest statements in Romans about the relative importance of secondary matters. The kingdom — the reign of God expressed in the community of his people — is not characterized by food and drink. It is characterized by righteousness peace and joy in the Holy Spirit. These three — dikaiosynē eirēnē and chara — are the marks of a community that is truly under God's reign. A community consuming itself in arguments about food and days is missing the point of what the kingdom is about.

Respected by others — the Greek is dokimos tois anthrōpois meaning approved or tested and found genuine by people. Paul is pointing to a witness dimension of the strong-weak conflict. The outside world observing a Christian community divided over food laws and calendar observances will not conclude that the Gospel is true and powerful. The Christian community living in righteousness peace and joy in the Holy Spirit will commend the Gospel to observers in a way that internal disputes over secondary matters never will.

Why It Still Matters

Don't let your food choices destroy someone for whom the Messiah died. The principle Paul establishes here is one of the most important for navigating genuine differences in the Christian community. The question is not only what am I free to do. The question is what does love require in this specific situation with this specific person. Freedom is real. The cost of exercising it carelessly is also real — damage to a person for whom Jesus died. That cost is high enough to warrant the voluntary limitation of freedom for the sake of the brother or sister whose faith is not yet strong enough to handle what yours can handle.

Build Each Other Up

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Let us aim for the things that bring peace and the things that help us build each other up. Do not tear down God's work over food. All food is clean but it is wrong to eat something that causes someone else to stumble. It is better not to eat meat or drink wine or do anything else that causes your brother or sister to fall. Keep your personal convictions about these things between yourself and God. Blessed is the person who does not feel guilty about what they have decided is right. But if someone has doubts and eats anyway they are condemned — because they are not acting from faith. And anything that does not come from faith is sin.

What You Might Not Know

Aim for the things that build each other up — the Greek word for build up is oikodomē meaning construction or edification — the building of a structure. Paul uses this architectural metaphor throughout his letters for the growth and strengthening of the Christian community. The question he is asking the strong to apply to their use of freedom is whether this action builds or tears down. Freedom exercised without regard for its effect on others tears down. Freedom voluntarily limited out of love for the other builds up. The strong are not being asked to give up their freedom permanently. They are being asked to ask the right question about when and how to exercise it.

Don't tear down God's work over food — the church in Rome is described as God's work — ergon theou. The community that God has been building through the Gospel is not to be dismantled over secondary matters. The strong believer who insists on exercising their freedom regardless of the damage it does to the weak believer is not just damaging a fellow Christian — they are tearing down something God has been building. The stakes of the dispute about food are therefore much higher than they appear on the surface.

Keep your personal convictions between yourself and God — Paul is not telling the strong to hide their convictions or to pretend to agree with the weak. He is telling them that their convictions — which are correct — do not need to be publicly demonstrated at the expense of the weak. There is a time and place for teaching about Christian freedom. The dinner table shared with a weak believer whose conscience is troubled is not that time or place. The conviction is real and valid and can be held and expressed appropriately without being weaponized against those who are not yet where you are.

Anything that doesn't come from faith is sin — this is one of the most far-reaching statements in Romans. Paul is not only talking about food. He is establishing a principle about the relationship between inner conviction and outer action. When a person acts against their own conscience — does something they genuinely believe is wrong — they sin even if the action is objectively permissible. The sin is not in the action itself but in the violation of the integrity between inner conviction and outer behavior. Acting from faith means acting from genuine conviction before God — not from social pressure external conformity or the desire to appear free.

Why It Still Matters

Blessed is the person who doesn't feel guilty about what they've decided is right. Paul's description of the blessed person in Chapter 14 is not the person who has the most correct theology about food or the most expansive freedom in practice. It is the person whose inner conviction and outer behavior are aligned — who acts from genuine faith before God rather than from performance anxiety or social pressure. That alignment — what you genuinely believe is right matching what you actually do — is one of the marks of spiritual health. The person who is always acting against their own conscience or always performing convictions they do not genuinely hold is not living from faith. And anything that does not come from faith is sin.

Romans Chapter 15

Put Others First

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Those who are strong in their convictions ought to help carry the burdens of those who are weak. They should not just live to please themselves. Each person should try to please their neighbor for their own good — to help build them up. Even the Messiah did not live to please himself. As the Scripture says — the insults of those who insulted you have fallen on me. Everything written in the past was written to teach us so that through endurance and the encouragement found in the Scriptures we might have hope. May the God who gives endurance and encouragement help everyone live in complete harmony — following the example of Jesus the Messiah. Then with one mind and one voice they can give glory to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

Those of us who are strong ought to help carry the burdens of those who are weak — Paul now includes himself among the strong. He is not giving instructions from a neutral position. He is speaking as one of the strong who has himself chosen to limit his freedom for the sake of others. The word carry — bastazein — means to bear a weight or take it upon yourself. The strong do not merely tolerate the weak or condescend to accommodate them. They actively take on the burden of the weak person's limitations as their own concern. That active bearing of another's burden is the shape of the love Chapter 12 described.

Even the Messiah did not live to please himself — this is the theological foundation of the entire argument about strong and weak. The example of Jesus is the ultimate ground for voluntary self-limitation. If the Son of God did not use his position and power for his own comfort and convenience — but instead absorbed the insults directed at God — then the strong believer who insists on exercising their freedom regardless of the cost to the weak has misunderstood what it means to follow Jesus. The pattern of Christ's life is the pattern of willing self-limitation for the sake of others.

The insults of those who insulted you have fallen on me — Paul quotes Psalm 69 which was widely understood in early Christianity as a messianic psalm describing Jesus's suffering. The verse Paul quotes describes the righteous sufferer absorbing reproaches that were directed at God. Jesus bore not only the sins of humanity but the hostility directed at God that should have landed elsewhere. This is the deepest possible version of bearing another's burden — bearing what was meant for someone else entirely.

Everything written in the past was written to teach us — Paul's statement about the purpose of Scripture is one of the most important in his letters. The Hebrew Scriptures were not written only for the people who first received them. They were written for every generation — specifically to produce endurance and encouragement that generates hope. The endurance comes from seeing how God has sustained his people through every crisis in their history. The encouragement comes from seeing how God has kept his promises even when circumstances argued against it. Both endurance and encouragement are produced by reading Scripture carefully and receiving what it was written to give.

With one mind and one voice — the harmony Paul prays for is not uniformity of opinion on every matter. Chapter 14 has established that the strong and the weak can hold different convictions about food and days. The unity Paul is after is deeper than agreement on secondary matters — it is a common orientation toward God expressed in common worship. One mind — one inner direction. One voice — one outward expression of praise. Both are possible even when secondary convictions differ if the primary commitment to the Lord is shared.

Why It Still Matters

Even the Messiah did not live to please himself. Paul's appeal to the example of Jesus as the basis for voluntary self-limitation is the most powerful argument available because it removes the question from the level of rights and places it at the level of identity. The question is not what am I allowed to do. The question is what does it look like to follow someone who gave up everything he was entitled to for the sake of others. The strong believer who refuses to limit their freedom for the sake of the weak has not only failed the weak — they have failed to grasp the fundamental pattern of the life they claim to be following.

Welcome One Another

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Welcome one another just as the Messiah welcomed you in order to bring glory to God. The Messiah became a servant to the Jewish people to prove that God is true and to confirm the promises made to their ancestors. He also did this so that the Gentiles could praise God for his mercy. As the Scriptures say — because of this I will praise you among the nations I will sing praises to your name. And again — rejoice you Gentiles along with his people. And — praise the Lord all you Gentiles let all the peoples praise him. And Isaiah says — the Root of Jesse will come the one who rises to rule the nations. In him the nations will put their hope. May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace as you trust in him so that you overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit.

What You Might Not Know

Welcome one another just as the Messiah welcomed you — the welcome Paul calls for is modeled on Christ's welcome of the believer. How did Christ welcome you? Not after you had resolved all your theological questions. Not after you had achieved a certain level of spiritual maturity. Not after you had demonstrated your convictions were correct. He welcomed you while you were still a sinner — as Chapter 5 established. The welcome that the strong should extend to the weak and the weak to the strong is the same welcome that neither of them deserved when they received it. That is the standard — not the welcome that feels comfortable or seems appropriate — but the welcome that mirrors the one you yourself received.

The Messiah became a servant to the Jewish people — Paul uses the word diakonos — servant or minister. Jesus's entire earthly ministry was a service to Israel — fulfilling the promises made to Abraham Isaac Jacob David and the prophets. He did not come to bypass Israel or to replace them. He came to confirm what God had promised them. The faithfulness of Jesus to Israel's covenant is the basis for the faithfulness of God that Paul has been arguing throughout Chapters 9 through 11. Jesus is proof that God keeps his promises.

The chain of Scripture quotations — Paul quotes from Psalm 18, Deuteronomy 32, Psalm 117, and Isaiah 11. The four quotations come from three different sections of the Hebrew Scriptures — the Law, the Writings, and the Prophets. Paul is demonstrating that the inclusion of the Gentiles in the praise of God was not a new idea introduced by the Gospel. It was present throughout the whole of the Hebrew Scriptures — the Law said it the Psalms said it and the Prophets said it. The unity of Jew and Gentile in worshiping the one God was always the destination the story was moving toward.

The Root of Jesse — Isaiah's image of the Root of Jesse coming to rule the nations is one of the great messianic prophecies. Jesse was David's father. The Root of Jesse is the Messiah from David's line who was always expected to extend his rule beyond Israel to the nations. Paul quotes it here as evidence that Gentile hope was always built into the messianic promise. The nations putting their hope in the Root of Jesse is not a surprise ending to Israel's story. It was written into the story from the beginning.

Overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit — Paul's benediction at the end of this section is one of the most beautiful in Romans. The hope he describes is not a thin trickle of optimism. It is an overflowing — the Greek word is perisseuein meaning to abound or be more than enough. The God of hope fills believers with joy and peace — which then produces the overflow. The Holy Spirit is the power source of the overflow. The same Spirit who raised Jesus from the dead is the one producing in believers a hope that exceeds what circumstances could naturally generate.

Why It Still Matters

Welcome one another just as the Messiah welcomed you. The welcome Paul calls for in Chapter 15 is the practical expression of everything Romans has been building toward. The theological argument of Chapters 1 through 11 — the universal problem the universal solution the plan for Israel and the Gentiles — produces a community in which people who differ welcome each other because they have all been welcomed by the same Lord. The welcome is not based on agreement or similarity or comfort. It is based on the shared experience of having been received by someone who had every reason not to receive you. That shared experience is the foundation of a community that can hold strong and weak Jewish and Gentile educated and uneducated in genuine fellowship.

Paul's Work Among the Gentiles

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Paul is personally convinced that the Roman believers are full of goodness filled with knowledge and more than capable of teaching one another. Even so he has written boldly on some points just to remind them. He does this because of the grace God gave him to be a servant of Jesus the Messiah to the Gentiles. He serves the good news of God like a priest so that the Gentiles might become an acceptable offering to God made holy by the Holy Spirit. In Christ Jesus Paul is proud of what God has done through him. He will not dare speak of anything except what the Messiah has accomplished through him — leading the Gentiles to obey God by what he has said and done through the power of signs and wonders and by the power of the Holy Spirit. From Jerusalem all the way around to Illyricum he has fully spread the good news of the Messiah. His ambition has always been to preach where the Messiah was not yet known — so he would not be building on someone else's foundation. As it is written — those who were never told about him will see and those who have never heard will understand.

What You Might Not Know

Full of goodness filled with knowledge and more than capable of teaching one another — Paul's compliment to the Roman church is genuine rather than flattering. He has been writing boldly throughout the letter — addressing specific issues with direct language. He now acknowledges that the Roman community is mature enough to teach each other and that his letter has been reminder rather than foundation-laying. He is not introducing them to the Gospel for the first time. He is reminding them of things they already know and helping them see their current conflicts in light of the full theological picture.

A servant of Jesus the Messiah to the Gentiles — Paul describes his apostleship using priestly language. He serves the Gospel like a priest — leitourgon meaning a public servant or liturgical minister. His priestly work is to present the Gentiles as an offering to God — acceptable because they have been made holy by the Holy Spirit. The image is striking — Paul is not just proclaiming the Gospel. He is functioning as a priest whose offering is not animals or grain but the Gentile peoples themselves transformed by the Spirit into a holy offering presented to God.

From Jerusalem all the way around to Illyricum — Illyricum was the Roman province on the eastern shore of the Adriatic — roughly modern-day Albania and the western Balkans. Paul is describing an arc from Jerusalem through Asia Minor Greece and Macedonia to the Adriatic coast. He has covered an enormous geographic territory. The phrase fully spread the good news does not mean every person in that region has heard. It means Paul has established the Gospel in strategic centers throughout the region from which it can continue to spread.

My ambition has always been to preach where the Messiah was not yet known — the Greek word for ambition is philotimoumenon meaning to be fond of honor or to make it a point of honor. Paul's ambition — usually a suspect motivation in the ancient world — has been redirected toward pioneer mission. He is not building on another person's foundation. He is going where no one has gone. The quotation from Isaiah 52 — those who were never told about him will see — is Paul's missionary charter. The unreached are his target not the already-reached.

Why It Still Matters

My ambition has always been to preach the good news where the Messiah was not yet known. Paul's pioneer missionary strategy — going where the Gospel has not been rather than reinforcing where it already is — is one of the most challenging models in the New Testament for the Church's ongoing mission. The comfortable strategy is to consolidate and strengthen what exists. The costly strategy is to go to where nothing exists yet and build from the ground up. Paul chose the costly strategy consistently across his entire ministry. The result was churches across the Roman world that would not have existed if he had stayed in Jerusalem or Antioch building on what was already there.

Paul's Plans to Visit Rome

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

This is why Paul has been kept from coming to Rome so many times. But now since he has finished his work in these regions and has longed to visit for many years he hopes to see them on his way to Spain. He looks forward to visiting and hopes they can help send him on his way after they have had some time together. Right now he is on his way to Jerusalem to serve the believers there. The believers in Macedonia and Achaia were glad to take up a collection for the poor among God's people in Jerusalem — happy to do it and honestly owing it to them. If the Gentiles have shared in the spiritual blessings of the Jewish people they ought to serve them in return with material help. Once he has finished this task and made sure the gift has been delivered he will head to Spain and stop by to see them. And he knows that when he comes he will come with the full blessing of the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

On my way to Spain — Spain was the western edge of the Roman Empire — the farthest west a traveler could go on the northern shore of the Mediterranean. Paul's plan to take the Gospel to Spain was consistent with his pioneer strategy — going to the unreached. Rome was not Paul's destination. It was his base of support for the next stage of the mission. He had finished the eastern arc from Jerusalem to Illyricum. Spain represented the western arc. The Gospel to the ends of the earth — the commission of Acts 1:8 — was driving Paul's geography. Whether Paul ever reached Spain is not recorded in the New Testament. Early church tradition suggests he may have.

The collection for Jerusalem — Paul had been organizing this offering among the Gentile churches across Macedonia and Achaia for an extended period. His letters to the Corinthians devote significant space to encouraging and organizing the collection. The offering was not merely charitable. It was theological — a tangible demonstration of the unity between Jewish and Gentile believers. The Gentiles had received spiritual blessings through Israel. They were now returning material blessing to the poor in Jerusalem. The exchange embodied in practice the argument Paul had made in theory throughout Romans about the relationship between Jew and Gentile in God's purposes.

They owe it to them — Paul's statement that the Gentile churches owe material support to Jerusalem is theologically grounded in the principle of Chapters 9 through 11. The root supports the branches. Everything the Gentile churches have received — the Scriptures the Messiah the apostles the Gospel itself — came through Israel. The debt of gratitude has a material expression. Supporting the poor Jewish believers in Jerusalem is not charity in the ordinary sense. It is the repayment of a spiritual debt in material currency.

I will come with the full blessing of the Messiah — Paul's confidence about his coming visit to Rome is grounded in the blessing of the Messiah rather than in his own plans. He knows he will come. He knows the visit will be blessed. He does not know — and Acts 28 reveals — that the manner of his coming will be as a prisoner rather than as a free apostle. The blessing of the Messiah was present in his arrival in Rome. The circumstances of the arrival were not what Paul had anticipated. But the substance of what the Lord had promised — you must testify in Rome — was fully kept.

Why It Still Matters

If the Gentiles have shared in the spiritual blessings of the Jewish people they ought to serve them in return with material help. Paul's principle of mutual obligation between Jewish and Gentile believers has practical implications that extend beyond the first century collection for Jerusalem. The Church that has received the Hebrew Scriptures the messianic promise and the apostolic tradition through Israel owes a debt of gratitude that should express itself in concrete care for Jewish people. The history of Christian anti-Semitism makes this passage one of the most urgent in Romans — the community that should have been most grateful for what came through Israel has often been the most hostile toward Jewish people. Paul's principle of owing stands as a permanent correction to that hostility.

A Request for Prayer

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Paul urges the brothers and sisters by our Lord Jesus the Messiah and by the love of the Spirit to join him in his struggle by praying to God for him. Pray that he will be kept safe from those in Judea who refuse to believe and that his service in Jerusalem will be gladly received by the believers there. Then if it is God's will he will be able to come to them with joy and find rest among them. May the God of peace be with you all. Amen.

What You Might Not Know

Join me in my struggle by praying — the Greek word for struggle is agōnia from which we get agony. Paul is not asking for casual prayer. He is asking for agonized intercession — the kind of prayer that costs something in effort and attention. The same word is used for Jesus's prayer in Gethsemane. Paul knew what lay ahead in Jerusalem. He had been warned by prophets in Tyre and by Agabus in Caesarea. He was asking the Roman church to enter with him into the spiritual battle that surrounded his journey.

Kept safe from those in Judea who refuse to believe — Paul's fear about Jerusalem was well founded. Acts 21 records exactly what he feared happening — Jewish opponents seizing him in the Temple and nearly killing him before the Roman soldiers intervened. The prayer he asked for was answered in the sense that his life was preserved — but through arrest and imprisonment rather than through the safety he had hoped for. God's answers to prayer sometimes look very different from what was requested while still accomplishing what was prayed for.

My service in Jerusalem will be gladly received — Paul's concern about the reception of the collection is significant. He feared that the Jewish Christian community in Jerusalem might refuse the offering from Gentile believers — which would be a public rupture in the unity between Jewish and Gentile Christianity that the collection was designed to express. Acts 21 records a warm reception from James and the elders when Paul arrived — they praised God for what he reported. The collection appears to have been received even if Acts does not specifically mention the amount.

Find rest among you — the Greek word is synanapausomai meaning to rest together with. Paul longed for the refreshment of genuine community after the sustained intensity of his missionary journeys. The man who had been beaten imprisoned shipwrecked and nearly killed multiple times was asking for rest — the rest that comes from being among people who love you and share your faith. He had never experienced the Roman community. He longed for it. He eventually arrived there — as a prisoner. Whether he found rest in that condition Acts 28 suggests he did — welcomed by the Roman believers who came out to meet him on the Appian Way.

May the God of peace be with you all — Paul closes Chapter 15 with a benediction that echoes throughout his letters. The God of peace — not the God of conflict or the God of theological correctness or the God of one faction over another. The God of peace whose character is shalom — wholeness right relationship harmony. That God is the one Paul asks to be present with a community that has been struggling with conflict between strong and weak Jewish and Gentile. The benediction is the prayer that the one who has the power to produce the peace that the community needs will exercise that power among them.

Why It Still Matters

Join me in my struggle by praying to God for me. Paul's request for prayer from a community he has never met is one of the most humanly transparent moments in Romans. The man who has written eleven chapters of profound theology and four chapters of practical instruction closes by asking people he does not know to pray for his safety and his mission. The apostle needed prayer. He knew he needed it. He was not too proud to ask for it from strangers. That combination — theological depth and personal vulnerability — is the shape of Paul's relationship with the communities he served. He gave them everything he had. He asked them to give him what only God could provide through their intercession.

Romans Chapter 16

Phoebe Is Recommended

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Paul introduces Phoebe who serves as a deacon of the church in Cenchreae. He asks the Roman believers to welcome her in the Lord in a way worthy of God's people and to give her whatever help she needs — because she has been a tremendous help and protector to many people including Paul himself.

What You Might Not Know

Phoebe carried this letter — it is almost certain that Phoebe was the courier who carried the letter to Rome. In the ancient world letters were delivered by personal messengers who were expected to read the letter aloud and answer questions about its contents. Phoebe was therefore not only the delivery mechanism for Romans — she was likely its first public reader and interpreter. The most theologically significant letter in the New Testament was first proclaimed to the Roman church by a woman from Cenchreae. That detail is preserved in the opening of Chapter 16 for anyone willing to notice it.

Deacon of the church in Cenchreae — the Greek word is diakonos — the same word used for male deacons throughout the New Testament and for Paul's own description of his apostolic ministry. Cenchreae was the eastern port of Corinth — the city from which Paul wrote Romans. Phoebe was not a marginal figure. She was a recognized minister of a specific church with an identified role. The letter of commendation Paul writes for her — introducing her to a church that did not know her — is the same kind of letter he describes in 2 Corinthians as the appropriate way to introduce traveling ministers to new communities.

A tremendous help and protector to many — the Greek word for protector is prostatis meaning patron benefactor or one who stands before others on their behalf. In the Roman world a patron was a person of social standing and resources who used their position to advocate for and support others. Phoebe was apparently a woman of some means who had used her resources and social position to support Paul and many others in their ministry. The word patron carries legal and social weight — she was not merely generous. She was a formal advocate who put her standing on the line for others.

Why It Still Matters

Give her whatever help she needs because she has been a tremendous help and protector to many people including me. Paul's commendation of Phoebe is a model of apostolic gratitude and mutual support. The man who wrote Romans needed help. He received it from a woman who used her resources and her position on his behalf. He acknowledged it publicly and asked an entire church community to reciprocate. The mutuality of ministry that Paul describes throughout his letters is visible here in its most concrete form — a woman supporting an apostle who publicly honors her service and asks others to do the same.

Greetings to Friends in Rome

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Paul sends greetings to Prisca and Aquila his co-workers who risked their lives to save his — all the Gentile churches are grateful for them. He greets the church that meets in their home. He greets Epaenetus the first person in Asia to follow the Messiah. He greets Mary who worked very hard for them. He greets Andronicus and Junia his fellow Jews and fellow prisoners who are highly respected among the apostles and were followers of the Messiah before Paul was. He greets Ampliatus Urbanus Stachys Apelles those in the household of Aristobulus Herodion those in the household of Narcissus who belong to the Lord Tryphaena and Tryphosa Persis Rufus and his mother Asyncritus Phlegon Hermes Patrobas Hermas and the brothers and sisters with them Philologus Julia Nereus and his sister Olympas and all of God's people with them.

What You Might Not Know

Twenty-six people are greeted by name in Romans 16 — more personal greetings than in any other Pauline letter. Paul had never visited Rome yet he knew this many people there by name. The explanation is the mobility of the Roman world — people traveled constantly for trade military service and other reasons. Many of the people Paul greets had encountered him elsewhere and subsequently moved to Rome. The list is a window into the social composition of the early Roman church — it includes both Jewish and Gentile names both Greek and Latin names both slave names and free names. The church in Rome was genuinely diverse.

Prisca and Aquila — the couple appears six times in the New Testament and Prisca is named first four of those times — which as noted in the Acts study notes suggests she was the more prominent figure in ministry. They had originally been expelled from Rome by Claudius's edict and had met Paul in Corinth. They had traveled with him to Ephesus where they taught Apollos. Now they are back in Rome — the edict having lapsed after Claudius's death — with a church meeting in their home. Their willingness to risk their lives for Paul is mentioned without explanation. Paul clearly expected the Roman readers to know the story.

Andronicus and Junia — Junia is a woman's name. Some later manuscripts and translations tried to make it masculine — Junias — but there is no evidence that Junias was ever a name used in the ancient world. Junia was a common Roman woman's name. Paul describes her and Andronicus as highly respected among the apostles and as followers of the Messiah before Paul was — which places their conversion in the earliest days of the movement possibly before Pentecost. Whether they are being described as apostles themselves or as highly regarded by the apostles is debated — but the most natural reading of the Greek supports the former.

Rufus a choice servant of the Lord and his mother who has been like a mother to me — Rufus is mentioned in Mark 15 as the son of Simon of Cyrene who carried Jesus's cross. If this is the same Rufus — and many scholars think it is — then Paul was greeting the son of the man who carried Jesus's cross. The tender note about Rufus's mother who had been like a mother to Paul himself is a glimpse into the personal relationships that sustained Paul through his years of ministry. She had apparently cared for him in some period of need. He had not forgotten it.

The households of Aristobulus and Narcissus — Paul does not greet Aristobulus and Narcissus directly — he greets those in their households who belong to the Lord. This suggests that Aristobulus and Narcissus themselves were not believers but that their households contained believers — possibly slaves or freedpersons. Aristobulus may have been the grandson of Herod the Great who lived in Rome. Narcissus may have been the powerful freedman of the emperor Claudius. If so the earliest Roman church included people from the households of the Herodian family and the imperial court — believers embedded in the centers of political power.

Why It Still Matters

The list of names in Romans 16 is one of the most humanly rich passages in the New Testament. Behind each name is a person with a story — a conversion a journey a sacrifice a ministry. Paul remembers them all by name. He knows who worked hard and who risked their life and who was there before him and who has been like family. The God who numbers the hairs of every head is reflected in the apostle who remembers every name. The church is not an abstraction or an institution. It is Phoebe and Prisca and Aquila and Junia and Rufus's mother. It is specific people known by name and loved for their particular contribution to the mission.

Watch Out for Troublemakers

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Paul urges the brothers and sisters to watch out for people who cause divisions and put obstacles in their way that go against the teaching they have received. Stay away from them. People like that are not serving the Lord the Messiah — they are serving their own appetites. With smooth talk and flattery they deceive people who do not know any better. Everyone has heard about their obedience and Paul could not be more glad. But he wants them to be wise about what is good and completely innocent when it comes to evil. The God of peace will soon crush Satan under their feet. May the grace of our Lord Jesus be with them.

What You Might Not Know

Watch out for people who cause divisions — the Greek word for divisions is dichostasias meaning standing apart or splitting. Paul interrupts his greetings with an urgent warning that appears without specific identification of the troublemakers. He does not name them. He describes them by their method — causing divisions creating obstacles teaching contrary to what the Roman believers have received. The contrast between the long list of trustworthy people Paul has just named and these unnamed divisive figures is stark. Paul knows who can be trusted. He is warning the community about those who cannot.

They are serving their own appetites — the Greek is tē koilia meaning belly or stomach. The people Paul is warning against have made their own desires — their appetite for influence followers or resources — the true master they serve. They use the language of the Gospel but the motivation is self-serving. The smooth talk and flattery are the tools of someone who has learned to use religious vocabulary to achieve personal ends. Paul's warning is not about doctrinal error in the abstract. It is about the character behind the teaching — what the teacher is actually serving.

Wise about what is good and completely innocent when it comes to evil — the combination Paul calls for is not naivety. It is a specific kind of discernment — well-developed wisdom about what is genuinely good combined with a deliberate inexperience of evil. The Roman believers should be experts in goodness and novices in evil rather than the reverse. The person who has spent their time studying what is good is better equipped to recognize evil when it appears in religious clothing than the person who has become sophisticated about evil while remaining underdeveloped in genuine goodness.

The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet — Paul quotes Genesis 3 where God promises that the seed of the woman will crush the head of the serpent. The promise was fulfilled in Christ's victory over sin and death. But Paul applies it to the community's struggle with divisive teachers — the same spiritual conflict is continuing in a different form. The God of peace — the same benediction that ended Chapter 15 — is the one who will bring the conflict to its conclusion. The crushing is certain. The soon indicates Paul's expectation of an imminent resolution to the opposition the community faces.

Why It Still Matters

With smooth talk and flattery they deceive people who don't know any better. Paul's warning about divisive teachers who serve their own appetites is one of the most consistently relevant passages in Romans for the ongoing life of the Church. Every generation has seen people who use the language and forms of Christian ministry in the service of personal ambition influence or financial gain. The marks Paul identifies — causing divisions creating obstacles smooth talk flattery self-serving motivation — are as recognizable now as they were in Rome. The antidote he prescribes is equally relevant — wisdom about what is good combined with innocence about evil and trust that God will ultimately deal with what human discernment cannot fully stop.

Greetings from Paul's Team

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Timothy Paul's co-worker sends his greetings. So do Lucius Jason and Sosipater Paul's fellow Jews. Tertius — the one who actually wrote the letter down — greets them in the Lord. Gaius who has welcomed Paul and the whole church into his home sends his greetings. Erastus the city treasurer and their brother Quartus send their greetings too.

What You Might Not Know

Tertius the one who actually wrote this letter down — Paul dictated his letters to a scribe — an amanuensis — who wrote as Paul spoke. Tertius identifies himself as the person who physically wrote out the letter of Romans. He adds his own greeting — in the Lord — which suggests he was himself a believer rather than merely a hired scribe. The existence of Tertius explains some of the stylistic variations in Paul's letters — different scribes had different levels of freedom to shape the language while preserving the content. Romans is generally considered among the most polished of Paul's letters and Tertius may have contributed to that quality.

Gaius who has welcomed me and the whole church into his home — Gaius is mentioned in 1 Corinthians 1 as one of the few people Paul personally baptized in Corinth. His home was apparently large enough to serve as the meeting place for the whole Corinthian church — which makes him a significant figure in the early Corinthian community. Paul was staying with him when he wrote Romans. The hospitality that Gaius extended to Paul and to the whole church was the practical foundation on which the letter to Rome was written.

Erastus the city treasurer — an inscription discovered in Corinth in 1929 reads Erastus in return for his aedileship laid this pavement at his own expense. The aedile was a city official responsible for public buildings and streets — roughly equivalent to a city commissioner. The name Erastus on a Corinthian inscription from the first century is widely regarded as referring to the same Erastus Paul mentions. If so Romans contains a greeting from a senior city official of Corinth — evidence that the early church included people of significant civic standing alongside slaves and freed persons.

Why It Still Matters

I Tertius — the one who actually wrote this letter down — greet you in the Lord. Tertius's brief self-identification is one of the most humanly touching moments in the New Testament. He has just spent hours or days writing out the most theologically significant letter in Paul's correspondence — taking dictation word by word while Paul paced and thought and spoke. He adds one sentence in his own voice. In the Lord. The man behind the pen of Romans was a believer who knew what he was transcribing and wanted to add his own greeting to the community that would receive it. His name is preserved because he wrote it down himself. Without that one sentence we would never have known he existed.

A Final Word of Praise

◀ 📖 Return to Romans Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Now to God — the only wise God — who has the power to make you strong. He does this through the good news Paul preaches about Jesus the Messiah. For ages and ages this was a mystery kept secret and hidden. But now it has been revealed. Through the words of the prophets by the command of the eternal God himself this mystery has been made known to all nations — so that people everywhere might hear and believe and obey. To him be glory forever through Jesus the Messiah. Amen.

What You Might Not Know

The closing doxology of Romans is one of the longest in Paul's letters and serves as the theological bookend to the doxology that ended Chapter 11. Both doxologies celebrate the wisdom and knowledge of God. The Chapter 11 doxology arose from the specific argument about Israel and the Gentiles. The Chapter 16 doxology arises from the completed letter — the whole argument from Chapter 1 through Chapter 15 — and celebrates the God whose mystery has now been revealed and whose glory extends forever through Jesus the Messiah.

The only wise God — the Greek is monō sophō theō — the one wise God. The emphasis on God's exclusive wisdom echoes the Chapter 11 doxology — his ways are unsearchable his wisdom unfathomable. The plan of salvation that Paul has spent sixteen chapters explaining is the work of a wisdom that no human mind devised and no human mind could have anticipated. The ending of Romans circles back to its beginning — the Gospel is the power of God for salvation. The wisdom of God is the source of that power. Both deserve the glory that Paul's final word assigns.

For ages and ages this was a mystery kept secret — the Greek word mystērion appears again as it did in Chapter 11. What God was doing in human history — the plan to bring Jew and Gentile together in one community through the Messiah — was hidden in previous ages. The Hebrew Scriptures contained the clues but the full picture was not visible until the Messiah came and the Spirit was poured out. Now it has been revealed — apokalyphthentos — unveiled or apocalypsed. The same word used for the book of Revelation. The revealing of the mystery is itself an apocalyptic event — the unveiling of what God had always been planning.

So that people everywhere might hear and believe and obey — the three verbs summarize the entire missionary purpose of Romans. Hear — the Gospel must be proclaimed. Believe — the proclamation calls for a response of trust. Obey — the trust expresses itself in the obedience of faith that Paul described in Chapter 1. The letter that began with the obedience of faith as Paul's missionary goal ends with the same three movements — hearing believing obeying — as the purpose for which the mystery has been revealed and the letter has been written.

To him be glory forever through Jesus the Messiah — the final word of Romans is the same as the final word of the doxology in Chapter 11. Glory. Forever. Through Jesus. The letter that began with Paul a servant of Jesus the Messiah ends with Jesus the Messiah as the channel through whom glory flows to God forever. The whole argument of Romans — from the wrath of God against sin through justification by faith through life in the Spirit through the plan for Israel and the Gentiles through the practical life of the community — has been building to this. Everything is from God through Christ and to God for his glory. Forever. Amen.

Why It Still Matters

To him be glory forever through Jesus the Messiah. Amen. Romans ends where all genuine theology ends — in worship. Paul has spent sixteen chapters making the most careful and comprehensive theological argument of his career. He has addressed sin and grace judgment and mercy Israel and the Gentiles law and faith the Spirit and the flesh present suffering and future glory. He has given instructions about government food days money and relationships. And when all of it has been said the only fitting response is the one he gives — glory to God forever through Jesus the Messiah. The letter that has equipped the Church to think about salvation for two thousand years closes not with a summary or a call to action but with a word of praise. That is the right ending. Amen.
