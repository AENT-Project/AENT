John Chapter 1

The Word Became Flesh

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

John opens his Gospel not with a birth narrative or a genealogy but with a cosmic prologue. In the beginning was the Word — the Word was with God and the Word was God. All things were made through him. In him was life and that life was the light of humanity. The Word came into the world the world did not recognize him. His own people did not receive him. But those who did receive him were given the right to become children of God. The Word became flesh and lived among us full of grace and truth.

What You Might Not Know

In the beginning — John's Gospel opens with the same three words that open the entire Bible in Genesis. This is deliberate and unmistakable. Genesis begins in the beginning God created. John begins in the beginning was the Word. John is placing Jesus at the origin point of everything — not as a creation but as the one through whom creation happened. The Gospel of John is presenting a new Genesis — a new beginning breaking into the world through the Word made flesh.

The Word — the Greek word is Logos. In Greek philosophy Logos referred to the rational principle that ordered and sustained the universe — the logic behind reality. In Jewish thought the Word of God was the creative and revealing power by which God made and spoke to his creation. John takes both frameworks and fills them with a person. The ordering principle of the universe is not an abstract force. It is someone. And that someone became flesh.

Tabernacled among us — the word translated dwelt or lived is the Greek word skenoo meaning to pitch a tent or tabernacle. It deliberately echoes the Tabernacle in the wilderness — the tent where God's presence dwelt among Israel during the Exodus. John is saying the presence of God that once filled a tent in the desert has now taken up residence in a human body. The glory that filled the Tabernacle is the same glory John and the disciples saw in Jesus — full of grace and truth.

Grace upon grace — the Greek is charin anti charitos — grace in place of grace or grace following grace. The image is of waves coming in one after another without stopping. The fullness of Jesus does not run out. Each gift of grace is followed by another.

Why It Still Matters

The darkness has not overcome it — the Greek word can mean either comprehend or overcome. Both meanings are true simultaneously. The darkness has not understood the light and the darkness has not extinguished it. John opens his Gospel with this statement and it functions as a promise that runs through every dark moment in the chapters that follow. Whatever darkness appears in John's Gospel — betrayal, denial, crucifixion — this opening statement stands over all of it. The light is still shining.

John the Baptist's Testimony

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Religious leaders send a delegation from Jerusalem to interrogate John the Baptist. Is he the Messiah? No. Elijah? No. The Prophet? No. Then who? He quotes Isaiah — I am a voice calling in the wilderness make straight the way for the Lord. They ask why he baptizes if he is none of these. He says among you stands one you do not know — the one whose sandals he is not worthy to untie.

What You Might Not Know

Are you the Prophet — this question refers to a specific expectation from Deuteronomy 18 where Moses promised that God would raise up a prophet like himself. This was understood as a specific messianic figure distinct from the Messiah and from Elijah. The questioners were working through a checklist of expected end-time figures. John's triple denial — not the Messiah not Elijah not the Prophet — strips away every category they have prepared and leaves only his actual role — a voice.

He did not fail to confess but confessed freely — the double positive emphasis in Greek draws attention to John's forthrightness. He was being interrogated by religious authorities and he answered them directly and completely. There was no evasion no strategic ambiguity no self-promotion. He knew exactly who he was and was entirely clear about who he was not.

Among you stands one you do not know — this statement is addressed to the delegation standing in front of John. The one they do not know is already present in the world already active already among the people of Israel. The religious establishment conducting this inquiry about John has entirely missed the actual arrival of the one they claim to be waiting for.

Why It Still Matters

John's entire identity was defined by pointing away from himself toward someone else. He was not the light — he came to testify about the light. He was not the Messiah — he was the voice preparing the way. That quality of self-definition entirely in relation to another rather than in relation to one's own achievements or status is one of the most striking features of John's character in this Gospel. He knew who he was by knowing clearly who he was not.

The Lamb of God

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

The next day John sees Jesus approaching and says look the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world. He testifies that he saw the Spirit come down from heaven like a dove and remain on Jesus. He was told that the one on whom the Spirit comes down and remains is the one who will baptize with the Holy Spirit. He has seen and testifies that this is the Son of God.

What You Might Not Know

The Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world — this title carries enormous weight and multiple layers of meaning. The Passover lamb whose blood protected Israel from the angel of death. The daily Temple sacrifices. The suffering servant of Isaiah 53 led like a lamb to the slaughter. All of these converge in this single title. John the Baptist announces Jesus's identity and mission in one phrase on their very first recorded encounter in this Gospel.

Who takes away the sin of the world — not the sins of Israel only. Not the sins of the religious. The sin of the world. The scope is universal from the very first introduction of Jesus in John's Gospel. The same universality that Luke signaled through the genealogy going back to Adam John signals through this opening declaration about the Lamb.

The Spirit come down and remain on him — the word remain is significant. The Spirit did not merely descend on Jesus and then lift off. It remained. Throughout John's Gospel the word remain or abide is one of the key theological terms — it describes the deep mutual indwelling of the Father and Son and eventually of the disciples in Jesus and Jesus in the disciples. It begins here with the Spirit remaining on Jesus.

Why It Still Matters

I myself did not know him — John the Baptist admits that he did not recognize Jesus by natural knowledge or family connection. He knew him only because God told him the sign to look for. The recognition of Jesus in John's Gospel is consistently presented as something that requires divine revelation rather than human perception. The world did not recognize him. His own did not receive him. Even the one sent to prepare his way needed a sign from heaven to identify him.

The First Disciples

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Two of John's disciples follow Jesus after hearing John call him the Lamb of God. Jesus turns and asks what they want. They ask where he is staying. He says come and see. They spend the day with him. One is Andrew who immediately finds his brother Simon and says we have found the Messiah and brings him to Jesus. Jesus looks at Simon and says you will be called Peter.

What You Might Not Know

What do you want — the first words Jesus speaks in John's Gospel are a question. Not a teaching, not a declaration, not a command. A question addressed to two people following him. That question — what do you want — echoes through the entire Gospel. It is the question Jesus asks the blind man in Chapter 9, the question beneath his conversation with Mary Magdalene at the tomb, the question that drives every encounter. John's Jesus begins by asking people what they are actually seeking.

It was about four in the afternoon — this specific time detail is one of the most discussed small details in John's Gospel. Why does John remember the exact hour? Most likely because he was one of the two disciples — the unnamed one — and the memory of that afternoon when he first met Jesus was permanently marked in his mind. The Gospel of John has the quality throughout of someone recording what they personally witnessed and when.

We have found the Messiah — Andrew's announcement to his brother is the first human confession of Jesus as Messiah in John's Gospel. It comes not from a religious leader or a scholar but from a fisherman who spent one afternoon with Jesus. The immediacy of his certainty and his instinct to share it immediately with his brother is the pattern John establishes for how genuine encounter with Jesus works.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus looked at him — when Simon is brought to Jesus the first thing Jesus does is look at him. The Greek suggests a sustained careful look — the look of someone seeing deeply into a person. Then he gives him a new name. Peter — the Rock. Simon had not yet done anything. He had not confessed anything or demonstrated anything. Jesus looked at who he was and named who he would become. That naming precedes everything Simon Peter will do in this Gospel and in Acts.

Philip and Nathanael

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Jesus finds Philip and calls him to follow. Philip finds Nathanael and says we have found the one Moses and the prophets wrote about — Jesus of Nazareth. Nathanael asks if anything good can come from Nazareth. Philip says come and see. Jesus sees Nathanael coming and says here is a true Israelite in whom there is no deceit. Nathanael asks how Jesus knows him. Jesus says he saw him under the fig tree before Philip called him. Nathanael declares Jesus to be the Son of God and the King of Israel. Jesus promises he will see heaven opened and angels ascending and descending on the Son of Man.

What You Might Not Know

Can anything good come from Nazareth — Nathanael's skepticism was geographically based. Nazareth was a small obscure village in Galilee with no prophetic or messianic significance. Nothing in the Hebrew Scriptures pointed to Nazareth as the origin of the Messiah. Nathanael's question was not cynicism for its own sake — it was honest theological puzzlement. The expected Messiah was associated with Bethlehem and with David's royal line not with an insignificant Galilean village.

An Israelite in whom there is no deceit — Jesus's description of Nathanael deliberately contrasts with Jacob whose name meant deceiver and who wrestled with God and was renamed Israel. Nathanael is a true Israel — the genuine article, someone whose inner life matches his outer profession. The contrast with Jacob is confirmed when Jesus promises Nathanael will see angels ascending and descending — the same vision Jacob saw at Bethel in Genesis 28 when he named the place the house of God and the gate of heaven.

I saw you under the fig tree — the fig tree had specific significance in Jewish thought as a place of private prayer and meditation on Scripture. Sitting under your fig tree was an idiom for peaceful private study. Jesus seeing Nathanael in that private moment — before any human contact — signals the same quality of knowledge he will demonstrate throughout the Gospel. He knows what is in people without being told.

Why It Still Matters

Come and see — Philip's answer to Nathanael's skepticism is not an argument. It is an invitation. He does not attempt to answer the theological objection about Nazareth. He simply invites Nathanael to encounter Jesus directly and decide for himself. That pattern — come and see rather than let me convince you — is the consistent evangelistic method in John's Gospel. Encounter with Jesus is the answer to skepticism not apologetic argument.

John Chapter 2

The Wedding at Cana

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Jesus attends a wedding in Cana with his mother and disciples. The wine runs out. His mother tells him and he responds that his hour has not yet come. She tells the servants to do whatever he says. He has six large stone purification jars filled with water — each holding twenty to thirty gallons. He turns the water into wine. The master of the feast tastes it and tells the bridegroom he has saved the best wine for last. This is the first of Jesus's signs and his disciples believe in him.

What You Might Not Know

John calls the miracles of Jesus signs rather than miracles or wonders. This is characteristic of John's Gospel throughout. A sign points beyond itself to something it signifies. The turning of water into wine is not just a demonstration of power — it is a sign pointing toward the identity and mission of Jesus. Every sign in John's Gospel is meant to produce belief not simply amazement.

What does that have to do with me and you woman — Jesus's response to his mother has puzzled readers for centuries. The address woman is not disrespectful in Greek — it is a formal respectful address Jesus uses again from the cross when he entrusts his mother to the beloved disciple. But the question suggests a gentle distance — you and I operate in different spheres now. My hour has not yet come points forward to the hour of his glorification through death and resurrection which John tracks throughout the Gospel.

Six stone water jars used for Jewish purification rites — the specific detail is significant. The water used for ritual purification becomes wine for a wedding feast. The old system of external cleansing is transformed into the new abundance of the Kingdom. The six jars holding between 120 and 180 gallons total produce an extravagant quantity of the best wine — abundance far beyond what the occasion required. That extravagance is characteristic of how the Kingdom works throughout John's Gospel.

You have kept the good wine until now — the master of the feast speaks better than he knows. The best has indeed been kept until now — not just for this wedding but for this moment in history. The arrival of Jesus is the arrival of what everything before was pointing toward.

Why It Still Matters

Do whatever he tells you — Mary's instruction to the servants is the last recorded statement she makes in John's Gospel. Her final words point entirely away from herself toward her son. That posture — complete redirection toward Jesus — is the model John presents for anyone who encounters him. The servants who did what Jesus said witnessed the transformation. Those who only watched the master of the feast taste the result knew something remarkable had happened but not how or why. Obedience positioned the servants to see what others missed.

Jesus Clears the Temple

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Jesus goes to Jerusalem for Passover and finds the Temple courts filled with animal sellers and money changers. He makes a whip of cords and drives them all out scattering coins and overturning tables. He tells the dove sellers to stop making his Father's house a marketplace. Religious leaders demand a sign proving his authority. He says destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up. They think he means the building. He means his body. After the resurrection the disciples remember this and believe.

What You Might Not Know

John places the Temple cleansing at the beginning of Jesus's ministry while Matthew Mark and Luke place it at the end during the final week before the crucifixion. Either there were two Temple cleansings — one early and one late — or John has arranged his Gospel thematically rather than strictly chronologically. John's Gospel throughout shows less concern for strict chronological sequence and more concern for theological arrangement. Placing the Temple cleansing early establishes immediately what kind of Messiah Jesus is — not a political liberator but one who comes to his Father's house and finds it unworthy of what it is meant to be.

Making a whip out of cords — this detail is unique to John. Jesus did not arrive at the Temple and spontaneously react. He stopped to make a whip. The action was deliberate and prepared. This was not an impulsive outburst but a considered prophetic act carried out with intention.

Destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up — this statement echoes through the rest of John's Gospel and appears as a charge against Jesus at his trial in the other Gospels. Spoken at Passover at the beginning of his ministry it is already pointing toward the Passover at the end when the temple of his body will be destroyed and raised. The disciples only understood it after the resurrection — John notes this explicitly. Understanding followed the event that the statement predicted.

Why It Still Matters

Stop making my Father's house a marketplace — the passion Jesus shows for the Temple is the passion of someone for whom worship is not a transaction. The buying and selling had made the Temple a place where you got what you paid for rather than a place where you encountered the living God. That transformation of sacred space into commercial space — where religion becomes a product and access to God becomes something you purchase — is not limited to first century Jerusalem.

Jesus Knows What Is in People

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Many people in Jerusalem believe in Jesus because of the signs he performs. But Jesus does not entrust himself to them because he knows all people and knows what is in a person's heart without needing anyone to tell him.

What You Might Not Know

Jesus did not entrust himself to them — the Greek word for entrust is the same word translated believe. Many believed in his name but he did not believe in them — did not entrust himself to them. John is making a distinction between two kinds of belief — belief based on signs and spectacle and belief that has genuinely encountered who Jesus is. Sign-based belief is real but fragile. It depends on continued performance. Jesus knew the difference and did not mistake enthusiasm for the signs for genuine faith in himself.

He knew what was in a person's heart — this quality of knowing what is inside people without being told appears throughout John's Gospel. He knows Nathanael under the fig tree. He knows the Samaritan woman's history. He knows who will betray him. He knows Thomas's doubts. This is not mere psychological perceptiveness. John presents it as a dimension of his divine knowledge — the same knowledge that allowed the Word to create all things also allows him to see into the things he created.

Why It Still Matters

The distinction John draws here is quietly important. Excitement about what Jesus can do is not the same as trust in who Jesus is. Many people in Jerusalem saw the signs and believed in his name — and Jesus looked at them and withheld himself. Not because their response was wrong but because it was incomplete. The signs were meant to lead somewhere deeper than admiration for the signs. That deeper place is what John's Gospel is designed to take the reader toward.

John Chapter 3

Jesus and Nicodemus

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

A Pharisee named Nicodemus comes to Jesus at night acknowledging him as a teacher from God. Jesus tells him no one can see the Kingdom of God without being born from above. Nicodemus takes this literally and asks how an old man can be born again. Jesus explains — born of water and Spirit, not flesh. The Spirit is like wind — you hear it but cannot control or predict it. Nicodemus still does not understand. Jesus speaks of heavenly things and points to the Son of Man being lifted up as Moses lifted the snake in the wilderness so that everyone who believes may have eternal life.

What You Might Not Know

He came to Jesus at night — Nicodemus's nocturnal visit has been interpreted as secrecy — he did not want to be seen. That is possible. But in John's Gospel night also carries theological weight — it is associated with darkness, with not yet understanding, with being outside the light. Nicodemus comes in darkness and the conversation is about coming into the light. He appears again in Chapter 7 defending Jesus before the council and finally in Chapter 19 bringing burial spices for Jesus's body. His journey from night to daylight is one of the quiet arcs of John's Gospel.

Born from above — the Greek word anothen means both from above and again. Nicodemus hears again and responds with the absurd image of re-entering the womb. Jesus means from above — a birth that originates not in human biology but in the Spirit of God. The misunderstanding is not Nicodemus being foolish. It is John's literary technique of double meaning — characters hear one level of meaning while Jesus speaks at another and the reader is meant to hold both.

As Moses lifted up the snake in the wilderness — this refers to Numbers 21 where Israel was being killed by snakes as a judgment for their complaining. God told Moses to make a bronze snake and lift it up on a pole — anyone who was bitten could look at it and live. Jesus uses this as an image of his own crucifixion. Lifted up in John's Gospel refers simultaneously to being raised on a cross and to being exalted in glory. The crucifixion and the glorification are the same event seen from different angles.

Why It Still Matters

The wind blows wherever it pleases — Jesus's description of the Spirit's freedom is one of the most liberating statements in John's Gospel. You cannot predict it or control it or contain it. You can only hear it and respond to it. The new birth Jesus describes is not something you produce or achieve or schedule. It originates from above and arrives like wind — in its own time by its own route through its own means. That unpredictability is not a problem to be managed but the nature of what the Spirit does.

For God So Loved the World

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

God loved the world so much that he gave his only Son — whoever believes in him will not perish but have eternal life. God sent the Son not to condemn the world but to save it. Whoever believes is not condemned. Whoever does not believe is already condemned. The verdict is that light has come into the world but people loved darkness because their deeds were evil. Those who live by truth come into the light.

What You Might Not Know

For God so loved the world — the word so in English translates the Greek houtos which means in this way rather than to this degree. The verse is saying God loved the world in this manner — by giving his Son — rather than God loved the world so very much. Both meanings are theologically true but the original emphasis is on the manner of the love — self-giving — rather than simply its intensity. The how of God's love is the point.

His only-begotten Son — the Greek word is monogenes meaning one of a kind or unique. It is not primarily about biological generation but about uniqueness — there is no other of this kind. The Son is not one of many divine beings or one of many ways God has communicated with humanity. He is the singular and unrepeatable self-giving of God into human history.

Whoever does not believe stands condemned already — the condemnation Jesus describes is not a future judgment imposed from outside. It is a present state that results from rejecting the light. The darkness people choose when they reject the light is itself the judgment. This is not God's arbitrary punishment — it is the natural consequence of turning away from the only source of life.

People loved darkness instead of light because their deeds were evil — John does not present unbelief as primarily intellectual. It is moral. People do not come to the light because they do not want their deeds exposed. The resistance to Jesus in John's Gospel is consistently rooted in self-protection — the desire to remain unexamined and unchanged.

Why It Still Matters

God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world but to save the world through him — this statement reframes the entire purpose of the incarnation. Jesus did not come as a judge arriving to evaluate and sentence. He came as a rescuer. The condemnation that exists is pre-existing — the darkness that was already there. What he brought was light and life and the possibility of not perishing. That is the frame within which everything else in John's Gospel is meant to be read.

He Must Become Greater

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

John's disciples are troubled that everyone is going to Jesus. John answers — a person can only receive what is given from heaven. I told you I am not the Messiah but sent ahead of him. The bride belongs to the bridegroom. The friend of the bridegroom rejoices at the bridegroom's voice. That joy is mine and it is complete. He must become greater and I must become less. The one from above is above all. Whoever believes in the Son has eternal life. Whoever rejects the Son will not see life.

What You Might Not Know

He must become greater I must become less — this is one of the most quoted statements in John's Gospel and one of the most radical. John the Baptist was at the height of his influence and popularity when he said this. He was not being forced out. He was choosing to step back. His joy was complete not because he was being honored but because the one he had been pointing toward had arrived and people were going to him. The success of Jesus was the fulfillment of John's mission not the end of it.

The friend of the bridegroom — the best man in a Jewish wedding had a specific role. He arranged the marriage, brought the couple together and stood with the bridegroom. His joy was real but derivative — he rejoiced in someone else's union. He was not the center of the celebration. His role was entirely in service of another's joy. John uses this image to describe his own relationship to Jesus perfectly — genuine joy, genuine involvement, entirely derivative and supportive.

God gives the Spirit without limit — this statement about the Son contrasts with the prophets who received the Spirit in measure for specific purposes. The Son does not receive a portion of the Spirit for a particular mission. The Spirit is given to him without restriction or limitation. That fullness is what makes him able to give the Spirit to others — from his fullness we have all received grace upon grace as the prologue said.

Why It Still Matters

That joy is mine and it is now complete — John the Baptist experienced complete joy not at the height of his own ministry but at the moment his ministry was being superseded. His joy was grounded not in his own success or recognition but in the arrival of the one he had been sent to announce. That kind of joy — complete because of what someone else has become rather than what you yourself have achieved — is one of the rarest and most beautiful qualities in the New Testament.

John Chapter 4

The Samaritan Woman at the Well

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 4
What Is Happening Here

Jesus sits alone at Jacob's well at noon while his disciples are in town. A Samaritan woman comes to draw water. He asks her for a drink. She is surprised a Jewish man would speak to her. He offers living water. She misunderstands — thinking he means water that removes thirst permanently. He tells her to get her husband. She says she has no husband. He reveals he knows she has had five husbands and the man she is with now is not her husband. She recognizes him as a prophet and raises the question of where to worship. He teaches about worshiping in spirit and truth. She mentions the coming Messiah. He says I am he.

What You Might Not Know

A Samaritan woman coming to draw water at noon — women typically drew water in the cool of the morning or evening in groups. Coming alone at midday suggests she was avoiding the other women — which her marital history makes understandable. She was socially marginalized even within her own community. Jesus chose to have his longest recorded one-on-one conversation in any of the Gospels with the person least likely to be sought out by a Jewish rabbi.

Jews and Samaritans had centuries of mutual hostility rooted in the Assyrian conquest of the northern kingdom in 722 BC. The remaining population intermarried with Assyrian settlers and developed their own version of Israelite religion centered on Mount Gerizim rather than Jerusalem. Jews considered them half-breeds and heretics. The disciples were surprised to find Jesus talking with a woman — the additional factor of her being Samaritan made it doubly scandalous.

You have had five husbands — Jesus does not condemn the woman for her history. He simply states it accurately. The effect is that she recognizes she is talking to someone who sees her completely — all of it — and is still having a conversation with her about living water and the nature of worship. The revelation of her history is not a disqualification. It is the moment she realizes who she is talking to.

Worship in spirit and in truth — Jesus is not contrasting sincere worship with formal worship. Spirit and truth together describe worship that originates from the inside — from genuine encounter with the God who is Spirit — rather than worship that is primarily about location or ritual. The hour is coming and is now here — the new mode of worship is not future. It is present in this conversation at this well.

Why It Still Matters

The Samaritan woman left her water jar — the detail is small and significant. She came to the well for water. She left without it. What she found at the well made the water jar irrelevant. She went into the town and invited people to come and see a man who told her everything she ever did. The woman who had been avoiding the community became its evangelist. The one who came alone at noon to avoid people went back and drew a crowd. That transformation — from isolation to witness — happened in a single conversation at a well.

My Food Is to Do His Will

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 4
What Is Happening Here

The disciples return and urge Jesus to eat. He says he has food they do not know about. They wonder if someone brought him food. He says his food is to do the will of the one who sent him and finish his work. He tells them to open their eyes — the fields are already ripe for harvest. The sower and the reaper will rejoice together. He sent them to reap what others have worked for.

What You Might Not Know

My food is to do the will of him who sent me — Jesus uses the disciples' concern about physical food to teach about the sustenance that actually drives him. The conversation with the Samaritan woman — the doing of God's will — has fed him in a way that physical food cannot. Throughout John's Gospel Jesus describes himself in terms of basic human necessities — I am the bread of life, I am the living water, I am the light of the world. Here he describes his mission in terms of food — what sustains and nourishes him is doing what he was sent to do.

Open your eyes and look at the fields — they are ripe for harvest — Jesus says this as the Samaritans are coming out of the town toward him. The fields he is pointing to are not agricultural fields. They are the people walking toward him across the landscape. The harvest is already happening. The disciples who went into town to buy physical food missed the spiritual harvest that was already underway.

Why It Still Matters

One sows and another reaps — Jesus acknowledges the gap between those who plant and those who harvest. The Samaritan woman sowed by going into town and telling people about the man at the well. The disciples will reap as the Samaritans come to Jesus. Neither role is more important than the other. Both are necessary. The willingness to sow without seeing the harvest — and to reap without having done the sowing — requires a generosity about credit and outcome that runs against ordinary human instincts.

The Samaritans Believe

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 4
What Is Happening Here

Many Samaritans believe because of the woman's testimony. They ask Jesus to stay and he stays two days. More believe because of his own words. They tell the woman they no longer believe just because of what she said — they have heard for themselves and know this man is the Savior of the world.

What You Might Not Know

The Savior of the world — this title is used only here and in 1 John 4 in the entire New Testament. It comes from Samaritans — Gentiles on the margins of Israel — not from Jews or disciples. The people least expected to make this declaration make it first and make it most comprehensively. Not the Savior of Israel or the Savior of the Jews but the Savior of the world. The scope of Jesus's mission is declared by outsiders before it is fully grasped by insiders.

We no longer believe just because of what you said — the progression of faith in this passage is carefully described. First the woman's testimony produces curiosity — come and see. Then direct encounter with Jesus produces conviction — we have heard for ourselves. The woman's witness was essential to bring them to Jesus. But genuine faith required their own encounter with him not just her report about him. Testimony opens the door. Personal encounter is what produces the deep knowing.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus stayed two days in Samaria — territory that observant Jews avoided entirely. He was not passing through reluctantly. He stayed. He taught. He let people come to genuine faith through extended time with him. The willingness to remain in unexpected places with unexpected people long enough for real transformation to happen is a consistent feature of how Jesus operates in John's Gospel.

The Royal Official's Son

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 4
What Is Happening Here

Back in Cana a royal official from Capernaum begs Jesus to come heal his dying son. Jesus says unless you people see signs and wonders you will never believe. The official says come before my child dies. Jesus says go — your son will live. The man takes Jesus at his word and leaves. On the way his servants meet him with news that the boy recovered at the exact hour Jesus spoke. He and his whole household believe. This is the second sign.

What You Might Not Know

Unless you people see signs and wonders you will never believe — the you is plural. Jesus is not rebuking this specific father but addressing a broader pattern — the demand for visible proof before faith is possible. The father's response is striking. He does not argue or ask for a sign. He simply intensifies his plea — come before my child dies. The urgency of his need cuts through the general observation and Jesus responds to it directly and personally.

The man took Jesus at his word and departed — this is the faith Jesus is looking for. No sign was given. No visible evidence was produced. Jesus spoke a word and the man believed it and acted on it by leaving. The healing happened at a distance — Jesus in Cana the boy in Capernaum twenty miles away. The verification came the next day when the servants confirmed the timing. The faith preceded the verification entirely.

He and his whole household believed — the healing of one child produced faith in an entire household. John notes this expansion of belief from the individual to the family consistently. The Samaritan woman's testimony brought a whole town. The official's experience brought his whole household. Faith in John's Gospel tends to spread outward from the person who first encounters Jesus.

Why It Still Matters

The second sign in John follows the same pattern as the first — an inadequate situation transformed by Jesus's word into abundant life. At Cana the inadequacy was wine. Here it is a dying child. In both cases Jesus speaks and the transformation happens. In both cases the sign produces belief. John is building a cumulative case through these signs — each one pointing toward the identity of Jesus and inviting the reader to the same conclusion the witnesses reached.

John Chapter 5

The Healing at the Pool

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Jesus goes to the pool of Bethesda in Jerusalem where a large number of disabled people lie waiting. He singles out a man who has been an invalid for thirty-eight years and asks him if he wants to get well. The man explains he has no one to help him into the pool when the water stirs. Jesus tells him to get up pick up his mat and walk. He does. It is the Sabbath. Religious leaders challenge him for carrying his mat. He says the man who healed him told him to. Later Jesus finds him in the Temple and tells him to stop sinning or something worse may happen.

What You Might Not Know

Do you want to get well — this question has puzzled readers because the answer seems obvious. Of course he wants to get well. But the man has been there thirty-eight years. He has organized his entire existence around his condition and his place by the pool. Jesus's question is not naive. It is penetrating. Sometimes people have adapted so thoroughly to their limitations that the prospect of being well — with all the responsibilities and changes that would bring — is not straightforwardly desirable. The question asks something real.

The pool of Bethesda — archaeological excavations in Jerusalem have found a pool matching John's description near the Sheep Gate with five covered colonnades. The pool existed and the detail is accurate. Some manuscripts add a note about an angel stirring the water and the first person in being healed — this is likely a later addition explaining the popular belief that brought people there.

Stop sinning or something worse may happen — Jesus's warning to the healed man has troubled readers. It seems to connect his thirty-eight years of illness to sin — the very connection Jesus rejects elsewhere. The warning may not be about the original illness at all but about the man's immediate action of reporting Jesus to the authorities — a betrayal of the one who healed him. Whatever its meaning it establishes that healing is not the end of the story. What you do with your restored life matters.

Why It Still Matters

The man did not know who healed him — Jesus had slipped away into the crowd. He could not identify his healer to the religious leaders even if he had wanted to. He was healed by someone whose name he did not know. The healing was entirely the initiative of Jesus — the man asked for nothing, expressed no faith, made no confession. Jesus saw him lying there learned how long he had been there and acted. That unilateral compassion without any precondition is one of the most striking features of this healing in John's Gospel.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 5

The Authority of the Son

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Because Jesus healed on the Sabbath the religious leaders persecute him. He responds that his Father works to this day and he works too. They try to kill him — not only for breaking the Sabbath but for calling God his own Father making himself equal with God. Jesus explains — the Son can do nothing by himself but only what he sees the Father doing. The Father loves the Son and shows him everything. The Son gives life to whom he pleases. All judgment has been entrusted to the Son so that all may honor the Son as they honor the Father.

What You Might Not Know

My Father is always at his work to this very day — Jesus's defense of Sabbath healing is theological. God does not cease sustaining the universe on the Sabbath — the sun rises, hearts beat, crops grow. If God worked on the Sabbath to sustain life then the Son working on the Sabbath to restore life is entirely consistent. The religious leaders understood the implication immediately — Jesus was claiming the same relationship to the Sabbath that God has. That is why they wanted to kill him.

The Son can do nothing by himself — this statement seems to limit Jesus's power but it actually describes the depth of his unity with the Father. He does not act independently not because he lacks power but because he and the Father are so completely united in will and purpose that independent action would be a contradiction of who he is. The limitation is relational not ontological.

All judgment has been entrusted to the Son — John's Gospel consistently holds together two things that seem in tension — Jesus came not to condemn but to save, and all judgment has been given to the Son. Both are true. His primary purpose is salvation. His authority includes judgment. The judgment that falls on those who reject him is real but it is the consequence of rejecting the one who came to save not the primary purpose of his coming.

Why It Still Matters

Whoever does not honor the Son does not honor the Father who sent him — this statement cuts through every form of religion that claims to honor God while rejecting Jesus. In John's Gospel there is no access to the Father that bypasses the Son. That is an exclusive claim and John presents it without apology. The question it raises — who do you say the Son is — is the question the entire Gospel is designed to press toward an answer.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 5

Testimonies About Jesus

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Jesus presents four witnesses to his identity — John the Baptist, his works, the Father himself, and the Scriptures. He tells the religious leaders that John testified to the truth and they enjoyed his light for a time. But his works are greater testimony than John. The Father who sent him has testified. The Scriptures they study diligently testify about him. Yet they refuse to come to him for life. They accept glory from each other but do not seek the glory that comes from God. Moses — on whom they set their hopes — wrote about him. Their failure to believe Moses means they will not believe Jesus.

What You Might Not Know

You study the Scriptures diligently because you think that in them you have eternal life — these are the very Scriptures that testify about me yet you refuse to come to me to have life. This is one of the most incisive statements in John's Gospel. The religious leaders were expert students of Scripture. They knew the texts. They could debate the details. But they were using Scripture as a destination rather than a road — finding life in the study itself rather than letting the study lead them to the one the Scripture pointed toward. The Bible is a witness to Jesus not a substitute for him.

You accept glory from one another but do not seek the glory that comes from the only God — Jesus identifies the social dynamics of the religious establishment as a barrier to faith. Reputation within the community, peer approval, the honor of being recognized as learned and devout — these created a social cost for following Jesus that the leaders were unwilling to pay. Their faith had become captive to what their community thought of them.

Moses wrote about me — Jesus claims that the entire Torah points toward him. This is the same claim he made on the road to Emmaus in Luke when he opened the Scriptures and explained everything written about him beginning with Moses. The Hebrew Scriptures in Jesus's understanding are not a separate religion that he supplements. They are the preparation for his arrival that finds its fulfillment in him.

Why It Still Matters

Your accuser is Moses on whom your hopes are set — the irony is complete. The very figure the religious leaders appeal to as the source of their authority against Jesus is the one Jesus says will accuse them. They claim Moses as their champion. Moses claimed Jesus as the one he wrote about. Their rejection of Jesus is therefore a rejection of Moses — which means their appeal to Moses against Jesus is self-defeating. The argument they are using against him is the argument that will ultimately be used against them.

John Chapter 6

Feeding the Five Thousand

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

A large crowd follows Jesus because of his healing signs. He tests Philip by asking where they can buy bread for the crowd. Philip says it would cost more than half a year's wages. Andrew finds a boy with five barley loaves and two fish. Jesus has the crowd sit down — about five thousand men — gives thanks and distributes the food. Everyone eats as much as they want. Twelve baskets of leftovers are gathered. The crowd concludes he is the Prophet who is to come and tries to make him king by force. Jesus withdraws alone to a mountain.

What You Might Not Know

He asked this only to test him for he already knew what he was going to do — John alone includes this editorial note. Jesus was not asking Philip for information or a solution. He was creating a moment of confrontation with impossibility so that what followed would be understood as a sign. The test was not whether Philip could solve the problem but whether Philip would recognize that the problem was beyond human solution — and therefore the solution would have to come from somewhere else.

Five barley loaves — barley bread was the cheapest bread available — the food of the poor. Not fine wheat bread but the most basic provision. The boy's lunch was not impressive by any standard. Andrew notes both its presence and its inadequacy — there is a boy here with this but how far will it go among so many? The combination of presence and inadequacy is the exact situation Jesus consistently works with throughout the Gospels.

The crowd concludes he is the Prophet who is to come — the feeding in the wilderness immediately suggested Moses and the manna to the crowd. They are making the right connection but drawing the wrong conclusion. Jesus is not a new Moses who will lead a national liberation movement. When they try to make him king by force he withdraws. The Kingdom he is inaugurating cannot be seized by political enthusiasm however genuine.

Why It Still Matters

Let nothing be wasted — after feeding thousands Jesus instructs his disciples to gather every fragment. The abundance of the miracle does not produce carelessness about what remains. The same attention to each person that produced the feeding extends to every leftover piece. Twelve baskets — one for each disciple — filled with what the crowd left behind. The provision exceeded the need and nothing was lost. Both the abundance and the careful stewardship of it are part of the sign.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6

I Am the Bread of Life

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

The crowd follows Jesus to Capernaum looking for more food. Jesus tells them they are seeking him because they ate and were filled not because they understood the signs. He tells them to work for food that endures to eternal life. They ask what works God requires. He says believe in the one he has sent. They ask for a sign — their ancestors ate manna. He says the true bread from heaven is the one who comes down and gives life to the world. They ask for this bread. He declares I am the bread of life — whoever comes to me will never hunger whoever believes in me will never thirst.

What You Might Not Know

I am the bread of life — this is the first of seven I am statements with a predicate noun in John's Gospel. I am the bread of life, the light of the world, the door, the good shepherd, the resurrection and the life, the way the truth and the life, the true vine. Each one takes a concrete image from human experience and applies it to Jesus as the ultimate reality that the image points toward. Bread sustains physical life. Jesus sustains eternal life. The image is not a metaphor for something vague — it is a claim about the specific and irreplaceable function he performs.

The work of God is this — to believe in the one he has sent — the crowd asks what works God requires. They expect a list of religious obligations. Jesus collapses the entire category of religious works into a single response — trust in him. This does not make faith passive or easy. In John's Gospel believing in Jesus is the most demanding thing a person can do because it requires a complete reorientation of where you place your ultimate trust.

All those the Father gives me will come to me and whoever comes to me I will never drive away — these two statements belong together. The sovereignty of the Father in drawing people to Jesus and the absolute guarantee that no one who comes will be rejected are both present in the same breath. John holds divine initiative and human response together without resolving the tension between them.

Why It Still Matters

You have seen me and still you do not believe — Jesus says this to people who have just eaten bread he multiplied from a boy's lunch. They saw the sign and followed him across a lake looking for more bread. They saw without seeing — they experienced the miracle without grasping what it signified. That gap between witnessing something extraordinary and actually believing in the one who did it is one of the central tensions John traces throughout his Gospel.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6

Eat My Flesh Drink My Blood

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

Jesus intensifies the bread of life teaching — this bread is my flesh which I will give for the life of the world. The crowd argues about how he can give his flesh to eat. Jesus says unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood you have no life in you. Whoever eats his flesh and drinks his blood has eternal life and remains in him. Many disciples say this is a hard teaching and turn back. Jesus asks the twelve if they want to leave too. Peter says Lord to whom shall we go — you have the words of eternal life. Jesus says one of the twelve is a devil — meaning Judas.

What You Might Not Know

Eating flesh and drinking blood was deeply offensive to a Jewish audience on multiple levels. Drinking blood was absolutely prohibited under Jewish Law — the blood contained the life and belonged to God. The imagery Jesus uses was calculated to be shocking. He does not soften it when people object. He intensifies it. The point is not cannibalism — the Spirit gives life the flesh counts for nothing. He is describing a total incorporation of himself — his life given for the world — that can only be received through radical trust and dependence. The Eucharist is the sacramental expression of this reality but the teaching goes deeper than any specific practice.

This is a hard teaching who can accept it — the disciples who say this are not the hostile crowd. They are people who have been following Jesus. The teaching is hard not because it is intellectually difficult but because of what it demands — complete dependence on Jesus as the source of life, complete incorporation of his death as the basis of your existence. That is genuinely hard for people who want to maintain some independent ground to stand on.

Lord to whom shall we go — Peter's response to Jesus's question is one of the most honest confessions of faith in the Gospels. He does not say I fully understand this teaching or I am completely comfortable with everything you said. He says there is nowhere else to go. You have the words of eternal life. The faith Peter expresses here is not the faith of someone who has resolved all difficulties. It is the faith of someone who has looked at the alternatives and found them all inadequate.

Why It Still Matters

From this time many of his disciples turned back and no longer followed him — this is one of the most sobering statements in John's Gospel. The departure is not of hostile outsiders but of disciples — people who had been following Jesus. The hard teaching produced a sifting. What Jesus said was true whether people accepted it or not. He did not modify it to retain the crowd. He let them go and asked the twelve if they wanted to go too. The question he asked them is the question the chapter leaves with every reader.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6

Peter's Confession

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

Jesus asks the twelve if they want to leave too. Peter answers — Lord to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. We have come to believe and know that you are the Holy One of God. Jesus responds that he chose the twelve and yet one of them is a devil — referring to Judas Iscariot who will betray him.

What You Might Not Know

We have come to believe and to know — the combination of believe and know is significant in John's Gospel. Believing precedes knowing — trust opens the door to understanding. This is the opposite of the modern assumption that you need to understand before you can trust. In John's Gospel the sequence is consistently belief first then deeper knowing that comes through continued relationship with Jesus. Peter is describing a process not a single moment — we have come to believe and through that believing we have come to know.

Yet one of you is a devil — Jesus's response to Peter's beautiful confession is jarring. He accepts the confession and immediately introduces its shadow — one of the twelve is already on the path of betrayal. John notes that he meant Judas. The confession of Peter and the betrayal of Judas are held in the same breath. The community of twelve contains both the rock and the devil. That honest darkness at the heart of the inner circle is characteristic of John's unwillingness to sanitize the story.

Why It Still Matters

To whom shall we go — this question is the most honest answer to Jesus's challenge in the entire Gospel. Peter does not claim to understand everything. He does not claim the hard teaching made sense to him. He says there is nowhere else to go because nowhere else has what Jesus has — the words of eternal life. That is not triumphant faith. It is the faith of someone who has looked honestly at everything else available and found it all insufficient. That kind of faith — born of having nowhere better to turn — is recognized and honored throughout John's Gospel.

John Chapter 7

Jesus at the Festival of Tabernacles

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Jesus's brothers taunt him to go public at the Festival of Tabernacles if he wants to be known. John notes they did not believe in him. Jesus says his time has not yet come and stays behind — then goes secretly. At the festival the crowds whisper about him. Halfway through he begins teaching in the Temple courts. The religious leaders are amazed at his learning. He explains his teaching comes from the one who sent him. He challenges them about trying to kill him. He defends healing on the Sabbath by pointing to circumcision on the Sabbath. He tells them to stop judging by appearances.

What You Might Not Know

The Festival of Tabernacles — Sukkot in Hebrew — was one of the three great pilgrimage festivals requiring Jewish men to come to Jerusalem. It lasted eight days and commemorated Israel's forty years in the wilderness living in temporary shelters. It was also associated with water — priests carried water from the Pool of Siloam to the Temple in a joyful procession each day — and with light — huge menorahs were lit in the Temple courts illuminating Jerusalem at night. Both the water and light imagery become central to what Jesus says and does during this festival.

For even his own brothers did not believe in him — this detail is quietly devastating. The people who grew up in the same house with Jesus did not believe he was who he claimed to be. They were not hostile — they encouraged him to go public and build his following. But their encouragement was the encouragement of someone who does not take the claims seriously. After the resurrection James the brother of Jesus became a leader of the Jerusalem church. The unbelief of the brothers during the ministry and their later faith is one of the evidences historians point to for the resurrection's reality.

My time is not yet here — the word time is kairos — the appointed moment. Throughout John's Gospel Jesus operates with an awareness of a specific appointed hour that governs his movements and decisions. He does not go to the festival publicly because the appointed time for his public glorification has not arrived. When that time comes in Chapter 12 he will enter Jerusalem publicly and everything will accelerate toward the cross.

Why It Still Matters

Anyone who chooses to do God's will will find out whether my teaching comes from God — Jesus offers a practical test for the truth of his teaching. The test is not intellectual analysis but volitional commitment. Choosing to do God's will — orienting your life toward obedience — creates the conditions in which the truth of Jesus's teaching becomes recognizable. That is the opposite of the modern assumption that you need to be intellectually convinced before you can commit. Jesus says commitment opens the door to recognition.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 7

Rivers of Living Water

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

On the last and greatest day of the festival Jesus stands and cries out — let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink. Whoever believes in him rivers of living water will flow from within them. John explains this referred to the Spirit whom believers would later receive after Jesus was glorified. The crowd is divided — some say he is the Prophet, some say the Messiah, some object that the Messiah cannot come from Galilee. Temple guards sent to arrest him return empty-handed saying no one ever spoke the way this man does.

What You Might Not Know

The last and greatest day of the festival — the eighth day of Tabernacles was the climax of the water-drawing ceremony. For seven days priests had carried water from Siloam in a golden pitcher and poured it on the altar while the crowd waved palm branches and sang Psalms 113 to 118. On the eighth day the ceremony stopped — no water was poured. Into that moment of symbolic absence Jesus stands and cries out let anyone who is thirsty come to me. The timing is deliberate and the imagery is electric for anyone who understood what the ceremony meant.

Rivers of living water will flow from within them — John specifies this refers to the Spirit. The image of the Spirit as living water flowing from the believer outward echoes Ezekiel 47 where a river flows from the Temple giving life to everything it touches. Jesus is presenting himself as the new Temple from whom the life-giving river flows — and believers as those through whom that river continues to flow into the world.

No one ever spoke the way this man does — the Temple guards sent to arrest Jesus return without him giving this as their reason. They were professional security personnel doing their job and they could not bring themselves to arrest him because of how he spoke. The authority of Jesus's words stopped people who had been given explicit orders to seize him. Even his opponents were not immune to the power of what he said.

Why It Still Matters

Let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink — the invitation is unconditional and universal. Anyone. Any kind of thirst. Any background. The only qualification is thirst itself — the recognition of need. That invitation spoken on the last day of a festival celebrating God's provision in the wilderness is Jesus presenting himself as the fulfillment of everything the festival pointed toward. The water the priests had been pouring on the altar for seven days was a symbol. He is the reality the symbol was always pointing to.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 7

Division and Nicodemus

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

The Pharisees are contemptuous of the crowd — this mob that knows nothing of the Law has a curse on them. Nicodemus — who had visited Jesus at night in Chapter 3 — asks whether the Law allows condemning someone without hearing them first. They dismiss him — are you from Galilee too? Look it up — no prophet comes from Galilee. Everyone goes home.

What You Might Not Know

This mob that knows nothing of the Law there is a curse on them — the Pharisees' contempt for the common people who were drawn to Jesus is one of the most revealing statements in John's Gospel. The am ha-aretz — the people of the land — were considered ignorant of the finer points of Torah observance. The religious establishment viewed them with a mixture of condescension and contempt. Jesus consistently moved toward exactly these people. The crowd the Pharisees cursed for their ignorance was the crowd Jesus blessed for their thirst.

Nicodemus appears here for the second time in John's Gospel — making a modest procedural defense of Jesus without committing himself openly. He is still operating in the shadows of his nighttime visit in Chapter 3. His defense is technically correct — the Law does require a hearing before judgment. But it is carefully limited — he does not say he believes Jesus is the Messiah. He will appear one final time in Chapter 19 bringing burial spices for Jesus's body. His journey from night to full daylight happens slowly and at great personal cost.

Why It Still Matters

Look into it and you will find that a prophet does not come out of Galilee — the Pharisees were wrong. Jonah came from Gath-hepher in Galilee. Elijah came from Tishbe in Gilead near Galilee. Their confident dismissal was based on incomplete knowledge of the very Scriptures they claimed to be the authoritative interpreters of. The people in Chapter 7 who object that the Messiah cannot come from Galilee are making a theological argument from geography without knowing that Jesus was actually born in Bethlehem. Both the objection and its answer illustrate a consistent pattern in John — people arguing confidently from partial information while the full truth stands in front of them.

John Chapter 8

The Woman Caught in Adultery

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Religious leaders bring a woman caught in adultery to Jesus in the Temple courts and ask if she should be stoned as Moses commanded. Jesus bends down and writes on the ground. When they press him he says let anyone without sin throw the first stone. He writes on the ground again. They leave one by one oldest first until only Jesus and the woman remain. He asks if anyone has condemned her. No one. Then neither do I condemn you — go and leave your life of sin.

What You Might Not Know

This passage — John 7:53 through 8:11 — is not found in the oldest and most reliable manuscripts of John's Gospel. Most scholars believe it was not part of John's original text but was a genuine early tradition about Jesus that circulated independently and was eventually inserted here. It appears in different locations in different manuscripts — some place it in Luke. Whatever its textual history the story is consistent with everything we know about Jesus from the rest of the Gospels and has been treasured by the church for nearly two thousand years.

They were using this question as a trap — the trap was legal. If Jesus said stone her he could be reported to Rome for inciting illegal execution since Jews did not have authority to carry out capital punishment under Roman rule. If he said do not stone her he could be accused of contradicting Moses. The question had no safe answer by ordinary logic. Jesus stepped outside the logic of the trap entirely.

Jesus bent down and wrote on the ground — this is the only recorded instance of Jesus writing anything. What he wrote is not told to us. The writing has generated enormous speculation — some suggest he was writing the sins of the accusers, some suggest he was following a Roman legal practice of writing the verdict before announcing it. John does not tell us and the silence is probably deliberate. What matters is the effect — the pause, the bending down, the indirect engagement that gave the accusers time to hear what he said and feel its weight.

The older ones first — the oldest left first. They had lived longest and perhaps accumulated the most awareness of their own failures. Or they had the most social standing to protect and recognized most quickly that they were trapped. Either way the departure from oldest to youngest is a detail that feels like eyewitness memory.

Why It Still Matters

Neither do I condemn you — go and leave your life of sin. Both parts of Jesus's response belong together. The refusal to condemn is not the same as approval of the sin. The call to leave her life of sin is not a reinstatement of the condemnation she just escaped. He holds both — complete acceptance of the person and complete honesty about the life. That combination — grace without minimizing sin, truth without condemnation — is the shape of every genuine encounter with Jesus in John's Gospel.

I Am the Light of the World

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Jesus declares I am the light of the world — whoever follows him will never walk in darkness but will have the light of life. The Pharisees challenge that his self-testimony is invalid. He responds that his testimony is valid because he knows where he came from and where he is going — they do not. He and the Father are the two witnesses the Law requires. They ask where his Father is. He says if they knew him they would know the Father. No one seizes him because his hour has not yet come.

What You Might Not Know

I am the light of the world — this declaration comes during the Festival of Tabernacles when enormous menorahs lit the Temple courts so brilliantly that it was said all Jerusalem was illuminated. Jesus stands in that light-filled context and declares himself to be the true light of the world. The festival imagery — water on the last day in Chapter 7 and light throughout — provides the backdrop against which Jesus's I am statements in these chapters land with maximum force.

The testimony of two witnesses — Jewish law required two witnesses to establish a legal claim. The Pharisees objected that Jesus was testifying about himself — one witness not two. Jesus says his two witnesses are himself and the Father. They dismiss this because they cannot see or identify the Father as a witness. Jesus says their inability to recognize the Father reveals that they do not know him — if they knew the Father they would recognize his testimony in everything Jesus does and says.

Why It Still Matters

Whoever follows me will never walk in darkness but will have the light of life — the promise is not that followers of Jesus will be exempt from dark circumstances. It is that they will have light in those circumstances — the light of life that comes from the one who is life itself. Walking in darkness in John's Gospel means more than difficulty or suffering. It means disorientation — not knowing where you came from or where you are going. The light Jesus offers is the light of knowing both.

The Truth Will Set You Free

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Jesus tells those who believed him that if they hold to his teaching they are truly his disciples and will know the truth and the truth will set them free. They object that as Abraham's descendants they have never been slaves. He says everyone who sins is a slave to sin. A slave has no permanent place in the family but a son belongs forever. If the Son sets you free you are free indeed. He tells them they are seeking to kill him because his word has no place in them — they are doing the works of their own father not Abraham's works.

What You Might Not Know

The truth will set you free — this phrase has become one of the most quoted statements in Western culture often removed entirely from its context. In John 8 the truth that sets free is not abstract knowledge or intellectual enlightenment. It is the truth that comes from holding to Jesus's teaching — from remaining in relationship with him as disciples. The freedom it produces is specifically freedom from slavery to sin. The statement is not about political freedom or freedom of thought in general. It is about liberation from the internal bondage that sin creates.

We have never been slaves of anyone — the claim is historically puzzling given Israel's slavery in Egypt, captivity in Babylon and current subjection to Rome. The speakers are either making a purely spiritual claim about their covenant status as God's free people or the claim reveals a kind of selective memory that has rewritten their own history. Jesus does not challenge the historical claim. He redirects to the deeper slavery — everyone who sins is a slave to sin — which is not resolved by ethnic heritage.

Why It Still Matters

A slave has no permanent place in the family but a son belongs to it forever — the distinction Jesus draws is between conditional and permanent belonging. A slave's place depends on continued usefulness and the master's goodwill. A son's place is secure by nature of the relationship itself. The freedom Jesus offers is not freedom from obligation but freedom into permanent belonging — the security of sonship rather than the anxiety of servitude.

Your Father the Devil

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Jesus tells his opponents that if God were their Father they would love him since he came from God. They cannot understand his language because they are unable to hear what he says. They belong to their father the devil who was a murderer from the beginning and the father of lies. Because Jesus tells the truth they do not believe him. Whoever belongs to God hears what God says — they do not hear because they do not belong to God.

What You Might Not Know

He was a murderer from the beginning — Jesus connects the devil to murder from the very beginning of human history — pointing toward Cain and Abel in Genesis 4 and to the lie in the garden in Genesis 3 that brought death. The devil's nature is defined by two things — murder and lies. He destroys life and he distorts truth. Those two activities are presented as connected — deception produces death. The religious leaders who are seeking to kill Jesus while believing they serve God are exhibiting both characteristics without recognizing their source.

Can any of you prove me guilty of sin — Jesus issues this challenge in the middle of a heated argument with people who are trying to discredit him. It is an extraordinary claim. He is not saying he is merely better than average or relatively righteous. He is asking them to produce evidence of specific wrongdoing. The challenge goes unanswered. They respond by calling him demon-possessed — which is not an answer to the question.

Why It Still Matters

Whoever belongs to God hears what God says — hearing in John's Gospel is not merely auditory reception. It is recognition and response. The sheep hear the shepherd's voice in Chapter 10. The dead hear the voice of the Son of God in Chapter 5. Hearing carries the sense of being attuned to — recognizing and responding to. Jesus is saying that the reason his opponents do not receive what he says is not intellectual difficulty but a deeper misalignment — they are not oriented toward God in a way that makes his voice recognizable.

Before Abraham Was I Am

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

The crowd calls Jesus a Samaritan and demon-possessed. He says he honors his Father and whoever obeys his word will never see death. They say this proves he is demon-possessed — Abraham died and so did the prophets. Who does he think he is? He says Abraham rejoiced to see his day and was glad. They say he is not fifty yet and has seen Abraham? He answers before Abraham was born I am. They pick up stones to stone him. He slips away.

What You Might Not Know

Before Abraham was born I am — this is the climactic statement of Chapter 8 and one of the most theologically significant statements in the entire Gospel. The grammar is deliberately jarring. Before Abraham was born uses the past tense — Abraham came into existence at a point in time. I am uses the present tense — Jesus does not say I was but I am. He is not claiming to be older than Abraham. He is claiming to exist outside of time in the eternal present tense that belongs only to God. I am is the divine name revealed to Moses at the burning bush in Exodus 3 — I AM WHO I AM. The crowd understood exactly what he was claiming and immediately reached for stones.

Abraham rejoiced to see my day — how Abraham saw Jesus's day is not explained. Some have suggested it refers to the birth of Isaac — the son of promise through whom the blessing to all nations would come — as a foreshadowing of Jesus. Others point to Genesis 18 where Abraham entertained three visitors one of whom was identified as the Lord. Whatever the specific reference Jesus is claiming a relationship with Abraham that transcends historical sequence.

Why It Still Matters

The I am statements of John's Gospel reach their most absolute form here — not I am the bread or I am the light but simply I am. No predicate. No image. Just the divine name applied to himself in a context where everyone present understood the claim being made. They did not misunderstand him and then pick up stones. They understood him perfectly and picked up stones because of it. The question John's Gospel poses to every reader is the same one the crowd faced in that Temple court — what do you do with this claim?

John Chapter 9

The Man Born Blind

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Jesus sees a man blind from birth. His disciples ask whose sin caused the blindness — his or his parents. Jesus says neither — this happened so the works of God could be displayed in him. He makes mud with saliva and puts it on the man's eyes and tells him to wash in the Pool of Siloam. The man washes and comes home seeing. His neighbors argue about whether he is the same man. He insists he is. He tells them the man called Jesus healed him. He does not know where Jesus is.

What You Might Not Know

Who sinned — this man or his parents — the disciples' question reflects a common assumption in the ancient world that suffering was caused by specific sin. Job's friends made the same argument. Jesus has already rejected this framework in Chapter 5 regarding the invalid at Bethesda. Here he rejects it again clearly — neither this man nor his parents sinned to cause this. The blindness is not a punishment. It is an occasion for the display of God's works.

He spit on the ground and made mud — the use of saliva in healing was not unusual in the ancient world. Saliva was thought to have medicinal properties and its use in healing practices was documented across cultures. Jesus uses this recognizable gesture but takes it in an entirely different direction — the mud goes on the eyes rather than being applied medicinally and the healing happens not at the point of application but at the Pool of Siloam when the man obeys and washes.

The Pool of Siloam — this is the same pool whose water was carried to the Temple during the Festival of Tabernacles. Archaeologists have excavated the pool and confirmed it existed exactly as John describes. The man's obedience — going to a specific real location and washing — is the point where the healing manifests. The healing required his active participation. He could have ignored the instruction. He went and washed and came home seeing.

Why It Still Matters

We must do the works of the one who sent me while it is still day — night is coming when no one can work. Jesus frames the healing as urgent obedience to a limited window of opportunity. He is not healing this man because someone asked or because a crisis demanded it. He sees him and acts because the opportunity is there and the time to act is now. That sense of urgency — doing the works of God while it is still day — shapes the whole of Jesus's ministry in John's Gospel as it moves toward the hour of his glorification.

The Investigation

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

The healed man is brought to the Pharisees who investigate the healing — it happened on the Sabbath. Some say Jesus cannot be from God because he breaks the Sabbath. Others say a sinner cannot perform such signs. They are divided. They ask the man what he thinks. He says Jesus is a prophet. They summon his parents who confirm he was born blind but refuse to say more out of fear — anyone acknowledging Jesus as Messiah would be put out of the synagogue. They summon the man again and tell him to give glory to God and admit Jesus is a sinner. He says I was blind and now I see. They throw him out.

What You Might Not Know

Anyone who acknowledged that Jesus was the Messiah would be put out of the synagogue — John's note here is significant. Exclusion from the synagogue was a serious social and religious penalty. The synagogue was the center of Jewish community life — worship education and social belonging all flowed through it. Being put out meant losing your community your social network and your religious identity simultaneously. The parents' fear was not irrational. The cost of following Jesus in this context was genuinely high.

One thing I do know — I was blind but now I see — this statement has become one of the most famous lines in John's Gospel and in Christian history. The man is not a theologian. He cannot answer all the Pharisees' questions about Jesus's identity and Sabbath observance. But he knows one thing with absolute certainty — his own experience. The Pharisees can dispute the theology. They cannot dispute what he experienced. His testimony is unassailable because it is personal and verifiable.

Do you want to become his disciples too — the man's sarcastic question to the Pharisees who keep asking him to repeat his story is one of the most delightful moments in John's Gospel. A formerly blind beggar is verbally outmaneuvering the religious establishment. His growing boldness as the interrogation proceeds — from simple factual reporting to theological argument to sarcasm — tracks the progression of someone whose encounter with Jesus is producing increasing courage.

Why It Still Matters

Nobody has ever heard of opening the eyes of a man born blind — the man's argument is simple and devastating. He reasons from what he knows — the unprecedented nature of what happened to him — to a conclusion the Pharisees refuse to accept. If this man were not from God he could do nothing. The logic is sound. The evidence is his own eyes. The Pharisees' response — you were steeped in sin at birth — is not a counter-argument. It is an insult that reveals they have no answer and know it.

Spiritual Blindness

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Jesus hears the man was thrown out and finds him. He asks if he believes in the Son of Man. The man asks who that is so he can believe. Jesus says you have seen him — he is the one speaking with you. The man says Lord I believe and bows before him. Jesus says he came for judgment so the blind will see and those who see will become blind. Some Pharisees ask if they are blind. He says if they were blind they would have no guilt — but because they claim to see their guilt remains.

What You Might Not Know

Jesus heard that they had thrown him out and when he found him — Jesus actively sought the man after his expulsion. The man did not come to Jesus. Jesus came to him. The one who was cast out of the religious community by its leaders was found by the one the leaders were rejecting. That pattern — Jesus moving toward the excluded while those with religious authority exclude — is the defining dynamic of Chapter 9.

You have now seen him — the double meaning is unmistakable. The man who was physically blind has now literally seen Jesus — his physical eyes are open. But he is about to see in a deeper sense — recognizing and believing in the Son of Man. The physical healing was a sign pointing toward this moment of spiritual recognition. John has been building to this throughout the chapter.

If you were blind you would not be guilty — Jesus's final statement to the Pharisees is a precise theological reversal. Genuine blindness — not knowing — would carry no guilt. Their guilt comes from claiming to see while refusing to acknowledge what is plainly in front of them. The sin is not ignorance. It is the pretense of knowledge in the face of evidence they have chosen to reject. That willful blindness while claiming vision is the deepest form of darkness in John's Gospel.

Why It Still Matters

Chapter 9 is one of the most carefully constructed chapters in John's Gospel — a story about physical blindness that is simultaneously a story about spiritual sight and blindness. The man who cannot see becomes the one who sees most clearly. The men who claim to see become the ones who are most completely blind. The progression from I am the man to he is a prophet to Lord I believe tracks the journey from physical healing to full faith. The Pharisees' progression moves in the opposite direction — from investigation to denial to expulsion to willful blindness. Both journeys happen in the same chapter on the same day.

John Chapter 10

The Good Shepherd

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

Jesus uses the image of a sheepfold to describe himself. The true shepherd enters by the gate — the thief climbs in another way. The shepherd calls his own sheep by name and they follow because they know his voice. Jesus declares I am the gate — whoever enters through him will be saved and find pasture. The thief comes to steal kill and destroy. He came that they may have life to the full. He is the good shepherd who lays down his life for the sheep. The hired hand runs when the wolf comes. He knows his sheep and they know him as the Father knows him. He has other sheep not of this fold who will also hear his voice — one flock one shepherd. He lays down his life of his own accord and has authority to take it up again.

What You Might Not Know

The sheepfold image would have been immediately understood by a rural audience. Shepherds in Palestine kept their sheep in a stone enclosure at night. A single gate was the only legitimate entrance. Thieves who wanted to steal sheep had to climb over the wall. The shepherd came to the gate and the gatekeeper recognized him and opened it. The sheep recognized the shepherd's voice — ancient shepherds developed distinctive calls that their flocks learned to respond to. Sheep from different flocks could be mixed in a common fold overnight and each shepherd could call out his own flock in the morning.

I am the gate — this is often overlooked in favor of the good shepherd image but it is equally important. Jesus is not just the shepherd who tends the sheep — he is the gate through which they enter safety. Access to the fold — to protection provision and belonging — comes through him specifically. All who have come before me are thieves and robbers refers not to the Old Testament prophets but to the false messiahs and self-appointed leaders who had exploited the people.

I have other sheep that are not of this sheepfold — Jesus is pointing beyond Israel to the Gentile world. The one flock and one shepherd he describes is the community of all who hear his voice regardless of ethnic or religious background. This statement anticipates the universal scope of the Gospel that will be fully realized in Acts and throughout the New Testament letters.

No one takes my life from me — I lay it down of my own accord — this is one of the most important statements about the crucifixion in John's Gospel. Jesus is not a victim of circumstances or of the religious and political forces arrayed against him. He is the one who chooses to lay down his life. The cross is not something that happens to him. It is something he does — an act of sovereign love rather than a defeat.

Why It Still Matters

I have come that they may have life and have it to the full — the word translated full is perissos meaning exceeding what is necessary, beyond measure, abundant. Jesus did not come to provide minimal survival — just enough to get by. He came to provide life that overflows its own boundaries. That abundance is not primarily material. It is the quality of life that comes from being known by name led by a voice you recognize and secured by a shepherd who lays down his life rather than running when danger comes.

I and the Father Are One

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

At the Festival of Dedication the religious leaders demand Jesus tell them plainly if he is the Messiah. He says he told them but they do not believe because they are not his sheep. His sheep hear his voice he knows them and they follow him. He gives them eternal life and they will never perish — no one can snatch them from his hand or from the Father's hand. I and the Father are one. They pick up stones. He asks for which good work they are stoning him. They say for blasphemy — because he a mere man claims to be God.

What You Might Not Know

The Festival of Dedication is Hanukkah — the celebration of the rededication of the Temple after its desecration by Antiochus Epiphanes in 167 BC. It celebrated the restoration of genuine worship in the Temple after a false ruler had defiled it. Jesus is walking in Solomon's Colonnade — part of the Temple complex — during a festival about the restoration of true worship, and the religious leaders are asking him if he is the Messiah while plotting to kill him. The irony is pointed.

I and the Father are one — the Greek word for one is neuter not masculine. Jesus is not saying I and the Father are one person. He is saying we are one thing — one in will purpose and action. The religious leaders understood this as a claim to equality with God and reached for stones. Jesus's subsequent argument — you call humans gods in your own Scripture so why do you accuse me of blasphemy for saying I am God's Son — is not a retreat from the claim. It is a legal defense that accepts the lesser claim while pointing toward the greater reality.

No one can snatch them out of my Father's hand — the security Jesus promises his sheep is grounded in the Father's power not in the sheep's faithfulness. They will never perish not because they will never stumble or doubt but because the hand that holds them is stronger than anything that might try to take them. The security flows from the character and power of the shepherd not from the performance of the sheep.

Why It Still Matters

My sheep listen to my voice — I know them — and they follow me. The relationship Jesus describes between himself and his sheep is mutual knowledge and recognition. He knows them. They know his voice. That mutual knowing — echoing the Father knowing the Son and the Son knowing the Father — is the foundation of the security he promises. You cannot be snatched from the hand of someone who knows your name and whose voice you recognize. That is not a contractual arrangement. It is a relationship.

Many Believe Across the Jordan

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

After escaping the attempt to seize him Jesus withdraws across the Jordan to where John had been baptizing. Many people come to him. They say John never performed a sign but everything John said about this man was true. Many believed in him there.

What You Might Not Know

John never performed a sign — the people across the Jordan make a significant observation. John the Baptist did no miracles. His entire ministry was words — testimony and pointing. Yet his testimony about Jesus proved completely accurate. The people who had heard John's words and now encountered Jesus found that the words matched the reality. John's testimony without signs produced faith in Jesus that the signs in Jerusalem had not produced in the religious leaders. This contrast between the reception in Jerusalem and the reception across the Jordan frames the entire section of John's Gospel from Chapter 7 to 10.

Why It Still Matters

All that John said about this man was true — the faithfulness of testimony matters across time. John had pointed to Jesus months or years earlier. The people who heard that testimony and then encountered Jesus found it was accurate. Faithful witness to Jesus — even without signs and wonders — plants something that grows into genuine faith when the person eventually encounters Jesus directly. John's ministry was complete. His testimony was still bearing fruit after his death. That is the enduring power of faithful witness.

John Chapter 11

I Am the Resurrection and the Life

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Lazarus is sick and his sisters send word to Jesus. Jesus says the sickness will not end in death but will bring glory to God — then stays two more days before going. He tells his disciples Lazarus has died and he is glad for their sake he was not there so they may believe. When he arrives Lazarus has been in the tomb four days. Martha meets him and says if he had been there her brother would not have died but even now she knows God will give him whatever he asks. Jesus says your brother will rise again. She says she knows about the last day resurrection. Jesus says I am the resurrection and the life — whoever believes in him will live even after dying. Does she believe this? Yes Lord she says — you are the Messiah the Son of God.

What You Might Not Know

He stayed where he was for two more days — the deliberate delay is one of the most theologically significant details in Chapter 11. Jesus loved Martha Mary and Lazarus — John states this explicitly. And because he loved them he waited. The delay was not indifference or distraction. It was purposeful. A healing of a sick man would have been a sign. The raising of a man four days dead would be something of an entirely different order — the definitive sign pointing toward Jesus's own resurrection and his identity as the resurrection and the life.

Four days dead — Jewish tradition held that the soul hovered near the body for three days after death before finally departing. By the fourth day there was no question — Lazarus was genuinely dead and gone. The delay ensured that what happened at the tomb could not be explained as a near-death recovery or a premature burial. Martha's comment about the smell confirms it. Four days in a sealed tomb in a warm climate left no ambiguity.

I am the resurrection and the life — this is the fifth of the seven I am statements in John's Gospel. Jesus does not say I will cause the resurrection or I will give you life at the resurrection. He says I am the resurrection and the life. The resurrection is not an event he will produce. It is a reality he embodies. Wherever he is death does not have the final word. The statement is made to Martha in the middle of her grief before Lazarus is raised — it is a claim about his identity not just about what he is about to do.

Why It Still Matters

Do you believe this — Jesus asks Martha directly after making the most extraordinary claim in the Gospel so far. He does not ask her to understand it or to explain it. He asks if she believes it. Her answer is one of the great confessions of faith in John's Gospel — yes Lord I believe you are the Messiah the Son of God the one coming into the world. She does not fully understand what is about to happen. She believes who Jesus is. That faith — in his identity rather than in a specific outcome she can predict — is what John presents as the model of genuine belief throughout his Gospel.

Jesus Weeps

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Mary comes to Jesus and falls at his feet saying the same thing Martha said — if you had been here my brother would not have died. Jesus sees her crying and the crowd crying with her and is deeply troubled. He asks where they have laid him. They say come and see. Jesus cried. The crowd says look how much he loved him. Some ask whether the one who opened the blind man's eyes could not have kept this man from dying.

What You Might Not Know

Jesus cried — this is the shortest verse in the Bible in most English translations and one of the most theologically significant. The Greek word is edakrusen — he shed tears. It is different from the word used for the loud wailing of Mary and the crowd. Jesus wept quietly. He was not performing grief for the benefit of the crowd. He was genuinely moved — the one who already knew he was about to raise Lazarus still wept at the tomb of his friend. The knowledge of the outcome did not eliminate the feeling of the moment.

He was deeply troubled — the Greek word used twice in this passage is embrimaomai which suggests a deep agitation or groaning — some translate it as deeply moved in spirit or indignant. It is a strong physical word suggesting something that disturbs the whole person. Jesus was not observing human grief from a distance with serene detachment. He was shaken by it. Whether the trouble was grief at Lazarus's death, anger at the power of death itself, or the weight of what he was about to do — or all three — John presents it as a real visceral response.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus wept at the tomb of someone he was about to raise from the dead. The tears were not a mistake or a performance. They were real. The one who is the resurrection and the life still grieved at death's presence. That combination — knowing the outcome and still weeping at the loss — is one of the most fully human moments in John's Gospel and one of the most important for understanding who Jesus is. He does not stand outside human suffering and observe it. He enters it and weeps inside it.

Lazarus Come Out

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Jesus comes to the tomb deeply troubled. He tells them to remove the stone. Martha objects — four days dead there will be a terrible smell. Jesus says did I not tell you that if you believe you will see God's glory? They remove the stone. Jesus prays aloud to the Father — thanking him for hearing him, noting he said it for the sake of those standing there so they will believe. Then he shouts Lazarus come out. The dead man comes out still wrapped in burial cloths. Jesus tells them to unwrap him and let him go.

What You Might Not Know

Father thank you for hearing me — I know you always hear me but I said this for the sake of all these people standing here. Jesus's prayer before the tomb is unique in John's Gospel. He does not ask God to raise Lazarus. He thanks God for already having heard him — past tense. The raising is already accomplished in the will and word of the Father before it is visible in the physical event. Jesus speaks the prayer aloud not because he needs to communicate with the Father but because the people around him need to understand where the power comes from.

Lazarus come out — Jesus does not touch the tomb or the body. He shouts one command. The word come out is addressed to a specific person by name. In Chapter 10 Jesus said the good shepherd calls his own sheep by name and leads them out. Here he calls Lazarus by name out of death itself. The same voice that called the disciples to follow him, that called the blind man to wash, that called Zacchaeus down from the tree calls Lazarus out of the tomb. The voice of Jesus reaches where no other voice can go.

Unwrap him and let him go — the burial cloths that bound Lazarus are removed by human hands after the resurrection. Jesus raised him. The community unwrapped him. The miraculous and the human work together — the divine action that no one else could do and the human action that anyone could do. That pattern — God doing what only God can do and people doing what they can do — runs throughout John's Gospel.

Why It Still Matters

The raising of Lazarus is the seventh and climactic sign in John's Gospel. It is also the sign that triggers the final plot to kill Jesus. The most dramatic demonstration of his power over death is what sets in motion his own death. John presents this without irony — the one who is the resurrection and the life lays down his life as a direct consequence of demonstrating that he is the resurrection and the life. The sign and the cross are inseparable.

The Plot to Kill Jesus

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Many who witnessed the raising believe in Jesus. Some report it to the Pharisees. The council meets — if they let him continue everyone will believe and Rome will destroy their Temple and nation. Caiaphas the high priest says it is better for one man to die for the people than for the whole nation to be destroyed. John notes this was unwitting prophecy — Jesus would die for the nation and to gather all God's scattered children into one. From that day they planned to kill him. Jesus withdrew to Ephraim near the wilderness. As Passover approached people wondered if he would come to the festival. The chief priests and Pharisees ordered anyone who knew where he was to report it.

What You Might Not Know

It is better for one man to die for the people than for the whole nation to be destroyed — Caiaphas intended this as a piece of cold political calculation. Eliminate the troublemaker before he provokes a Roman crackdown. John sees in his words something Caiaphas did not intend — genuine prophecy. The high priest's office carried a tradition of prophetic speech even when the person holding it was unworthy of it. Caiaphas spoke more truly than he knew. Jesus would indeed die for the nation — and not only for the nation but to gather all God's scattered children into one.

All of God's scattered children — John's editorial note expands Caiaphas's narrow political calculation into the universal scope of the Gospel. The death Caiaphas planned as a pragmatic solution to a local problem would accomplish something of cosmic significance — the gathering of God's people from every nation into one. The high priest who plotted the crucifixion inadvertently described its purpose more accurately than he could have imagined.

Why It Still Matters

The raising of Lazarus produced two responses — belief and a plot to kill. The same event the same day the same location produced both. That division — the sign that produces faith in some and intensified opposition in others — is the pattern of Jesus's entire ministry in John's Gospel. The light that illuminates also exposes. The same truth that sets some free hardens others. John does not resolve this tension. He presents it as the reality of how the light works in a world that has learned to prefer darkness.

John Chapter 12

Mary Anoints Jesus

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Six days before Passover Jesus is at dinner in Bethany. Mary takes expensive perfume worth a year's wages and pours it on his feet then wipes them with her hair. The whole house fills with the fragrance. Judas objects — why wasn't this sold and given to the poor. John notes he said this not because he cared about the poor but because he was a thief who stole from the money bag. Jesus says leave her alone — she was keeping this for the day of my burial. The chief priests plan to kill Lazarus too because many are believing in Jesus because of him.

What You Might Not Know

Pure nard was an aromatic oil imported from the Himalayan region — extremely expensive and rare. A pint of it worth a year's wages was an extravagant gift by any measure. Mary pours it not on Jesus's head as in Matthew and Mark but on his feet — the most humble possible gesture. She then wipes his feet with her hair — an act of profound intimacy and self-giving. Her hair was her glory in that culture. She uses it as a towel at Jesus's feet.

She was keeping this for the day of my burial — whether Mary consciously intended this as burial preparation or whether Jesus is interpreting her act prophetically is left open. Either way Jesus defends her against Judas's criticism by placing her action in the context of his coming death. She has done something whose full significance she may not have understood but which Jesus recognized and honored.

The chief priests planned to kill Lazarus too — the man Jesus raised from the dead had become a walking piece of evidence that could not be silenced. His very existence was drawing people to faith. The solution the religious establishment reached for was to eliminate the evidence. The same logic that drove them to kill Jesus drove them to plan the murder of the man he had raised. Death was their answer to life.

Why It Still Matters

She did what she could — Mark's version adds this phrase that captures something John's account implies. Mary's act was not calculated or strategic. It was total. The most valuable thing she had poured out completely at Jesus's feet. Jesus's defense of her against the criticism of the practical minded is a consistent pattern — extravagant love directed toward him is never waste regardless of what it costs or what else it could have purchased.

The Triumphal Entry

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

The crowd that came for the festival hears Jesus is coming and goes out to meet him with palm branches shouting Hosanna blessed is the King of Israel. Jesus finds a young donkey and rides it into Jerusalem fulfilling Zechariah's prophecy. John notes the disciples did not understand this until after the glorification. The crowd that witnessed Lazarus's raising kept telling everyone about it. The Pharisees say the whole world has gone after him.

What You Might Not Know

Palm branches were a symbol of Jewish national identity and victory — they had been used to celebrate the Maccabean victories over a century earlier. Waving palms and shouting Hosanna — save now — was a politically charged act. The crowd was welcoming Jesus as a national liberator king. Jesus's choice of a donkey rather than a warhorse was a deliberate counter-statement — this is not the kind of king you are expecting.

His disciples did not understand this at first — John's honesty about the disciples' limited understanding during Jesus's ministry is consistent throughout his Gospel. They were participants in events whose meaning they only grasped later through the lens of the resurrection and the Spirit. John writes from that later vantage point — he can tell us what the disciples did not yet know because he is writing after they knew it.

Why It Still Matters

The whole world has gone after him — the Pharisees said this in frustration but John records it as something more. Within days Greeks will arrive asking to see Jesus — the first representatives of the Gentile world coming to him. The Pharisees' exasperated hyperbole was truer than they knew. The one riding into Jerusalem on a donkey would within a generation be known across the Roman Empire and eventually across the whole world.

The Hour Has Come

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Greeks ask to see Jesus. Philip and Andrew bring the request. Jesus announces the hour has come for the Son of Man to be glorified. Unless a grain of wheat falls and dies it remains alone — if it dies it produces many seeds. Anyone who loves their life loses it. Anyone who hates their life in this world keeps it for eternal life. A voice comes from heaven — I have glorified my name and will glorify it again. The crowd hears thunder or an angel. Jesus says the ruler of this world will be thrown out and when he is lifted up he will draw all people to himself. He tells them to walk in the light while they have it and then hides from them.

What You Might Not Know

Greeks coming to see Jesus triggers the announcement that the hour has come. Throughout John's Gospel Jesus has said my hour has not yet come — at Cana, at the Festival of Tabernacles, when attempts were made to seize him. Now when Gentiles arrive seeking him the hour arrives. The coming of the nations to Jesus — anticipated in Isaiah and throughout the prophets — signals that the moment of his glorification through death has arrived. The arrival of the Greeks is not incidental. It is the trigger.

Unless a grain of wheat falls into the ground and dies it remains alone — this is Jesus's explanation of why the hour must involve death. The single seed that refuses to die remains a single seed. The seed that dies produces a harvest beyond counting. He is describing both his own death and resurrection and the principle that governs all genuine fruitfulness in the Kingdom. Death is not the enemy of life in John's Gospel. It is the pathway to it.

Now is the time for judgment on this world — now the ruler of this world will be thrown out — the cross is not a defeat of Jesus by the powers of the world. It is the defeat of the ruler of this world by Jesus. What looks like the world's victory over Jesus is actually Jesus's victory over the world. John presents the crucifixion as the moment when the power that has held the world in darkness is broken and expelled.

Why It Still Matters

When I am lifted up from the earth I will draw all people to myself — lifted up in John refers simultaneously to being raised on the cross and to being exalted in glory. The cross that looks like shame and defeat is the very thing that draws all people — not some people, not one nation, but all people — to Jesus. The instrument of execution becomes the instrument of universal invitation. That paradox is at the heart of John's theology of the cross.

Jesus Summarizes His Mission

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Despite all the signs many do not believe — fulfilling Isaiah's words about blinded eyes and hardened hearts. Even many leaders believe but will not confess it publicly for fear of being put out of the synagogue — they love human praise more than God's. Jesus cries out his final public summary — believing in him is believing in the Father, seeing him is seeing the Father, he came as light so no one who believes stays in darkness. He did not come to judge but to save. His words will judge on the last day. He speaks exactly what the Father told him to say.

What You Might Not Know

Isaiah said this because he saw Jesus's glory — John makes a remarkable claim about the vision Isaiah had in the Temple in Isaiah 6 where he saw the Lord seated on a throne high and exalted. John says Isaiah saw Jesus's glory. The pre-incarnate Christ was present in the throne room vision that commissioned Isaiah. This is one of the boldest claims about Jesus's pre-existence and divine identity in John's Gospel outside of the prologue.

They loved praise from people more than praise from God — this diagnosis of the leaders who believed but would not confess is one of the most penetrating observations in John's Gospel. Their faith was genuine enough to produce private conviction but not strong enough to override social calculation. The cost of public identification with Jesus — expulsion from the synagogue — was too high. Their belief stayed private and therefore stayed incomplete. Faith that cannot be spoken is faith that has not fully landed.

Why It Still Matters

I have come into the world as a light so that no one who believes in me will stay in darkness — this is Jesus's final public statement before the passion narrative begins. John's Gospel opened with the light shining in the darkness. It closes its public ministry section with the same image. The light has come. The invitation is open. What happens next — the cross the resurrection the empty tomb — is the fulfillment of everything the light was always pointing toward.

John Chapter 13

Jesus Washes His Disciples' Feet

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Before the Passover meal Jesus knows his hour has come. Knowing the Father has put everything under his control and that he came from God and is returning to God — he gets up removes his outer robe wraps a towel around his waist and washes his disciples' feet. Peter objects. Jesus says unless I wash you you have no part with me. Peter asks for hands and head too. Jesus says those who have bathed only need feet washed — and you are clean though not all of you. After finishing he explains — I your Lord and Teacher have washed your feet so you should wash each other's feet. Do as I have done.

What You Might Not Know

Foot washing was the work of the lowest household servant — in a Jewish household it was considered beneath even a Jewish slave and was assigned to Gentile slaves or done voluntarily by disciples for their rabbi as an act of extreme devotion. Jesus reverses the entire structure — the rabbi and Lord becomes the one washing feet. John places this at the moment when Jesus knew the Father had put everything under his control. The foot washing is not an act of weakness or humility in the sense of self-deprecation. It is an act of sovereign love by someone who knew exactly who he was and what he had.

Unless I wash you you have no part with me — Peter's refusal and Jesus's response reveal that the foot washing is more than a lesson in servant leadership. It is a participation in something Jesus does — a receiving of his ministry of cleansing that is necessary for relationship with him. Those who cannot receive from Jesus — who insist on maintaining their dignity or independence — have no part with him. The willingness to be served by Jesus is as important as the willingness to serve.

You are clean though not every one of you — Jesus knows Judas is about to betray him. He washes Judas's feet anyway. The one who will hand him over to death receives the same act of service as the one who will confess him as Lord. The foot washing is not conditional on loyalty or worthiness. It is given to all twelve including the one whose heart has already turned.

Why It Still Matters

I have given you an example — the foot washing is not a one-time ceremony to be commemorated. It is a pattern to be reproduced. The shape of Jesus's life — knowing who you are and what you have and using it in service of others rather than to elevate yourself — is the shape his followers are called to take on. You'll be blessed if you do them. The blessing is not in knowing the principle. It is in practicing it.

The Betrayal Foretold

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Jesus becomes troubled and says one of them will betray him. The disciples look at each other. The beloved disciple is reclining next to Jesus. Peter signals him to ask who it is. The beloved disciple leans back and asks. Jesus says it is the one he gives bread to after dipping it. He gives it to Judas. Satan enters Judas. Jesus tells him to do quickly what he is going to do. What You Might Not Know

Giving a piece of dipped bread to someone was an act of honor at a Jewish meal — the host would select a choice morsel and offer it to a guest as a sign of favor. Jesus identifies his betrayer by giving him a gesture of honor. The one who receives the most intimate sign of the host's favor is the one who will hand him over. That combination — receiving honor and betraying the one who gives it — is the full weight of what Judas does.

Satan entered him — John notes that the devil had already put the idea of betrayal into Judas's mind earlier in the chapter. The entrance of Satan happens at the moment Judas receives the bread from Jesus and makes his final decision. The progression from idea to decision to full possession is tracked carefully. Judas was not suddenly seized against his will. He had been entertaining this path and at this moment he chose it completely.

It was night — John's two-word conclusion to Judas's departure is one of the most theologically loaded short sentences in the Gospel. Judas walks out of the light into the darkness — literally into the night, and spiritually into the darkness that John has been describing since the prologue. The light shines in the darkness and Judas chose the darkness. Night is where this chapter ends and where the betrayal will be carried out.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus told him what you are about to do do it quickly — Jesus does not stop Judas. He releases him to do what he has chosen. The sovereignty of Jesus over his own death is consistent throughout John's Gospel — no one takes his life from him he lays it down of his own accord. Even the betrayal happens within his knowledge and with his release. That does not make Judas less responsible. It means that even the worst act of treachery in human history could not derail the purpose of God.

The New Commandment

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

After Judas leaves Jesus says now the Son of Man is glorified and God is glorified in him. He will only be with them a little longer — where he is going they cannot come. He gives them a new commandment — love one another as he has loved them. This is how everyone will know they are his disciples — if they love one another.

What You Might Not Know

Now the Son of Man is glorified — the departure of Judas into the night triggers this announcement. The betrayal that will lead to the cross has been set in motion. Jesus describes this as glorification. In John's Gospel the cross is not a defeat that leads to glorification — the cross itself is the glorification. The moment the process toward the cross begins is the moment glory arrives. That complete inversion of the ordinary meaning of glory is one of John's most distinctive theological contributions.

A new commandment — the commandment to love is not new in the sense of being previously unknown. Leviticus 19 already commanded love of neighbor. What is new is the standard — as I have loved you. The measure of the love required is no longer love your neighbor as yourself. It is love one another as I have loved you. The cross redefines what love means and sets the standard that his followers are called to meet.

This is how everyone will know — the identifying mark of Jesus's disciples is not correct doctrine or religious practice or ethnic identity. It is love for one another of the kind Jesus demonstrated. That is the sign by which the world is meant to recognize the community he is building. Whether the church in any generation has been recognizable by that sign is one of the most searching questions the passage raises.

Why It Still Matters

Just as I have loved you — the as I have loved you sets a standard that cannot be reduced to sentiment or preference. Jesus's love washed feet includes Judas and lays down its life. That is the love his followers are commanded to practice toward one another. Not the love that comes naturally when people are pleasant and agreeable. The love that serves the undeserving includes the difficult and gives itself completely regardless of the cost.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

What You Might Not Know

Peter's declaration — I will lay down my life for you — uses the exact same language Jesus used in Chapter 10 about the good shepherd laying down his life for the sheep. You will follow me later — Jesus's word to Peter is not only a prediction of the denial. It is a promise of eventual following. Peter will follow — just not now and not in the way he imagines. The later following will cost him everything including ultimately his own life according to the early church tradition. Jesus sees past the failure to the faithfulness that lies on the other side of it. That vision of who Peter will become — spoken before the denial — is the grace that made the restoration possible.

John Chapter 14

I Am the Way the Truth and the Life

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Jesus tells his disciples not to let their hearts be troubled — trust in God and trust in him. There are many rooms in his Father's house and he is going to prepare a place for them. He will come back and take them to be with him. Thomas says they do not know where he is going so how can they know the way. Jesus says I am the way the truth and the life — no one comes to the Father except through him. Philip asks to be shown the Father. Jesus says anyone who has seen him has seen the Father. He and the Father are in each other. Those who believe will do the works he has been doing and even greater ones because he is going to the Father. He will do whatever they ask in his name.

What You Might Not Know

Many rooms in my Father's house — the word translated rooms is mone in Greek meaning dwelling places or abiding places. It is related to the word meno — to remain or abide — which is one of the key theological words in John's Gospel. The Father's house is not a temporary way station. It is a place of permanent abiding. Jesus is going to prepare permanent dwelling places — not guest rooms but homes.

I am the way the truth and the life — this is the sixth of the seven I am statements in John's Gospel. All three nouns belong together and interpret each other. He is the way — the path to the Father. He is the truth — the reality that the path leads to and that the path itself embodies. He is the life — the nature of the destination and the sustenance for the journey. You cannot separate the three. The way is not a method or a religion. It is a person.

No one comes to the Father except through me — this is the most exclusive claim in John's Gospel and one of the most debated statements in all of Scripture. In context Jesus is speaking to his disciples the night before his death about where he is going and how they will follow. He is not primarily making a statement about world religions. He is telling his followers that the access to the Father that they have experienced through him is not one option among many — it is the only access there is because he is the only one who has come from the Father and returned to him.

Anyone who believes in me will do the same works and even greater things — greater works has puzzled readers. How can anyone do greater works than Jesus? The greater works are not greater in power or significance than the resurrection of Lazarus or the healing of the blind. They are greater in scope — the Gospel spreading across the whole world through the work of his followers empowered by the Spirit is greater in reach than what Jesus accomplished in three years in one small region.

Why It Still Matters

Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father — Philip's request show us the Father was the deepest longing of the human heart expressed in one sentence. Jesus's answer is that the longing has already been fulfilled without Philip realizing it. Every conversation every healing every meal every moment of Jesus's three years with his disciples was an encounter with the Father. That answer does not make the Father less than the Father. It makes Jesus more than a teacher or a prophet. He is the visible face of the invisible God.

The Promise of the Holy Spirit

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Jesus promises another Helper — the Spirit of truth — who will be with them forever. The world cannot receive him but the disciples know him because he lives with them and will be in them. Jesus will not leave them as orphans. Because he lives they will live too. Anyone who has his commands and obeys them loves him and will be loved by the Father. The Father and Son will come and make their home with anyone who loves Jesus and obeys his teaching. The Holy Spirit will teach them everything and remind them of all Jesus has said. He leaves them his peace — not as the world gives. The ruler of this world is coming but has no power over him. Get up — let's leave.

What You Might Not Know

Another Helper — the Greek word is Paraclete — parakletos — meaning one called alongside to help. It carries the sense of an advocate in a legal setting, a counselor, a comforter and a helper all at once. The word another is significant — Jesus has been their Paraclete, their helper and advocate. The Spirit will be another of the same kind. The Spirit's ministry is not different in character from Jesus's ministry — it is the continuation of the same presence in a different mode.

I won't leave you as orphans — the word orphan describes someone who has lost their parent and protector — someone alone and vulnerable in a world that does not care for them. Jesus uses this word to describe what it would mean for the disciples if he left without sending the Spirit. His departure without the Spirit would be abandonment. The sending of the Spirit is the guarantee that his departure is not abandonment — the presence continues in a new form.

My peace I give you — not as the world gives — the peace Jesus offers is fundamentally different from the peace the world offers. The world's peace depends on favorable circumstances — the absence of conflict, security, prosperity. Jesus offers peace that exists independently of circumstances — a settled orientation toward God that holds even when everything externally is falling apart. Don't let your hearts be troubled and don't be afraid — he says this knowing exactly what is coming in the next few hours.

The Father is greater than I am — this statement has been discussed by theologians for two thousand years. In context Jesus is explaining why the disciples should be glad he is going to the Father rather than sad. He is returning to the source from which he came. The statement reflects his position in his incarnate state — sent from the Father, obedient to the Father, returning to the Father — rather than a claim about eternal ontological inferiority.

Why It Still Matters

We will come to them and make our home with them — the Father and Son making their home in the person who loves Jesus and obeys his teaching is one of the most intimate promises in John's Gospel. The same word for home is mone — the dwelling place Jesus went to prepare in the Father's house. The dwelling goes both ways. He prepares a place for them in the Father's house and the Father and Son make their home in them. The mutual indwelling that characterizes the relationship between Father and Son extends to include everyone who loves and obeys Jesus.

The Work of the Holy Spirit

What Is Happening Here

Jesus warns the disciples they will be thrown out of synagogues and that people who kill them will think they are serving God. He is telling them now so when it happens they will remember he warned them. He is going to the one who sent him. He tells them it is better that he goes — unless he goes the Helper will not come. The Spirit of truth will guide them into all truth speaking only what he hears from the Father and making known what belongs to Jesus.

What You Might Not Know

Anyone who kills you will think they are doing God a service — Jesus is not speaking hypothetically. Within a generation of this conversation Stephen would be stoned, James would be executed, Peter and Paul would be martyred, and Christians across the Roman Empire would be killed by people who genuinely believed they were purifying society or honoring their gods. The prediction was precise and it was fulfilled. Jesus told them ahead of time so that when it happened their faith would be strengthened rather than destroyed by the shock of it.

It is better for you that I go away — this statement must have been incomprehensible to the disciples in that moment. Better that Jesus leaves? The physical presence of Jesus — the one who calmed storms and raised the dead — being replaced by an invisible Spirit seemed like an obvious loss. Jesus insists it is a gain. The Spirit will be in them not merely with them. The localized presence of Jesus in one body in one place is replaced by the universal presence of the Spirit in every believer everywhere simultaneously.

He will prove the world wrong about sin righteousness and judgment — the Spirit's convicting work operates on three levels. About sin — because the world does not believe in Jesus and unbelief is the root sin. About righteousness — because Jesus is going to the Father and his departure vindicates his righteous character against all the accusations made against him. About judgment — because the ruler of this world has already been condemned at the cross and the Spirit makes that verdict known.

Why It Still Matters

He will guide you into all truth — the promise is not that the Spirit will give new revelation beyond Jesus but that he will lead the disciples into the full understanding of what Jesus said and did. The Spirit's ministry is not to add to Jesus but to illuminate him — taking what is mine and making it known to you. Everything the Spirit does points back to Jesus and forward into deeper understanding of what Jesus accomplished. That is the ongoing work of the Spirit in the church in every generation.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Jesus tells them in a little while they will not see him and then after a little while they will see him again. The disciples are confused and discuss it among themselves. Jesus explains — they will weep and mourn while the world celebrates but their sadness will turn to joy. Like a woman in labor whose pain is forgotten when the child is born their grief will become joy that no one can take away. On that day they will ask in the Father's name directly and receive so their joy will be complete.

What You Might Not Know

A little while you will not see me and then after a little while you will see me — the disciples are confused because Jesus seems to be describing two different disappearances and reappearances. The most natural reading is that the first little while refers to the period between the crucifixion and the resurrection — three days during which they will not see him. The second little while refers to the resurrection appearances when they will see him again. The labor and birth image that follows supports this — the pain of the crucifixion and the joy of the resurrection.

Your grief will turn to joy — the Greek word used is not that grief will be replaced by joy as if the grief simply ends. The grief itself will be transformed into joy. The same events that produced the weeping — the cross the death the burial — will be seen differently after the resurrection and will become the very source of the deepest joy. The thing that broke them will be the thing that completes them. That transformation is the resurrection's effect on everything that preceded it.

No one will take that joy away from you — the joy Jesus promises after the resurrection is permanent and unassailable. It is not dependent on circumstances because it is grounded in a historical event that cannot be undone. The resurrection happened. That fact cannot be taken away. The joy that flows from it therefore cannot ultimately be taken away either — even by suffering persecution or death.

Why It Still Matters

Ask and you will receive so that your joy will be complete — Jesus connects answered prayer directly to complete joy. The completeness of joy he describes is not a passive state of contentment. It is the active fullness that comes from living in ongoing relationship with the Father through prayer — asking receiving and experiencing the Father's generosity repeatedly. Joy in John's Gospel is not a feeling to be cultivated. It is the natural result of abiding in the vine receiving from the Father and bearing fruit that lasts.

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Jesus says the time is coming when he will speak plainly about the Father. He came from the Father into the world and is leaving the world to go back to the Father. The disciples say now he is speaking clearly and they believe he came from God. The Father himself loves you — Jesus makes a remarkable statement here. He does not say the Father will love them because Jesus intercedes for them or because they are covered by his merit. He says the Father himself directly personally loves them — because they have loved Jesus and believed he came from God. The love of the Father for the disciples is not mediated or conditional on Jesus's ongoing pleading. It is direct and personal and based on their relationship with Jesus.

You will be scattered each to his own home and leave me alone — Jesus predicts the disciples' abandonment of him at the arrest with complete accuracy. Within hours of this declaration of faith — now we believe you came from God — they will all flee. Jesus knows this and tells them. He does not respond to their declaration with rebuke or with a test. He accepts it and immediately tells them the truth about what is coming. Their faith is real even though it will fail under pressure. The failure does not invalidate the faith.

I have defeated the world — the verb tense is perfect — a completed action with ongoing results. Jesus says this before the cross before the resurrection. From his perspective the victory is already accomplished. What is about to happen in the next few hours is not a defeat that will need to be reversed by the resurrection. It is the means by which the already-certain victory becomes historically visible.

Why It Still Matters

In this world you will have trouble — be brave — I have defeated the world. Jesus does not promise his followers exemption from trouble. He promises them courage in the middle of it grounded in a victory that has already been won. The trouble is real. The victory is also real. The courage he calls for is not the courage of someone who does not know the danger. It is the courage of someone who knows both the danger and the one who has already overcome it.

John Chapter 17

Jesus Prays for Himself

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 17
What Is Happening Here

Jesus looks up to heaven and prays. The time has come — glorify the Son so the Son can glorify the Father. He has been given authority over all people to give eternal life to everyone the Father has given him. Eternal life is defined — to know the only true God and Jesus the Messiah he sent. He has brought glory on earth by finishing the work he was given. Now he asks to be brought back into the glory he had with the Father before the world was created.

What You Might Not Know

This prayer — John 17 — is the longest recorded prayer of Jesus in any Gospel. It is sometimes called the High Priestly Prayer because Jesus is interceding for his disciples and for all future believers the way a high priest interceded for the people on the Day of Atonement. He prays this prayer before the cross — not after. He intercedes from a position of complete trust that the Father will accomplish what he is asking rather than from a position of having already seen the outcome.

This is eternal life — to know you the only true God and Jesus the Messiah you sent — this is one of the most important definitions in John's Gospel. Eternal life is not primarily a duration — living forever. It is a quality of relationship — knowing God and knowing Jesus. The Greek word for know is ginosko — the kind of knowing that comes from personal relationship and experience rather than intellectual information about someone. Eternal life begins now in the knowing not only after death in the living forever.

The glory I had with you before the world was created — Jesus's prayer here reaches back before the incarnation to his pre-existent glory with the Father. He is asking to return to what he had before — the glory he set aside when he became flesh. John's prologue opened with in the beginning was the Word and the Word was with God. Jesus is now asking to return to that beginning — the eternal fellowship with the Father that preceded creation itself.

Why It Still Matters

I have brought you glory on earth by finishing the work you gave me to do — Jesus says this before the cross. The work is finished in his prayer before it is finished on the cross. That confidence — treating the not-yet-completed work as already done — is the faith of someone who knows with absolute certainty that the Father will accomplish what he has begun. The same confidence will appear in his final word from the cross — it is finished. What he prays here he declares there.

Jesus Prays for His Disciples

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 17
What Is Happening Here

What You Might Not Know

Protect them by the power of your name — the name you gave me — in the ancient world a name was not merely an identifier. It carried the character and authority of the person it belonged to. To be kept in the name of God meant to be kept within the sphere of God's character and authority — within who God is. Jesus is asking that the disciples be held within the reality of who the Father is — the same reality that has held Jesus throughout his ministry.

Not one of them has been lost except the one destined for destruction so that Scripture would be fulfilled — the loss of Judas is acknowledged within the prayer without bitterness or confusion. Jesus does not present Judas's betrayal as a failure of his protection. He presents it as the fulfillment of Scripture — something that happened within the purposes of God rather than outside them. The one who kept all the others kept them by the same sovereign purpose that allowed Judas to follow his chosen path.

Make them holy by the truth — your word is truth — the word holy in Greek is hagiazo meaning to set apart for sacred use. Jesus is not asking for the disciples to become morally perfect. He is asking for them to be consecrated — set apart for the sacred purpose he is sending them into the world to fulfill. The instrument of that consecration is truth — the word of God that Jesus has given them and that the Spirit will continue to illuminate in them.

Why It Still Matters

I am not asking you to take them out of the world but to protect them from the evil one — Jesus deliberately leaves his disciples in the world. The place of danger is also the place of mission. Removal from the world would mean safety but it would also mean the end of the witness. The prayer is not for a protected enclave separated from the world but for people sent into the world who are kept by the Father's power while they are there. That is the shape of the Christian life Jesus prays for — in the world, not of it, protected within it, sent through it.

What You Might Not Know

I pray for everyone who will believe in me through their message — Jesus prays for people who do not yet exist. The prayer reaches forward across twenty centuries to everyone who will come to faith through the witness of the first disciples — which includes every Christian in every generation including the present one. This prayer was prayed for you specifically if you believe in Jesus. That reality is worth sitting with — on the night before his crucifixion Jesus was thinking about and praying for people two thousand years in the future.

I have given them the glory you gave me so they can be one — the unity Jesus prays for is not organizational uniformity or institutional merger. It is the same kind of unity that exists between the Father and the Son — a unity of love purpose and mutual indwelling. The glory that is given to believers as the basis of this unity is not human achievement or theological agreement. It is a participation in the divine life itself — the love that flows between Father and Son extended to include those who belong to them both.

So the world will know that you sent me and have loved them just as you have loved me — this statement is one of the most astonishing in the entire Gospel. The Father loves the disciples with the same love with which he loves the Son. Not a lesser version of that love adapted for creatures. The same love. The unity of believers is meant to be the evidence to the world of both the Father's sending of Jesus and the Father's love for the people Jesus gathered.

Why It Still Matters

I want those you have given me to be with me where I am and to see my glory — the final request of Jesus's prayer is not for the disciples' effectiveness or protection or holiness. It is for their presence with him. The ultimate goal of everything — the incarnation the cross the resurrection the sending of the Spirit — is that the people the Father gave him would be where he is and see his glory. The mission ends not in successful evangelism or transformed societies but in the eternal presence of his people with him. That is what he wanted most.

The Arrest in the Garden

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 18
What Is Happening Here

Jesus and his disciples cross the Kidron Valley to a garden. Judas leads a group of soldiers and officials there. Jesus steps forward and asks who they are looking for. Jesus of Nazareth. I am he — and they step back and fall to the ground. He asks again — if you are looking for me let these others go. Peter draws a sword and cuts off the servant Malchus's right ear. What You Might Not Know

When Jesus said I am he they stepped back and fell to the ground — this detail is unique to John and it is theologically significant. The same I am that identified God to Moses at the burning bush and that Jesus used throughout John's Gospel produces a physical response in the soldiers and officials who came to arrest him. They did not simply stop — they fell. Jesus was not arrested because he was overpowered. He was arrested because he chose to be. The power differential is made visible before the arrest proceeds.

If you are looking for me let these others go — this happened so the words he had spoken would come true — I have not lost one of those you gave me. John connects the protective release of the disciples to the promise Jesus made in Chapter 17. Even at the moment of his own arrest his first concern is for the safety of the disciples. The shepherd lays down his life and in doing so ensures the sheep escape.

Should I not drink the cup the Father has given me — the cup is the Old Testament image for the experience of God's judgment and wrath — the cup that must be drunk completely. Jesus's rebuke of Peter is not just a rejection of violence as a strategy. It is a statement of willing acceptance of everything the cross involves. The arrest is not a disaster to be prevented. It is a cup to be received from the Father's hand.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus knew everything that was about to happen to him and stepped forward — John's consistent emphasis on Jesus's sovereign knowledge and initiative throughout the passion narrative is deliberate. He is not a victim of circumstances or of superior force. He is the good shepherd who lays down his life of his own accord. Every step toward the cross in John's Gospel is a chosen step. That changes the meaning of everything that follows.

Annas was the father-in-law of Caiaphas and had been high priest from 6 to 15 AD before being deposed by the Romans. Jesus being taken first to Annas reveals the family network and political structure that was driving the prosecution. This was not a spontaneous popular uprising against Jesus. It was cold and they had made a fire — John's specific detail about the cold and the charcoal fire is the kind of detail an eyewitness remembers. Peter warming himself at a fire while Jesus is being interrogated inside creates a visual contrast — the one who said he would lay down his life standing at a fire with the servants of the high priest trying to stay warm while his Lord faces his accusers alone inside. The man whose relative Peter had attacked with a sword was asking him directly. The denial at that moment required deliberate choice under the most personal pressure.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus before Annas said I have spoken openly — ask those who heard me. Peter outside at the fire said I am not. The contrast between Jesus's consistent honest self-disclosure and Peter's three denials is placed side by side in John's account deliberately. The one on trial speaks the truth under pressure. The one who is safe outside lies under far less pressure. The courage required to tell the truth when it costs everything is precisely what Peter did not yet have — and what the resurrection and the Spirit would eventually produce in him.

They did not enter the palace to avoid ceremonial uncleanness — entering a Gentile dwelling made a Jew ceremonially unclean and would have prevented them from eating the Passover. The same people who are scrupulous about not defiling themselves before Passover are handing over the Passover Lamb to be killed. The external religious observance and the internal moral reality could not be more completely disconnected.

My kingdom is not of this world — Jesus's statement to Pilate is not a claim that his kingdom is purely spiritual or otherworldly in the sense of having no relevance to earthly reality. It is a claim about the origin and nature of his kingdom. It does not derive its authority from military force political maneuvering or popular uprising. It comes from another place — from the Father. That origin is what makes it unlike every earthly kingdom Pilate has ever encountered.

What is truth — Pilate's question is one of the most famous in the Gospels. He asks it and immediately walks away without waiting for an answer. The irony is complete — he is asking the question of the one who said I am the truth in Chapter 14. The answer was standing in front of him and he turned his back on it. Whether his question was philosophical cynicism weary pragmatism or genuine searching John does not say. He simply records that Pilate asked and left.

Why It Still Matters

I find no basis for any charge against him — Pilate's declaration of innocence is the first of several in John's passion narrative. The Roman governor who has the authority to execute Jesus keeps declaring him innocent and keeps failing to act on that declaration. The gap between knowing what is right and doing it — which is the gap Pilate lives in throughout Chapters 18 and 19 — is one of the most honest portraits of moral failure in the entire Gospel.

What Is Happening Here

What You Might Not Know

Here is the man — Pilate's declaration when he brings Jesus out wearing the crown of thorns and purple robe is in Greek Idou ho anthropos — behold the man. It has been read on multiple levels throughout Christian history. Pilate intends it as a statement of pitiful harmlessness — look at this beaten man surely he is no threat. John intends the reader to hear something deeper — here is the man, the human being in the fullest sense, the one in whom humanity reaches its truest expression. The crown of thorns and purple robe meant as mockery become in John's account the inadvertent coronation of the true king.

You would have no power over me if it weren't given to you from above — Jesus's response to Pilate's claim of power is one of the most important statements in the passion narrative. He does not dispute Pilate's authority. He locates its source. All authority comes from above — which means even this moment of apparent defeat is within the sovereign purpose of God. The one who handed Jesus over — Caiaphas or Judas or both — bears greater guilt because they acted with more knowledge and deliberate intent. Pilate's guilt is real but lesser because his authority was given not self-generated.

We have no king but Caesar — the chief priests' declaration is the final apostasy of the religious establishment in John's Gospel. Israel's entire history was built on the confession that God alone was their king — the prophets had repeatedly called them back from making human kings their ultimate allegiance. In this moment the chief priests formally renounce that confession. To pressure Pilate into crucifying Jesus they declare Caesar their king. The irony is complete and devastating.

Why It Still Matters

Pilate tried repeatedly to release Jesus and repeatedly failed to act on his own verdict. The progression from I find no basis for a charge to handing him over to be crucified is a portrait of moral collapse under political pressure. Each attempt to release Jesus was met with a new escalation from the crowd — and each time Pilate retreated. The final threat — you are no friend of Caesar — was the one he could not withstand. His career his position his security mattered more than the innocent man standing in front of him. That calculation has been made by people in positions of authority in every generation since.

The sign in Aramaic Latin and Greek — the three languages of the ancient world at that time and place. Aramaic was the language of the Jewish people. Latin was the language of Roman government and law. Greek was the language of commerce and culture across the Mediterranean world. Pilate's inscription announcing Jesus as King of the Jews was posted in every language that mattered — an unintentional proclamation to the whole known world. The chief priests wanted it changed. The seamless garment woven in one piece from top to bottom — the high priest's garment in the Old Testament was described as woven without seam. The soldiers' decision not to tear it and to cast lots for it fulfills Psalm 22 — the psalm Jesus may have been reciting from the cross. John sees in every detail of the crucifixion the fulfillment of Scripture — not as a script being mechanically followed but as the deep pattern of God's purposes finding their expression in these specific events.

Woman here is your son — here is your mother — Jesus's provision for his mother from the cross is one of the most tender moments in John's passion narrative. In the middle of his own suffering his concern is for her welfare after he is gone. He entrusts her to the beloved disciple — the one who has stayed. From that time on this disciple took her into his own home. The community that forms around the cross begins with this act of mutual care entrusted by the dying Jesus.

Why It Still Matters

What I have written I have written — Pilate's refusal to change the inscription was a small act of defiance against the chief priests born probably from irritation and wounded pride rather than theological conviction. Yet John preserves it because the inscription was true — more true than Pilate intended and in a different sense than the chief priests feared. The king of the Jews was being crowned on a cross. The title they wanted removed was the title that defined everything happening on that hill.

What Is Happening Here

It is finished — the Greek word is tetelestai — a single word that was used in the ancient world on commercial receipts to mean paid in full. It was stamped on documents when a debt had been completely discharged. Jesus's final word from the cross in John is not a cry of defeat or exhaustion. It is a declaration of completion — the work the Father gave him to do is fully accomplished. The debt is paid. Nothing remains undone. In Chapter 17 he prayed I have finished the work you gave me to do — now from the cross he declares it complete.

A hyssop branch — hyssop was the plant used to apply the blood of the Passover lamb to the doorposts in Exodus 12. John's detail about the hyssop branch connecting the drink offered to Jesus at his death to the Passover ritual is characteristic of his theological precision throughout the passion narrative. Jesus is the Passover lamb — he dies at the hour when the Passover lambs were being slaughtered in the Temple and hyssop appears at his death as it appeared at the first Passover.

Blood and water came out — John emphasizes this detail and insists on its eyewitness reliability. Medically the separation of blood and water from a wound to the chest cavity can indicate that death has already occurred — the blood has begun to separate. John may not have understood the medical mechanism but he recorded what he saw accurately. The detail also carries theological weight — blood and water pointing toward the sacraments of Communion and Baptism that flow from the death of Jesus.

Why It Still Matters

Not one of his bones will be broken — the Scripture fulfilled by the soldiers' decision not to break Jesus's legs is Exodus 12 and Numbers 9 about the Passover lamb — no bone of it shall be broken. John is presenting the crucifixion as the ultimate Passover. The lamb whose blood protected Israel from death in Egypt was a sign pointing toward the Lamb of God whose death protects all who believe from eternal death. The soldiers who chose not to break his legs did not know they were fulfilling a fifteen hundred year old requirement for the Passover sacrifice.

Joseph and Nicodemus — two secret disciples who had not been able to follow Jesus publicly during his ministry — came forward at the moment of maximum danger when all the public disciples had fled. The cross produced in them what the miracles and teachings had not — open public identification with Jesus regardless of the cost. Sometimes the thing that finally breaks through the fear and brings a person into the open is not the evidence of power but the evidence of love. They wrapped his body with their own hands. That act of love was the fullest confession either of them had yet made.

What Is Happening Here

Mary Magdalene comes to the tomb while it is still dark and finds the stone moved. She runs to Peter and the beloved disciple and says they have taken the Lord. Both run to the tomb. What You Might Not Know

While it was still dark — Mary comes in darkness just as Nicodemus came at night and just as Judas went out into the night. The darkness of the early morning is the last darkness of the Gospel — the resurrection is about to break it permanently. She comes expecting to find a sealed tomb and finds the stone removed. Her first interpretation is not resurrection but theft — they have taken the Lord. The empty tomb by itself does not produce faith. It produces questions.

The head cloth was folded up by itself in a different place — this detail has generated much discussion. A grave robber would not stop to fold a cloth. The orderly arrangement of the burial cloths — lying there as if the body had simply passed through them and the head cloth folded separately — suggests not a hasty removal of a body but a deliberate and unhurried departure. Peter sees and wonders. The beloved disciple sees and believes. The same evidence produces different responses.

He saw and believed — John notes that the beloved disciple believed at the sight of the empty tomb and the burial cloths. He also notes immediately that they still did not understand from Scripture that Jesus had to rise. The belief that came from seeing the empty tomb was real but incomplete — it preceded the full understanding that came later through opened Scripture and direct encounter with the risen Jesus. Faith in John's Gospel grows — it is not always born fully formed.

Why It Still Matters

The disciples went back home — after seeing the empty tomb they simply went home. They did not announce the resurrection or celebrate or gather the community. They went home uncertain and wondering. The resurrection had happened but they did not yet know what to do with it. That gap — between the event occurring and the understanding of what it means — is honestly preserved in John's account. The meaning of the resurrection unfolded gradually through encounter testimony and opened Scripture not all at once in a single moment of revelation.

Do not hold on to me — the Greek word is haptomai which can mean touch or cling or hold on to. Jesus is not saying do not touch me as if he is untouchable. The relationship is about to be transformed by the ascension and the sending of the Spirit into something deeper and more permanent than physical proximity. Let go of what was so you can receive what is coming.

Why It Still Matters

Mary Magdalene is the first witness to the resurrection in John's Gospel — the first human being to see and recognize the risen Jesus. She was a woman whose testimony was not considered legally valid in first century Jewish culture. If the resurrection story were invented the inventors would not have chosen a woman as the primary witness. The fact that all four Gospels agree on this detail — that women were the first witnesses — is one of the strongest arguments that the accounts are reporting what actually happened rather than constructing a persuasive story.

The doors were locked — Jesus appears in a locked room. John does not explain how. He simply states it. Jesus came through the locked doors and the first thing he said was peace. The one they had abandoned and whose execution they had feared was standing in their locked room offering peace. That is the shape of the resurrection's first word to the frightened community.

He breathed on them and said receive the Holy Spirit — this action deliberately echoes Genesis 2 where God breathed into the nostrils of the man he formed from dust and he became a living being. The new creation begins in this upper room with the same gesture that began the first creation. The risen Jesus breathing the Spirit into his disciples is John's version of Pentecost — a quieter and more intimate account than Luke's in Acts but pointing to the same reality.

If you forgive anyone's sins they are forgiven — the authority to forgive and retain sins given to the disciples has been interpreted differently across Christian traditions. What is clear in John's context is that the community Jesus is forming is meant to be the place where the forgiveness he accomplished on the cross is made available to people. The disciples sent as the Father sent the Son carry the message and ministry of reconciliation into the world.

Why It Still Matters

As the Father sent me I am sending you — the mission of the disciples is patterned on the mission of Jesus. He was sent from the Father into the world — they are sent from Jesus into the world. The same shape — leaving the place of safety entering the place of need carrying the message of the one who sent you — defines both missions. The resurrection does not end the mission. It launches it. The disciples who had been hiding behind locked doors are commissioned through those same locked doors to go into the world they had been hiding from.

What Is Happening Here

Thomas was not present. The others tell him they have seen the Lord. He says he will not believe unless he sees and touches the nail marks and puts his hand in Jesus's side. What You Might Not Know

Thomas has been called Doubting Thomas for two thousand years but John's account is more sympathetic than that label suggests. Thomas was not present at the first appearance — he missed it through no fault described in the text. When told by the other disciples he responded with the same skepticism they would have shown if they had not seen. His demand for physical evidence was not exceptional — it was the standard the others had already received. Jesus does not rebuke Thomas for his doubt. He meets it exactly where it is and offers precisely what Thomas asked for.

My Lord and my God — Thomas's confession is the climax of John's Gospel and the highest Christological statement made by any human being in any of the four Gospels. No one else in John calls Jesus God directly to his face. The one who doubted most explicitly makes the most complete confession. Thomas moved from I will not believe to my Lord and my God in a single encounter. Jesus accepted the confession without correction or qualification — confirming that the title was appropriate.

Blessed are those who believe without seeing — this beatitude is addressed to everyone who will ever read John's Gospel. The readers of John cannot see the risen Jesus physically. They are in the position of those who must believe on the basis of testimony. Jesus pronounces a specific blessing on them — on us. The faith that comes without physical sight is not inferior faith. It is the faith Jesus specifically blesses.

Why It Still Matters

Thomas's story matters because his doubt was honest and his confession was complete. He did not pretend to believe when he did not. He stated his condition plainly. Jesus met him in that condition and brought him through it to the fullest confession in the Gospel. The path from honest doubt to genuine faith — taken honestly and without pretense — is honored in John's account. The blessing Jesus pronounces is not on those who never doubted but on those who came to faith through whatever path brought them there.

The Purpose of This Book

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 20
What Is Happening Here

John's statement of purpose is one of the clearest authorial statements of intent in ancient literature. He is not writing a complete biography or a historical chronicle. Why It Still Matters

These are written so you can believe — the you is every reader in every generation. John wrote for the person holding this text right now. The signs he selected the conversations he recorded the details he preserved — all of it was chosen with you in mind. The question the Gospel has been pressing toward from the first verse is the question it ends with — do you believe that Jesus is the Messiah the Son of God? Everything in between has been written to make that question unavoidable and to give you everything you need to answer it.

John Chapter 21

Breakfast on the Shore

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 21
What Is Happening Here

Seven disciples go fishing on the Sea of Tiberias and catch nothing all night. At dawn Jesus stands on the shore unrecognized. He tells them to cast the net on the right side. They cannot haul it in for the weight of fish. The beloved disciple says it is the Lord. Peter jumps in and swims to shore. The others follow with the net. What You Might Not Know

A charcoal fire — the Greek word is anthrakia — a charcoal fire. This is the same word used in Chapter 18 for the fire Peter warmed himself at in the high priest's courtyard when he made his three denials. John uses this specific word only twice in his entire Gospel — both times in connection with Peter. The charcoal fire on the beach is a deliberate echo of the charcoal fire in the courtyard. Jesus is recreating the setting of Peter's failure as the setting for his restoration. The smell of charcoal smoke would have taken Peter straight back to that courtyard.

One hundred fifty-three fish — this specific number has generated more commentary than almost any other detail in John's Gospel. Jerome noted that ancient naturalists counted exactly 153 species of fish in the sea — suggesting the catch represents the fullness of all nations gathered into the net of the Gospel. Others have found mathematical patterns in the number. John may simply be recording the number because someone counted — the detail has the feel of eyewitness memory. Whatever its symbolic significance the precision of the number is the precision of someone who was there.

None of the disciples dared ask who he is — they knew it was the Lord. The resurrection appearances in John are consistently marked by this quality of knowing without being able to explain how you know. Mary recognized him when he spoke her name. The disciples recognized him when he showed his wounds. Now they recognize him on the shore in the early morning without a word being exchanged — and none of them dares ask the question because the answer is already certain.

Why It Still Matters

Jesus came and took the bread and gave it to them and did the same with the fish — the same four actions from the feeding of the five thousand — taking giving thanks breaking giving — appear here on the resurrection shore. The risen Jesus feeds his disciples breakfast. He does not appear in glory and commission them from a distance. He builds a fire cooks fish and shares a meal. The resurrection did not make Jesus remote or unapproachable. It made him permanently present in the most ordinary human moments — a fire a meal a shore at dawn.

Jesus Restores Peter

◀ 📖 Return to John Chapter 21
What Is Happening Here

After breakfast Jesus asks Simon Peter three times do you love me. Three times Peter says yes Lord you know I love you. Three times Jesus gives him a commission — feed my lambs take care of my sheep feed my sheep. The third time Peter is hurt that Jesus asks a third time. What You Might Not Know

Three questions — three answers — three commissions. The number three is not accidental. Peter denied Jesus three times at a charcoal fire. Jesus restores him three times at a charcoal fire. Each denial is met with a reinstatement. The restoration is as specific and deliberate as the failure. Jesus does not give Peter a general absolution or a vague reassurance. He walks him back through the exact number of his failures and replaces each one with a commission. Peter is not just forgiven — he is restored to the specific role the denials seemed to disqualify him from.

Do you love me more than these — the first question includes a comparison. In the upper room Peter had declared he would lay down his life for Jesus — implying greater devotion than the others. Jesus's first question gently revisits that claim. Peter's answer drops the comparison entirely — yes Lord you know that I love you. He no longer claims to love more than the others. The failure in the courtyard has produced a humility about comparative devotion that was not present before.

When you are old you will stretch out your hands and someone else will dress you and lead you where you do not want to go — John notes that Jesus said this to indicate the kind of death by which Peter would glorify God. Early church tradition consistently records that Peter was crucified — and that he requested to be crucified upside down because he did not consider himself worthy to die in the same manner as Jesus. The prediction is of death by crucifixion. Jesus told Peter this and then said follow me. Follow me knowing where it leads.

Why It Still Matters

Feed my sheep — the commission Jesus gives Peter three times is pastoral not administrative. He is not putting Peter in charge of an organization. He is giving him the responsibility of a shepherd — caring for the people Jesus loves with the same quality of attention the good shepherd gave in Chapter 10. The one who denied knowing Jesus three times at a fire is given three times the charge to demonstrate that love by caring for others. The restoration is complete and the mission is clear — follow me and feed my sheep.

The path of following Jesus is individual and specific — what he requires of one person is not the measure of what he requires of another. Comparison is the wrong framework entirely.

This is the disciple who testifies to these things and wrote them down — John identifies himself indirectly as the author in the third person as he has throughout the Gospel. The we know that his testimony is true suggests a community around John — perhaps the church at Ephesus where tradition says he settled — vouching for the accuracy of his account. The Gospel ends with a claim about the reliability of what has just been read grounded in eyewitness testimony.

Why It Still Matters

If every one of them were written down I don't think the whole world would have room for all the books that would be written — John's closing statement is a literary expression of the inexhaustibility of Jesus. Everything he did could not be contained in any number of books. What John has written is a selection — carefully chosen signs and conversations and moments designed to bring the reader to belief and life. The Gospel ends not with a sense of completion but with a sense of overflow. There is always more. The one who is the Word through whom all things were made cannot be fully contained in words. John knew this better than anyone — and ended his Gospel by saying so.
