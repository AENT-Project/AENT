The Vision

The AENT — Accessible English New Testament — was built to prove something simple. The entire New Testament can be translated into plain, readable language and published to the world for free using tools anyone can access. No seminary degree required. No publishing budget required. No technical background required.

We built the English edition from scratch using only free AI tools, free software, and free web hosting. The whole project — translation, website, and publishing — cost nothing except time and faithfulness.

Now we want to show you exactly how we did it so you can do the same thing for your own people in your own language. The word of God belongs to everyone. In God's world, we are all brothers, sisters, and neighbors. Every person on earth deserves to read it in language they actually understand. Love God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength. Spread his good news far and wide.

The AENT Translation Principles

These are the guidelines we followed for every chapter of every book. Follow them and your translation will have the same warm, clear, readable quality as the English edition.

  • Plain language always. If an 8th grade student cannot understand the sentence, rewrite it. Never use archaic or religious language when a plain word exists.
  • Use familiar names. Use the names people already know — Jesus, not Yeshua. God, not Elohim. The goal is understanding, not scholarship.
  • One idea per sentence. Long complex sentences lose readers. Break them up. Keep them clear.
  • THE LORD in capitals. Whenever the text refers to the personal sacred name of God, use THE LORD in all capitals. When it is a title of respect, use Lord in normal letters.
  • Companion framing. This translation is a companion to the reader's existing Bible — not a replacement. Say so clearly at the start of every book.
  • Warm readable voice. Write the way a trusted friend would explain scripture — not the way an academic would write a commentary.
  • Stay faithful to meaning. Plain language does not mean loose paraphrase. Every translation choice should honor the meaning of the original Greek text.

Step by Step — How to Build Your Own Edition

1Set Up Your Free Google Account

Go to google.com and create a free account. This single account gives you Gmail for your project email address and Google Docs for writing and storing your translation. Name your project email something clear — like spanishaent@gmail.com or frenchaent@gmail.com.

2Get Your Source Text

You need a reliable Greek New Testament as your foundation. We recommend the SBL Greek New Testament — search online for "SBL Greek New Testament free PDF" to find it. You do not need to read Greek yourself. The AI tools will handle the translation from Greek. You just need the source text available to reference.

3Open Grok or Gemini and Begin Translating

Go to grok.com or gemini.google.com and create a free account. Both tools are excellent for translation work and have generous free tiers that will carry you through the full New Testament without running out of session time.

For each chapter give Grok or Gemini a prompt like this — "Please translate Matthew Chapter 1 from the Greek New Testament into plain [your language] following these principles — use familiar names like Jesus and God, write at an 8th grade reading level, one idea per sentence, warm friendly voice, THE LORD in capitals when referring to God's personal name. This is a companion translation not a replacement for existing Bibles."

Review each chapter carefully. Adjust anything that does not sound natural in your language. Paste the finished chapter into Google Docs. Save it. Move to the next chapter.

When your translation is complete and you are ready to build your website — that is when you bring in Claude at claude.ai. Claude excels at building the HTML pages, refining plain language, and solving technical problems. Use each tool for what it does best.

4Work Book by Book

Translate one book at a time. Start with the Gospel of Mark — it is the shortest Gospel and the most straightforward narrative. Once you have finished Mark you will have a strong feel for the translation voice and the rest will go faster.

Store each completed book as a separate document in Google Docs. Your work is automatically saved to the cloud and accessible from any device. It will never be lost.

5Download SeaMonkey Composer

For all website editing, we recommend SeaMonkey. It is a powerful old-school tool that works exactly like Microsoft Word. If you can copy text from a document and paste it into a new window, you already have the skills to build a website with SeaMonkey. It respects your formatting, keeps things simple, and requires zero coding knowledge.

To get started, go to seamonkey-project.org and download the software for free. Once installed, use the Composer tool to convert your Google Docs text into professional HTML web pages.

6Build Your HTML Pages

If you would like to start your own translation project, please contact us at aentproject@gmail.com for the free AENT HTML Template. This pre-formatted file ensures your site remains clean, professional, and easy to read on both computers and phones — without you ever having to learn a single line of code.

The template handles the tech work for you. Simply open it in SeaMonkey Composer, paste your translated text into the Normal view, and save it as an HTML file. We recommend building one HTML file per book containing all chapters.

7Set Up GitHub and Publish

Go to github.com and create a free account. Create a new repository and enable GitHub Pages in the repository settings. Upload all your HTML files. Your translation is immediately live on the internet — free, permanent, and accessible to anyone in the world.

That is it. From that moment on anyone who finds your website can read the New Testament in plain language in your language for free.

You do not have to figure this out alone. We have been through every step of this process and we want to help you succeed. Email us at aentproject@gmail.com with questions, to receive the HTML template, or just to let us know you are working on a translation. We will walk with you through the whole process.

AI Tools We Recommend

Claude (Anthropic) — claude.ai — The heart of the AENT project. Claude handled the translation, the plain language refinement, and built every single HTML page on this website. Claude excels at nuance, tone, and rendering meaning in warm readable language that feels like a trusted friend explaining scripture rather than a scholar lecturing about it. Best for translation refinement, plain language rendering, website building, and anything that requires a personal and relatable voice. If you only use one AI tool for this project make it Claude.

Gemini (Google) — gemini.google.com — The deep research specialist. When a translation question goes beyond the surface — when you need to understand what a Greek word actually meant in its first century context, or settle a theological debate about a difficult passage — Gemini is your tool. Best for word studies, theological research, and technical translation questions.

ChatGPT (OpenAI) — chatgpt.com — The artist of the team. ChatGPT created the AENT logo — the open Bible with the lamp and the golden path of light — and it can do the same for your project. Ask for a PNG with a transparent background for best results on your parchment page. Best for logos, illustrations, and any visual asset your project needs.

Grok (xAI) — grok.com — The most willing member of the team. When another AI hesitates on a particular passage or runs short on session time Grok steps in without complaint and delivers solid translation work. Best for bulk translation, compliance with specific requests, and picking up where others leave off.

Free Publishing Tools

Google Docs — docs.google.com — Free cloud-based word processor. This is where you write, store, and polish your translation chapter by chapter. Your work is automatically saved, accessible from any device, and never lost. When a chapter is ready simply copy it from Google Docs and paste it directly into SeaMonkey Composer — the formatting transfers cleanly.

Gmail — gmail.com — Free email from Google. Set up a dedicated project email address for your translation ministry. Since Gmail and Google Docs share the same Google account you only need to sign up once to get both.

SeaMonkey Composer — seamonkey-project.org — Free HTML editor. Works like Microsoft Word. Paste your translated text, apply headings, save as HTML. No coding knowledge required.

GitHub Pages — github.com — Free website hosting for anyone. Upload your HTML files and your translation is live on the internet instantly, for free, forever.
