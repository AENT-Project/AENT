1 Corinthians Chapter 1

Greeting to the Church in Corinth

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul opens the letter identifying himself as an apostle called by the will of God and co-sending with their brother Sosthenes. He writes to the church of God in Corinth — to those set apart in Jesus the Messiah and called to be his holy people. He extends the address to everyone everywhere who calls on the name of the Master Jesus the Messiah. He wishes them grace and peace from God the Father and from the Master Jesus the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

Called by the will of God to be an apostle — Paul's opening establishes his authority before he addresses the divisions tearing the Corinthian church apart. The Corinthians were aligning themselves with different teachers — Paul Apollos Peter. Paul's response begins by grounding his own identity not in his personal accomplishments or rhetorical skill but in the will of God. He did not choose to be an apostle. He was called. That distinction matters enormously for what follows — a man who was called by God rather than self-appointed has no need to compete for followers.

Sosthenes — this is almost certainly the same Sosthenes who was the synagogue leader in Corinth beaten in front of the tribunal in Acts 18 when Gallio dismissed the Jewish case against Paul. If so he had subsequently become a believer and was now a trusted co-worker close enough to Paul to be named alongside him in a letter to the community where he had been beaten. His presence in the opening is a quiet testimony — the man the Corinthian crowd attacked is now helping write a letter to the Corinthian church.

To those set apart in Jesus the Messiah — the Greek word is hēgiasmenois meaning sanctified or made holy. Paul addresses the Corinthians as already sanctified — not as people who need to achieve holiness but as people who have been made holy in Christ. The letter will go on to describe serious moral failures in the Corinthian community. Paul's decision to address them first as already sanctified is not naive. It is the theological grounding from which he will call them to live consistently with who they already are.

Everyone everywhere who calls on the name of our Master — Paul expands the address beyond Corinth to include all believers everywhere. Romans 10 established that everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved. Here Paul includes that universal community in the letter's address. The problems he is about to address in Corinth are not unique to Corinth. They are the struggles of a community learning what it means to belong to a Lord who transcends every human faction and loyalty.

Why It Still Matters

Paul opens by calling Jesus the Messiah our Master — and then immediately Master of everyone everywhere who calls on his name. The lordship of Jesus is not a tribal possession of one group of believers. It is a universal reality that every person who calls on him shares equally. The Corinthians who were dividing themselves into factions behind human teachers had forgotten that they all shared the same Master. That forgotten reality is the theological foundation of everything Paul is about to say about unity.

Thankful for the Corinthians

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul always thanks God for the Corinthians because of the grace given them through Christ Jesus. In him they have been made rich in every way — in their ability to speak and in their knowledge. This shows that the witness of the Messiah has been firmly established among them. They are not lacking any spiritual gift while they eagerly wait for the Master Jesus the Messiah to be revealed. He will keep them strong until the very end so that they will be blameless on the day of our Master Jesus the Messiah. God is faithful — he is the one who called them into fellowship with his Son Jesus the Messiah our Master.

What You Might Not Know

Paul thanks God for a church that is about to receive the most severe correction in any of his letters. The Corinthians had divisions sexual immorality lawsuits between believers abuse of the Lord's Supper misuse of spiritual gifts and confusion about the resurrection. Paul knew all of this when he wrote the opening thanksgiving. He thanks God anyway — not for their failures but for the genuine grace that was also present among them. The thanksgiving is not flattery or diplomatic throat-clearing. It is Paul's genuine recognition that God's work in Corinth was real even where the community's response to it was seriously flawed.

Made rich in every way — in your ability to speak and in your knowledge — the Corinthians were genuinely gifted. They had remarkable spiritual gifts including eloquent speech and knowledge — two of the very gifts that were also causing problems. Their eloquence had become a source of pride and division — people were aligning with whoever spoke most impressively. Their knowledge was producing arrogance — as Paul will say in Chapter 8 knowledge puffs up but love builds up. The gifts were real. The use of them was disordered. Paul acknowledges the gifts before addressing their misuse.

Not lacking any spiritual gift — this is both a compliment and a setup for Chapters 12 through 14. The Corinthians had every gift. What they lacked was the understanding of what gifts were for — not personal status or community ranking but the building up of the body in love. A church that has every gift and does not understand the purpose of gifts is in a more dangerous position than a church with fewer gifts and clearer understanding. Abundance without wisdom produces exactly the chaos Paul is writing to address.

God is faithful — he is the one who called you — Paul ends the thanksgiving by grounding the Corinthians' future not in their own consistency but in God's faithfulness. They had already demonstrated they were not entirely consistent. The assurance Paul offers is not you have proven yourselves reliable. It is God who called you is faithful. The security of the Corinthian believers rested on the character of the one who called them not on their own performance. That grounding is essential given what Paul is about to say about their actual behavior.

Why It Still Matters

He will keep you strong until the very end so that you will be blameless on the day of our Master Jesus the Messiah. Paul's assurance to a deeply troubled church is that their ultimate standing before God does not depend on whether they resolve all their current problems correctly. It depends on the faithfulness of the God who called them. That is not a license for continuing in the problems. It is the secure foundation from which genuine change becomes possible. A community that knows its future is secure in God's hands is free to do the hard work of repentance and correction without the fear that one wrong step will end everything.

Stop the Divisions

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Paul urges them in the name of the Master Jesus the Messiah to get on the same page. Do not let there be divisions among you. Be completely united in the same mind and the same purpose. People from Chloe's household have reported that they are arguing with each other — some saying I follow Paul others I follow Apollos or I follow Peter or I follow the Messiah. Has the Messiah been chopped into pieces? Was Paul executed on a stake for them? Were any of them baptized in the name of Paul? Paul thanks God he did not baptize any of them except Crispus and Gaius and the household of Stephanas — so no one can claim they were baptized into his name. The Messiah did not send Paul to baptize but to proclaim the good news — and not with clever or fancy speeches because that would strip the cross of its real power.

What You Might Not Know

Chloe's household — Chloe is mentioned only here in the New Testament. She was apparently a person of some standing whose household — which could include family members slaves and business associates — had traveled between Corinth and Ephesus where Paul was writing. They had given Paul a firsthand report about the state of the Corinthian church. Paul names his source — a mark of transparency and honesty. He is not working from rumor. He has specific information from specific people and he is willing to say so.

I follow Paul — I follow Apollos — I follow Peter — I follow the Messiah — the four factions in Corinth were not theological parties with distinct doctrines. They were personality cults organized around admiration for particular teachers. The Corinthian culture had a deep appreciation for skilled rhetoric and philosophical teaching — the sophist tradition valued eloquent speakers who could attract followers and charge fees for their instruction. The Corinthians had imported this cultural value into the church and were treating apostles and teachers like competing sophists whose followers needed to support their favorite.

Has the Messiah been chopped into pieces — the Greek is memeristai ho Christos — has Christ been divided. Paul's rhetorical question cuts to the theological absurdity of the factions. If the Messiah is one and all believers are united in him then the factions are not just socially destructive — they are theologically incoherent. You cannot divide Christ into a Paul-portion and an Apollos-portion and a Peter-portion. The one who died for them is undivided. The community that belongs to him should be undivided too.

Not with clever or fancy speeches because that would strip the cross of its real power — the Greek is sophia logou meaning wisdom of word or rhetorical eloquence. Paul deliberately chose not to compete in the Corinthian culture's preferred mode of persuasion. The cross as a message of salvation was already socially embarrassing — a crucified criminal as Lord. Dressing it up in sophisticated rhetoric would have made it more culturally acceptable and less genuinely powerful. The power of the cross works through its own inherent truth not through the eloquence of its packaging.

Why It Still Matters

Please all of you get on the same page. Paul's appeal for unity is grounded in the theological reality of the undivided Messiah rather than in the practical benefits of organizational harmony. The factions in Corinth were not primarily a management problem. They were a theological contradiction — a divided community claiming to belong to an undivided Lord. Every generation of the Church has reproduced some version of the Corinthian factions — organizing around personalities platforms and traditions in ways that contradict the unity of the one body. Paul's question remains — has the Messiah been chopped into pieces? The answer is still no. The community's behavior should reflect that answer.

The Power of the Cross

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

The message of the cross sounds like total nonsense to those headed for destruction but to those being saved it is the power of God. As it is written — I will destroy the wisdom of the wise and set aside the intelligence of the intellectuals. So where does that leave the philosopher the scholar the expert debater of this age? God has shown that the wisdom of this world is just plain foolishness. Since the world with all its wisdom failed to know God God was pleased to use the so-called foolishness of the message to save those who believe. Jews demand miraculous signs and Greeks look for logic and philosophy — but we proclaim a Messiah executed on a cross. To the Jews this is a scandal. To the Greeks it is just plain stupid. But to those who are called — both Jews and Greeks — the Messiah is the power of God and the wisdom of God. Because the foolishness of God is wiser than any human wisdom and the weakness of God is stronger than any human strength.

What You Might Not Know

Total nonsense — the Greek word is mōria from which we get the English word moron. The message of the cross sounded moronic to educated first-century hearers. A man executed by the Roman state as a criminal and enemy of public order being proclaimed as the Lord of the universe and the savior of the world was not just theologically unusual. It was laughable by the standards of Greek philosophy and offensive by the standards of Jewish expectation. Paul is not softening this. He is saying the message was as scandalous as it sounds.

Jews demand miraculous signs — the Jewish expectation of the Messiah included a figure of power who would overthrow Israel's enemies and establish God's kingdom through dramatic demonstration. A Messiah who was crucified by the Romans rather than overthrowing them was not just unexpected — it was the exact opposite of what the Messiah was supposed to do. The cross was a skandalon — a stumbling block or trap — that made the Gospel impossible to accept within the framework of messianic expectation most Jews held.

Greeks look for logic and philosophy — Greek intellectual culture evaluated ideas by their logical coherence and philosophical sophistication. The resurrection of a specific person from the dead was not a philosophically respectable claim. As Paul discovered at Athens in Acts 17 the resurrection was the point at which even curious Greek intellectuals stopped listening. The Gospel's central claim was not just counterintuitive to Greek philosophy — it was philosophically repugnant to those who believed the body was a prison the soul escaped at death.

The foolishness of God is wiser than any human wisdom — Paul is not anti-intellectual. He is making a specific claim about the relationship between divine and human wisdom. The thing that looks foolish by human standards — the cross — turns out to be the most powerful and wise act in human history. The thing that looks strong by human standards — political power military force philosophical sophistication — turns out to be unable to accomplish what the apparently foolish cross accomplished. The comparison is not between intelligence and stupidity. It is between two entirely different kinds of wisdom operating by entirely different logics.

Why It Still Matters

We proclaim a Messiah who was executed on a cross. To the Jews this is a scandal. To the Greeks it is just plain stupid. But to those who are called it is the power of God and the wisdom of God. The offense of the cross has not diminished with time. The claim that a first-century Jewish man executed by Rome is the Lord of the universe and the only means of salvation is still scandalous to those who evaluate it by the standards of political power and still foolish to those who evaluate it by the standards of philosophical sophistication. Paul's response to that offense is not to soften the message or find more culturally acceptable ways to frame it. It is to proclaim the cross plainly and trust that the power of God operates through the very thing the world finds offensive.

God Chose the Unlikely

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 1
What Is Happening Here

Just look at yourselves brothers and sisters. When you were called not many of you were wise by human standards not many were powerful and not many came from important families. Instead God chose the foolish things of the world to embarrass the wise and the weak things to embarrass the strong. God chose the insignificant things the despised things — the things the world counts as nothing — to bring to nothing the things the world thinks are everything. He did this so that no one could ever stand before him and boast. It is because of him that you belong to Christ Jesus — who has become for us the wisdom of God our righteousness our separation and our ransom. So as the Scripture says — if anyone is going to boast let them boast about the Master.

What You Might Not Know

Not many of you were wise by human standards not many were powerful not many came from important families — the social composition of the Corinthian church was largely working class and lower status. Archaeological and historical research on Corinth confirms that the early church there included slaves freed persons tradespeople and working people. There were some exceptions — Crispus the synagogue leader Erastus the city treasurer Gaius who had a house large enough for the whole church. But these were the minority. The majority of the Corinthian church were people the surrounding culture considered unimportant. God had deliberately gathered the unlikely.

To embarrass the wise — the Greek word is kataischynē meaning to put to shame or humiliate. God's choice of the foolish weak and insignificant is not merely inclusive. It is strategically embarrassing to the categories the world uses to rank people. When the Gospel takes root most powerfully among those the world dismisses as unimportant it exposes the bankruptcy of the world's ranking system. The people who were supposed to matter most — the wise powerful and well-born — had largely missed the Gospel. The people who were supposed to matter least had received it. That inversion was God's doing and it made a point.

So that no one could ever stand before him and boast — this is the purpose Paul names for God's pattern of choosing the unlikely. If the Corinthian church had been composed primarily of the wise powerful and well-born they would have had human grounds for pride in their standing. They could have attributed their reception of the Gospel to their own insight or status. The actual composition of the church eliminated that possibility. No one in the Corinthian church could look at their own background and conclude that their standing before God was a natural result of who they were.

Our righteousness our separation and our ransom — Paul uses three words to describe what Christ has become for believers. Righteousness — dikaiosynē — right standing before God. Separation — hagiasmos — sanctification or holiness. Ransom — apolytrōsis — redemption or being bought back. All three are things the Corinthians were trying to build through their factions eloquence and spiritual gifts. Paul is saying all three are already theirs in Christ. The competition for status through human achievement was pursuing something that had already been given.

Why It Still Matters

If anyone is going to boast let them boast about the Master. Paul quotes Jeremiah 9 to close the chapter. The Corinthian factions were fundamentally about boasting — whose teacher was most impressive whose spiritual gifts were most spectacular whose knowledge was most sophisticated. Paul redirects all of that competitive energy toward its only legitimate object. If you are going to be proud of something be proud of the Master — not of your connection to a human teacher not of your spiritual gifts not of your theological knowledge. The boasting that the Gospel eliminates is self-boasting. The boasting it permits and encourages is boasting in the one who gave everything you have.

1 Corinthians Chapter 2

Paul Preaches Christ Crucified

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

When Paul came to the Corinthians he did not arrive with impressive words or deep philosophy to tell them about the mystery of God. He made a firm decision to focus on one thing only — Jesus the Messiah and his death on the cross. He came in weakness feeling timid and honestly trembling with fear. His message and preaching were not delivered with clever persuasive talk designed to sound wise. Instead they came with a demonstration of the Spirit and of power — so that their faith would not rest on human wisdom but on the power of God.

What You Might Not Know

I came to you in weakness feeling timid and honestly trembling with fear — this is one of the most surprising self-descriptions in all of Paul's letters. The man who had debated philosophers at the Areopagus in Athens and who would later stand before kings and governors arrived in Corinth afraid. The most likely explanation connects to what had just happened before Corinth — Paul had been in Athens where his carefully constructed philosophical speech had produced a modest response and he had then traveled to Corinth alone. The trembling Paul describes was not performance anxiety. It was the genuine fear of a man who had learned something about his own limitations and was more dependent on God than on his own abilities.

I made a firm decision — the Greek is ekrina meaning I decided or I judged. The simplicity of Paul's message in Corinth was not accidental or the result of inadequate preparation. It was a deliberate strategic and theological choice. He had concluded that the way the message was delivered would shape what kind of faith it produced. A faith produced by impressive rhetoric would rest on the rhetorician. A faith produced by the plain proclamation of the cross would rest on the power behind the cross. Paul chose the second kind of faith-production even at the cost of looking unimpressive.

A demonstration of the Spirit and of power — Paul does not explain what this demonstration looked like. It may have included healings or other signs. It certainly included transformed lives — people whose encounter with the Gospel produced genuine change. The contrast he is drawing is between two kinds of persuasion. Human wisdom persuades through the quality of the argument and the skill of the speaker. The Spirit persuades through the direct action of God on the heart of the hearer. Both may produce assent. Only the second produces genuine faith.

So that your faith wouldn't rest on human wisdom but on the power of God — this is the pastoral reason behind Paul's deliberate simplicity. He was building for the long term. A community whose faith rested on admiration for Paul's eloquence would collapse if Paul stopped being eloquent or if a more eloquent teacher arrived. A community whose faith rested on the power of God would hold because God's power does not diminish. The Corinthian factions had already demonstrated the danger Paul was trying to prevent — they were resting their identity on human teachers rather than on the God those teachers pointed toward.

Why It Still Matters

My message and my preaching weren't delivered with clever persuasive talk designed to sound wise. Instead they came with a demonstration of the Spirit and of power. Paul's deliberate choice to avoid rhetorical sophistication in Corinth was not a rejection of intelligence or preparation. It was a recognition that the Gospel's power is not located in the skill of its presentation. When the Gospel is dressed in impressive rhetoric people tend to admire the dress rather than receive the message. When it is proclaimed plainly the hearer has nowhere to look except at the content itself and at the God the content points toward. The faith that results is more durable because it rests on something that cannot disappoint.

God's Hidden Wisdom

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

Paul and his co-workers do speak wisdom among those who are spiritually mature — but it is not the wisdom of this world or of the leaders of this age who are all fading away. They speak the hidden wisdom of God — a mystery he planned for glory before the world even began. None of the rulers of this world understood this wisdom. If they had they never would have executed the Master of Glory on a cross. But as the Scripture says — no eye has seen no ear has heard and no human mind has ever imagined the things God has prepared for those who love him.

What You Might Not Know

We do speak wisdom among those who are spiritually mature — Paul is not anti-intellectual and he is not saying the Gospel has no depth. He is distinguishing between two kinds of wisdom operating by two different logics. The wisdom of this world — the philosophical and rhetorical sophistication the Corinthians admired — is competent within its own domain but blind to the things of God. The wisdom Paul speaks among the mature is not less rigorous or less profound than worldly wisdom. It is a different kind of wisdom entirely — one that requires the Spirit to receive and that the world cannot access through its own methods.

The leaders of this age who are all fading away — the Greek word for fading away is katargoumenōn meaning being rendered inoperative or being brought to nothing — the same word used in Chapter 1 for the things the world counts as everything being brought to nothing. The rulers of this age — whether understood as human political leaders or as spiritual powers behind them — are operating on borrowed time. Their wisdom is real within the current age. It is becoming obsolete as the new age inaugurated by Christ's resurrection progressively displaces the old.

None of the rulers of this world understood this wisdom — if they had they never would have executed the Master of Glory on a cross. This is one of the most theologically significant statements in 1 Corinthians. The crucifixion was a profound miscalculation by the powers that ordered it. Pilate Herod the chief priests the Roman soldiers — all the human and spiritual forces that converged on the cross — thought they were eliminating a threat. They were actually accomplishing the redemption of the world. The wisdom of God was hidden precisely so that those who would have prevented it if they had understood it would carry it out instead.

No eye has seen no ear has heard — Paul quotes from Isaiah 64 with a significant adaptation. The Isaiah passage describes what God will do for those who wait for him. Paul applies it to the things God has prepared for those who love him — the full inheritance described in Romans 8 the glory that will be revealed in believers the renewal of all creation. These things are beyond human imagination not because they are vague but because they are so much better than what human experience can supply as a basis for imagination. The best human life cannot provide adequate data for envisioning what God has prepared.

Why It Still Matters

If they had understood this wisdom they never would have executed the Master of Glory on a cross. The cross was the result of the world's wisdom operating at full capacity and getting it catastrophically wrong. The most sophisticated political and religious minds of the first century converged on the decision to crucify Jesus and in doing so accomplished the very thing they were trying to prevent — the establishment of his kingdom. God's hidden wisdom operated through the world's confident miscalculation. That pattern — God working through what the world sees as defeat to accomplish what the world cannot see as victory — is the deepest logic of the Gospel and the pattern it continues to work through in every generation.

The Spirit Reveals What Is Hidden

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 2
What Is Happening Here

God has revealed these things through his Spirit. The Spirit searches through everything — even the deepest secrets of God. Who knows a person's thoughts except their own spirit living inside them? In the same way no one knows the thoughts of God except the Spirit of God. And the believers have not received the spirit of the world — they have received the Spirit who comes from God so that they can understand the things God has freely given them. They do not talk about these things using words taught by human wisdom but words taught by the Spirit explaining spiritual truths in spiritual terms. A person who lives only by natural instincts does not accept the things that come from the Spirit of God — to them it is all nonsense and they cannot understand it because these things can only be understood through the Spirit. But the person who has the Spirit can evaluate everything clearly while they themselves are not subject to the judgment of the world. As it is written — who has known the mind of the Lord well enough to teach him? But we have the mind of the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

The Spirit searches through everything — even the deepest secrets of God — the Greek word for searches is eraunaō meaning to investigate or probe thoroughly. Paul is describing the Spirit's comprehensive knowledge of God's inner life. The analogy he uses is the human spirit's knowledge of its own person — the only one who fully knows what you think and feel is the spirit within you. By the same logic the only one who fully knows what God thinks and intends is the Spirit of God. The Spirit does not search God as an outside investigator seeking information. The Spirit searches as one who is within God and knows God from the inside.

We have received the Spirit who comes from God so that we can understand the things God has freely given us — Paul is describing the epistemological function of the Spirit — how the Spirit enables believers to know things they could not otherwise know. The things God has freely given include everything described in Chapters 1 and 2 — the hidden wisdom the mystery planned before time the inheritance prepared for those who love God. These are not accessible through natural intelligence or philosophical inquiry. They are received as gifts and the Spirit is the one who enables the recipient to understand what they have received.

A person who lives only by natural instincts — the Greek is psychikos anthrōpos meaning a person of the soul or a natural person. This is not a person who is stupid or morally worse than average. It is a person whose entire framework of understanding is limited to what natural human perception and reasoning can access. Within that framework the Gospel's claims about a crucified savior a bodily resurrection and a Spirit who communicates God's thoughts cannot be evaluated as true. They look like nonsense because the evaluative tools available to natural human wisdom are not the right tools for the job.

The person who has the Spirit can evaluate everything clearly — the Greek word is anakrinō meaning to examine carefully to interrogate or to judge. The Spirit-equipped person is not less rigorous in their evaluation than the natural person. They are actually more comprehensive — they can evaluate both the things of the Spirit and the things accessible to natural wisdom. The reverse is not true. The natural person can evaluate what natural wisdom can access but cannot evaluate the things of the Spirit. The Spirit expands rather than replaces the capacity for careful thought.

But we have the mind of the Messiah — this closing statement is one of the most remarkable in 1 Corinthians. Paul quotes Isaiah 40 — who has known the mind of the Lord — as a rhetorical question implying the answer no one. Then he immediately answers it differently — we have the mind of the Messiah. The Spirit who searches the depths of God has been given to believers. They therefore have access to the mind of Christ — not omniscience but genuine knowledge of what God has revealed through Christ and his Spirit. This is the ultimate answer to the Corinthian factions — no human teacher's wisdom competes with the mind of the Messiah that every believer already has.

Why It Still Matters

But we have the mind of the Messiah. Paul's closing statement of Chapter 2 is the most direct answer to the factionalism of Chapter 1. The Corinthians were dividing themselves by claiming allegiance to human teachers whose wisdom they admired. Paul's response is that the wisdom they were competing to access through human teachers was already available to every believer through the Spirit. The mind of the Messiah — access to the things God has revealed through Christ and his Spirit — is not the possession of a spiritual elite or the reward for following the most impressive teacher. It is the inheritance of every person who has received the Spirit of God. The competition for wisdom through human faction was pursuing at great social cost something that had already been freely given.

1 Corinthians Chapter 3

You Are Still Not Ready

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

Paul tells the Corinthians he could not talk to them as spiritual people. He had to speak to them as people still driven by their natural human desires — like infants in the faith. He fed them milk not solid food because they were not ready for it. And honestly they are still not ready. They are still living by their own human nature. As long as there is jealousy and fighting among them are they not just acting like people of the world? When one of them says I follow Paul and another says I follow Apollos — are they not acting just like everyone else?

What You Might Not Know

I couldn't talk to you as spiritual people — the Greek word for spiritual is pneumatikois meaning Spirit-people or people governed by the Spirit. Paul is not saying the Corinthians were not believers or that they did not have the Spirit — Chapter 2 established that every believer has the Spirit. He is saying their behavior did not reflect the Spirit's governance. They had the Spirit but they were not living by the Spirit. The gap between possessing the Spirit and being governed by the Spirit is one of the central pastoral concerns of 1 Corinthians.

Like infants in the faith — the milk and solid food analogy describes a developmental problem rather than a permanent condition. Infants need milk because they cannot yet process solid food. This is appropriate for infants. It becomes a problem when someone remains an infant by choice or neglect rather than progressing to the solid food their development should allow them to receive. The Corinthians had been believers long enough to have progressed beyond infancy. Their continued need for milk — the most basic teaching — was not developmental appropriateness. It was arrested development.

Jealousy and fighting — the Greek words are zēlos and eris meaning zeal turned competitive and strife or contention. These two qualities were the markers of worldly culture in Corinth. The city's commercial competitiveness and social ambition produced exactly these characteristics in its public life. The Corinthian believers had brought these cultural values into the church without examining them. The community that should have been shaped by the Spirit of God had instead been shaped by the spirit of Corinth.

Aren't you acting just like everyone else — the Greek is kata anthrōpon peripatein meaning walking according to human standards or living by merely human principles. Paul's indictment is that the Corinthian factions were not distinguishable from the factional behavior of any group of people competing for status and influence in Corinth. The Gospel was supposed to produce a community that looked different from the surrounding culture. The factions were making it look the same. That failure of distinctiveness was itself evidence of spiritual immaturity.

Why It Still Matters

As long as there is jealousy and fighting among you aren't you just acting like people of the world? Paul's diagnostic question cuts through the Corinthians' sense of spiritual sophistication. They thought they were the most gifted church in Paul's network — and in terms of spiritual gifts they probably were. But the presence of jealousy and faction demonstrated that the gifts had not produced the maturity that should have accompanied them. Spiritual gifts and spiritual maturity are not the same thing. A community can be spectacular in its gifts and infantile in its character. The Corinthian church was both simultaneously and Paul names the contradiction plainly.

We Are Just Servants

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 3
What Is Happening Here

Who is Apollos anyway? And who is Paul? They are just servants through whom the Corinthians came to believe — each doing the specific job the Master gave them. Paul planted the seed and Apollos watered it but it was God who made it grow. So the one who plants does not matter and the one who waters does not matter either. The only one who matters is God — because he is the one who makes things grow. The one who plants and the one who waters are on the same team and each will be rewarded based on their own work. They are coworkers with God. The Corinthians are God's field. They are God's building.

What You Might Not Know

Who is Apollos anyway? And who is Paul? — the Greek uses the word ti meaning what rather than who — what is Apollos? What is Paul? The diminishment is deliberate. Paul is not merely being modest. He is making a theological point about the category of being that apostles and teachers occupy. They are not rival sources of spiritual power between whom believers must choose. They are instruments — tools in the hands of the God who is actually doing the work. Asking which tool you prefer misses the point. The question is what the craftsman is building.

I planted the seed and Apollos watered it but it was God who made it grow — the agricultural metaphor places both Paul and Apollos in a subordinate relationship to the same agricultural process. In farming the planter and the waterer both contribute necessary work. Neither can claim credit for the growth because neither controls it. The growth depends on forces — sun rain soil fertility — that are beyond the planter's and waterer's power. Paul is saying that the same dynamic operates in ministry. He and Apollos contributed what they could. The life that resulted was God's doing not theirs.

Each will be rewarded based on their own work — Paul acknowledges that individual ministers will receive individual recognition from God based on the quality and faithfulness of their work. This is not a leveling that says all ministry is equally effective or equally faithful. It is a redirection — the evaluation belongs to God not to the Corinthian factions. The Corinthians were doing the evaluation by comparing rhetorical impressiveness. God will do the evaluation by examining faithfulness to the task assigned. Those are very different evaluative standards and they produce very different rankings.

You are God's field. You are God's building — Paul shifts from agricultural to architectural metaphor mid-paragraph. Both images make the same point — the Corinthians belong to God not to any human teacher. They are God's field not Paul's plot or Apollos's plot. They are God's building not Paul's construction project or Apollos's construction project. The human teachers work in a field and on a building that belongs to someone else. Their job is to tend and build faithfully — not to claim ownership of what they are tending and building.

Why It Still Matters

It was God who made it grow. Paul's insistence that growth belongs to God rather than to the minister is one of the most practically important statements in 1 Corinthians for anyone involved in Christian ministry. The temptation to measure ministry success by attendance numbers conversion counts or the impressiveness of the community's gifts is exactly the temptation the Corinthians had succumbed to — evaluating ministers by visible impressive outputs. Paul insists that the visible outputs are not in the minister's control. The faithfulness of the planting and watering is. The growth is God's business. Keeping that distinction clear protects ministers from both pride when things go well and despair when things do not.

Paul is applying this image to apostolic ministry — he and his co-workers have been entrusted with the mysteries of God not as owners but as managers accountable to the owner. Why It Still Matters

Wait until the Master comes. He will bring to light everything currently hidden in darkness and reveal the true intentions of every heart. Paul's call to wait for the Master's judgment rather than rushing to human verdicts is one of the most practically important instructions in 1 Corinthians. The Corinthians were rendering judgments on apostles — deciding which ones were most worthy of their allegiance — based on visible and impressive qualities. Paul insists that the visible and impressive qualities are not the right evaluative criteria and that the Corinthians are not the right evaluators. Patience — waiting for the Master's comprehensive and accurate judgment — is the appropriate response to both the limits of human evaluation and the certainty of divine assessment.

He is describing what he and his co-workers actually did when they were insulted persecuted and slandered. The credibility of these instructions comes from the fact that Paul had been in exactly these situations and had responded exactly this way. The Corinthians who were fighting with each other and competing for status were being shown by contrast what the life shaped by the cross actually looks like.

What Is Happening Here

What You Might Not Know

A man is living with his father's wife — the phrase his father's wife rather than his mother indicates a stepmother. The man was in a sexual relationship with his stepmother — likely after the father had died or the parents had divorced. This was explicitly forbidden in Jewish law in Leviticus 18 and was also considered scandalous in Roman law and custom. The fact that even the surrounding Gentile culture found this unacceptable — of a kind that doesn't even happen among the heathens — makes the Corinthian community's tolerance of it even more remarkable and more damning.

Instead of being brokenhearted you are acting proud — the Corinthian response to this situation is one of the most disturbing details in the letter. They were not embarrassed or troubled. They were proud. The most likely explanation is that the Corinthian understanding of Christian freedom had become so distorted that they were celebrating the man's behavior as an expression of spiritual liberty — as if the freedom from the law that the Gospel brought meant freedom to do anything without moral consequence. Hand this man over to Satan — this is one of the most difficult phrases in 1 Corinthians. In the framework of Paul's thought the church is the community of the Spirit — the space of God's protective presence and blessing. Outside the church is the domain of the world which is under the influence of Satan. To hand someone over to Satan is to remove them from the protective community and place them in the outside domain. The purpose Paul names is not punishment for its own sake but the destruction of the fleshly desires that are driving the behavior — with the ultimate goal of the person's salvation on the day of the Master.

So that his spirit may be saved on the day of the Master — the disciplinary action Paul prescribes is ultimately redemptive in purpose. He is not writing off the man. He is describing a process designed to bring him to his senses. The removal from the community deprives the man of the social support and approval that has been enabling his behavior. The hoped-for result is that he will recognize the seriousness of his situation and turn back. Paul's concern is not the community's reputation but the man's ultimate salvation. The severity of the action is in proportion to the seriousness of the danger the man is in.

Why It Still Matters

Instead of being brokenhearted about it you are acting proud. The Corinthian response to serious sin in their community was pride rather than grief. That inversion — celebrating what should produce sorrow — is one of the clearest signs that a community has lost its moral compass. Paul's expectation is not that the community would be harsh or judgmental toward the sinning member. It is that they would be brokenhearted — genuinely grieved that someone they loved was in a situation that endangered their soul. The pastoral response to serious sin in a community member begins with grief not with congratulation and not with condemnation but with the kind of sorrow that takes the person's situation seriously enough to act.

The fermentation process spread through the entire batch from a small starting point. Paul uses this as an image for the way moral compromise spreads through a community. One tolerated sin does not stay contained. It changes the moral atmosphere of the whole community — normalizing what should be abnormal and making it progressively harder to recognize where the lines are.

The Messiah our Passover Lamb has been sacrificed for us — Paul introduces one of the most significant theological connections in 1 Corinthians. The Passover lamb was sacrificed at the beginning of the Feast of Unleavened Bread — the seven-day festival during which all leaven was removed from Jewish homes. Jesus's crucifixion at Passover was not accidental timing. He is the fulfillment of the Passover lamb — the sacrifice that makes deliverance possible. And just as the Passover was followed by the removal of leaven the sacrifice of Christ calls his community to remove the moral leaven that corrupts the community's integrity.

Clean out the old leaven so that you can be a fresh new batch — which is what you really are — the logic here is identical to the logic of Romans 6. You are what you are being called to become. The community is already unleavened in Christ — already cleansed and set apart. The call to clean out the old leaven is a call to live consistently with what is already true rather than to achieve something that has not yet happened. The indicative — you are a fresh new batch — grounds the imperative — clean out the old leaven. You clean out the leaven not to become what you are not but to live as what you already are.

Not with the old leaven of malice and wickedness but with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth — Paul extends the Passover imagery into an ethical description. The old leaven represents the corrupting moral influences that should have no place in the community — malice and wickedness. The unleavened bread represents the qualities that should characterize the community's life — sincerity and truth. The festival they are called to keep is not a one-week Jewish observance. It is the ongoing life of the community shaped by the sacrifice of the Messiah who is their Passover.

Why It Still Matters

Don't you know that a little leaven spreads through the whole batch of dough? The leaven principle is one of the most practically observable truths about community life. Tolerated compromise does not stay in one corner of the community. It changes the moral culture of the whole — what is acceptable what is expected what is normal. The community that celebrates one man's immoral relationship as an expression of spiritual freedom is not just making an error about that one situation. It is communicating to everyone in the community that the boundaries are negotiable and that spiritual sophistication means freedom from moral constraint. That message spreads. Paul's insistence on removing the leaven is not rigidity. It is the protection of a moral culture that has to be actively maintained.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Paul wrote in a previous letter not to associate with people who are sexually immoral. He did not mean the people of the world who are immoral or greedy or swindlers or idol worshipers — to avoid them you would literally have to leave the planet. What he meant was this — do not associate with anyone who calls themselves a brother or sister but is sexually immoral or greedy or an idol worshiper or a slanderer or a drunkard or a swindler. What You Might Not Know

A previous letter — Paul mentions a letter he had written to Corinth before this one. That letter has not survived. It is sometimes called the Previous Letter or the Lost Letter to the Corinthians. Paul's reference to it here tells us that 1 Corinthians is not his first communication with this community — there had been at least one prior letter and possibly multiple visits and communications. The instruction he refers to — not to associate with the sexually immoral — had apparently been misunderstood as requiring the Corinthians to separate themselves from all immoral people in the surrounding world.

To avoid them you would literally have to leave the planet — Paul's clarification is both practically realistic and theologically important. The church does not exist in a sealed environment separated from the world. Believers live and work and do business alongside people who are immoral greedy dishonest and idolatrous. That is the normal condition of life in the world. The instruction to not associate was never about withdrawing from the world — it was about maintaining the integrity of the community by not extending full fellowship to someone who claims to be a believer while living in unrepentant serious sin.

Anyone who calls themselves a brother or sister — the key phrase that distinguishes Paul's instruction from a general withdrawal from the world. The non-association he prescribes is specifically for someone who claims membership in the community — who presents themselves as a fellow believer — while living in the patterns of behavior listed. The claim to be a brother or sister creates an expectation of consistency with that claim. When the claim is persistently contradicted by the behavior the community's response to the person must be different from its response to someone who makes no such claim.

Don't even sit down to eat a meal with someone like that — table fellowship in the ancient world was not merely a social courtesy. Sharing a meal was an expression of acceptance endorsement and relationship. To eat with someone was to declare that you were in genuine community with them. Paul's instruction not to eat with the unrepentant person in the community is not about personal coldness or social shunning as a punishment. It is about not communicating through the social language of table fellowship that the person's continued unrepentant behavior is acceptable within the community.

Why It Still Matters

I didn't mean the people of this world who are immoral — to avoid them you would literally have to leave the planet. Paul's clarification is one of the most important boundary-setting statements in the New Testament for the Church's relationship to the surrounding culture. The Church is not called to retreat from the world into a sealed community of the pure. It is called to live in the world while maintaining internal integrity. The distinction Paul draws — between engaging the world and extending full fellowship to someone who claims to be a believer while living in unrepentant serious sin — is the distinction between mission and compromise. Both matter and keeping them clear matters enormously.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 5
What Is Happening Here

Is it Paul's job to judge those outside the community? Of course not. But are the Corinthians not responsible for judging those inside? God will judge those on the outside. As for the community — remove the wicked person from among yourselves.

What You Might Not Know

Is it my job to judge those outside the community — Paul is addressing a fundamental question about the scope of the church's moral authority. The church has no jurisdiction over the behavior of people who do not claim to belong to it. Trying to hold non-believers to the standards of the believing community is a category error — applying rules to people who have not accepted the framework those rules belong to. This has been one of the most consistently misunderstood principles in the history of the Church. When the Church attempts to enforce its moral standards on the surrounding society it is doing something Paul explicitly says is not its job.

God will judge those on the outside — the final judgment of those outside the community belongs to God not to the church. This does not mean the church has no opinion about what is right and wrong in the broader society. It means the church's role regarding outsiders is proclamation and witness — sharing the Gospel — rather than enforcement and condemnation. The church speaks to the world. It does not rule the world. The ruling belongs to God who will judge everyone at the appointed time.

Remove the wicked person from among yourselves — Paul quotes Deuteronomy 17 and 19 — passages about removing evil from the community of Israel. The application to the Corinthian situation connects the discipline Paul is prescribing to the long history of Israel's responsibility to maintain the integrity of the covenant community. The remove the evil language is strong and unambiguous. Paul is not suggesting that the community consider whether to act. He is directing them to act. The man's situation — living in open unrepentant sexual immorality — had put him in a position where the community's only faithful response was removal from fellowship.

Why It Still Matters

Is it my job to judge those outside the community? Of course not. But aren't you responsible for judging those inside? Paul's two-part distinction is one of the most clarifying statements in the New Testament about the nature and limits of church discipline. The church is responsible for the moral integrity of its own fellowship. It is not responsible for policing the behavior of the world outside its fellowship. Getting these two jurisdictions confused produces both failures — a church that ignores serious sin in its own community while campaigning against the world's behavior has inverted Paul's priorities exactly. The chapter closes with the church's clear responsibility — remove the wicked person from among yourselves — not from the world but from among yourselves.

1 Corinthians Chapter 6

Don't Take Each Other to Court

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

How dare any of them when they have a legal dispute with another believer take it to a secular court to be judged by people who do not follow God's ways? Why are they not taking it to God's people instead? Do they not realize that God's people will one day judge the whole world? If they are going to judge the world surely they can handle these small disputes. They will even judge angels — so they can certainly handle everyday matters. So why do they appoint judges who have no standing in the faith community to settle their disputes? Paul says this to shame them. Is there really not a single wise person among them who can settle a disagreement between brothers? Instead one brother sues another right in front of unbelievers. The very fact that they are suing each other means they have already lost. Why not just accept the wrong done to them? Why not just let themselves be cheated? Instead they are the ones doing wrong and cheating — and doing it to their own brothers and sisters.

What You Might Not Know

Take it to a secular court to be judged by people who don't follow God's ways — the Greek phrase is epi tōn adikōn meaning before the unrighteous. Paul is not making a general statement that secular courts are corrupt or that their judges are immoral people. He is making a specific point about jurisdiction and competence. The matters being disputed between believers belong within the community that shares the values and commitments the disputes are ultimately about. A secular court has no framework for evaluating disputes within a community organized around the lordship of Jesus. It is the wrong court for the wrong kind of dispute.

God's people will one day judge the whole world — Paul is drawing on the Jewish apocalyptic tradition in which the righteous would participate in the final judgment of the nations. Daniel 7 describes the saints of the Most High receiving judgment and ruling. Paul applies this future reality to the present situation — the people who will one day be entrusted with cosmic judgment cannot find anyone among them capable of handling a property dispute between two members. The gap between the community's future dignity and their present behavior is part of what Paul finds so absurd.

We will even judge angels — Paul's claim that believers will judge angels is one of the most striking in 1 Corinthians. Whether he means fallen angels or some other category of spiritual beings is not specified. The point is the same as the world judgment claim — the scope of the believer's future responsibility is so vast that handling present earthly disputes should be entirely manageable by comparison. The community that cannot settle a dispute between two of its members is failing at something far simpler than what God has prepared them for.

The very fact that you are suing each other means you have already lost — Paul's statement turns the legal logic upside down. In a lawsuit one party wins and one loses. Paul says both parties have already lost by the very act of taking the dispute to a secular court. The loss is not the verdict — it is the damage done to the witness of the community before watching unbelievers and the damage done to the relationship between the two parties. Whatever one party gains through the lawsuit is less than what both parties have lost by pursuing it this way.

Why not just accept the wrong done to you — Paul's suggestion that believers should be willing to accept being wronged rather than pursuing litigation sounds radical. It is rooted in the ethics of the Sermon on the Mount and in the pattern of Christ who accepted the ultimate wrong done to him without retaliating. Paul is not saying that justice is unimportant. He is saying that the way justice is pursued matters and that preserving the community's integrity and witness is worth more than recovering what was taken.

Why It Still Matters

Is there really not a single wise person among you who can settle a disagreement between brothers? Paul's question is a challenge to every Christian community to develop the internal capacity to handle its own conflicts. A community that immediately exports its disputes to outside courts has not yet learned to be a community. The ability to sit down together hear each other honestly and find a resolution that honors the relationship and the Lord is a mark of genuine communal maturity. Paul expected the Corinthians to have this capacity. The fact that they were suing each other in public courts was evidence that they had not yet developed it. That development is still the call.

What Is Happening Here

Do they not know that people who do unrighteous things will not inherit the kingdom of God? Do not be fooled. People who are sexually immoral idol worshipers adulterers those who practice homosexuality thieves the greedy drunkards slanderers or swindlers — none of these will inherit the kingdom of God. And some of them used to be exactly like that. But they were washed clean they were set apart and they were made right with God — in the name of the Master Jesus the Messiah and by the Spirit of our God.

What You Might Not Know

People who do unrighteous things will not inherit the kingdom of God — Paul's list of behaviors that exclude people from the kingdom follows directly from his discussion of the lawsuits and connects to the sexual immorality of Chapter 5. He is not introducing a new topic. He is establishing the seriousness of the moral failures he has been addressing. The kingdom that the Corinthians claimed to be citizens of has entrance requirements — not for justification which comes by faith but for the ongoing orientation of life that genuine membership in God's kingdom involves. A community that tolerates the behaviors on this list while claiming kingdom citizenship is contradicting itself.

Those who practice homosexuality — the Greek text uses two words here. Malakoi meaning soft ones — a term used for the passive partner in male same-sex intercourse. And arsenokoitai — a compound word Paul apparently coined from the Leviticus 18 and 20 passages in the Greek Old Testament meaning males who lie with males. Paul lists both alongside the other behaviors without singling them out for special condemnation. They appear in a list of behaviors incompatible with kingdom inheritance that also includes greed slander and drunkenness — behaviors that the Church has historically been far more tolerant of than same-sex behavior.

And some of you used to be exactly like that — this is one of the most important statements in Chapter 6. The list Paul has just given is not a description of people outside the community who should be judged. It is a description of what some people in the community used to be. The Corinthian church included former sexually immoral people former idolaters former adulterers former thieves former drunkards former swindlers. The community was made up of people whose lives had been genuinely transformed. The past tense is essential — used to be.

You were washed clean you were set apart and you were made right with God — the three verbs describe three dimensions of what happened at conversion. Washed — apelousasthe — the cleansing that removes the defilement of past behavior. Set apart — hēgiasthēte — the sanctification that establishes a new identity and orientation. Made right — edikaiōthēte — the justification that establishes a new legal standing before God. All three happened in the name of the Master Jesus the Messiah and by the Spirit of our God. The transformation was not self-produced. It was received from outside through the name of Jesus and the power of the Spirit.

Why It Still Matters

And some of you used to be exactly like that. But you were washed clean you were set apart and you were made right with God. The past tense and the but are the most important words in this passage for anyone reading it today. The list of behaviors that excludes from the kingdom is real and the exclusion is serious. And the Gospel's power to transform people whose lives were characterized by those behaviors is equally real. The community Paul is writing to was living proof of both realities simultaneously — people who had been everything on the list and who had been genuinely changed. That combination — the seriousness of the behaviors and the reality of transformation — is the whole Gospel in two sentences.

Your Body Belongs to God

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 6
What Is Happening Here

You say I am allowed to do anything — maybe so but not everything is good for you. And even if you are allowed to do anything you will not let anything become your master. You say food is for the stomach and the stomach for food — true but God will do away with both in the end. The body was not made for sexual immorality. It was made for the Master and the Master cares about the body. Just as God raised the Master from the dead by his power he will also raise us up. Do you not realize that your bodies are actually parts of the Messiah's own body? Should you take a part of the Messiah and join it to a prostitute? Absolutely not. Do you not know that a man who joins himself to a prostitute becomes one body with her? As the Scripture says the two will become one flesh. But the person who is joined to the Master is one spirit with him. Run away from sexual immorality. Every other sin a person commits happens outside the body but the person who is sexually immoral sins against their own body. Do you not know that your body is a temple for the Holy Spirit who lives in you — the Spirit given to you by God? You do not belong to yourselves. You were bought at a great price. So use your body to bring glory to God.

What You Might Not Know

You say I am allowed to do anything — Paul is quoting a slogan that was apparently circulating in the Corinthian community. The Greek is panta moi exestin — all things are permitted to me. This was likely a distortion of Paul's own teaching about Christian freedom — the freedom from the law as a system of earning righteousness that he had taught them. Some Corinthians had taken this genuine teaching and extended it to mean that physical behavior was morally irrelevant — that what you did with your body had no spiritual significance. Paul pushes back on this twice with the same slogan but adds qualifications — not everything is good for you and I will not let anything become my master.

Food is for the stomach and the stomach for food — this appears to be another Corinthian slogan. The argument behind it was that bodily appetites — hunger and sexual desire — were morally equivalent. Just as eating food is a natural bodily function with no spiritual significance so sexual activity is a natural bodily function with no spiritual significance. You satisfy one appetite with food and the other with a sexual partner and neither has any more moral weight than the other. Paul's response dismantles this analogy — food and the stomach are temporary arrangements that will pass away. The body is permanent — it will be raised — and it belongs to the Lord.

Your bodies are actually parts of the Messiah's own body — Paul is extending the body of Christ imagery from Chapter 12 into the discussion of sexual ethics. If believers are members of Christ's body then their physical bodies are not their private property to use as they choose. What they do with their bodies affects their union with Christ. To join a member of Christ's body to a prostitute is to bring Christ himself into that union — which Paul treats as an outrageous violation of the nature of the relationship between the believer and the Lord.

The two will become one flesh — Paul quotes Genesis 2 — the description of the marriage union in which a man and woman become one flesh. He applies it to sexual union with a prostitute to make the point that sexual union is never merely physical. It creates a bond — a becoming-one — that Genesis describes as characteristic of the most intimate human relationship. The Corinthians who were treating sexual activity as morally equivalent to eating were ignoring the Genesis reality that sexual union creates a kind of bond that eating does not.

You were bought at a great price — the language of purchase — agorazō — was the language of the slave market. A slave who had been purchased belonged entirely to the new owner. Paul applies this image to the believer's relationship to God through the cross. The great price is the death of Jesus. The purchase transfers ownership — the believer no longer belongs to themselves. Their body — as the physical instrument through which they live in the world — is not their private property to be used according to their own preferences. It belongs to the one who bought it.

Why It Still Matters

Don't you know that your body is a temple for the Holy Spirit who lives in you — the Spirit given to you by God? You don't belong to yourselves. You were bought at a great price. So use your body to bring glory to God. Paul's theology of the body is one of the most important correctives to both ancient and modern tendencies to treat the physical body as spiritually irrelevant. The ancient Corinthian slogan — all things are permitted — treated the body as morally neutral raw material for satisfying appetites. Modern versions of the same error treat the body as personal property to be used however the individual chooses. Paul's answer to both is the same — the body belongs to God who made it bought it and will raise it. That ownership does not diminish the person. It gives the body its highest possible dignity and purpose.

1 Corinthians Chapter 7

Marriage and Self-Control

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Paul addresses the questions the Corinthians wrote to him about. It is actually a good thing for a man not to have sexual relations with a woman. But because of so much sexual immorality in the world each man should have his own wife and each woman should have her own husband. A husband should fulfill his marital duty to his wife and a wife should do the same for her husband. The wife does not have sole authority over her own body — her husband shares it. In the same way the husband does not have sole authority over his own body — his wife shares it. Do not deprive each other of intimacy unless you both agree to it for a short time to focus on prayer — but then come back together so that Satan does not tempt you because of your lack of self-control. Paul says this as a concession not a command. He wishes everyone could be single like he is — but each person has their own gift from God one kind for one person and a different kind for another.

What You Might Not Know

Regarding the questions you wrote to me about — Chapter 7 marks a significant shift in 1 Corinthians. The first six chapters addressed problems Paul had heard about from Chloe's household. From Chapter 7 onward Paul is responding to a letter the Corinthians had sent him — a series of questions about specific issues in their community. The phrase it is a good thing for a man not to have sexual relations with a woman may itself be a quote from the Corinthians' letter — a position some in the community were advocating. Paul is responding to their statement rather than introducing his own new topic.

It is actually a good thing for a man not to have sexual relations — some in the Corinthian community had apparently concluded that the highest form of Christian spirituality required complete sexual abstinence — even within marriage. This may have been a reaction against the sexual immorality Paul addressed in Chapters 5 and 6 or it may have been influenced by Greek philosophical ideas that treated the physical body and its appetites as inferior to the spiritual life. Paul does not simply agree or disagree. He affirms that celibacy is a genuine gift — but insists that it is a gift not a requirement and that marriage with its sexual dimension is equally legitimate and necessary for most people.

The wife does not have sole authority over her own body — her husband shares it — the mutuality Paul describes here is remarkable in its ancient context. In both Jewish and Roman culture the husband's authority over the wife's body was assumed. The wife's reciprocal authority over the husband's body was not. Paul establishes complete symmetry — neither partner has unilateral authority over their own body. Both have authority over the other's body. This mutual claim is the basis for Paul's argument that neither partner should unilaterally withhold sexual intimacy from the other.

I wish everyone could be single like I am — Paul's personal preference for celibacy is stated clearly and without apology. He is not embarrassed by it and he is not suggesting that married people are spiritually inferior. He is being honest about his own gift and his own experience. The celibate life he lives allows him a freedom of movement and focus that married life would not permit. He values that freedom for what it enables him to do. But he is equally clear that not everyone has the same gift and that the person who does not have the gift of celibacy should not try to live as if they do.

Why It Still Matters

Each person has their own gift from God — one kind for one person and a different kind for another. Paul's affirmation that both celibacy and marriage are gifts from God is one of the most important statements in 1 Corinthians for the ongoing life of the Church. Both states have been dishonored in different eras and communities — celibacy treated as the only truly spiritual option in some traditions and singleness treated as a failure or problem to be solved in others. Paul treats both as genuine gifts with genuine value. The person who has the gift of celibacy and the person who has the gift of marriage are equally honoring God when they live faithfully within the gift they have been given.

To the Unmarried and Widows

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

To the unmarried and to the widows Paul says it is good to stay single as he is. But if you cannot control yourself get married. It is far better to marry than to burn with passion.

What You Might Not Know

To the unmarried and to the widows — Paul distinguishes between different categories of people who are not currently married. The unmarried could include those who have never married and those who are divorced. The widows are specifically named as a separate group — women whose husbands have died. Both groups receive the same basic counsel — staying single is good but marriage is the right choice if celibacy cannot be maintained with integrity. The widow's situation is addressed more specifically in the final section of the chapter.

If you cannot control yourself get married — the Greek is ei de ou enkrateuontai gamēsatōsan meaning if they do not have self-control let them marry. The bluntness is characteristic of Paul. He is not romanticizing marriage or suggesting that the only valid reason to marry is uncontrollable passion. He is being realistic about the alternative. A person who is trying to maintain celibacy without the gift of celibacy is in a position of ongoing temptation and struggle that serves no one well. The pastoral counsel is practical — marriage is the God-given context for sexual expression and if that is what you need that is what you should pursue.

It is far better to marry than to burn with passion — the Greek is kreitton gar estin gamēsai ē pyrousthai — it is better to marry than to be aflame. The word pyrousthai means to be set on fire or to burn. The burning Paul describes is the sustained passionate desire that cannot be resolved through celibacy. Paul's counsel is not that this burning is shameful or that the person experiencing it is spiritually inferior. It is that the God-given solution to that burning is marriage — and that pursuing the solution is better than enduring the burning indefinitely in hopes of achieving a celibacy you do not have the gift for.

Why It Still Matters

It is far better to marry than to burn with passion. Paul's practical counsel to the unmarried and widows cuts through the false spirituality that treats celibacy as inherently more holy than marriage. The person who is genuinely called to celibacy will find it sustainable — difficult at times but manageable with grace. The person who is not called to it will find the burning to be a persistent reality that marriage is designed to address. Pretending to have a gift you do not have is not spirituality. It is self-deception that tends to produce either ongoing frustration or moral failure. Paul's counsel is to be honest about what you actually have and to live accordingly.

To the Married

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

To those who are already married Paul has a command — and this comes from the Master not just from Paul. A wife must not leave her husband. But if she does leave she should either remain unmarried or be reconciled to her husband. And a husband must not divorce his wife.

What You Might Not Know

This comes from the Master not just from me — Paul explicitly distinguishes between instructions he receives as direct commands from Jesus and instructions he gives from his own Spirit-guided judgment. This distinction appears several times in Chapter 7 and is significant. Paul had access to traditions about Jesus's teaching that he treats as authoritative commands. The prohibition on divorce is one of these — it echoes the teaching of Jesus recorded in the Gospels where Jesus addresses divorce directly. Paul is not inventing this teaching. He is passing on what Jesus taught.

A wife must not leave her husband — in Roman law both men and women could initiate divorce. Paul addresses the wife first — which may reflect the situation in Corinth where some wives influenced by the ascetic teaching that celibacy was more spiritual were considering leaving their marriages to pursue a celibate life. Paul forbids this — the marriage commitment is not something to be abandoned in pursuit of a supposedly higher spiritual state. The marriage itself is a legitimate and holy context for the Christian life.

But if she does leave she should either remain unmarried or be reconciled — Paul's concession acknowledges the reality that some separations will happen despite the command. He does not pretend the command will always be followed. His counsel for the person who has separated is to remain unmarried or to seek reconciliation — keeping the door open for restoration while not compounding the separation by immediately entering a new marriage. The hope behind the counsel is reconciliation — the temporary separation resolving back into the restored marriage.

Why It Still Matters

A husband must not divorce his wife. Paul's application of Jesus's teaching on divorce to both partners equally — the wife must not leave and the husband must not divorce — maintains the symmetry he established at the beginning of the chapter. The marital commitment is mutual and the command to honor it is addressed to both partners equally. The Corinthians who were considering ending their marriages in pursuit of a supposedly more spiritual celibate life needed to hear that the spiritual life Paul commends is lived within the commitments already made — not by abandoning them.

What Is Happening Here

To the rest Paul gives his best judgment though not a direct command from the Master. If a brother has a wife who is not a believer and she is happy to live with him he should not divorce her. And if a woman has a husband who is not a believer and he is happy to live with her she should not divorce him. The unbelieving husband is brought closer to God through his believing wife and the unbelieving wife is brought closer through her believing husband. Otherwise their children would have no connection to God at all — but as it is they are covered. But if the unbelieving partner chooses to leave let them go. This is my best judgment though not a direct command from the Master — Jesus's teaching on marriage addressed people who were both within the covenant community. He did not address the specific situation Paul is now handling — what happens when one partner in a marriage becomes a believer and the other does not. Paul is not contradicting Jesus. He is applying the principles of Jesus's teaching to a situation Jesus did not directly address. His willingness to distinguish between direct commands from the Master and his own Spirit-guided judgment is an example of intellectual and theological honesty.

The unbelieving husband is brought closer to God through his believing wife — the Greek word is hēgiastai meaning is sanctified or is made holy. Paul is not saying the unbelieving spouse is saved by their partner's faith. He is saying they are in a sphere of influence — the marriage relationship — where the grace of God operating through the believing partner has access to them in a way it would not have otherwise. The believing partner's presence in the marriage is itself a form of God's grace toward the unbeliever. That presence is a reason to stay rather than to leave.

If the unbelieving partner chooses to leave let them go — this is the passage that has traditionally given rise to what theologians call the Pauline privilege. If the unbelieving partner initiates the separation and refuses reconciliation the believing partner is not bound — not obligated to pursue an impossibly one-sided attempt to maintain the marriage. The commitment to peace that Paul names as God's calling for the believer is not compatible with being trapped in a relationship the other partner has unilaterally abandoned. The believer is freed from that particular obligation.

How do you know whether you will bring your wife to God — the question Paul asks cuts against any certainty about the outcome of remaining in the marriage. The believing partner cannot guarantee conversion. They can only provide the ongoing presence and witness that makes conversion possible. The uncertainty of the outcome is a reason for humility — not a reason to stay — but combined with the potential good that might come it is a reason to consider staying seriously before deciding to separate.

Why It Still Matters

If the unbelieving partner chooses to leave let them go. In that case the brother or sister is not bound. God has called us to live in peace. Paul's counsel for the believer in a marriage where the unbeliever has chosen to leave is one of the most pastorally important in 1 Corinthians. The person who has been abandoned by an unbelieving spouse has not failed. They have done what Paul commends — maintained the marriage as long as the other partner was willing — and are now released from the obligation they could not fulfill alone. The calling to peace is God's calling for the believer. Pursuing a marriage that the other partner has definitively ended is not peace — it is a different kind of bondage.

Stay as You Are

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Generally speaking each person should live in the situation the Master assigned to them when God called them. This is the rule Paul gives in all the churches. Were you already circumcised when you were called? Do not try to hide it. Were you uncircumcised? Do not get circumcised. Circumcision or the lack of it does not matter — what matters is keeping God's commandments. Stay as you were when God called you. Were you a slave when you were called? Do not let it bother you — though if you have the chance to become free take it. A slave who is called by the Master is the Master's freed person. And a free person who is called belongs to the Messiah. You were bought at a great price — do not become slaves of men. Brothers and sisters stay close to God in whatever state you were in when he called you.

What You Might Not Know

Each person should live in the situation the Master assigned to them when God called them — this principle of staying in your calling is the theological backbone of the entire chapter. Paul is not saying that circumstances can never change or that improvement is wrong. He is saying that conversion does not require a wholesale reconstruction of one's social situation. The person who was married when they came to faith does not need to become single to be fully spiritual. The person who was single does not need to marry. The person who was a Jew does not need to become a Gentile. The person who was uncircumcised does not need to be circumcised. The calling of God meets people where they are and transforms them there.

Don't try to hide it — the Greek is mē epispasthō meaning do not draw over the foreskin — a reference to a surgical procedure that some circumcised Jewish men underwent to appear uncircumcised in the Greek athletic environment where nudity was normal. The pressure to conform to Gentile cultural norms by hiding one's Jewish identity was real for some Jewish believers. Paul's instruction is to not hide it — and equally not to require it of those who do not have it. The physical status is religiously irrelevant. The attempt to change it in either direction out of social pressure is a form of capitulation to human standards.

Were you a slave when you were called — do not let it bother you — Paul's counsel to slaves is one of the most discussed in the New Testament given the history of slavery and its abolition. Paul is not endorsing slavery as an institution or saying that slaves should never seek freedom. He explicitly says that if the chance to become free arises the slave should take it. What he is saying is that the slave's standing before God — their dignity as a person bought by the Messiah — is not diminished by their social status. Their value and identity are not determined by whether they are enslaved or free.

A slave who is called by the Master is the Master's freed person — this reversal of categories is one of the most theologically subversive statements in 1 Corinthians. In the world's terms the slave is owned property with no freedom. In God's terms the slave who belongs to the Messiah is a freed person — liberated from the most fundamental bondage which is the bondage to sin and death. The social category of slave does not define the person's actual status. Their status before God defines them and that status is one of freedom in Christ.

Why It Still Matters

Stay close to God in whatever state you were in when he called you. Paul's principle of staying in your calling is a permanent corrective to the anxiety about circumstances that afflicts people in every generation. The person who believes they cannot fully serve God until their circumstances are different — until they are married or single or free or educated or healthy — has misunderstood the nature of the calling. God calls people in their actual circumstances and works through those circumstances rather than requiring them to be resolved first. The transformation the Gospel brings happens in the midst of real life not after real life has been rearranged into more favorable conditions.

Time Is Running Out

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Time is short. From now on those with wives should live with such focus that it is as if they had none. Those who mourn should not be consumed by grief. Those who are happy should not be distracted by joy. Those who buy things should not act as if they own them. And those who use the things of this world should not get wrapped up in them. This world in its present form is passing away.

What You Might Not Know

Time is short — the Greek is ho kairos synestalmenos estin meaning the appointed time has been compressed or drawn together. Paul is describing the eschatological urgency he shares with the rest of the New Testament — the conviction that the resurrection of Jesus has inaugurated the last days and that the present age is running out. This sense of urgency shapes his counsel throughout Chapter 7. It is not that marriage or grief or joy or buying things are wrong. It is that the shortness of the remaining time should produce a kind of lightness in relation to all of these — a non-attachment that keeps the really important things in focus.

Those with wives should live as if they had none — Paul is not telling married men to ignore their wives or to treat the marriage as unimportant. He is describing an orientation — a holding of the marriage relationship with open hands rather than gripping it so tightly that it becomes the center of everything. The same logic applies to every other experience he names — grief joy possessions and the things of this world. None of them should be held so tightly that they become consuming. The compression of time calls for a compression of attachment to temporal things.

This world in its present form is passing away — the Greek is to schema tou kosmou toutou paragei meaning the outward form or fashion of this world is passing away. Paul is not saying the world itself will be destroyed and replaced by something else entirely — Romans 8 established that creation will be renewed not abandoned. He is saying that the present arrangement — the current configuration of human society with its values its hierarchies its economic structures and its social patterns — is temporary. The person who invests everything in the current arrangement is investing in something that is already in the process of passing.

Why It Still Matters

This world in its present form is passing away. Paul's eschatological perspective on the shortness of time and the transience of the present world order is not a counsel of despair or withdrawal. It is a counsel of freedom — freedom from the anxiety of trying to secure your life through the arrangements of a world that is not the final reality. The person who knows the present form is passing is free to hold all of it — marriage grief joy possessions — with appropriate lightness. That lightness is not indifference. It is the freedom of someone who knows where the story is going and whose security rests in that destination rather than in the arrangements of the present moment.

Undivided Devotion

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 7
What Is Happening Here

Paul wants the Corinthians to be free from worry. An unmarried man is focused on the Master's business — on how he can please the Master. But a married man is focused on the things of this world — on how he can please his wife — and his attention is divided. An unmarried woman or a young woman who has never married is focused on the Master's business devoted in body and spirit. But a married woman is focused on the things of this world — on how she can please her husband. Paul says this for their own good not to restrict them. He wants them to live in a way that is honorable and allows them to serve the Master without distraction. If a man feels he is not being fair to the woman he is engaged to and his passions are strong and he feels they should marry let him do what he wants — it is not a sin. They should get married. But if a man has the self-control to stay single and has made up his mind to do so he does a good thing too. The one who marries does well but the one who stays single does even better. A wife is bound to her husband as long as he lives but if her husband dies she is free to marry whoever she wants as long as he belongs to the Master. In Paul's opinion though she will be happier if she stays as she is. And he believes he has the Spirit of God in saying this.

What You Might Not Know

An unmarried man is focused on the Master's business — on how he can please the Master — Paul is describing the practical freedom that comes with the unmarried state. The unmarried person has one primary loyalty and one primary concern — the Lord. The married person necessarily has two — the Lord and the spouse. Neither is wrong. Both are real. The practical difference is the divided attention that marriage creates. Paul values the undivided attention the celibate person can give to God not because the married person's attention is in the wrong place but because the celibate person's attention can be in one place rather than two.

Devoted in body and spirit — the Greek is hina ē hagia kai tō sōmati kai tō pneumati meaning that she may be holy both in body and in spirit. Paul is describing a particular quality of consecration that the unmarried woman can maintain — a wholeness of orientation in which both the physical and the spiritual dimensions of her life are directed toward the Lord rather than divided between the Lord and a husband. This is not a criticism of the married woman. It is an honest description of what the celibate vocation makes possible.

I want you to live in a way that is honorable and allows you to serve the Master without distraction — the Greek word for distraction is aperispastōs meaning without being pulled in different directions. Paul's goal for the counsel of the whole chapter is a community of people who can serve the Master with integrity and focus. Whether that service happens in marriage or in celibacy is secondary. The primary concern is that it happens without the divided loyalties and anxious distraction that would prevent genuine wholehearted service.

I believe I have the Spirit of God in saying this — Paul closes Chapter 7 with a remarkable claim of Spirit-guided authority for counsel he has consistently distinguished from direct commands of the Master. He has been careful throughout the chapter to signal when he was giving his own judgment rather than direct divine commands. But he does not thereby disqualify his own judgment. He claims Spirit-guidance for the wisdom he has offered. The distinction between thus says the Lord and thus says Paul guided by the Spirit is real — but both can be trustworthy.

Why It Still Matters

The one who marries does well but the one who stays single does even better. Paul's final assessment of marriage and celibacy honors both while honestly stating his preference. He is not denigrating marriage — he has spent the whole chapter defending its legitimacy and holiness. He is being honest that the celibate vocation offers a kind of undivided devotion that marriage by its nature cannot. Both are good. One is better in the specific dimension of freedom for undistracted service. The Church has done better when it has honored both rather than elevating one at the expense of the other.

1 Corinthians Chapter 8

Knowledge vs. Love

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Regarding food sacrificed to idols — we all know the saying we all have knowledge. But knowledge tends to make people arrogant while love is what actually builds people up. If anyone thinks they really know something they have not yet reached the kind of understanding they ought to have. But if someone truly loves God that person is known and recognized by him.

What You Might Not Know

Food sacrificed to idols was one of the most practically significant issues in the early Gentile church. In Corinth as in most ancient cities the slaughter of animals was intimately connected to religious practice. Animals were killed at temples as offerings to the gods. Some of the meat was burned on the altar. Some went to the priests. The remainder was sold in the marketplace or served at banquets — including banquets held in the temple precinct itself. A Corinthian Christian who wanted to participate in normal social and commercial life would encounter this meat constantly. The question was whether eating it constituted participation in idol worship.

We all have knowledge — this phrase appears to be another Corinthian slogan that Paul is quoting back to them. The knowledge in question was the theological understanding that idols are not real — that there is only one God and that meat sacrificed to a nonexistent deity is just meat. This knowledge was accurate. Paul does not dispute it. He disputes the use being made of it — the assumption that possessing correct theological knowledge entitles one to exercise freedom without regard for its effect on others.

Knowledge tends to make people arrogant while love builds people up — the Greek word for arrogant is physioi meaning puffed up or inflated. The contrast Paul draws is not between knowledge and ignorance but between knowledge and love. Both knowledge and love are real goods. The difference is their social effect. Knowledge possessed without love tends to inflate the one who has it and to look down on those who do not. Love directs attention toward the wellbeing of the other. The Corinthians had knowledge. What they lacked was the love that would have shaped how they used it.

If someone truly loves God that person is known and recognized by him — the Greek is egnōstai hyp' autou meaning has been known by him. Paul reverses the direction of knowing. The Corinthians were focused on what they knew about God — their theological sophistication. Paul redirects attention to what God knows about them — their standing as people who are loved by and known to God. The most important knowledge in any relationship is not what you know about the other person but whether you are genuinely known by them. The believer who loves God is known by God in the deepest possible sense.

Why It Still Matters

Knowledge tends to make people arrogant while love is what actually builds people up. Paul's contrast between the social effects of knowledge and love is one of the most practically important observations in 1 Corinthians. Theological knowledge is genuinely valuable — Paul never suggests that ignorance is preferable to understanding. But knowledge that is not governed by love tends to produce a particular kind of pride — the pride of those who know better than others and are not slow to demonstrate it. The community built on love looks different from the community built on shared correct belief. Both belief and love matter. The one without the other produces either warm ignorance or cold correctness. Paul wants both together with love governing the use of knowledge.

There Is Only One God

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

About eating meat sacrificed to idols — there is no God but one and an idol in this world is not actually anything real. Even if there are so-called gods in heaven or on earth and indeed many people believe in many gods and many lords — for us there is only one God the Father. Everything comes from him and we live for him. And there is only one Master Jesus the Messiah. Everything exists through him and we have our life through him.

What You Might Not Know

An idol in this world isn't actually anything real — the Greek is ouden eidōlon en kosmō meaning an idol is nothing in the world. Paul is affirming the Jewish and early Christian conviction that the gods of the surrounding culture do not actually exist as divine beings. The statues and images worshiped in Corinth's many temples were representations of nonexistent deities. This is a bold claim in a culture saturated with divine images and religious practice. It is also the theological basis for the strong believer's argument that eating meat sacrificed to a nonexistent god is morally neutral — the meat was never actually consecrated to a real divine being.

Even if there are so-called gods in heaven or on earth — Paul's qualification here is interesting. He acknowledges that many people believe in many gods and that there may be spiritual realities behind the idols — though not divine ones. His letters to the Corinthians and Galatians suggest he believed there were spiritual powers at work in pagan religion — not gods in the true sense but real forces. The full development of this thought appears in Chapter 10 where Paul says that what pagans sacrifice they sacrifice to demons. The idol is nothing. The spiritual reality that may lurk behind the idol is not nothing — which adds a dimension to the idol meat question that simple monotheism does not fully resolve.

For us there is only one God the Father — everything comes from him and we live for him — Paul's brief creed echoes the Shema of Deuteronomy 6 — the foundational Jewish confession that God is one. He expands it christologically — adding Jesus the Messiah as the one through whom everything exists and through whom we have our life. The incorporation of Jesus into the divine identity alongside the Father is one of the earliest and clearest expressions of what would become trinitarian theology. Paul is not explaining the relationship between Father and Son in philosophical terms. He is confessing in the language of devotion what he knows to be true about both.

Why It Still Matters

For us there is only one God the Father — everything comes from him and we live for him. And there is only one Master Jesus the Messiah — everything exists through him and we have our life through him. Paul's compressed creed in the middle of a discussion about food sacrificed to idols is one of the clearest statements of Christian monotheism in the New Testament. The practical question about what to eat is grounded in a theological reality about who God is. Good ethics always flows from accurate theology. The Corinthians' problem was not that they had the wrong theology about idols — they were correct that idols are not real. Their problem was that they had not connected their theology to their responsibility toward others in the community.

Don't Wound a Weak Conscience

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 8
What Is Happening Here

Not everyone has this understanding. Some people are so used to idols that even now when they eat such meat they think of it as a sacrifice to a real god. Because their conscience is not fully settled in the truth they feel guilty eating it. Food itself does not bring us closer to God — we are not worse off if we do not eat and not better off if we do. But be very careful. Do not let your freedom to eat become a stumbling block for someone whose faith is still fragile. If someone with a sensitive conscience sees you the one who knows better sitting down to eat in an idol's temple won't they be encouraged to eat that meat even though they feel it is wrong? Your knowledge ends up destroying a brother or sister for whom the Messiah died. When you wound the conscience of a fellow believer like that you are sinning against the Messiah himself. So if what Paul eats is going to cause his brother or sister to stumble he will never eat meat again — he refuses to be the reason they fall.

What You Might Not Know

Some people are so used to idols that even now they think of the meat as a sacrifice to a real god — the weak believers Paul describes are most likely Gentile converts who had spent their whole lives in the world of pagan religion. Their past was saturated with the reality of idol worship — they had participated in temple meals they had offered sacrifices they had eaten the meat with full awareness of its religious significance. For these people eating the meat was not a neutral act. It was connected by memory and association to everything they had left behind. The theological argument that idols are nothing had not yet penetrated deeply enough to override those associations.

Their conscience is not fully settled in the truth — the Greek is hē syneidēsis autōn asthenēs ousa meaning their conscience being weak. A weak conscience is not a corrupted or morally defective conscience. It is a conscience that does not yet have the full theological grounding to evaluate a situation accurately. The weak believer's conscience tells them that eating the meat is wrong — not because it actually is wrong — but because their conscience is operating with an incomplete understanding. Acting against that conscience — even to do something objectively permissible — is damaging to them because it trains them to override their conscience which is a dangerous habit to develop.

Sitting down to eat in an idol's temple — Paul's concern is not just about eating meat purchased in the marketplace. He is specifically addressing the practice of eating in the temple precinct itself — attending banquets held in the dining rooms attached to Corinthian temples. This was a normal part of Corinthian social life — business associations guilds and social networks all held meals in these settings. The strong believer's attendance at such meals — eating meat that was explicitly part of the temple's sacrificial system in the temple's own dining room — was the specific behavior that was damaging the weak believer's conscience.

Your knowledge ends up destroying a brother or sister for whom the Messiah died — the word destroying is apollymi — the same word Paul used in Chapter 1 for those headed for destruction. The strong believer's theological sophistication is being used in a way that moves a fellow believer toward spiritual ruin. The knowledge that should have produced humility and care is instead producing pride and carelessness with devastating consequences. The person being destroyed is not an abstraction — they are someone for whom the Messiah gave his life. To treat them carelessly is to treat the Messiah's sacrifice carelessly.

I will never eat meat again — I refuse to be the reason they fall — Paul's personal commitment at the end of Chapter 8 is the most concrete expression of the principle he has been establishing. He is not talking abstractly about what someone with correct theology might choose to do. He is making a personal binding commitment — if my eating meat causes a brother or sister to stumble I will stop eating meat. The voluntary limitation of his own legitimate freedom for the sake of another person's spiritual wellbeing is the shape of love governing the use of knowledge.

Why It Still Matters

When you wound the conscience of a fellow believer like that you are sinning against the Messiah himself. Paul's identification of sin against a fellow believer with sin against the Messiah is one of the most theologically weighty statements in Chapter 8. The person whose conscience you wound carelessly is a person in whom the Messiah lives. To damage them is to damage someone the Messiah died for and someone the Messiah lives in. The strong believer who uses their freedom without regard for its effect on the weak has not simply made a social mistake. They have sinned — against a person and against the Messiah who identifies himself with that person. That identification should produce the kind of care that Paul demonstrates in his own commitment at the end of the chapter.

1 Corinthians Chapter 9

Paul Defends His Apostleship

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Am I not free? Am I not an apostle? Have I not seen Jesus our Master with my own eyes? And are not the Corinthians the living proof of Paul's work for him? Even if others do not accept him as an apostle the Corinthians certainly should — because they are the living seal that proves his mission. They exist because he came to them.

What You Might Not Know

Chapter 9 appears to interrupt the discussion of food sacrificed to idols that runs from Chapter 8 through Chapter 10. In fact it continues the argument by providing Paul's personal example of the principle he established in Chapter 8 — the voluntary limitation of legitimate rights for the sake of others. Paul had established the right to eat all foods. He had also established the principle that love sometimes requires setting that right aside. Now he demonstrates with his own apostolic conduct how that principle works in practice — he had rights he did not exercise and he explains why.

Am I not an apostle? Haven't I seen Jesus our Master with my own eyes? — The two questions are connected. Apostleship in the earliest Christian usage required direct commissioning by the risen Jesus. Paul's apostleship was questioned in Corinth — partly because he had not been one of the Twelve and partly because his refusal to accept financial support made him look like someone who knew his credentials were weak. Paul's defense is direct — he has seen the risen Jesus. The Damascus road encounter was the basis of his apostolic claim. The Corinthian church was the evidence that the claim was legitimate.

You are the living seal that proves my mission — the Greek word for seal is sphragis — the official mark stamped on a document to authenticate it. The Corinthian church was Paul's authentication document. Whatever anyone said about his credentials the existence of a community of people who had come to faith in Jesus through his preaching was the undeniable evidence that his apostleship was genuine. You cannot argue with a community of transformed lives. They exist because Paul came and preached and God worked through him.

Why It Still Matters

You are the living seal that proves my mission. Paul's identification of the Corinthian church as the authentication of his apostleship is one of the most important statements about the relationship between ministry and its fruit. The evidence of genuine apostolic and pastoral work is not the impressive credentials of the minister but the transformed lives of the people ministered to. The community that exists because someone came and faithfully proclaimed the Gospel is the most powerful possible testimony to the legitimacy of that person's calling. Credentials matter. Fruit matters more.

The Right to Be Supported

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

This is Paul's defense to those who want to put him on trial. Do we not have the right to food and drink? Do we not have the right to bring a believing wife along with us just like the other apostles the Master's brothers and Peter? Or is it only Barnabas and Paul who are required to work a day job to support themselves? Does a soldier go to war at his own expense? Who plants a vineyard and does not eat its grapes? Who shepherds a flock and does not drink some of the milk? The law says the same — do not muzzle an ox while it is treading out the grain. This was written for our sake. The one who plows and the one who threshes should both expect to share in the harvest. If we have sown spiritual seeds among you is it too much to expect some material support in return? If others have this right over you should we not have it even more? But we have not used this right — instead we put up with everything so we do not create any obstacle for the good news. Those who work in the temple get their food from the temple and those who serve at the altar share in the offerings. In the same way the Master commanded that those who preach the good news should make their living from it.

What You Might Not Know

The right to bring a believing wife along — Paul mentions in passing that the other apostles including Peter traveled with their wives who presumably were supported along with the apostles from the communities they visited. Peter's mother-in-law is mentioned in the Gospels — which confirms he was married. Paul was either unmarried or had been widowed. His mention of the other apostles' wives is not a complaint. It is an argument — if they have the right to travel with a wife and to be supported then surely Paul has the right to material support even without a wife.

Do not muzzle an ox while it is treading out the grain — Paul quotes Deuteronomy 25. In ancient grain threshing oxen walked over the grain to separate the kernels from the stalks. The ox that did the work was permitted to eat some of the grain it was processing. Paul's application is direct — the minister who does the work of the Gospel should share in the material resources the Gospel produces. He then adds the explicit teaching of Jesus — the Master commanded that those who preach the good news should make their living from it. Paul is not inventing this principle. He is appealing to both Scripture and the direct teaching of Jesus.

We put up with everything so we don't create any obstacle for the good news — this phrase reveals Paul's actual reason for not accepting support from Corinth. It was not that he lacked the right. It was that in Corinth's specific cultural context accepting payment would have made him look like one of the sophist teachers who charged fees for their eloquent performances. Taking money from the Corinthians would have associated his ministry with the competitive pay-for-wisdom culture that was producing the factions he was trying to address. He gave up his right because exercising it would have harmed the very mission the right was designed to support.

Why It Still Matters

Those who preach the good news should make their living from it. Paul's establishment of the principle of material support for ministry is one of the most important in 1 Corinthians for the ongoing life of the Church. The person who devotes their life to the work of the Gospel has a legitimate claim on the material support of the community that benefits from that work. Paul himself chose not to exercise this right in Corinth for specific strategic reasons. But he is careful to establish that the right exists and is grounded in Scripture and in the direct teaching of Jesus. Communities that treat their ministers as volunteers who should find their own support are violating a principle Paul considers non-negotiable even while he personally chose not to invoke it.

Paul Preaches Free of Charge

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Paul has not used any of these rights and he is not writing this now to ask for anything. He would rather die than have anyone take away what he takes pride in. Preaching the good news is not something he boasts about — it is something he has to do. Woe to him if he does not preach it. If he did this by his own choice he could expect a reward. But he did not choose this — it was entrusted to him. So what is his reward? Simply this — that in his preaching he can offer the good news completely free of charge without making full use of his rights as a preacher.

What You Might Not Know

I would rather die than have anyone take away what I take pride in — the thing Paul takes pride in is not his apostolic credentials or his theological knowledge or his missionary achievements. It is his ability to offer the Gospel free of charge. In a culture where teachers charged for their teaching Paul's refusal to charge was itself a proclamation — this message is not a product being sold. It is a gift being given. His pride in that giving is not vanity. It is the satisfaction of someone who has found a way to embody in the very act of ministry the grace he is proclaiming.

Woe to me if I don't preach it — the Greek is ouai gar moi estin ean mē euangelisomai — woe is to me if I do not announce the good news. The woe Paul pronounces on himself echoes the woes of the prophets. He is not speaking metaphorically. He feels the weight of a commission that was laid on him not chosen by him. The Damascus road encounter left him with a task he could not avoid without incurring the consequences of disobedience to a direct divine call. The preaching is not optional for Paul. It is as necessary as breathing.

It was entrusted to me — the Greek is pepisteumai meaning I have been trusted with it or it has been committed to me. The verb is the same root as faith — pistis. Paul has been faithed with the Gospel — entrusted with it as a steward is entrusted with a master's property. A steward does not choose what they are given to manage. They choose only how faithfully they manage it. Paul's reward is not payment for a service rendered by choice. It is the satisfaction of faithful stewardship — of offering freely what was freely given to him to give.

Why It Still Matters

Woe to me if I don't preach it. Paul's sense of compulsion in preaching is one of the marks of a genuinely called person. The person who preaches because they chose ministry as a career has a relationship to the work that is fundamentally different from the person who preaches because they cannot do otherwise — because the commission was laid on them and the woe of non-compliance is real to them. Paul did not preach for payment. He did not preach for recognition. He preached because not preaching was not an option he could live with. That kind of compulsion — not neurotic obligation but genuine commission — is the mark of apostolic calling in every generation.

All Things to All People

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

Even though Paul is a free man and answers to no one he has made himself a servant to everyone in order to win as many as possible. To the Jews he became like a Jew to win the Jews. To those under the law he became like one under the law — though he is not personally bound by it — to win those under the law. To those outside the law he became like one outside the law — though he is not without God's law but bound by the law of the Messiah — to win those outside the law. To the weak he became weak to win the weak. He has become all things to all people so that by every possible means he might save some. He does all this for the sake of the good news so that he can share in its blessings.

What You Might Not Know

To the Jews I became like a Jew — Paul's adaptability to different cultural contexts is grounded in his own freedom from those contexts. He is not a Jew trying to pass as a Gentile or a Gentile trying to adopt Jewish practice. He is a person whose identity in Christ gives him freedom from mandatory cultural identification — which paradoxically makes him able to adopt the cultural patterns of any group he is trying to reach without being dishonest. The circumcision of Timothy the Nazirite vow at Cenchreae the participation in Temple purification in Acts 21 — all of these are expressions of the same principle. In Jewish contexts Paul lived as a Jew. In Gentile contexts he lived without Jewish distinctives.

Not without God's law but bound by the law of the Messiah — Paul is careful to distinguish between his freedom from the Torah as a system of earning righteousness before God and his continued obligation to the moral law as fulfilled and summarized in the Messiah. Becoming like those outside the law does not mean becoming lawless. It means not imposing Jewish cultural and ritual practice on Gentiles as a condition of fellowship. The ethical content of the law — love for God and neighbor — remains binding in the form of the law of the Messiah.

To the weak I became weak — this is the specific application of the all things to all people principle to the situation Paul has been addressing in Chapters 8 and 9. The weak believers who cannot eat idol meat without guilty conscience are the weak he is describing. His willingness to refrain from eating meat entirely so as not to cause them to stumble is his concrete example of becoming weak with the weak. The theological sophistication of the strong is not what Paul deploys in ministry. He deploys whatever posture and practice enables him to genuinely connect with the person he is trying to reach.

So that by every possible means I might save some — Paul's goal is explicit — saving people. Every cultural adaptation every voluntary limitation of freedom every becoming-like is in service of that goal. The adaptability is not relativism or lack of conviction. It is strategic flexibility in service of an unwavering conviction. The Gospel Paul proclaimed did not change. The package it came in adapted to whoever was receiving it. The distinction between the unchanging content and the adaptable package is one of the most important in all of missiology.

Why It Still Matters

I have become all things to all people so that by every possible means I might save some. Paul's principle of contextual adaptation is one of the most discussed and most misunderstood in 1 Corinthians. It has been used to justify everything from genuine cultural sensitivity to unprincipled accommodation that abandons the Gospel's content in order to be more acceptable. Paul's own practice shows where the line is — he adapted his cultural presentation radically while maintaining the theological content absolutely. The cross remained offensive. The resurrection remained non-negotiable. The call to repentance and faith remained constant. What adapted was everything else — the cultural context the social posture the rhetorical approach. The line between content and container is the line Paul never crossed.

Run to Win

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 9
What Is Happening Here

In a race all the runners run but only one gets the prize. Run in such a way that you win. Everyone who competes trains with strict discipline — they do it for a prize that fades away but we do it for a prize that lasts forever. So Paul does not run without a clear goal. He does not box like someone just swinging at the air. He disciplines his body and keeps it under control so that after preaching to others he himself will not be disqualified.

What You Might Not Know

In a race all the runners run but only one gets the prize — the Isthmian Games were held near Corinth every two years and were second only to the Olympics in prestige in the ancient world. The Corinthians knew athletic competition from direct experience. Paul's use of athletic imagery would have landed immediately and concretely for his audience. The runner who runs without discipline the boxer who swings without aiming — these were recognizable failures of competitive athleticism. Paul applies them to the spiritual life with the same directness.

They do it for a prize that fades away — the Greek is phtharton stephanos meaning a perishable or corruptible crown. The victor's crown in ancient games was made of pine leaves or celery or olive branches depending on the specific games. It was an honor but a temporary one — the wreath would wither within days. The spiritual prize Paul is running for is incorruptible — it does not fade. The comparison makes the athletic discipline of the Greek athlete look reasonable and the spiritual discipline of the believer look even more reasonable by comparison. If an athlete trains that hard for a perishable crown how much more should the believer train for an imperishable one.

I discipline my body and keep it under control — the Greek is hypōpiazō meaning to beat under the eye — to give a black eye or to strike a decisive blow. The athletic metaphor continues — Paul is describing his relationship to his own physical appetites and desires as a fight in which he is the one throwing the punches. The body with its desires for comfort ease food and sexual gratification is not allowed to run his life. He runs it. The discipline Paul describes is not self-punishment. It is the trained self-mastery of a serious competitor who knows that undisciplined appetites will disqualify them from the prize.

After preaching to others I myself won't be disqualified — the Greek word for disqualified is adokimos meaning not approved or rejected after testing. This is one of the most sobering statements Paul makes about himself in any of his letters. He is not assuming his own spiritual security on the basis of his apostolic calling or his theological knowledge or his impressive missionary resume. He knows that the same moral drift he is warning the Corinthians about could in principle affect him. His discipline of his own body is the practice that guards against it. The preacher who does not practice what he preaches is moving toward disqualification.

Why It Still Matters

After preaching to others I myself won't be disqualified. Paul's concern about his own potential disqualification is one of the most important pastoral realities in 1 Corinthians. The person most at risk of the spiritual failure Paul has been warning the Corinthians about is not always the newest or least educated believer. Sometimes it is the most experienced and most gifted — the person whose very competence in ministry can produce a kind of carelessness about the disciplines that keep that ministry honest. Paul's discipline of his own body is his refusal to assume that his apostolic calling exempts him from the same vigilance he calls the Corinthians to. The race must be run to the end. The discipline must be maintained throughout.

1 Corinthians Chapter 10

Lessons from Israel's History

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

Paul does not want the Corinthians to forget what happened to their ancestors. They were all under the cloud of God's protection and they all walked through the sea on dry ground. In a sense they were all baptized into Moses' leadership through that cloud and that sea. They all ate the same spiritual food and drank the same spiritual drink — drinking from a spiritual Rock that followed them and that Rock was the Messiah. But even with all those blessings God was not pleased with most of them and their bodies ended up scattered across the wilderness. These things happened as a warning — so that the Corinthians would not crave evil things the way their ancestors did. Do not become idol worshipers like some of them were. Do not commit sexual immorality as some of them did — twenty-three thousand died in a single day. Do not test the Messiah as some of them did only to be destroyed by snakes. And do not grumble and complain as some of them did which led to their destruction. All these things happened to them as examples written down to warn us — the people living at the very end of the ages. So if you think you are standing firm be careful that you do not fall. No temptation has come your way that is not common to everyone. But God is faithful — he will never let you be tempted beyond what you can handle. When you are tempted he will always provide a way out so that you can endure it.

What You Might Not Know

They were all baptized into Moses' leadership through that cloud and that sea — Paul reads the Exodus narrative through a christological lens. The cloud that led Israel through the wilderness and the sea through which they passed were sacramental experiences — genuine encounters with divine grace that functioned for Israel the way baptism functions for the Church. Paul's point is not that Israel had the same sacraments as the Church. It is that Israel had genuine encounters with God's saving grace and still fell into the failures he is about to catalog. The Corinthians who were confident in their own sacramental participation — baptism and the Lord's Supper — needed to hear that the sacraments do not automatically protect against spiritual failure.

That Rock was the Messiah — Paul identifies the rock that Moses struck in the wilderness with the pre-incarnate Christ. There was a Jewish tradition that the rock traveled with Israel through the wilderness. Paul does not simply adopt that tradition — he transforms it by identifying the rock as Christ. The identification is grounded in the broader New Testament understanding that Christ was present and active in Israel's history before his incarnation. The Rock that sustained Israel in the wilderness was the same one whose death would sustain the world.

God was not pleased with most of them and their bodies ended up scattered across the wilderness — Paul is referring to the generation of Israelites who died in the wilderness between Egypt and Canaan. They had experienced the Exodus the cloud the sea the manna and the water from the rock. They had received extraordinary divine grace. And most of them died in the wilderness because of idolatry sexual immorality testing God and grumbling. The inventory of their failures is not accidental — each one corresponds to a failure Paul sees in the Corinthian community.

No temptation has come your way that is not common to everyone — the Greek is anthrōpinos meaning human or belonging to human experience. Paul is establishing that the temptations the Corinthians face are not unique to them or beyond what any human being can resist with God's help. This is important because the Corinthians' over-realized eschatology — their sense that they had already arrived at spiritual maturity — made them feel immune to the kinds of failures that had destroyed Israel. Paul punctures that immunity claim by pointing to the ordinary human nature of their temptations.

God will always provide a way out so that you can endure it — the Greek word for way out is ekbasis meaning an exit or a way through. Paul does not promise that God will remove the temptation. He promises that God will provide the means to endure it. The endurance is the point — not the removal of difficulty but the capacity to stand through it. This is a significant pastoral distinction. The person who expects God to eliminate their temptations will be disappointed. The person who looks for God's provision of the means to endure will find what was promised.

Why It Still Matters

So if you think you are standing firm be careful that you do not fall. Paul's warning to the confident is one of the most important in 1 Corinthians. The person most at risk of the failures Israel experienced is not the person who knows they are weak and dependent on God. It is the person who has concluded they are beyond that kind of danger. The Corinthians' confidence in their own spiritual status — their knowledge their gifts their sacramental participation — was precisely the kind of standing-firm self-assessment that Paul is warning against. History has consistently demonstrated that the generation most confident of its spiritual immunity is often the generation most vulnerable to spiritual failure.

Run from Idolatry

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

My dear friends — run away from idolatry. When we bless the cup of blessing is that not a shared participation in the blood of the Messiah? When we break the bread is that not a shared participation in the body of the Messiah? Because there is one loaf of bread we — though many — are one body for we all share that one loaf. Look at the people of Israel — do not those who eat the sacrifices participate in what happens at the altar? So what is Paul saying — that an idol is something real or that meat sacrificed to an idol is special? No. What pagans sacrifice they sacrifice to demons not to God. And he does not want the Corinthians anywhere near that. You cannot drink the cup of the Master and the cup of demons at the same time. You cannot sit at the Master's table and the table of demons. Are we trying to make the Master jealous? Do we really think we are stronger than he is?

What You Might Not Know

A shared participation in the blood and body of the Messiah — the Greek word is koinōnia meaning fellowship communion or participation. Paul is describing the Lord's Supper as a genuine participation in the Messiah — not merely a memorial of something past but a real sharing in his body and blood. The cup and the bread create a bond — a koinōnia — between the participants and the one whose body and blood they share. This bond is not abstract. It is the most intimate possible connection to the Messiah and through him to each other. This is the theological basis for the warning that follows — if the Lord's Supper creates genuine participation in Christ then sitting at a demon's table creates genuine participation in something incompatible with that bond.

What pagans sacrifice they sacrifice to demons not to God — this is Paul's most direct statement about the spiritual reality behind pagan idolatry. In Chapter 8 he established that an idol is nothing — the deity it represents does not exist. Here he goes further — behind the nonexistent deity there is a real spiritual power that is demonic. The idol is nothing. The spiritual force that may operate through idol worship is not nothing. The strong believer who concluded from Chapter 8 that eating idol meat anywhere was therefore morally neutral has missed this dimension of the problem.

You cannot drink the cup of the Master and the cup of demons at the same time — the incompatibility Paul asserts is not merely about the symbolism of the acts. It is about the spiritual realities the acts create participation in. The Lord's Supper creates participation in Christ. The temple banquet creates participation in whatever spiritual reality operates behind the idol. These two participations are mutually exclusive. The believer cannot be simultaneously bonded to Christ through the cup of blessing and bonded to demonic spiritual powers through the cup of the idol's temple. The physical act of eating creates a spiritual reality.

Are we trying to make the Master jealous — Paul quotes the language of Deuteronomy 32 — the Song of Moses that he also quoted in Romans 10. God's jealousy in the biblical tradition is not petty self-centeredness. It is the appropriate response of a covenant partner to the betrayal of the covenant bond. The exclusive loyalty the Master rightly expects from those who have been bonded to him through the cup and the bread is the basis for his jealousy when they pursue participation in competing spiritual powers. The Master's jealousy is the measure of how seriously he takes the bond created through the Lord's Supper.

Why It Still Matters

You cannot drink the cup of the Master and the cup of demons at the same time. Paul's absolute statement about incompatible participations is one of the clearest in 1 Corinthians. The Corinthians who were attending temple banquets — sitting at the demon's table — while also participating in the Lord's Supper — sitting at the Master's table — were not simply exercising their freedom as strong believers. They were attempting to maintain two incompatible bonds simultaneously. The Lord's Supper creates a participation in Christ that excludes competing participations. The person who understands what the Lord's Supper actually is will understand why Paul treats the temple banquet as a fundamental contradiction of it.

Freedom and the Good of Others

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

You say I am allowed to do anything — maybe so but not everything is good for you. You say I am allowed to do anything — but not everything builds people up. Stop looking out only for your own interests. Look out for the interests of others. Here is the practical rule — eat anything sold in the meat market without asking questions because the earth belongs to the Master and everything in it. If an unbeliever invites you to dinner and you want to go eat whatever is put in front of you without asking questions. But if someone says to you this meat was offered to an idol then do not eat it — not for your own sake but for the sake of the person who told you. I am talking about their conscience not yours. Why should my freedom be judged by someone else's conscience? If I give thanks to God for my food why should I be criticized for eating it?

What You Might Not Know

Eat anything sold in the meat market without asking questions — Paul establishes a default position for the strong believer navigating daily life in Corinth. The meat market — the macellum — was where meat from temple sacrifices was sold to the public alongside other meat. The strong believer does not need to interrogate the butcher about the provenance of every cut of meat. The earth belongs to the Lord — the theological reality that makes the idol nothing also makes the meat morally neutral in ordinary market transactions. Asking questions is not required and in fact draws unnecessary attention to distinctions that do not matter in that context.

If an unbeliever invites you to dinner — the scenario Paul describes is a private social dinner hosted by a non-believing acquaintance or business contact. This is different from a temple banquet — it is ordinary social hospitality not a religious event. The same default applies — eat what is served without asking questions. The Christian guest is not required to interrogate the host about where every dish came from. The fact that some of the food may have passed through a temple at some point in its journey to the table does not make the dinner a participation in idol worship.

But if someone says this meat was offered to an idol then don't eat it — the situation changes when someone at the dinner explicitly identifies the meat as sacrificial. The identification makes the issue visible and creates a context where eating becomes a statement. The person who told you — whether a weak believer present at the dinner or the host drawing attention to the religious significance of the meal — has made the meat a topic. In that context eating would communicate something Paul does not want communicated. Not eating communicates respect for the person who raised the issue.

Why should my freedom be judged by someone else's conscience — Paul is not saying the strong believer must always defer to the weak. He is navigating a specific situation where someone has spoken up. The principle is that in that specific moment the other person's conscience takes priority not because the strong believer's freedom is wrong but because exercising it in that specific moment would not build up the person who spoke. The freedom remains real. Its exercise is governed by love for the person whose conscience has been brought into the situation.

Why It Still Matters

Stop looking out only for your own interests. Look out for the interests of others. Paul's practical instruction about food decisions in various contexts is organized around this simple principle. The strong believer's default is freedom — eat whatever is available without anxiety. The modification of that default is love — when eating something will damage someone else do not eat it. The organizing principle throughout is not self-protection or personal righteousness but the wellbeing of the other. The Christian who asks in every food situation not what am I allowed to eat but what will serve the people I am with is living the principle Paul has been building toward throughout Chapters 8 through 10.

Do Everything for God's Glory

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 10
What Is Happening Here

So whether you eat or drink or whatever you do — do it all for the glory of God. Do not cause anyone to stumble — not Jews not Gentiles and not the church of God. Follow Paul's example. He tries to please everyone in every way — not looking for what is best for him but for what is best for the many so that they may be saved.

What You Might Not Know

Whether you eat or drink or whatever you do — do it all for the glory of God — this is one of the most comprehensive ethical principles in the New Testament. Paul has spent three chapters working through the specific question of food sacrificed to idols. He now summarizes the entire discussion with a principle that extends far beyond food. Every action — eating drinking working speaking relating — is an opportunity to bring glory to God or to fail to do so. The specifics matter — Paul has worked through the specifics carefully. But the specifics are all applications of this one organizing principle. Does this action glorify God? Does it serve the people around me? These two questions, held together, are the practical expression of love for God and love for neighbor.

Don't cause anyone to stumble — not Jews not Gentiles and not the church of God — Paul identifies three groups whose spiritual wellbeing the strong believer must consider. Jews — whose connection to the one God and whose sensitivity to idolatry makes certain behaviors potential obstacles to their reception of the Gospel. Gentiles — whose unfamiliarity with the Gospel makes the behavior of believers a primary source of their impressions of the Gospel. And the church of God — the community of believers whose faith can be damaged by careless exercise of freedom. The strong believer's actions have potential consequences for all three groups simultaneously.

I try to please everyone in every way — not looking for what is best for me but for what is best for the many so that they may be saved — Paul's personal practice is the living example of the principle he has established. He is not describing an abstract ideal. He is describing what he actually does. The all things to all people of Chapter 9 is the same principle as the do everything for God's glory of Chapter 10. Both describe a life organized around the wellbeing of others rather than around self-interest. The goal in both cases is the same — so that they may be saved. Every practical decision about food or drink or social participation is ultimately evaluated by its contribution to or interference with that goal.

Why It Still Matters

Whether you eat or drink or whatever you do — do it all for the glory of God. Paul's comprehensive principle at the end of Chapter 10 is the practical summary of the entire argument from Chapter 8 through Chapter 10. The question behind every decision — from what to eat at a dinner party to what to do about a legal dispute to how to use spiritual gifts — is whether it glorifies God and serves the people around you. Those two questions asked consistently would have resolved every problem Paul has been addressing in 1 Corinthians. The Corinthians were asking what am I allowed to do. Paul is teaching them to ask what will glorify God and serve others. The shift in question changes everything about the answer.

1 Corinthians Chapter 11

Follow My Example

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Follow my example just as I follow the example of the Messiah. Paul wants to commend the Corinthians for remembering him in everything and for holding to the traditions just as he passed them on. But he wants them to understand something important about how things are ordered — the head of every man is the Messiah the head of a woman is the man and the head of the Messiah is God.

What You Might Not Know

Follow my example just as I follow the example of the Messiah — this verse actually closes Chapter 10 in many ancient manuscripts before beginning Chapter 11. Its placement here connects Paul's personal example from Chapter 9 and 10 to the specific instructions that follow. The logic is the same throughout — Paul points to his own practice as a model not out of arrogance but because he genuinely lives what he teaches and because the Corinthians need a concrete embodied example to follow rather than abstract principles.

The head of every man is the Messiah the head of a woman is the man and the head of the Messiah is God — the Greek word for head is kephalē which in Greek can mean either the physical head or the source of something. The debate about whether Paul means authority or source has occupied scholars for decades. Both meanings are present in Greek usage. What is clear is that Paul is describing a relational ordering — not a ranking of worth or value — in which each relationship reflects and participates in the relationship between the Messiah and God. The head of the Messiah is God — which does not mean the Messiah is inferior to God but that he relates to the Father in a particular way characterized by willing submission. The ordering Paul describes in human relationships mirrors that divine ordering.

Why It Still Matters

The head of the Messiah is God. Paul's grounding of the human relational ordering in the relationship between the Messiah and God is one of the most important theological moves in Chapter 11. Whatever the ordering means in practice it does not mean inequality of worth or dignity — because the Messiah is not inferior to God in worth or dignity. The ordering is relational and functional not ontological. That distinction matters enormously for how the passage is applied. The woman whose head is the man is not less valuable than the man any more than the Messiah is less valuable than God.

Head Coverings

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Every man who prays or prophesies with his head covered brings shame on the Messiah. But every woman who prays or prophesies with her head uncovered brings shame on her husband — it is the same as if her head were shaved. If a woman will not cover her head she might as well cut all her hair off. But if it is a disgrace for a woman to have her hair cut short or her head shaved she should keep it covered. A man should not cover his head because he is the image and glory of God — but the woman is the glory of the man. Man did not come from woman but woman from man. And man was not created for the sake of the woman but woman for the sake of the man. Because of this and because the angels are watching a woman should wear a symbol of authority on her head. Even so in the Master woman is not independent of man and man is not independent of woman. Just as woman originally came from man now every man comes into the world through a woman — and everything ultimately comes from God. Judge for yourselves — is it proper for a woman to pray to God in public with her head uncovered? Does not nature itself teach that if a man has long hair it is a disgrace but if a woman has long hair it is her glory? Her hair is given to her as a natural covering. If anyone wants to argue about this just know that we have no other custom and neither do any of the other churches of God.

What You Might Not Know

Head coverings in the ancient world carried significant social meaning. In Roman culture a woman with an uncovered head in public was associated either with a prostitute or with a woman making a statement of social independence from her husband. In Jewish culture a married woman's covered head was a sign of modesty and honor within the covenant community. Paul is not primarily legislating a universal dress code. He is addressing a specific cultural situation in Corinth where certain women were apparently removing their head coverings during worship — possibly as an expression of the spiritual freedom and equality they had in Christ — in a way that was sending signals in their cultural context that Paul did not want the church to be sending.

Every woman who prays or prophesies — Paul's instructions assume that women were praying and prophesying in the Corinthian worship gatherings. This is significant. He does not tell them to stop. He tells them how to do it in a culturally appropriate way. Whatever the later instruction in Chapter 14 about women being silent means it cannot mean a blanket prohibition on women speaking in worship — because Chapter 11 explicitly assumes they are praying and prophesying and addresses only the question of head covering while they do so.

Because the angels are watching — this cryptic phrase has generated extensive discussion. The most likely interpretation is that the angels are the heavenly witnesses to the worship of God's people — the celestial court that observes the earthly church's worship. The ordering of creation — the relational structure Paul has been describing — is something the angels recognize and honor. The woman who disrupts that ordering in worship is doing so in front of witnesses whose perspective extends beyond the human social dynamics of the Corinthian assembly.

In the Master woman is not independent of man and man is not independent of woman — Paul qualifies the entire head covering argument with this statement of mutuality. Whatever the ordering he has described means it does not mean the independence of one from the other. The mutuality Paul established in Chapter 7 — neither the husband nor the wife has sole authority over their own body — reappears here. The ordering exists within a framework of profound interdependence. Neither is complete without the other and everything comes from God.

If anyone wants to argue about this we have no other custom — Paul's closing statement suggests that some in Corinth were inclined to argue about the head covering instruction. His response is not to provide more theological justification but to appeal to universal practice — this is how it is done everywhere in the churches of God. The universality of the practice across all his churches was itself an argument for maintaining it in Corinth. Whatever the specific theological rationale the consistency of practice across the church was a value Paul considered worth defending.

Why It Still Matters

In the Master woman is not independent of man and man is not independent of woman. Paul's statement of mutuality within the ordering he has described is the key to reading the head covering passage faithfully. The passage has been used to establish a hierarchy of worth that Paul explicitly does not intend. It has also been dismissed as entirely culturally relative with no ongoing significance. The more faithful reading is that Paul is addressing a specific cultural situation with a principle — that how we present ourselves in worship should reflect the relational ordering God has established — while insisting simultaneously on the profound mutuality and interdependence of men and women in the Lord. Both the principle and the mutuality are permanent. The specific cultural expression of covering or not covering belongs to its time.

Divisions at the Lord's Supper

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

Regarding what Paul is about to say — he cannot praise them. When they meet together it seems to do more harm than good. He hears that when they gather as a community there are cliques and divisions among them — and he believes it. There have to be factions among them so that those who are genuine can be clearly seen. When they get together they are not really eating the Master's Supper at all. Each of them rushes ahead to eat their own meal first — one person goes hungry while another gets drunk. Do they not have homes to eat and drink in? Or do they despise the church of God and want to humiliate those who have nothing? What can Paul say? Should he praise them for this? Absolutely not.

What You Might Not Know

When you meet together it seems to do more harm than good — the Greek is ouk eis to kreitton alla eis to hēsson synerchesthe meaning you come together not for the better but for the worse. The Lord's Supper was supposed to be the community's most unifying practice — the shared meal that expressed their common participation in Christ. Instead it was becoming a demonstration of the community's divisions. The gathering that should have been the community's greatest expression of unity was intensifying its greatest problem.

One person goes hungry while another gets drunk — the Lord's Supper in the early church was held in the context of a full meal — an agape feast or love feast. The Corinthian practice appears to have been that wealthier members who arrived earlier ate the food they had brought without waiting for poorer members — many of whom were slaves who could not arrive until their work obligations were fulfilled. By the time the poorer members arrived the food was gone and the wealthier members were already full or drunk. The social hierarchies of Corinthian culture were being reproduced at the meal that was supposed to transcend those hierarchies.

Do you despise the church of God and want to humiliate those who have nothing — Paul names what the behavior communicates even if it was not intended. The wealthy Corinthians who ate without waiting were not necessarily consciously intending to humiliate the poor. But the effect of their behavior was humiliation — the poor arrived to find nothing left while the rich sat satisfied. The unintended communication of contempt is still contempt as far as the person on the receiving end is concerned. Paul holds the wealthy Corinthians responsible for the message their behavior sent regardless of their intentions.

Why It Still Matters

When you come together it does more harm than good. Paul's indictment of the Corinthian Lord's Supper practice is one of the most uncomfortable passages in 1 Corinthians because it applies so directly to any community that reproduces social hierarchies within the worship gathering. The Lord's Supper is the community's declaration that all who participate are equally purchased by the same price — the body and blood of the Messiah. A practice of the Lord's Supper that leaves some members humiliated while others feast contradicts the very thing the Supper is supposed to proclaim. The meal that should level every social distinction becomes the occasion for reinforcing them. Paul cannot praise this. And he says so plainly.

The Lord's Supper

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

This is what Paul received from the Master and passed on to them — the Master Jesus on the night he was betrayed took bread. After giving thanks he broke it and said — this is my body which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me. In the same way after supper he took the cup and said — this cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this every time you drink it in remembrance of me. Every time you eat this bread and drink this cup you are announcing the Master's death until he comes back. So whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Master in an unworthy way is guilty of sinning against the body and blood of the Master.

What You Might Not Know

This is what I received from the Master and passed on to you — Paul's language of received and passed on is the technical language of oral tradition transmission in both Jewish and Greek contexts. Paul is describing himself as a link in a chain of transmission — he received this account and he passed it on faithfully. The account of the Lord's Supper in 1 Corinthians is the earliest written version of the institution narrative — predating the Gospel accounts by at least a decade. Paul received this tradition early in his Christian life — possibly from the Jerusalem apostles — and it was already in established form when he passed it to the Corinthians during his founding visit.

On the night he was betrayed — Paul's inclusion of this temporal detail is significant. The Lord's Supper was instituted in a context of betrayal — in the very hours when Judas was executing his plan and the disciples were about to scatter. The meal of remembrance and covenant was established in the darkest moment of Jesus's earthly ministry. Every subsequent celebration of the Lord's Supper carries that darkness with it — the awareness that the one being remembered was betrayed handed over and killed. The joy of the remembrance is inseparable from the sorrow of what made the remembrance necessary.

This cup is the new covenant in my blood — Paul's version of Jesus's words over the cup connects the Lord's Supper explicitly to the new covenant prophecy of Jeremiah 31. The old covenant at Sinai was sealed with blood — Moses sprinkled the blood of sacrifices on the people and declared this is the blood of the covenant. The new covenant is sealed with Jesus's own blood. Every cup at the Lord's Supper is a declaration that the new covenant — the one in which God writes his law on hearts and forgives sin completely — has been inaugurated through the death of Jesus.

You are announcing the Master's death until he comes back — the Lord's Supper has both a backward and a forward orientation. Backward — do this in remembrance of me — it looks to the past event of the cross. Forward — until he comes back — it looks to the future return. Between those two poles the community gathers regularly to announce what happened and to declare their expectation of what is coming. The Supper is not a private spiritual exercise. It is a public announcement — a proclamation to the watching world and to the spiritual powers that Jesus died and that his death matters and that he is coming again.

Why It Still Matters

Every time you eat this bread and drink this cup you are announcing the Master's death until he comes back. Paul's description of the Lord's Supper as an announcement — a proclamation — is one of the most important statements in 1 Corinthians about the meaning of the practice. The Supper is not merely a private moment of remembrance between an individual and God. It is a communal act of proclamation — the gathered community declaring together that Jesus died for sin and that he is coming again. Every celebration of the Lord's Supper is a small act of defiance against the powers that killed Jesus — a declaration that the cross was not the end and that the story is not over.

Examine Yourself First

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 11
What Is Happening Here

A person should examine themselves before eating the bread and drinking from the cup. Anyone who eats and drinks without recognizing what the body means is eating and drinking judgment upon themselves. This is why many of them are weak and sick and quite a few have even died. If they would honestly evaluate themselves they would not fall under judgment. But when the Master judges them he is disciplining them so that they will not be condemned along with the rest of the world. So then brothers and sisters — when they come together to eat they should wait for one another. If someone is starving they should eat at home first so that their meeting does not result in judgment. Paul will sort out the rest of the details when he arrives.

What You Might Not Know

A person should examine themselves before eating — the Greek word is dokimazō meaning to test or to evaluate carefully. Paul is not calling for an exhaustive spiritual inventory that would leave no one feeling qualified to participate. He is calling for honest self-awareness — specifically awareness of what the body means. The body is both the body of Christ in the bread and the body of Christ as the community gathered around the table. The person who eats without recognizing the community dimension — who consumes without waiting for others who eats while others go hungry — has failed to recognize what the body means.

This is why many of you are weak and sick and quite a few have even died — Paul connects physical illness and death among the Corinthians directly to their unworthy participation in the Lord's Supper. This is one of the most unusual statements in any of Paul's letters. He is describing divine discipline in physical form — the judgment that falls on those who participate in the covenant meal while acting in ways that contradict the covenant. Whether this is to be understood as direct divine action or as the natural consequences of community division and the spiritual damage it produces is debated. What is clear is that Paul treats the physical consequences as real and as connected to the spiritual failure.

When the Master judges us he is disciplining us so that we won't be condemned along with the rest of the world — the discipline Paul describes is not punitive in the final sense. It is corrective — the Father's hand on the child to redirect them before they go further in the wrong direction. The Corinthians who were experiencing weakness and illness were not being condemned. They were being disciplined — called to attention before the behavior became something worse. The discipline is evidence of belonging to the family rather than evidence of being cast out of it.

Wait for one another — the practical instruction that summarizes the entire section is remarkably simple. The wealthy Corinthians who were eating without waiting did not need a theology lecture — though Paul gave them one. They needed to wait. The solution to the social division being enacted at the Lord's Supper was the practice of waiting — holding back from eating until everyone was present and able to share together. The most basic act of community — making room for everyone before beginning — was what the Corinthian practice had abandoned.

Why It Still Matters

When you come together to eat wait for one another. Paul's simple instruction is the most practical application of everything he has said about the Lord's Supper. The Supper proclaims the death of a Lord who gave himself for all. The community that gathers to celebrate that Supper must embody in its practice the same self-giving — waiting for the last to arrive before the first begins to eat. The Lord's Supper is not just a spiritual practice. It is a social practice whose shape should visibly contradict the social hierarchies of the surrounding culture. When it reproduces those hierarchies instead it has failed to be what it is — an announcement of the Master's death until he comes back.

1 Corinthians Chapter 12

Don't Be Confused About Spiritual Gifts

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Paul does not want the Corinthians to be confused about spiritual gifts. They remember that back when they were pagans they were led astray by silent idols following wherever they were pushed. So he wants to make this clear — no one speaking by the Spirit of God ever says Jesus is cursed and no one can truly say Jesus is Master except by the Holy Spirit.

What You Might Not Know

You were led astray by silent idols — the contrast between the silent idols and the speaking Spirit is deliberate. The idols of the pagan world did not speak. Any supposed communication from them came through human intermediaries — priests oracles dream interpreters — who could be manipulated or deceived. The Spirit of God actually communicates — actually produces speech and gifts and transformation. The Corinthians who had come from that background needed a reliable way to distinguish genuine Spirit-activity from its counterfeits — which is what Paul is about to provide.

No one speaking by the Spirit of God ever says Jesus is cursed — this statement raises the question of who might have been saying this in Corinth. It may refer to people in the ecstatic state of pagan worship who were speaking under spiritual influence but not the Spirit of God. It may refer to Jewish opponents of the Christian community. Or it may be a hypothetical test case Paul is establishing to make a principle clear — whatever the source of the spiritual experience the content of what is said is the test. The Spirit of God will not produce speech that dishonors Jesus.

No one can truly say Jesus is Master except by the Holy Spirit — the confession Jesus is Lord — Kyrios Iesous — was the foundational Christian confession. Paul has been using the term Master throughout 1 Corinthians for Jesus. The ability to genuinely make this confession — not just to say the words but to mean them from the heart — is itself a work of the Spirit. The test Paul establishes is bidirectional. Speech that dishonors Jesus is not from the Spirit. Speech that genuinely confesses Jesus as Lord is produced by the Spirit. The content of the confession is the most basic criterion of genuine Spirit-activity.

Why It Still Matters

No one can truly say Jesus is Master except by the Holy Spirit. Paul's criterion for genuine spiritual experience is centered on the person of Jesus rather than on the intensity of the experience or the impressiveness of the accompanying phenomena. The Corinthians who were impressed by dramatic spiritual manifestations needed to hear that the test of whether something is from the Spirit is not how dramatic it is but what it does with Jesus. A spiritual experience that leads people toward genuine submission to Jesus as Lord is from the Spirit. An experience that leaves people more impressed with themselves than with Jesus raises questions regardless of how dramatic it was.

One Spirit, Many Gifts

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

There are different kinds of spiritual gifts but they all come from the same Spirit. There are different ways to serve but all serve the same Master. There are different ways that God works but it is the same God who powers everything in everyone. The Spirit shows up in each person for the good of the whole group. To one the Spirit gives a message of wisdom. To another a message of knowledge from that same Spirit. To someone else special faith. To another gifts of healing. One person receives the power to work miracles another to prophesy another to tell the difference between spirits another to speak in different languages and another to translate those languages. But the one and only Spirit is behind all of these — giving them to each person exactly as he chooses.

What You Might Not Know

Different kinds of spiritual gifts — different ways to serve — different ways that God works — Paul uses three different Greek words to describe the diversity of Spirit-activity. Charismata — grace-gifts — rooted in the word charis meaning grace. Diakoniai — services or ministries. Energēmata — workings or operations. The three terms suggest that what Paul is describing is not a narrow list of supernatural phenomena but a comprehensive account of how the Spirit operates in the community — through dramatic gifts through practical service and through the less visible workings of God's power in and through people. The gift list that follows illustrates rather than exhausts the Spirit's work.

The Spirit shows up in each person for the good of the whole group — the Greek is pros to sympheron meaning for the benefit or for the profit. Every manifestation of the Spirit in every person has as its purpose the building up of the whole community. This is the foundational principle that Paul will apply throughout Chapters 12 through 14. The test of a spiritual gift is not how impressive it is or how good it makes the person who has it feel. The test is whether it serves the common good. A gift that builds only the individual who has it and does nothing for the community is not functioning as Paul describes spiritual gifts functioning.

The gift list — Paul's list includes wisdom knowledge faith healing miracles prophecy discerning of spirits tongues and interpretation of tongues. This list differs from the gift lists in Romans 12 and Ephesians 4 — they overlap but are not identical. Paul is not providing an exhaustive catalog of all possible spiritual gifts. He is illustrating the diversity of Spirit-activity. The diversity itself is the point — the Spirit does not give everyone the same gift and the community needs the full range of what the Spirit distributes. No single gift defines the Spirit's presence and no single person has all the gifts.

Giving them to each person exactly as he chooses — the distribution of gifts is the Spirit's prerogative not the believer's achievement or choice. You cannot earn a particular gift by being more spiritual than someone else. You cannot acquire a gift by practicing harder. The Spirit distributes as he wills — which means the person who has a particular gift has it because the Spirit gave it not because they deserved it. And the person who lacks a particular gift lacks it because the Spirit has given them something else not because they are spiritually deficient.

Why It Still Matters

The Spirit shows up in each person for the good of the whole group. Paul's purpose statement for spiritual gifts is the corrective to every use of gifts for personal spiritual status. The Corinthians were apparently ranking gifts by their impressiveness and competing for the most spectacular ones — particularly tongues which seems to have been the most valued in their community. Paul redirects the entire discussion — the question is not which gift is most impressive but which gifts serve the community most effectively in a given situation. The Spirit's distribution is designed to serve the whole not to distinguish the spiritually elite from the spiritually ordinary.

One Body, Many Parts

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

Think of it this way — the human body is one unit but it has many parts. Even though there are many parts they all make up one body. It is the same with the Messiah. We were all baptized by one Spirit into one body — whether Jews or Gentiles slaves or free — and we were all given that one Spirit to drink. The body is not just one part. If the foot says I am not a hand so I do not belong to the body that does not make it any less a part of the body. If the ear says I am not an eye so I do not belong that is still part of the body. If the whole body were an eye how would you hear? If the whole body were an ear how would you smell? God has placed each part in the body exactly where he wanted it. The eye cannot say to the hand I do not need you. The head cannot say to the feet I do not need you. In fact the parts that seem weakest are the most necessary. The parts thought less honorable we treat with extra care. We give special attention to the parts we would rather not show while the parts we are proud of do not need that. God put the body together so that more honor goes to the parts that had less — so there would be no division in the body but every part would have the same concern for the others. If one part suffers every part suffers. If one part is honored every part celebrates.

What You Might Not Know

The body metaphor was not invented by Paul. It was a common image in ancient political philosophy — Livy and Plutarch both record versions of the story of the body and its members being used to argue for the interdependence of social classes. Paul takes a familiar image and fills it with new content. In the political versions the image was used to argue that lower classes should accept their subordinate position for the good of the whole. Paul's use is different — he uses the same image to argue that the apparently weaker and less honorable parts are the most necessary and deserve the most care. The political image of hierarchy becomes Paul's image of mutual honor and interdependence.

We were all baptized by one Spirit into one body — whether Jews or Gentiles slaves or free — the diversity within the body of the Messiah is not just the diversity of gifts. It is the diversity of people from every social category — the most fundamental social divisions of the ancient world — gathered into one body by one Spirit. The baptism Paul describes is not water baptism alone but the Spirit's work of incorporating every believer into the one body regardless of their social background. The social divisions that structured Corinthian life were supposed to be transcended in the body of the Messiah.

The parts that seem weakest are actually the most necessary — Paul's reversal of the body's honor structure is one of the most counterintuitive moves in Chapter 12. The parts of the body we consider most important — the eyes the brain the heart — are the most protected and most attended to. The parts considered least important are the most exposed. But Paul inverts this — the seemingly weakest parts are actually the most necessary. Applied to the community the gifts and people that the Corinthians were dismissing as less impressive may be the ones the community cannot function without. The person with the gift of helps or administration — less spectacular than tongues or prophecy — may be more essential to the body's actual functioning.

If one part suffers every part suffers — the solidarity Paul describes is not a sentiment. It is a structural reality of the body. When one part of the physical body is injured the whole body's resources are redirected toward that injury. Pain in one place registers throughout. The application to the community is direct — a member of the body who is suffering is not suffering alone. The body's appropriate response is to redirect its attention and resources toward the suffering member the way the physical body responds to pain. The Corinthian community that was humiliating its poorer members at the Lord's Supper was inflicting suffering on part of its own body.

Why It Still Matters

If one part suffers every part suffers. If one part is honored every part celebrates. Paul's description of the body's solidarity is one of the most powerful visions of community in the New Testament. The community that genuinely lives this way looks radically different from the surrounding culture — and from most communities in general. When a member of the community suffers the response is not sympathy from a distance but the redirection of the whole community's resources and attention toward that suffering. When a member is honored the community does not feel diminished by their honor but celebrates with them. That kind of community does not happen naturally. It is the result of understanding what the body of the Messiah actually is and choosing to live accordingly.

You Are the Body of the Messiah

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 12
What Is Happening Here

You are the body of the Messiah — and each one of you is a specific part of it. In the church God has appointed first apostles second prophets third teachers then those who work miracles then those with gifts of healing those who help others those who lead and organize and those who speak in different languages. Is everyone an apostle? No. Is everyone a prophet? No. Is everyone a teacher? No. Does everyone work miracles or have gifts of healing? No. Does everyone speak in languages or translate them? Of course not. You should value the greater gifts deeply. And now Paul is going to show them a way that is better than all of them.

What You Might Not Know

You are the body of the Messiah — and each one of you is a specific part of it — the Greek word for specific part is meros meaning a portion or a member. Each believer in Corinth is a specific identifiable part of the body — not interchangeable with other parts not redundant not optional. The word each is emphatic — each one. No one in the community is without a specific role and a specific belonging. The person who felt they did not belong because they lacked the spectacular gifts — the foot that said it was not a hand — is addressed directly. They are a specific part. Their belonging is not conditional on having the right gift.

God has appointed first apostles second prophets third teachers — Paul lists roles in order and the order is significant. Apostles first prophets second teachers third. The gifts that involve the most sustained engagement with the community's foundational life — apostolic mission prophetic direction and doctrinal teaching — are placed highest. The more spectacular gifts — miracles healing tongues — appear later in the list. The Corinthians had it backwards — they were most impressed by the most spectacular gifts and least impressed by the less dramatic ones. Paul's ordering corrects that evaluation without dismissing any of the gifts.

Is everyone an apostle? Is everyone a prophet? — The rapid-fire rhetorical questions with their implied negative answers are Paul's way of establishing the obvious point that diversity of gifting is the design not the problem. The Corinthians who were treating tongues as the supreme gift and treating those without it as spiritually deficient had missed the point entirely. The body does not consist of all eyes or all ears. It consists of many different parts each essential in its own way. The uniformity the Corinthians seemed to want would not produce a more spiritual community. It would produce a non-functional one.

I'm going to show you a way that is better than all of them — the transition at the end of Chapter 12 is one of the most famous in Paul's letters. He has been discussing spiritual gifts. He is about to pivot to something he considers superior to any specific gift. The way he is pointing toward is not a better gift. It is the framework within which all gifts must operate to fulfill their purpose. The pivot prepares for what is universally recognized as the most beautiful passage in 1 Corinthians — and arguably in all of Paul's letters.

Why It Still Matters

You are the body of the Messiah — and each one of you is a specific part of it. Paul's declaration about the identity of the Corinthian community is one of the most important in 1 Corinthians. They were a fractured community divided by factions intellectual pride social hierarchy and competition over gifts. Paul declares over all of that — you are the body of the Messiah. Not you should try to become the body or you will be the body if you resolve your problems. You are. The identity precedes the behavior Paul is calling them to. The call to live as the body is grounded in the declaration that they already are the body. That is still the most powerful basis for calling any community to live differently than it currently does.

1 Corinthians Chapter 13

The Greatest Gift

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Even if Paul could speak every human language and the languages of angels but does not have love — he is nothing more than a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal. Even if he has the gift of prophecy and understands every deep mystery and possesses all knowledge — and even if he has enough faith to move mountains — but does not have love he is nothing at all. Even if he gives away everything he owns to feed the poor and even if he hands over his body to be burned — but does not have love it means nothing.

What You Might Not Know

Chapter 13 is the better way Paul promised at the end of Chapter 12. He did not say he would show them a better gift. He said he would show them a better way — a better manner of living and operating in the community. Love is not a spiritual gift in the same category as tongues or prophecy or healing. It is the framework within which all gifts must function to accomplish their purpose. Without love the gifts become instruments of pride faction and division — exactly what the Corinthian gifts had become. Chapter 13 is not a detour from the discussion of spiritual gifts. It is the answer to what has gone wrong with them in Corinth.

A noisy gong or a clanging cymbal — the instruments Paul mentions were associated with pagan religious worship in Corinth. The mystery cult of Cybele used cymbals and gongs to produce the ecstatic state in worshipers. Paul may be drawing a deliberate contrast — the spectacular spiritual speech of tongues without love sounds no different from the noise of pagan religious ecstasy. It is sound without meaning sound without relationship sound that impresses but does not build. The comparison would have been pointed for the Corinthians who prided themselves on their spiritual sophistication.

Even if I have enough faith to move mountains — the echo of Jesus's teaching about faith and mountains is deliberate. Paul is taking the highest possible expression of spiritual power — the kind of faith Jesus himself described — and saying that even that without love produces nothing. The point is not that faith is unimportant. It is that faith operating without love has been severed from the context that gives it its purpose. A faith that moves mountains but does not serve the person standing next to you has misunderstood what faith is for.

Even if I give away everything I own to feed the poor — Paul's inclusion of extreme generosity in the list of things that count for nothing without love is perhaps the most surprising. Giving away all your possessions to feed the poor looks like love. It may be love. But it can also be performed without love — as a demonstration of one's own spiritual seriousness or as a way of gaining status in the community. The action without the interior reality of love for the person being served is philanthropy without the heart that makes philanthropy genuine.

Even if I hand over my body to be burned — some manuscripts read even if I hand over my body so that I may boast rather than to be burned — which would make the meaning more obviously about pride than martyrdom. Either reading points to the same conclusion — even the most extreme personal sacrifice can be performed without love. Martyrdom for the sake of the community's admiration or one's own spiritual reputation is not the same as martyrdom that flows from genuine love for God and others. The act without the love behind it means nothing.

Why It Still Matters

Even if I give away everything I own to feed the poor but don't have love it means nothing. Paul's list of things that count for nothing without love is a direct challenge to every form of spiritual performance that substitutes impressive activity for genuine love. The Corinthians were competing in spiritual performance — whose tongues were more eloquent whose knowledge was deeper whose faith was more dramatic. Paul says all of it is nothing without love. That nothing is absolute. Not diminished. Not incomplete. Nothing. The community that replaces love with impressive spiritual activity has not achieved something less than it should have. It has achieved nothing at all.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 13

What Love Looks Like

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Love is patient. Love is kind. Love is not jealous. It does not brag and it is not arrogant. It is not rude. It does not push for its own way. It is not quick-tempered and it does not keep a record of wrongs. Love does not celebrate when things are unfair — but it celebrates whenever truth wins. Love protects everything trusts everything hopes for everything and endures everything.

What You Might Not Know

Paul's description of love in Chapter 13 is not an abstract definition. It is a portrait of the Corinthian community's failures viewed through a positive lens. Every quality of love Paul names corresponds to a failure Paul has already identified in the letter. Love is not jealous — the Corinthians had jealousy and fighting. Love does not brag and is not arrogant — the Corinthians were arrogant. Love is not rude — the wealthy Corinthians were humiliating the poor at the Lord's Supper. Love does not push for its own way — the strong believers were exercising their freedom without regard for the weak. Love does not keep a record of wrongs — the factions were doing exactly that. Chapter 13 is a diagnosis of the Corinthian church written in the positive vocabulary of what they should have been.

Love is patient — the Greek word is makrothymei meaning to be long-tempered or to have a long fuse. It is the opposite of the quick-tempered quality Paul names a few phrases later. Patience in the biblical sense is not passive endurance of difficulty. It is the active choice not to retaliate when you have been wronged — to extend the same long-suffering toward others that God extends toward us. The Corinthians who were suing each other in court were demonstrating the opposite of this patience.

Love does not keep a record of wrongs — the Greek is ou logizetai to kakon meaning does not count up evil or does not reckon wrongdoing. The accounting language — logizetai — is the same word used in Romans 4 for God crediting righteousness to Abraham and in Chapter 4 for God not counting sin against us. Love operates by the same accounting logic that God uses — not keeping a running total of offenses against the person who has wronged you. The community that keeps detailed accounts of who wronged whom cannot experience the kind of reconciliation Paul is calling for.

Love celebrates whenever truth wins — the Greek is sygchairei de tē alētheia meaning rejoices with the truth. Truth is not merely correct information in Paul's usage. It is the reality of who God is and what he has done — the Gospel itself. Love rejoices when the Gospel advances when people are set free when truth is honored and lived. The contrast with celebrating when things are unfair — rejoicing over another person's downfall or failure — is direct. Love's joy is never at another person's expense.

Love protects everything trusts everything hopes for everything and endures everything — the four all things clauses close the description of love with a comprehensive reach. The Greek word for protects is stegei meaning to cover or to bear a burden without complaining — it can mean either to protect by covering or to endure by bearing. The four clauses together describe a love that is inexhaustible — that does not run out of protection trust hope or endurance no matter what the circumstances.

Why It Still Matters

Love does not keep a record of wrongs. The Corinthian community was keeping careful records — of which faction you belonged to of who had wronged whom of who was more spiritually gifted than whom. Paul's description of love is the systematic dismantling of every accounting system the Corinthians had built. Love does not count offenses. It does not compare gifts. It does not rank members. It does not celebrate another's failure. Every specific failure Paul has addressed in the letter is addressed again here — not by prohibition but by the positive description of its opposite. This is what you should have been. This is what love looks like. This is what the Spirit produces when gifts are governed by love rather than operating in service of pride.

Love Never Fails

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 13
What Is Happening Here

Love never fails. Prophecies will come to an end. Tongues will stop. Knowledge will be surpassed. Right now we only know in part and we only prophesy in part. But when what is perfect arrives these partial things will be set aside. When Paul was a child he talked like a child thought like a child and reasoned like a child. But when he became a man he put those childish ways behind him. Right now we are looking into a dim mirror seeing only a blurry reflection. But then we will see face to face. Right now his knowledge is partial — but then he will know fully just as he is fully known by God. So three things remain — faith hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

What You Might Not Know

Love never fails — the Greek is hē agapē oudepote piptei meaning love never falls or love never comes to an end. The verb piptō can mean to fall or to fail or to come to nothing. Every gift Paul has described in Chapter 12 has a shelf life — they serve purposes in the present age that will no longer be needed when the age to come arrives. Love has no shelf life. It is the permanent reality of the kingdom of God that perdures through the transition from this age to the next. The gifts are temporary instruments. Love is the permanent reality they are meant to serve.

Prophecies will come to an end — tongues will stop — knowledge will be surpassed — the three gifts Paul specifically names as temporary are the three that the Corinthians most prized — prophecy tongues and knowledge. The gifts they were most competitive about are the gifts with an expiration date. Paul is not saying these gifts are invalid now. He is saying they are partial — instruments suited to the present age of incomplete knowledge and incomplete revelation. When the complete arrives the partial becomes unnecessary.

When what is perfect arrives — the Greek is to teleion meaning the complete or the mature or the perfect. What Paul means by this has been debated extensively. The most common interpretations are the completion of the New Testament canon the return of Christ or the age to come. The contrast Paul draws — partial knowledge now versus full knowledge then — and the face to face imagery both point toward the return of Christ and the age to come rather than toward a historical event within the present age. The full knowledge Paul describes is not the knowledge of having a complete Bible. It is the knowledge of seeing God face to face.

Right now we are looking into a dim mirror seeing only a blurry reflection — Corinth was famous in the ancient world for its bronze mirrors which were among the best available. Even the finest ancient mirror produced a less clear reflection than a modern glass mirror. Paul's image is of looking at a reflection rather than at the thing itself — knowledge mediated through partial revelation rather than direct encounter. The dim mirror is not bad or false. It is simply less than the face to face vision that is coming. Present knowledge and prophecy are reliable but partial. The fullness of knowing is still ahead.

I will know fully just as I am fully known by God — the asymmetry is significant. Right now God knows Paul fully — completely comprehensively without remainder. Paul knows God partially — through a dim mirror through partial prophecy and knowledge. The promise of the age to come is that the knowledge will become mutual — Paul will know God as fully as God knows Paul. That full mutual knowledge is what the gifts of prophecy and knowledge are pointing toward but cannot themselves deliver. Love is the relational reality that will characterize that full knowing — which is why love is the greatest of the three that remain.

Three things remain — faith hope and love — the three that survive the transition from this age to the next are described as remaining or abiding — menei. Unlike the gifts that will become unnecessary faith hope and love persist. Whether faith and hope persist in their present form into the age to come or whether they are transformed — hope into fulfillment faith into sight — is debated. What Paul is clear about is that love is the greatest of the three. Love is the ultimate reality of God himself — the character of the one who is love — and therefore the most permanent and most fundamental of all.

Why It Still Matters

So three things remain — faith hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. Paul's conclusion to Chapter 13 is the conclusion to the entire argument from Chapter 8 through Chapter 14. The Corinthians were pursuing the spectacular gifts with the energy and competition of people who had found the ultimate spiritual treasure. Paul has shown them that the spectacular gifts are temporary instruments suited to the present partial age. The ultimate spiritual treasure — the reality that endures beyond this age into the age to come — is love. Not because love is a warm feeling or a therapeutic orientation toward others. But because love is the character of God himself — the reality that was present before any gift existed and will remain when all gifts have served their purpose and been set aside.

1 Corinthians Chapter 14

Make Love Your Goal

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Make love your primary goal. At the same time truly desire spiritual gifts — especially the ability to prophesy to speak God's truth clearly. Someone who speaks in a tongue is not speaking to people but to God. No one understands them — they are speaking deep mysteries in the Spirit. But the person who prophesies speaks to people to build them up encourage them and comfort them. The person speaking in a tongue builds up themselves but the person who prophesies builds up the entire church. Paul would be happy if all of them spoke in tongues — but he would much rather they prophesy. The one who prophesies is more useful than the one who speaks in tongues unless someone translates so the whole church can learn something.

What You Might Not Know

Make love your primary goal — the Greek is diōkete tēn agapēn meaning pursue love or chase after love. The same verb is used for persecution — to pursue something with intense deliberate energy. Paul is not saying be nice or have warm feelings toward people. He is saying direct the same intense focused energy toward love that a persecutor directs toward their target. This is the opening of Chapter 14 because Chapter 13 ended with love as the greatest. Now love is the goal to be pursued — actively aggressively intentionally — as the framework within which the spiritual gifts are to function.

Especially the ability to prophesy — Paul's preference for prophecy over tongues is the organizing principle of Chapter 14. Throughout the chapter he will argue that prophecy serves the community better than tongues because prophecy communicates intelligibly to everyone present while tongues communicates only to God unless interpreted. This is not a condemnation of tongues — Paul explicitly says he speaks in tongues more than any of them and he explicitly forbids the Corinthians from forbidding tongues. It is a prioritization based on the criterion established in Chapter 12 — what builds up the community is more valuable than what builds up only the individual.

Someone who speaks in a tongue isn't speaking to people but to God — Paul's description of tongues as directed toward God rather than toward people is important for understanding what he means by the gift. Tongues in 1 Corinthians is not primarily a missionary gift — a supernatural ability to speak in foreign human languages for evangelism as in Acts 2. It is a form of prayer and praise directed toward God — a Spirit-enabled form of communication that bypasses the normal cognitive processes and speaks directly from the spirit to God. The person speaking in a tongue is worshiping not teaching.

The person who prophesies speaks to people to build them up encourage them and comfort them — the three purposes Paul gives for prophecy — oikodomē paraklēsis paramythia — are building up encouragement and comfort. These are the three things the community needs that tongues cannot provide without interpretation. Prophecy meets the community where it is and speaks into its actual situation — strengthening the weak encouraging the discouraged comforting the grieving. The community-oriented character of prophecy is precisely what makes it more valuable in the gathered worship setting than tongues.

Why It Still Matters

The person speaking in a tongue builds up themselves but the person who prophesies builds up the entire church. Paul's distinction between the gift that builds the individual and the gift that builds the community is the principle that should govern the use of every spiritual gift in worship. The question is not which gift produces the most impressive or emotionally satisfying experience for the person who has it. The question is which gift serves the people gathered. A community that organizes its worship around the most personally satisfying spiritual experiences rather than around what most effectively builds up everyone present has misunderstood what gathered worship is for.

Make Yourself Understood

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Brothers and sisters — if Paul comes to them speaking in tongues how does that help them? It only helps if he brings a revelation some knowledge a prophecy or a clear teaching. Even with instruments like a flute or a harp — if the notes are not clear how will anyone recognize the tune? If a bugle gives a confused signal who is going to prepare for battle? It is the same with them. If they do not speak clear understandable words they are just talking into the air. There are all kinds of languages in the world and none of them are without meaning. But if you do not understand the language being spoken you are a foreigner to the speaker and the speaker is a foreigner to you. So since they are so eager for spiritual gifts they should focus on the ones that build up the church.

What You Might Not Know

Paul uses three analogies to establish the principle of intelligibility — the musical instrument the military bugle and the foreign language. Each illustrates the same point from a different angle. The flute or harp that produces indistinct sounds cannot be recognized as music. The bugle that gives a confused signal cannot direct soldiers for battle. The foreign language that the hearer does not understand creates mutual incomprehension rather than communication. All three analogies establish that communication requires both a message and a receiver capable of receiving it. Tongues without interpretation has the message but lacks the receiver — the community cannot receive what it cannot understand.

If a bugle gives a confused signal who is going to prepare for battle — the military analogy is particularly vivid. Ancient armies communicated through bugle calls — different patterns of notes signaled different maneuvers. A bugle player who improvised rather than playing the recognized signal would not produce inspired music. They would produce chaos on the battlefield. The analogy applies directly — the gathered community needs clear intelligible communication to know how to respond. Unintelligible speech however Spirit-inspired cannot direct the community any more than a confused bugle signal can direct an army.

You are a foreigner to the speaker and the speaker is a foreigner to you — the Greek word is barbaros from which we get barbarian. In the ancient world a barbarian was literally someone whose language sounded like bar-bar — meaningless syllables to the non-speaker. Paul uses the word without its negative connotations — it simply describes the condition of mutual incomprehension between people who do not share a language. The tongues speaker and the non-understanding hearer are in the same position as two people who do not share a language. Neither is at fault. But the communication has failed regardless.

Why It Still Matters

If you don't speak clear understandable words you're just talking into the air. Paul's standard for evaluating speech in the gathered community is radical in its simplicity — can people understand what is being said? The Corinthians who were filling their worship gatherings with impressive spiritual speech that many present could not understand were not building the community. They were talking into the air — producing sound that satisfied the speaker and perhaps impressed observers but communicated nothing to most of the people present. The principle applies beyond tongues to any form of speech in worship — theological jargon technical language insider references — that communicates only to those already inside the circle while leaving others as foreigners.

What Is Happening Here

Anyone who speaks in a tongue should pray for the ability to interpret it. If Paul prays in a tongue his spirit is praying — but his mind is completely uninvolved. So what should he do? He will pray with his spirit and he will also pray with his mind. He will sing with his spirit and he will also sing with his mind. Otherwise if you give a blessing in the spirit how can someone else say Amen to your prayer of thanks? They have no idea what you are saying. You might be giving a wonderful prayer but the other person is not being built up at all. Paul thanks God that he speaks in tongues more than any of them. But in a church gathering he would rather speak five understandable words to teach others than ten thousand words in a tongue.

What You Might Not Know

My spirit is praying but my mind is completely uninvolved — the Greek is ho de nous mou akarpos estin meaning but my mind is unfruitful or my mind produces nothing. Paul is describing tongues as a form of prayer in which the spirit engages with God directly while the cognitive faculty — the nous — is not processing the content. This is why interpretation is necessary — the tongues-speaker themselves may not know in cognitive terms what they have prayed unless interpretation is given. Paul does not treat this as a deficiency to be criticized. He treats it as a feature to be managed — pray with the spirit and also pray with the mind. Both have their place.

How can someone else say Amen to your prayer of thanks — the Amen at the end of a prayer was the community's affirmation of what had been prayed. It was a communal act — the gathered people declaring their agreement with and participation in the prayer that had been offered. If the prayer was in tongues without interpretation the community could not participate in it because they did not know what had been prayed. The one who prayed had an experience with God. The community was excluded from it. The purpose of gathered prayer — communal engagement with God — was not served.

I thank God that I speak in tongues more than any of you — this statement is often overlooked in discussions of Chapter 14. Paul is not dismissing tongues or arguing against them from a position of not having the gift. He has the gift more extensively than any of the Corinthians who were making it the centerpiece of their worship. His argument for the priority of intelligible speech is coming from someone who prays in tongues extensively in his private devotional life. His point is the distinction between private spiritual practice and gathered community worship — what serves the individual in private does not automatically serve the community in public.

Why It Still Matters

I will pray with my spirit and I will also pray with my mind. Paul's both-and approach to prayer is one of the most balanced statements in Chapter 14. He does not pit spirit against mind or emotion against reason. He does not say the spirit-prayer is inferior to the mind-prayer or vice versa. He says both. The full person — spirit and mind — is engaged in prayer. The community in gathered worship needs the mind-engaged prayer because only mind-engaged prayer can be received and affirmed by those present. The spirit-engaged prayer has its place — in personal devotion where the community's comprehension is not the issue. The distinction is not about which kind of prayer is more spiritual. It is about which kind serves which context.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

Brothers and sisters — do not be childish in thinking. Be innocent when it comes to evil but be mature in how you think. It is written in the law — by people of strange tongues and by the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people and even then they will not listen to me says the Lord. The secrets of their heart are laid bare and they will fall on their face and worship God declaring — God is truly among you.

What You Might Not Know

Don't be childish in your thinking — Paul's accusation of childishness connects to Chapter 3 where he called the Corinthians infants who needed milk rather than solid food. The Corinthians who were treating tongues as the supreme spiritual gift and filling their gatherings with uninterpreted tongues speech were being childish in their thinking — prioritizing what was personally impressive and exciting over what actually served the community. Childishness in this context is not lack of sophistication. It is the inability or unwillingness to think beyond one's own immediate experience and preferences to consider their effect on others.

By people of strange tongues and by the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people — Paul quotes Isaiah 28 where God warned Israel that if they refused to hear his word in their own language he would speak to them through the incomprehensible speech of foreign conquerors — the Assyrians. The tongues of foreign nations would be God's judgment on a people who would not listen. Paul applies this to the sign character of tongues — tongues function as a sign to unbelievers not as edification for believers. The unintelligible speech is a sign that something significant is happening — but it does not build up those who hear it.

Won't they say you've all lost your minds — the Greek is mainesthe meaning you are mad or you have gone crazy. An outsider walking into a Corinthian worship service where everyone was simultaneously speaking in tongues would have recognized the behavior as similar to the ecstatic worship of pagan mystery cults — the kind of religious frenzy associated with the worship of Dionysus or Cybele. Rather than communicating that the living God was present the tongues-filled gathering would communicate that these people were no different from the frenzied pagans whose worship was familiar in Corinth.

They will fall on their face and worship God declaring God is truly among you — the contrast with the tongues scenario is complete. When prophecy is operating — when God's truth is being spoken intelligibly into the actual situations of the people present — an outsider who walks in can receive it. The conviction Paul describes — the secrets of their heart being laid bare — is the Spirit's work through intelligible proclamation. The result is worship — the outsider recognizing that they have encountered the living God and responding appropriately. This is the evangelistic power of prophecy that tongues without interpretation cannot produce.

Why It Still Matters

If everyone is prophesying and an unbeliever walks in they are convicted by what they hear. The secrets of their heart are laid bare and they will fall on their face and worship God. Paul's vision of the evangelistic power of prophecy in the gathered community is one of the most striking in the New Testament. The gathered community that is speaking God's truth intelligibly and specifically into actual human situations becomes a place where outsiders encounter God directly. The worship gathering is not only for the community already gathered. It is potentially the space where the outsider who wanders in has their heart exposed and their life redirected. That possibility requires intelligible speech — speech that can be received understood and responded to by someone who does not yet know God.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 14
What Is Happening Here

If anyone speaks in a tongue limit it to two or three at most one at a time and someone must interpret. If there is no interpreter they should stay quiet and speak only to themselves and to God. What You Might Not Know

When you meet together everyone has something to contribute — Paul's description of the Corinthian worship gathering is one of the most participatory in the New Testament. It was not a passive audience watching a performance by designated leaders. It was a gathering in which multiple people contributed — psalms teachings revelations tongues interpretations. The order Paul is establishing is not to reduce participation but to make the participation functional — to ensure that the many contributions actually build up the community rather than producing chaos.

Let two or three prophets speak and let the others weigh what is said — the principle of weighing or evaluating prophetic speech is important. Prophecy was not to be received uncritically. The community had the responsibility to evaluate what was spoken — to test it against what they knew of God's character and the Gospel's content. This is the application in practice of the discernment of spirits mentioned in Chapter 12. Prophetic speech is genuine Spirit-activity but it passes through a human being and can be mixed with human misunderstanding or agenda. The community's responsibility to weigh it protects against both naivety and the misuse of prophetic authority.

The spirit of a prophet is under the prophet's own control — the Greek is pneumata prophētōn prophētais hypotassetai meaning the spirits of prophets are subject to prophets. This is a direct challenge to the Corinthian assumption that spiritual experience was uncontrollable — that if the Spirit moved you to speak you had no choice but to speak and you had no responsibility for the timing or the effect. Paul asserts that the Spirit's gifting does not override human self-control. The prophet who says they could not stop themselves from interrupting because the Spirit was moving them has misunderstood both the Spirit and their own responsibility.

God is not a God of disorder but of peace — the Greek is akatastasias — tumult confusion instability. The God who designed the body of Christ with each part functioning in its proper place — who gives gifts for the common good — does not produce chaos when those gifts are exercised. The disorder in the Corinthian worship gathering was not a sign of the Spirit's abundance. It was evidence that the gifts were being exercised outside the framework of love and mutual service for which they were given. The God of peace produces peace — and the community's worship should reflect that character.

Why It Still Matters

God is not a God of disorder but of peace. Paul's statement about the character of God as the basis for orderly worship is one of the most theologically grounded arguments in Chapter 14. The order Paul calls for is not organizational preference or cultural convention. It reflects the character of the God being worshiped. A community whose worship is chaotic and self-serving is not displaying the character of the God it claims to know. A community whose worship is ordered toward the building up of everyone present — where each person contributes and each contribution is evaluated and no one monopolizes and everyone can learn — is reflecting in its gathered life the peace and order of the God who designed the body to function as one.

What You Might Not Know

Women should keep quiet during the church gathering — this instruction creates an immediate tension with Chapter 11 where Paul gave instructions for women praying and prophesying in the gathered worship — instructions that assume the activity and address only the manner of it. A blanket prohibition on women speaking would contradict Chapter 11 directly. The most likely resolution is that Paul is addressing a specific disruptive behavior in the Corinthian context rather than issuing a universal prohibition on all speech by women. The instruction to ask their husbands at home suggests the specific behavior was asking questions during the evaluation of prophecy — interrupting the weighing process with questions that could be handled privately.

They are not permitted to speak out of turn — some interpreters read the Greek lalein as a specific kind of speech rather than all speech — the chattering or question-asking that was disrupting the evaluation of prophecy. In the context of the preceding instruction about prophets speaking in turn and others weighing what is said a group of women asking evaluative questions — or asking their husbands to explain what was being said — would have been exactly the kind of disruption Paul addresses. The instruction is for order in the specific context of the prophetic evaluation process not a universal prohibition on women speaking in any church context.

Did the word of God start with you — Paul's sharp question cuts against any Corinthian faction that might resist his instructions on the basis of its own spiritual authority or uniqueness. The word of God did not originate in Corinth. It came to them through Paul and through the apostolic tradition. Their spiritual gifts and experiences do not give them the authority to override the instruction of the one who brought them the Gospel. The question is pointed but not angry — it is Paul reminding a community prone to over-realized eschatology that they are part of a larger story they did not begin.

Be eager to prophesy and do not forbid speaking in tongues — Paul's final instructions on spiritual gifts maintain the balance he has been establishing throughout the chapter. He does not end with a prohibition but with a positive encouragement toward what builds up combined with a protection of what is genuine even when easily misused. The eagerness for prophecy and the protection of tongues both serve the principle that has governed the entire discussion — the Spirit's gifts are genuine and valuable and should be pursued and exercised in ways that build up the community.

Why It Still Matters

Just make sure everything is done in a proper and orderly way. Paul's closing instruction for Chapter 14 is the summary of everything he has said about worship and spiritual gifts. The proper and orderly way is not a bureaucratic standard imposed from outside. It is the organic expression of love for the community — the commitment to exercise every gift in every gathering in the way that most effectively builds up the people present. Order is not the enemy of the Spirit. It is the shape the Spirit's work takes when love governs the use of gifts. The community that is truly led by the Spirit will find that its gatherings become more ordered not less — because the Spirit who gives the gifts also produces the love that makes their orderly use possible and natural.

1 Corinthians Chapter 15

The Heart of the Good News

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Paul wants to remind the Corinthians of the good news he preached to them. They received it built their lives on it and it is what saves them — provided they hold firmly to the message. Otherwise their faith was just for show. Here is the core of what was passed on to him and what he passed on to them — the Messiah died for our sins exactly as the Scriptures predicted. He was buried. He was raised to life on the third day exactly as the Scriptures predicted. And he was seen — by Peter then by the Twelve then by more than five hundred brothers and sisters at the same time most of whom are still alive though some have died. Then he appeared to James then to all the apostles. Last of all as if to someone born at the wrong time he appeared to Paul. Paul is the least of the apostles — he does not even deserve the title because he used to persecute the church of God. But by the grace of God he is what he is. His grace toward Paul was not wasted — he has worked harder than all of them though it was not really him but the grace of God working through him. Regardless of who preached it this is what they proclaim and this is what they believed.

What You Might Not Know

Here is the core of what I passed on to you which was passed on to me — Paul uses the same technical language of tradition transmission as in Chapter 11 — received and passed on. The formula he is about to recite was already established before Paul wrote 1 Corinthians. Scholars generally date the tradition he is quoting to within a few years of the crucifixion — possibly as early as Paul's visit to Jerusalem three years after his conversion described in Galatians 1. The resurrection tradition Paul cites is among the earliest dateable material in the entire New Testament — closer to the events than any of the Gospel accounts.

The Messiah died for our sins exactly as the Scriptures predicted — the four-part formula Paul recites — died buried raised appeared — is the most compact summary of the Gospel in the New Testament. Each element is stated as historical fact. The death is qualified — for our sins — giving it its theological meaning. The burial establishes the reality of the death — buried people are really dead. The resurrection is dated — on the third day — anchoring it to a specific historical moment. The appearances are listed with named witnesses — providing the kind of evidence that could in principle be checked by anyone willing to investigate.

He appeared to more than five hundred brothers and sisters at the same time most of whom are still alive — this statement is one of the most remarkable in the New Testament as evidence for the historicity of the resurrection. Paul is writing while many of the five hundred witnesses are still alive — which means the claim could be checked. He is not appealing to distant historical reports that cannot be verified. He is pointing to living people who could be asked directly. If the appearances were fabricated this would be a staggeringly reckless claim to make in a letter that would circulate to the very communities where such checking was most likely to happen.

As if to someone born at the wrong time he appeared to me — the Greek is hōsperei tō ektrōmati meaning as to an abortion or a premature birth. Paul uses this startling image to describe his own experience of the risen Christ. He was not one of the original disciples who knew Jesus during his earthly ministry. His encounter with the risen Christ came after the ascension — out of sequence with the other appearances listed. The image of a premature birth captures the sense of being brought into something before you were ready and in a way that was painful and disorienting. Paul's apostleship was real but it arrived through a disruption rather than through the normal process.

By the grace of God I am what I am — this phrase is one of the most theologically precise self-descriptions in Paul's letters. Everything Paul is — apostle missionary theologian church planter — is the result of grace operating in a person who by his own prior conduct had forfeited any claim to such a role. The grace did not make him passive — he worked harder than all the other apostles. But the working was itself grace-powered — it was not really me but the grace of God working through me. The combination of intense effort and complete attribution to grace is characteristic of Paul's understanding of the Christian life.

Why It Still Matters

The Messiah died for our sins exactly as the Scriptures predicted. He was buried. He was raised to life on the third day exactly as the Scriptures predicted. And he was seen. Paul's four-part summary of the Gospel is still the irreducible core of Christian proclamation. Not a philosophy not a moral system not a set of spiritual practices but a series of events that happened to a specific person at a specific time in a specific place and were witnessed by specific people. The Gospel's claim is historical before it is theological. The history is what makes the theology possible. And the history is what Paul is defending in the rest of Chapter 15 against those in Corinth who were denying the resurrection of the dead.

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

If we preach that the Messiah was raised from the dead how can some of them say there is no resurrection of the dead? If there is no resurrection then the Messiah was not raised either. And if the Messiah was not raised our preaching is useless and your faith is empty. More than that — we would be liars about God because we testified that he raised the Messiah which he would not have done if the dead are not raised. If the dead are not raised then your faith is a delusion and you are still trapped in your sins. It would also mean that everyone who died believing in the Messiah is simply gone. If our hope in the Messiah is only for this life we are the most pitiful people on earth.

What You Might Not Know

Some of you say there is no resurrection of the dead — the problem Paul is addressing is not straightforward atheism or rejection of the Gospel. It is a more subtle theological error — some Corinthians apparently believed in Christ and in some form of spiritual salvation while denying the bodily resurrection of the dead. This was consistent with the Greek philosophical framework that treated the body as inferior to the soul. In this view the spiritual reality was what mattered — perhaps the soul survived death — but the body was not worth resurrecting. Paul's response is to show that you cannot accept the resurrection of Jesus while denying the general resurrection — they stand or fall together.

Our preaching is useless and your faith is empty — the Greek words are kenos meaning hollow or empty and mataia meaning purposeless or futile. Paul is not using hyperbole. He is making a logical argument. If the dead are not raised then Jesus was not raised. If Jesus was not raised then the Gospel is not true. If the Gospel is not true then faith in it is misplaced. The entire structure of Christian existence — the peace with God the hope of glory the Spirit's indwelling the ethical transformation — all of it rests on the historical reality of the resurrection. Remove the resurrection and you remove the foundation of everything built on it.

We would be liars about God — this is one of Paul's most direct stakes-raising statements. The apostles were not offering helpful spiritual insights or useful moral guidance. They were making truth claims about what God had done in history. If those claims are false the apostles are not merely mistaken — they are liars. False testimony about God is not a minor theological error. It is a fundamental violation of the integrity that should characterize those who speak in God's name. Paul is forcing the Corinthians to understand that the resurrection is not a peripheral doctrine they can quietly set aside. It is the claim on which the truthfulness of everything else depends.

If our hope in the Messiah is only for this life we are the most pitiful people on earth — the Greek is eleeinoteroi pantōn anthrōpōn meaning more pitiable than all people. Paul is making a wry observation. The Christian life involves real costs — suffering persecution self-denial the voluntary limitation of freedom. If none of it leads anywhere beyond death then the Christians who have paid those costs for a false hope are in a worse position than people who paid no costs at all. A comfortable pagan who enjoyed life without the Gospel's demands would be better off than a believer who suffered for a resurrection that never happened.

Why It Still Matters

If the Messiah was not raised our preaching is useless and your faith is empty. Paul's logical dismantling of the resurrection-denying position is still the most effective response to the same error in any generation. The attempt to have Christian faith without bodily resurrection — to keep the ethical teaching the spiritual community the sense of transcendence while setting aside the historical claim of the empty tomb — is not a sophisticated update of Christianity. It is the removal of the foundation. Everything built on a false foundation will eventually collapse. Paul is not being rigid or unimaginative. He is being honest about what the removal of the resurrection actually means for everything else the community claimed to believe.

What Is Happening Here

But the truth is — the Messiah has been raised from the dead. He is the first of the harvest — the guarantee that those who have died will follow. Since death came through one man the resurrection of the dead also comes through one man. Just as everyone dies because of Adam everyone will be made alive because of the Messiah. What You Might Not Know

He is the first of the harvest — the Greek is aparchē meaning firstfruits — the first portion of the harvest that represents and guarantees the rest. The image comes from the Jewish practice of offering the first of the harvest to God as a dedication of the whole. Jesus's resurrection is not an isolated miracle. It is the first installment of a larger harvest — the guarantee that everyone who belongs to him will follow in resurrection. The firstfruits and the rest of the harvest are the same kind of thing. The resurrection of Jesus and the resurrection of believers are the same kind of event — not a unique miracle that has no implications for others but the beginning of a resurrection harvest of which believers are the continuation.

The very last enemy to be destroyed is death — Paul's eschatological vision is comprehensive. The resurrection of Christ has begun the defeat of the powers that hold humanity in bondage. Sin was dealt with at the cross. Death will be dealt with at the resurrection of the dead. The order of events — Christ's resurrection now the resurrection of believers at his return then the defeat of all remaining powers then the handing of the kingdom to the Father — describes the progressive completion of a victory that was decisively won at the resurrection but is not yet fully implemented. Death is real and still operating. But it is already a defeated enemy operating on borrowed time.

Why do people get baptized on behalf of the dead — this is one of the most obscure references in 1 Corinthians. Paul mentions a practice apparently known in Corinth of people being baptized vicariously for those who had already died — presumably to extend to the dead whatever benefits baptism conveyed. Paul does not endorse this practice or explain it fully. He uses it as a logical argument — if there is no resurrection this practice makes no sense at all. It shows that even those who denied the resurrection were implicitly operating with assumptions that required it. Paul cites their own practice as evidence against their theological position.

If I fought wild beasts in Ephesus for purely human reasons what did I gain — whether Paul literally fought wild animals in the Ephesian arena or whether this is a metaphor for the intense human opposition he faced in Ephesus is debated. He says in 2 Corinthians that he faced death repeatedly and in Acts 19 the Ephesian situation was genuinely life-threatening. Either way the point is the same — the suffering Paul endured in ministry was only worth enduring if the resurrection is real. If there is no resurrection then the rational response to dangerous missionary work is to stop doing it and enjoy life while you can. The eat and drink for tomorrow we die quotation from Isaiah 22 is the logical conclusion of a world without resurrection.

Why It Still Matters

But the truth is — the Messiah has been raised from the dead. The but that opens this section is the pivot of the entire chapter — and arguably of the entire letter. Paul has spent the previous section methodically demonstrating what follows if the resurrection is false. Now he asserts that it is true. The assertion is not a retreat from the logical argument. It is the conclusion of it. The empty tomb the appearances the transformed lives of the witnesses the willingness to die for the claim — all of it converges on the same conclusion. The Messiah has been raised. That past-tense perfect — has been raised — describes an event with permanent ongoing consequences. He is still raised. Death has not reclaimed him. And because he is still raised the harvest of which he is the firstfruits is still coming.

What Is Happening Here

What You Might Not Know

When you plant a seed it doesn't grow unless it first dies — Paul's seed analogy addresses the philosophical objection to bodily resurrection — if the body decays how can it be raised? The objection assumes that the resurrection body must be materially identical to the body that died. Paul's seed analogy challenges that assumption. The plant that grows from a seed is genuinely continuous with the seed — it comes from the seed it is the seed's development — but it looks completely different from what was planted. The resurrection body will be genuinely continuous with the present body — the same person raised — but transformed into something that cannot be predicted from the appearance of the present body.

Buried in decay — raised in immortality — Paul's four contrasts between the burial and resurrection conditions are among the most theologically rich in the chapter. Decay versus immortality — the present body is subject to entropy and death the resurrection body is not. Shame versus glory — the present body is associated with weakness failure and mortality the resurrection body will reflect the glory of God. Weakness versus power — the present body is limited exhaustible and eventually fails the resurrection body will be energized by a different power source entirely. Natural versus spiritual — the present body is animated by biological life the resurrection body will be animated by the Spirit.

A natural body — raised a spiritual body — the Greek is sōma psychikon raised sōma pneumatikon. The natural body is literally a soul-body — a body animated by the psyche or natural life. The spiritual body is a body animated by the Spirit — by the same Spirit who raised Jesus from the dead. Paul is not describing a body that is spiritual in the sense of being immaterial or ghostly. He is describing a body animated by a different power source — the Spirit rather than biological life. The resurrection body is more physical than the present body in the sense that it is more real and more permanent — not less physical in the sense of being spiritual-instead-of-material.

The last Adam — the Messiah — became a life-giving spirit — Paul's Adam-Christ parallel from Romans 5 reappears here applied specifically to the resurrection. The first Adam received life — he became a living soul. The last Adam — Jesus — gives life. His resurrection is not only the restoration of his own life but the source of resurrection life for all who belong to him. The direction of movement is reversed — Adam received life from God and then transmitted death to all his descendants. Jesus received death from Adam's descendants and then transmits life to all who belong to him.

Why It Still Matters

Just as we have carried the image of the man of dust we will also carry the image of the Heavenly Man. Paul's vision of the resurrection is not the restoration of the present mortal body to what it was before death. It is the transformation of the present body into the image of the risen Christ. The resurrection body will bear the same relationship to the present body that the plant bears to the seed — continuous but transformed beyond recognition. That transformation is not a loss but a gain — the mortal puts on immortality the weak puts on power the natural puts on the spiritual. The destination of the human body is not dust but glory — the image of the one who rose from the dead and who is already what every believer will one day become.

We Will All Be Changed

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

Listen carefully brothers and sisters — flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom of God. What decays cannot inherit what lasts forever. Paul is telling them a mystery — we will not all die but we will all be changed. It will happen in an instant in the blink of an eye at the sound of the last trumpet. The trumpet will blast the dead will be raised immortal and we will be transformed. This body that decays must be clothed with a body that never will. When that happens the prophecy will come true — Death has been swallowed up in victory. O Death where is your victory? O Death where is your sting? The sting of death is sin and the power of sin is the law. But thank God — he gives us the victory through our Master Jesus the Messiah.

What You Might Not Know

We won't all die but we will all be changed — Paul anticipates a generation of believers who will still be alive when the Lord returns. For those believers the transformation from mortal to immortal will not come through death and resurrection but through a direct transformation of the living body. Both paths lead to the same destination — the resurrection body clothed with immortality. Whether you arrive there through death and resurrection or through direct transformation at the return of Christ the result is the same. The universality of the change — we will all be changed — is as absolute as the universality of the problem — everyone dies because of Adam.

In an instant in the blink of an eye at the sound of the last trumpet — Paul emphasizes the suddenness and finality of the transformation. The Greek is en atomō en rhipē ophthalmou — in an atom of time in the twinkling of an eye. The transformation will not be a gradual process. It will be instantaneous — the passage from mortality to immortality accomplished in less time than it takes the eye to register a flash of light. The trumpet was the signal in Jewish apocalyptic tradition for the final gathering of God's people — the moment when history reaches its appointed culmination.

Death has been swallowed up in victory — Paul combines Isaiah 25 — he will swallow up death forever — and Hosea 13 — O Death where are your plagues O Sheol where is your destruction — into a single triumphant cry. Both prophets had looked forward to a future when death's power would be broken. Paul announces that the mechanism of that breaking has already been established in the resurrection of Christ. The cry O Death where is your victory is not a question seeking an answer. It is a taunt — the declaration of a victor over a defeated enemy. Death's sting — the fact that it leads to condemnation before God — has been removed because sin has been dealt with at the cross.

The sting of death is sin and the power of sin is the law — Paul compresses a sophisticated theological argument into a single sentence. Death stings — is made fatal — because sin earns death and the law makes sin's penalty explicit. Remove sin's guilt and death loses its ultimate threat. The resurrection does not remove the fact of death — believers still die. It removes death's sting — the condemnation that made death the ultimate enemy. The person who dies in Christ dies into the resurrection rather than into condemnation. Death is still real but it is no longer the final word.

Why It Still Matters

O Death where is your victory? O Death where is your sting? Paul's taunt at death is one of the most defiant statements in the New Testament — and one of the most pastorally significant. The community that stands at a graveside and speaks these words is not denying the reality of death or the reality of grief. It is asserting that death does not have the last word. The last word belongs to the God who raised Jesus from the dead and who will raise everyone who belongs to Jesus at the last trumpet. The taunt at death is the community's declaration that it knows how the story ends — and that knowledge changes everything about how it faces the story's most frightening chapter.

Stand Firm

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 15
What Is Happening Here

So then my dear brothers and sisters — stand firm. Let nothing move you. Always give yourselves fully to the work of the Master because you know that your hard work for him is never a waste.

What You Might Not Know

Stand firm — the Greek is hedraioi ginesthe meaning become stable or become firmly planted. The command uses the same root as the word for foundation — the same foundation Paul laid in Chapter 3. The Corinthians who had been shaken by false teaching about the resurrection unstable in their factions and easily moved by impressive personalities are called to become immovable — rooted in the resurrection reality that the whole chapter has been establishing. The stability Paul calls for is not the rigidity of someone who has stopped thinking. It is the security of someone who stands on ground they know cannot give way.

Let nothing move you — the Greek is ametakinētoi meaning immovable or unable to be shifted. The Corinthians had been moved by many things — by the rhetoric of impressive teachers by social pressure toward the factions by Greek philosophical assumptions about the body and resurrection. Paul's call to immovability is a call to stop being moved by things that are less than the resurrection — things that will pass away while the resurrection hope endures. The person who is genuinely settled in the resurrection reality is free from the anxiety that makes communities susceptible to every new teaching and every impressive personality.

Always give yourselves fully to the work of the Master — the Greek is perisseuontes en tō ergō tou Kyriou pantote meaning abounding or overflowing in the work of the Lord always. The resurrection is not only a future hope — it is the present motivation for full engagement in the work the Lord has given. The Corinthians who had been using the Lord's Supper as an occasion for social display their spiritual gifts as occasions for personal status and their freedom as an occasion for self-indulgence are called to direct all that energy toward the work — the service the building up of the community the proclamation of the Gospel.

Your hard work for him is never a waste — the Greek is ouk estin kenos meaning is not empty or hollow — the same word Paul used for the faith that would be empty if the resurrection were false. The work is not empty because the resurrection is real. Every act of love every gift exercised for the community every suffering endured for the Gospel every sacrifice made in service of others — none of it is wasted because the resurrection guarantees that it all counts. The future that the resurrection opens is the future into which all present faithful work flows and finds its completion.

Why It Still Matters

Your hard work for him is never a waste. Paul closes the resurrection chapter and the theological heart of 1 Corinthians with one of the most encouraging statements in the New Testament. The community that has heard about its divisions its sexual immorality its lawsuits its abuse of the Lord's Supper its misuse of spiritual gifts and its denial of the resurrection now hears that none of the work done faithfully in the Lord is empty. The resurrection is the guarantee. What is done for the Lord in the present age — however modest however invisible however costly — is not wasted because it is being deposited into a future that cannot be taken away. Stand firm. Let nothing move you. The work matters. The resurrection guarantees it.

1 Corinthians Chapter 16

The Collection for God's People

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Regarding the collection for God's people — follow the same instructions Paul gave to the churches in Galatia. On the first day of every week each person should set aside a portion of their income saving it up according to how much they have been blessed. Do this so that no rushed collections have to be made when Paul arrives. When he gets there he will send the people they approve — with letters of introduction — to take their gift to Jerusalem. If it seems right for him to go too they can travel with him.

What You Might Not Know

The collection for God's people — this is the same Jerusalem collection Paul describes in Romans 15 and 2 Corinthians 8 and 9. It was one of Paul's most sustained pastoral projects — a financial offering gathered from Gentile churches across Macedonia Achaia and Asia to be delivered to the poor among the believers in Jerusalem. For Paul the collection was not primarily charity. It was a theological statement — the Gentile churches returning material blessing to the Jewish community from which the Gospel had come. The collection embodied in practice the unity of Jewish and Gentile believers that Paul had argued for theologically throughout his letters.

On the first day of every week — Paul's instruction to set aside the offering on the first day of the week is one of the earliest references to Sunday as the primary gathering day for Christians. The resurrection had happened on the first day of the week. The Troas community gathered on the first day of the week in Acts 20. Now Paul instructs the collection to be organized around the first day of the week. The rhythm of Christian life — gathering remembering the resurrection giving generously — was already taking its weekly shape around the day the Lord had risen.

According to how much you've been blessed — the Greek is hoti an euodōtai meaning as one may prosper or according to whatever prosperity God has given. Paul does not set a percentage or a fixed amount. He sets a principle — proportional generosity. The person who has been greatly blessed gives more. The person who has been less blessed gives less. Both give — the collection is not optional — but the amount is calibrated to what God has given rather than to a fixed external standard. This proportional principle appears throughout Paul's teaching on giving.

The people you approve with letters of introduction — the community would choose their own representatives to carry the offering to Jerusalem. Paul would not handle the money himself — his consistent practice was to avoid any appearance of financial impropriety. The letters of introduction were the ancient world's equivalent of financial accountability documentation — official community authorization of the people entrusted with the funds. Paul's care about the integrity of the financial process is one of the marks of his pastoral conscientiousness throughout his ministry.

Why It Still Matters

On the first day of every week each of you should set aside a portion of your income saving it up according to how much you have been blessed. Paul's instruction for systematic proportional giving is one of the most practical in 1 Corinthians. The occasional rushed collection is less faithful than the regular disciplined habit of setting aside what belongs to God before it gets absorbed into everything else. The principle of giving regularly proportionally and planned in advance rather than spontaneously and whatever happens to be left over is as applicable now as it was in Corinth. The community that practices it will find that generosity becomes a habit rather than a crisis response.

Paul's Travel Plans

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Paul plans to visit after passing through Macedonia. He might stay for a while or even spend the winter so that they can help send him on his way to wherever he goes next. He does not want to just see them in passing — he hopes to spend some real time with them if the Master permits. He will be staying in Ephesus until Pentecost because a great door for ministry has opened there even though there is strong opposition.

What You Might Not Know

I don't want to just see you in passing — I hope to spend some real time with you — the Greek is chronon tina epimenō meaning to remain for some time. Paul is not planning a speaking tour with brief stops at each location. He is planning a genuine pastoral visit — extended time with a community he founded and loves and has been deeply concerned about throughout this letter. The depth of the relationship Paul had with the communities he founded is evident in his desire not to pass through but to stay — to be present long enough for real ministry and real mutual encouragement.

A great door for ministry has opened here even though there is strong opposition — the combination of great opportunity and strong opposition is characteristic of Paul's missionary experience. He does not treat opposition as a sign that he is in the wrong place. He treats opportunity and opposition as the normal conditions of genuine Gospel advance. The door that has opened in Ephesus — almost certainly related to the three-year Ephesian ministry described in Acts 19 — was producing both remarkable results and remarkable resistance. Both were reasons to stay rather than to leave.

If the Master permits — Paul's consistent qualification of his travel plans with the Master's permission is not a rhetorical device. It reflects his genuine conviction that the Spirit directs the mission — sometimes closing doors that Paul wanted to walk through and opening ones he had not planned on. The second missionary journey's redirections in Acts 16 — blocked from Asia blocked from Bithynia then the Macedonian vision — were the formative experience of this conviction. Paul planned and the Lord directed and Paul followed the Lord's direction.

Why It Still Matters

A great door for ministry has opened here even though there is strong opposition. Paul's pairing of opportunity and opposition is one of the most useful pastoral observations in Chapter 16. Communities and individuals who are expecting ministry to flow without resistance are consistently surprised when opposition arrives. Paul treats opposition not as the sign that something has gone wrong but as the companion that almost always accompanies genuine Gospel advance. The great door and the strong opposition were not in tension for Paul. They were two features of the same landscape — the landscape of effective mission in a world that does not welcome the Gospel without resistance.

Timothy and Apollos

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

If Timothy comes make sure he feels welcome among them — he is doing the Master's work just as Paul is. No one should look down on him. Send him on his way in peace so he can return to Paul as Paul is expecting him along with the other brothers. As for their brother Apollos Paul strongly encouraged him to visit with the other brothers. He was not ready to come just yet but he will visit when the time is right.

What You Might Not Know

Make sure he feels welcome — don't let anyone look down on him — Paul's concern for Timothy's reception in Corinth reveals something about the dynamics of the Corinthian community. Timothy was younger than many in the community and apparently less naturally commanding than Paul. In a community that valued impressive rhetoric and spiritual authority Timothy may have been at risk of being dismissed or treated as a junior figure rather than as Paul's genuine representative. Paul's instruction to not look down on him is the same instruction he would give in 1 Timothy — let no one despise your youth. Timothy's youth and temperament were apparently real vulnerabilities in the Corinthian context.

He is doing the Master's work just as I am — Paul identifies Timothy's work with his own — not because Timothy is Paul's equal in apostolic authority but because both are doing the same work for the same Master. The work is not Paul's work — it is the Master's work being done through Paul and through Timothy. This framing both elevates Timothy's status — he shares Paul's commission — and relativizes Paul's status — he is a servant doing the Master's work just as Timothy is. The Corinthians who had been ranking apostles against each other needed to understand that all of the apostolic workers were doing the same Master's work.

Apollos was not ready to come just yet — the relationship between Paul and Apollos at this point in the story is interesting given that some Corinthians had aligned themselves with Apollos against Paul. Paul's strong encouragement of Apollos to visit and Apollos's apparent reluctance suggests that Apollos himself was troubled by the way his name was being used in the Corinthian factions. His decision to wait until the time was right rather than arriving in the middle of the conflict reflects a pastoral wisdom consistent with what Acts 18 shows us about his character.

Why It Still Matters

He is doing the Master's work just as I am. Paul's identification of Timothy's work with his own ministry cuts through the hierarchy of spiritual importance that the Corinthians had constructed. The same principle applies in every generation — the person who is doing the Master's work faithfully in whatever capacity they have been given deserves the same respect as the most prominent apostle. The community that learns to recognize and honor faithful service regardless of the profile of the person doing it has begun to understand what Paul has been teaching throughout the letter about the body of Christ and the Spirit's diverse distribution of gifts.

Final Encouragements

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

Stay alert. Stand firm in the faith. Be brave. Be strong. Let everything you do be done in love. The household of Stephanas were the first believers in Achaia and have dedicated themselves to serving God's people. Paul urges the brothers and sisters to follow the lead of people like them and everyone who joins in the hard work of ministry. He was overjoyed when Stephanas Fortunatus and Achaicus arrived — they made up for the Corinthians' absence and gave his spirit a real lift. Make sure you recognize people like that for what they are worth.

What You Might Not Know

Stay alert — stand firm — be brave — be strong — the four commands Paul issues in rapid succession are drawn from the language of military readiness. The Greek words — grēgoreite stēkete andrizesthe krataiousthe — describe a soldier on watch standing their post showing courage and maintaining strength. Paul applies this military vocabulary to the community's spiritual life — the community is in a genuine struggle and the qualities that produce victory in battle are the qualities needed to maintain faithfulness in the face of opposition temptation and the pressures of the surrounding culture.

Let everything you do be done in love — the single qualifying command that follows the four military commands is the same word that has governed the entire letter. After all the warnings corrections instructions and arguments the final word is love. The community that is alert standing firm brave and strong must do all of it in love. Without love the military virtues become hardness rigidity and aggression. With love they become the qualities of a community that is genuinely committed to the wellbeing of everyone in it. The love that was greater than all the gifts in Chapter 13 is the governing principle of all the virtues in Chapter 16.

The household of Stephanas — they were the first believers in Achaia — Stephanas appears at the beginning of the letter where Paul thanks God he baptized the household of Stephanas. Now at the end of the letter Paul commends the entire household for their service to God's people. The first converts in the region had become some of the most faithful servants. Their dedication to serving God's people — diaconia — was the practical expression of the love Paul has been calling the whole community toward. Paul holds them up not as celebrities but as examples of the kind of faithful unglamorous service that builds the body.

They gave my spirit a real lift — the Greek is anepausant to emon pneuma meaning they refreshed or gave rest to my spirit. Paul the apostle who faced death every day who worked harder than all the others who prayed and suffered and planted and watered — needed refreshment. The arrival of Stephanas Fortunatus and Achaicus from Corinth gave him what he needed. The ministry flows in both directions — the community sustains the minister as well as the minister sustaining the community. Recognizing this mutuality and honoring the people who provide it is itself a form of the love Paul has been commending throughout the letter.

Why It Still Matters

Let everything you do be done in love. Paul's final instruction before his greetings is the summary of the entire letter in five words. Everything he has addressed — the factions the sexual immorality the lawsuits the Lord's Supper the spiritual gifts the resurrection — all of it would have been different if love had been governing the community from the beginning. And everything he calls them toward — unity mutual service orderly worship faithful generosity standing firm — all of it must be done in love or it becomes something less than what Paul intends. The letter that began with grace and peace ends with love as the governing principle of the entire Christian life.

Final Greetings

◀ 📖 Return to 1 Corinthians Chapter 16
What Is Happening Here

The churches in Asia send their greetings. Aquila and Prisca send warm greetings in the Master along with the church that meets in their home. All the brothers and sisters here send their greetings. Greet one another with a holy kiss. I Paul am writing this greeting with my own hand. If anyone does not love the Master let them be cut off. Come Master Jesus — come quickly. May the grace of the Master Jesus be with you. My love to all of you in Christ Jesus.

What You Might Not Know

Aquila and Prisca send warm greetings — the couple last seen in Acts 18 in Corinth and Ephesus are still in Ephesus with a church meeting in their home. Paul is writing from Ephesus and the couple who had first hosted Paul in Corinth and had taught Apollos in Ephesus are now sending their own greetings to the community where they had originally met Paul. The network of relationships that held the early church together across geography is visible in this single verse — the same people appearing in Corinth Ephesus and Rome across Paul's letters binding the scattered communities into one family.

I Paul am writing this greeting with my own hand — Paul normally dictated his letters to a scribe as we saw with Tertius in Romans 16. At the close of letters he would sometimes take the pen himself to write the final greeting in his own handwriting — providing a kind of personal authentication that could not be forged. The contrast between the dictated body of the letter and the handwritten closing was the ancient equivalent of a personal signature. The Galatians and Colossians also received personal handwritten closings from Paul.

If anyone does not love the Master let them be cut off — the Greek is anathema meaning cursed or devoted to destruction. This sharp statement coming at the end of a letter addressed to people Paul has called his dear children is startling. It echoes Galatians 1 where Paul pronounces anathema on anyone who preaches a different Gospel. The love of the Master is the non-negotiable center of Christian identity. The community that has been called to let everything be done in love must understand that the love of the Master himself is the foundation of everything. Without it the rest is performance.

Come Master Jesus — come quickly — the Greek is Marana tha — an Aramaic phrase preserved untranslated in this Greek letter. It is one of the earliest Christian prayers preserved in any document — the longing cry of the early community for the return of their Lord. The Aramaic suggests it was a prayer already in use in the earliest Aramaic-speaking Christian communities that predated Paul's Greek-speaking mission. Its preservation in a Greek letter to a Greek-speaking community shows how deeply embedded this prayer was in the common life of the earliest Church. Every celebration of the Lord's Supper was accompanied by this longing — the community announcing the Lord's death until he comes back and praying for that coming.

Why It Still Matters

Come Master Jesus — come quickly. The prayer that closes 1 Corinthians is the same prayer that closes the entire New Testament — Revelation's final words are the same cry in Greek — come Lord Jesus. The community that has worked through everything Paul has addressed in sixteen chapters — the factions the immorality the lawsuits the Lord's Supper the gifts the resurrection — ends not with a checklist of accomplished corrections but with a prayer for the one whose return will make all correction complete. The Church is not ultimately a project to be perfected through correct teaching and disciplined practice. It is a community waiting for a person. The waiting shapes everything else. Come Master Jesus.
